Abstract

Context

cAMP-induced intestinal chloride secretion plays a pivotal role in the pathogenesis of secretory diarrheas.

Objective

In this study, we investigated the antisecretory effects of α,β-dehydromonacolin K, a derivative of lovastatin from Aspergillus sclerotiorum, on cAMP-induced chloride secretion in human T84 cells and fluid secretion in human colonoids.

Materials and Methods

Short-circuit current analyses and swelling assays were used to investigate the effects of α,β-dehydromonacolin K on chloride transport and fluid secretion, respectively. Proteomic analyses were performed to determine the potential anti-diarrheal mechanisms of α,β-dehydromonacolin K.

Results

In T84 cells, α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibited cAMP-induced chloride secretion with an IC50 of ∼ 6.32 μM. Apical chloride current analyses demonstrated that α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibited CFTR chloride channels stimulated by cAMP agonists with an IC50 of ∼ 1 μM. Basolateral potassium current analyses indicated that α,β-dehydromonacolin K had no effect on basolateral potassium channel activities. In a three-dimensional (3D) model of human colonoids, α,β-dehydromonacolin K (20 µM) suppressed both cAMP-induced and calcium-induced fluid secretion by ∼ 70%. Proteomic analyses of human colonoids revealed that α,β-dehydromonacolin K interacted with 33 proteins, including those associated with non-sense-mediated mRNA decay (NMD). Notably, the inhibitory effects of α,β-dehydromonacolin K on cAMP-induced chloride and fluid secretion were significantly diminished in the presence of SMG1i, an inhibitor of serine/threonine-protein kinase SMG1 involved in NMD, suggesting that α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibits cAMP-induced chloride-driven fluid secretion in human intestinal epithelial cells by mechanisms involving SMG1-dependent NMD pathways.

Discussion and conclusions

α, β-Dehydromonacolin K represents a promising class of natural compounds that exert antisecretory effects in human intestinal epithelia via a novel mechanism of action involving SMG1 in NMD pathways.

Keywords: α,β-Dehydromonacolin K; diarrhea; CFTR; human colonoid; NMD

Introduction

Secretory diarrhea is a common intestinal disorder that causes morbidity and mortality in children with a mortality rate of approximately 78.4 deaths per 100,000 (Guerrant et al. 2013). Common causes of secretory diarrheas are intestinal infections with pathogens and adverse effects of drugs including anticancer drugs. Anticancer agents cause diarrheas in 60% of patients, with approximately 10% of patients experiencing severe diarrheas (Stein et al. 2010). The main driver of secretory diarrhea is the overactivity of cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator (CFTR), a cAMP-dependent chloride channel expressed in the apical membranes of intestinal epithelial cells (Keely and Barrett 2022). Excessive flow of chloride through CFTR leads to efflux of sodium (Na+) and water into the intestinal lumen, resulting in intestinal fluid secretion. This ultimately leads to significant fluid loss, dehydration, and, in severe instances, hypovolemic shock (Muanprasat and Chatsudthipong 2013; Thiagarajah et al. 2015). Mainstay therapy of secretory diarrheas is an oral rehydration solution, which has limited efficacy in patients with severe diarrhea or compromised health status. Therefore, CFTR inhibitors have been proposed as potential adjunctive therapies of secretory diarrheas. Recent studies using both in vitro and in vivo models have demonstrated the therapeutic potential of various CFTR inhibitors derived from synthetic chemical libraries and natural resources for the treatment of secretory diarrheas (Ma et al. 2002; Muanprasat et al. 2004; Cil et al. 2017; Akrimajirachoote et al. 2020). Although several compounds have been discovered and demonstrated to hold promise for the treatment of secretory diarrheas, identification and evaluation of novel anti-diarrheal drug candidates, especially anti-secretory agents from natural sources, are still required to offer novel drug candidates, ensuring the successful development of anti-secretory therapy.

Lovastatin, a natural compound derived from red yeast rice, is produced by fermenting cooked rice with Monascus purpureus mold. This compound has a long-standing history in East Asian countries, where it has traditionally been used to enhance blood circulation, alleviate indigestion and diarrheas, and reduce muscle weakness (Zhu et al. 2019). Interestingly, α,β-dehydromonacolin K (Figure 1(A)), a lovastatin derivative, has been shown to possess anti-diarrheal effects in vitro and in vivo (Noitem et al. 2022). It was isolated from the soil-derived fungus Aspergillus sclerotiorum (Phainuphong et al. 2016). However, the anti-secretory effect and exact molecular mechanism of action of α,β-dehydromonacolin K in the human intestinal epithelial cells remain unknown.

Human mini-intestines, referred to as human enteroids/colonoids (miniature models of the small intestinal and colonic epithelia, respectively), are derived from crypt cells that contain a mixture of cell types (Sato et al. 2011). Studies have shown that the functions of human enteroids/colonoids closely mimic those of human intestinal epithelial cells, including their capabilities for intestinal fluid secretion, sodium-hydrogen exchange activity, and glucose absorption (Foulke-Abel et al. 2016; Cil et al. 2017; Hasan et al. 2021). Therefore, the human enteroid/colonoid model is particularly suitable for investigating intestinal function, particularly fluid secretion. This study aimed to explore the anti-secretory effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K synthesized from lovastatin in T84 cells, a human colon cancer cell line suitable for studying chloride secretion, and in human colonoids, in which its effect on fluid secretion and mechanisms of action were investigated using both functional assays and proteomic analyses.

Materials and methods

Materials

α,β-Dehydromonacolin K was synthesized from lovastatin by the following method. Acetic anhydride (6 equiv.) and triethylamine (77 equiv.) were added to a solution of lovastatin (1 equiv.) in dichloromethane (0.4 M) at 0 °C. The reaction mixture was then stirred overnight at room temperature. The completion of the reaction was monitored using thin-layer chromatography. The mixture was diluted with dichloromethane and washed with sodium bicarbonate and water. The organic layer was dried over anhydrous Na2SO4, filtered, and concentrated under reduced pressure to give the crude product, which was purified using column chromatography (20% EtOAc/hexane) to give α,β-dehydromonacolin K as a white solid (91% yield). The structure was confirmed by 1H and 13C NMR data, which are in accordance with those previously reported by Vederas et al. (Sorensen et al. 2003). The 1H and 13C NMR data of α,β-dehydromonacolin K are in Supplemental File 1. CFTRinh-172 was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (catalog number 219670, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA). Isobutylmethylxanthine (IBMX) was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (catalog number 410957, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA). 8-(4-chlorophenylthio) adenosine 3′,5′-cyclic monophosphate (CPT-cAMP) was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (catalog number C3912, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA). Genistein was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (catalog number 345834, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA). Compound C was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (catalog number 171260, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA). Sodium orthovanadate was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (catalog number 567540, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA). Sodium fluoride was purchased from Merck Millipore (catalog number 106449, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany). GlyH-101 was purchased from R&D Systems (catalog number 5485, R&D Systems, Minneapolis, MN, USA). SMG1i was purchased from MedChemExpress (catalog number HY-124719, MedChemExpress, Monmouth Junction, NJ, USA). Carbachol was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (catalog number 212385, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA).

Ethics statement

According to the International Conference on Harmonization in Good Clinical Practice (ICH-GCP), the Belmont Report, the CIOMS Guidelines, the Declaration of Helsinki, and other international guidelines for human research protection, this study has been approved by the Faculty of Medicine Ramathibodi Hospital’s Human Research Ethics Committee (COA number MURA2024/212) on March 18, 2024.

Cell lines

T84 cells and L-WRN cells were from American Type Culture Collection (ATCC) (catalog number CCL-248, catalog number CRL-3276, respectively; Manassas, VA, USA). Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium F12 (DMEM/F12), supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (catalog number F7524, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) and 1% penicillin–streptomycin (catalog number 15140122, Gibco, Waltham, MA, USA), was used to sustain T84 cells. High glucose Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) supplemented with 0.5 mg/mL of G418 (catalog number 11811031, Gibco, Waltham, MA, USA) and 0.5 mg/mL of Hygromycin B (catalog number 10687010, Gibco, Waltham, MA, USA) was used to maintain L-WRN cells to produce human colonoid-conditioned media. The ATCC guidelines were followed for the preparation of the conditioned medium.

Crypt isolation for human colonoid culture

Human colon tissues were obtained from Faculty of Medicine, Ramathibodi Hospital, Mahidol University. As previously stated, tissues from the human colon were collected and crypt isolation was carried out with a few adjustments (Foulke-Abel et al. 2016). Briefly, human colons were sliced into tiny pieces and cleaned using the whole chelated solution, which contained 5.55 mM Na2HPO4 (catalog number 106559, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), 7.9 mM KH2PO4 (catalog number 104873, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), 95.82 mM NaCl (catalog number 106404, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), 1.6 mM KCl (catalog number 104936, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), 43.82 mM sucrose (catalog number 107651, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), 54.89 mM D-sorbitol (catalog number 56755, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), and 0.5 mM dithiothreitol (catalog number 10197777001, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA). Crypts from human colons were subsequently isolated by 0.5 M EDTA and formed into 3D colonoids in the Matrigel® (catalog number 356237, Corning, NY, USA) mixed with enteroid-growing media containing L-WRN-conditioned media, 1X B27 (catalog number 17504044, Gibco, Waltham, MA, USA), 1 mM N-acetyl cysteine (catalog number A9165, Sigma- Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA), 100 µg/mL Primocin (catalog number ant-pm-1, Invivogen, San Diego, CA, USA), 50 ng/mL EGF (catalog number 236-EG-200, R&D Systems, Minneapolis, MN, USA), 10 nM gastrin (catalog number G9145, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA), 10 µM SB202190 (catalog number 1264/10, Tocris Bioscience, Minneapolis, MN, USA), and 500 nM A83-01 (catalog number 2939, Tocris Bioscience, Minneapolis, MN, USA). Human colonoids were used for experiments after more than ten passages.

Cell viability assay

Viability of T84 cells was assessed using MTT assays, specifically the 3-(4,5-dimethyl-2-thiazolyl)−2,5-diphenyl-2H-tetrazolium bromide method (catalog number M5655, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA). In brief, T84 cells were seeded at a density of 1 × 105 cells per well in 96-well plates and allowed to grow overnight. On the following day, cells were incubated for 24 h with culture media containing either 0.5% DMSO (vehicle; catalog number 102952, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), α,β-dehydromonacolin K at concentrations ranging from 1 to 100 µM, or 10% DMSO (catalog number 102952, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany) as a positive control. After 24-h incubation, the media were removed and replaced with culture media containing MTT reagent (0.5 mg/mL) for 4 h at 37 °C. Subsequently, 100 µL of DMSO was added to each well to stop the reaction. The absorbance was measured at 570 nm using the Synergy Neo2 Multi-Mode Microplate Reader (BioTek Instruments, VT, USA) to quantify the colorimetric results.

Electrophysiological experiments

T84 cells were cultured on Snapwell inserts (catalog number 3801; Corning, NY, USA) to optimize their growth and functionality. With media changes every other day, these cells matured for at least seven days, achieving a remarkable transepithelial electrical resistance greater than 1,000 Ω cm2, as confirmed by an EVOM2 volt-ohm meter (World Precision Instruments, Sarasota, Florida, USA). These robust cells were carefully transferred to Ussing chambers (Physiologic Instruments, Reno, Nevada, USA) for further analysis.

To measure the short-circuit current (Isc), Kreb’s buffer was prepared with a pH of 7.4 using the following components: 120 mM NaCl (catalog number 106404, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), 25 mM NaHCO3 (catalog number 106329, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), 3.3 mM KH2PO4 (catalog number 104873, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), 0.8 mM K2HPO4 (catalog number 105104, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), 1.2 mM MgCl2 (catalog number 814733, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA), 1.2 mM CaCl2 (catalog number 102378, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), and 10 mM glucose (catalog number 108337, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany). These components were used as bathing solutions.

To analyze the apical chloride current (ICl-), high- and low-chloride solutions were introduced into the basolateral and apical hemichambers, respectively, to establish a basolateral-to-apical chloride gradient. The high-chloride solution (pH 7.4) consisted of 130 mM NaCl (catalog number 106404, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), 2.7 mM KCl (catalog number 104936, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), 1.5 mM KH2PO4 (catalog number 104873, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), 1 mM CaCl2 (catalog number 102378, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany), 0.5 mM MgCl2 (catalog number 814733, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA), 10 mM Na-HEPES (catalog number H3784, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA), and 10 mM glucose (catalog number 108337, Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany). In the low-chloride solution (pH 7.4), 65 mM NaCl was replaced with 65 mM Na-gluconate (catalog number 822058, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA), and the concentration of CaCl2 was increased from 1 mM to 2 mM. To permeabilize the basolateral membrane of T84 cells, amphotericin B (250 µg/mL) was added to the basolateral solution and incubated for at least 20 min before measuring ICl-.

To analyze the basolateral potassium current (IK+), high-K and low-K solutions were added to the apical and basolateral hemichambers, respectively, to establish an apical-to-basolateral potassium gradient. The high potassium solution (pH 7.4) consisted of 145 mM Na-gluconate, 5 mM KCl, 1 mM CaCl2, 1 mM MgCl2, 5 mM Na-HEPES, and 10 mM glucose. In the low-potassium solution (pH 7.4), 145 mM Na-gluconate was replaced with 145 mM K-gluconate (catalog number 820601, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA). To permeabilize the basolateral membrane of T84 cells, amphotericin B (250 µg/mL) (catalog number A9528, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) was added to the basolateral solution and incubated for at least 20 min before measuring ICl-.

Swelling assay

Human colonoids were seeded onto 48-well plates containing Matrigel and cultured for 2 days. Following this, the colonoids were treated for 1 h with α,β-dehydromonacolin K at concentrations of 1, 2, 5, 10, and 20 µM, or with DMSO as a control. After treatment, forskolin (5 µM) (catalog number F6886, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) or carbachol (100 µM) (catalog number C4382, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) was added to stimulate the chloride-driven fluid secretion. GlyH-101 (50 μM) was used as positive control. Images were captured every ten minutes from areas containing at least ten colonoids each, using a Cytation 5 Cell Imaging Multi-Mode Reader (Biotek, Santa Clara, CA, USA). The area of each colonoid in the images was measured using ImageJ (Schindelin et al. 2012). Colonoid swelling was measured by assessing the area of the colonoid two hours after stimulation with a stimulator (forskolin or carbachol) relative to its initial size. The average relative swelling was calculated.

Lable-free quantification of the α,β-dehydromonacolin K treated human enteroids using proteomics approaches

Human colonoids were treated with or without 5 µM forskolin, in combination with various concentrations of α,β-dehydromonacolin K (1, 2, 5, 10, and 20 µM). Treated human enteroids (n = 3 biological replications/group, total 21 samples) were lysed in RIPA buffer and protein amounts were quantified using Bradford’s assay.

Fifty micrograms of protein were then reduced by DTT and alkylated by IAA before tryptic digestion. The peptides were desalted on a C18 stage tip before being dissolved in 0.1% formic acid, and 5 µg was injected into an Eksigent nanoLC ultra-nanoflow high-performance liquid chromatograph coupled with a Triple-TOF 6600+ (ABSciex, Toronto, Canada) mass spectrometer in information-dependent acquisition and data-independent acquisition modes (technical duplication/mode/sample, total 42 acquisitions/mode). The peptides were loaded onto a C18 trap column (NanoTrap RP-1, 3 μm 120 Å, 10 mm × 0.075 mm; Phenomenex, CA, USA) at a flow rate of 3 μl/min in 0.1% formic acid for 10 min to desorb and concentrate the sample before separation on a C18 analytical column (bioZen Peptide Polar C18 nanocolumn, 75 μm × 15 cm, C18 particle sizes of 3 μm, 120 Å; Phenomenex) with a gradient of 3-30% acetonitrile (ACN)/0.1%formic acid for 60 min, following 30-40%ACN/0.1% formic acid for 10 min, 40-80% ACN/0.1%formic acid for 2 min, isocratic 80%ACN/0.1%formic acid for 6 min, 80-3%ACN/0.1%formic acid for 2 min, and isocratic 3%ACN/0.1%formic acid for 25 min. The eluate was ionized and sprayed onto a mass spectrometer using an OptiFlow Turbo V Source (ABSciex). Ion source gas 1 (GS1), ion source gas 2 (GS2), and the curtain gas were set at 19, 0, and 25 vendor units, respectively. The interface heater temperature and ion spray voltage were maintained at 150 °C and 3.3 kV, respectively.

Mass spectrometry was performed in the positive ion mode for 3,500 cycles with a 105-minute gradient elution. Each cycle underwent one time-of-flight (TOF) scan (250 ms accumulation time, 350–1250 m/z window with a charge state of +2), followed by IDA of the 100 most intense ions, and the minimum MS signal was set to 150 counts. The MS/MS scan was operated in high-sensitivity mode with a 50 ms accumulation time and 50 ppm mass tolerance. The former MS/MS candidate ions were excluded for a period of 12 s after their first occurrence to reduce redundancy of the identified peptides. The DIA mode was performed in the 350–1500 m/z range using a predefined mass window of 7 m/z with an overlap of 1 m/z for 157 transmissible windows. The MS scan was set to 2,044 cycles, and each cycle performed one TOF-MS scan (50 ms accumulation time across the 100–1500 precursor mass range) acquired in every cycle for a total cycle time of 3.08 sec. MS spectra at 100–1500 m/z were collected with an accumulation time of 96 ms per SWATH window. The resolution for the MS1 scan was 35,000 and that for SWATH-MS2 was 30,000. Rolling collision energy mode was applied with a collision energy spread of 15 eV. IDA and DIA data (.wiff) were recorded using Analyst-TF v.1.8 software (ABSciex).

Forty-two wiff files of IDA experiments were combined and searched using Protein Pilot v.5.0.2.0 software (ABSciex) against the Swiss-Prot database (UniProtKB 2022_01) Homo sapiens (20,385 proteins in the database) with the following search parameters: alkylation of cysteine by iodoacetamide, trypsin enzymatic digestion, 1 missed cleavage allowed, monoisotopic mass, and 1% false discovery rate. The group file (Protein Pilot search result) was loaded into SWATH Acquisition MicroApp v.2.0.1.2133 in PeakView software v.2.2 (Sciex) to generate a spectral library. The maximum number of proteins was set as those identified at 1% global FDR from the fit. RT alignment was performed using highly abundant endogenous peptides that covered the chromatographic range. SWATH data extraction of 42 DIA files (6 files/group) was performed using the SWATH Acquisition MicroApp (ABSciex) with the following parameters: 10-min extraction window, 25 peptides/protein, six transitions/peptide, excluding shared peptides, >99% peptide confidence, <1% FDR, and 20 ppm XIC width. SWATH extraction data, including the identities and quantities of peptides and proteins, were exported to Marker View (ABSciex). The protein area was normalized using multiple linear regression, and differential protein expression was calculated using Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) and fold-change in protein area. Protein interaction and pathway analyses were performed using STRING (https://string-db.org) and Reactome (https://reactome.org/) databases, respectively.

Western blot analysis

T84 cells were seeded onto six-well plates. Cells were treated with or without α,β-dehydromonacolin K for 2 h and proteins were collected using RIPA buffer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) with protease inhibitor (catalog number 05892970001, Roche, Basel, Switzerland) and phosphatase inhibitor (Roche, Basel, Switzerland). Twenty micrograms of proteins were loaded into an SDS-polyacrylamide gel and subsequently transferred to a nitrocellulose membrane (catalog number 1620115, Biorad, Hercules, CA, USA). The membrane was divided into sections according to the molecular weight of the proteins and then blocked with 5% nonfat dry milk (catalog number 1706404, Biorad, Hercules, CA, USA) for an hour. The proteins were incubated overnight with either rabbit CFTR antibody (dilution 1:1000) (catalog number 78335, Cell Signaling, Danvers, MA, USA) or rabbit β-actin antibody (dilution 1:1000) on a separate membrane (catalog number 4970, Cell Signaling, Danvers, MA, USA). Membrane washing was performed using Tris-Boric Saline with 0.05% Tween 20. The membranes were then incubated with anti-rabbit IgG conjugated to horseradish peroxidase (dilution 1:10000) (catalog number ab6721, Abcam, Cambridge, UK) and treated with Clarity Western ECL Substrate (catalog number 1705061, Biorad, Hercules, CA, USA). The membranes were exposed to a Chemi-Doc (Biorad, Hercules, CA, USA) for imaging, and band intensity was analyzed using Fiji ImageJ (Schindelin et al. 2012).

Statistical analysis

Data are presented as the mean ± S.E.M. One-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used to compare and analyze the groups, followed by Tukey’s post hoc test. A p-value < 0.05 was considered statistically significant. All data were analyzed using GraphPad Prism 5 (La Jolla, CA, USA). Principal component analysis and heat maps were generated using SRplot (www.bioinformatics.com.cn)(Tang et al. 2023).

Results

Cytotoxic effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K and effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K on the cAMP-induced chloride secretion in T84 cells

The cytotoxic effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K was evaluated in T84 cells using MTT assays. As shown in Figure 1(B), after 24-h incubation, α,β-dehydromonacolin K did not affect T84 cell viability at concentrations from 1 µM to 20 µM. However, α,β-dehydromonacolin K at concentrations of 50 µM and 100 µM significantly reduced cell viability by ∼ 30% and 50%, respectively. Based on this result, subsequent experiments were performed using α,β-dehydromonacolin K at concentrations of not more than 50 µM. The effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K on cAMP-induced chloride secretion across T84 cells was evaluated using short-circuit current (Isc) analyses. Chloride secretion was stimulated by forskolin (20 µM), an adenylate cyclase activator. In this experiment, the forskolin-induced Isc was attributed to CFTR, as evidenced by the near-complete inhibition observed after an addition of a specific CFTR inhibitor, CFTRinh-172, as shown in the left panel of Figure 1(C). As shown in the right panel of Figure 1(C), the accumulative additions of α,β-dehydromonacolin K in both the apical and basolateral solutions resulted in the concentration-dependent inhibition of forskolin-induced Isc. The half-maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) of α,β-dehydromonacolin K was ∼ 6.32 µM with maximal inhibition (>80%) being observed at 50 µM (Figure 1(D)). Furthermore, as shown in Figure 1(E), α,β-dehydromonacolin K at concentrations from 0.1 µM to 5 µM did not affect basal Isc in T84 cells. These findings indicated that α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibited cAMP-induced chloride secretion in T84 cells without affecting basal Isc in T84 cells.

Mechanism of action of α,β-dehydromonacolin K in inhibition of cAMP-induced chloride secretion

To investigate the mechanism by which α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibited cAMP-induced chloride secretion, T84 cells were basolaterally permeabilized with amphotericin B (250 µg/mL) and bathed with basolateral-to-apical chloride gradient buffers to establish a chloride gradient, which allowed measurement of the apical chloride current (ICl-) (Figure 2(A)). To evaluate the effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K on CFTR transport activities, cells were stimulated with forskolin (20 µM) to induce CFTR-mediated apical ICl-, which was inhibited by α,β-dehydromonacolin K in a concentration-dependent manner with an IC50 of 0.85 µM and near complete inhibition (∼85%) was obtained at 5 µM (Figure 2(B)).

It is well established that phosphodiesterase (PDE) is responsible for degrading cAMP into AMP (O’Grady et al. 2002). To investigate whether α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibited chloride secretion via the PDE, apical ICl- was stimulated by a combination of forskolin and isobutylmethylxanthine (IBMX), a PDE inhibitor. As illustrated in Figure 2(C), α,β-dehydromonacolin K effectively inhibited apical ICl- stimulated by forskolin plus IBMX (IC50 = 1.09 µM), indicating that the mechanism of CFTR inhibition by α,β-dehydromonacolin K did not involve PDE.

In addition, apical ICl- was stimulated by 8-(4-chlorophenylthio) adenosine-3′,5′-cyclic monophosphate (CPT-cAMP), a non-metabolizable cAMP analog, to test the hypothesis that CFTR inhibition occurs downstream of intracellular cAMP metabolism. As depicted in Figure 2(D), α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibited the cAMP-induced apical ICl- with an IC50 of 1.46 µM, indicating that α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibited CFTR via mechanisms that did not involve perturbation of intracellular cAMP metabolism. Genistein, a direct CFTR activator, was also used to induce CFTR-mediated apical ICl-. As shown in Figure 2(E), α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibited the genistein-induced apical ICl- with an IC50 of 4.35 µM, which was higher than that obtained from previous experiments, suggesting that α,β-dehydromonacolin K may inhibit CFTR function in part through mechanisms involving altered intracellular signaling pathways.

It has been established that phosphatases negatively regulate CFTR activities through the dephosphorylation of CFTR (Travis et al. 1997). To investigate whether α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibited CFTR via phosphatases, T84 cells were pre-treated with a phosphatase inhibitor cocktail containing sodium orthovanadate (Na3VO4) and sodium fluoride (NaF) before stimulating CFTR-mediated apical ICl- with forskolin. Figure 2(F) shows that α,β-dehydromonacolin K significantly inhibited forskolin-induced apical ICl- in the presence of a phosphatase inhibitor cocktail, with an IC50 of 0.95 µM. This finding suggests that the mechanism of CFTR inhibition by α,β-dehydromonacolin K may not be via CFTR dephosphorylation.

AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) is known to negatively regulates CFTR activities (Kongsuphol et al. 2009). To investigate whether α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibited CFTR through AMPK, T84 cells were pre-treated with compound C (20 μM), an AMPK inhibitor, before stimulation of CFTR-mediated apical ICl- with forskolin. As shown in Figure 2(G), α,β-dehydromonacolin K suppressed the forskolin-induced apical ICl- in the presence of compound C with an IC50 of 2.06 µM, indicating that α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibited CFTR via mechanisms not directly involving AMPK. In addition, we confirmed that the apical ICl- induced by forskolin was mediated by CFTR using CFTRinh-172 in Figure 2(H).

No effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K on basolateral potassium channels

To investigate the effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K on calcium-activated potassium channels (i.e., Kca3.1), T84 cells were apically permeabilized with amphotericin B (250 µg/mL) and bathed with apical-to-basolateral potassium solutions to allow basolateral IK+ analyses, reflecting basolateral potassium channel activities, as illustrated in Figure 3(A). Figure 3(B) shows that basolateral IK+ induced by NS309, a Kca3.1 activator, was not significantly inhibited by α,β-dehydromonacolin K at concentrations up to 15 µM. TRAM-34 (20 µM), a Kca3.1 channel inhibitor, was used as a positive control. The effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K on cAMP-induced basolateral potassium channels (KCNQ4) was investigated using a similar approach. As shown in Figure 3(C), α,β-dehydromonacolin K did not significantly inhibit the basolateral IK+ induced by CPT-cAMP using BaCl2, a KCNQ4 channel inhibitor, as a positive control. These results indicated that α,β-dehydromonacolin K did not inhibit calcium- or cAMP-induced basolateral potassium channels in the intestinal epithelial cells.

Anti-secretory effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K in a 3D human colonoid model

To examine the anti-secretory effects of α,β-dehydromonacolin K on cAMP-fluid secretion in human colonoids, human colonoids derived from colon tissues were treated with vehicle, GlyH-101 (50 µM; a known CFTR inhibitor), or dehydromonacolin K at various concentrations ranging from 1 µM to 20 µM for an hour before incubation for 2 h with forskolin (5 µM). As illustrated in Figure 4(A), forskolin induced swelling in the colonoids, indicating fluid secretion, whereas vehicle-treated colonoids showed no swelling. Notably, α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibited forskolin-induced colonoid swelling in a concentration-dependent manner, with approximately 70% inhibition being observed at a concentration of 20 µM (Figure 4(B)).

The effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K on fluid secretion induced by carbachol (100 µM), a muscarinic receptor agonist, in human colonoids was investigated using a similar protocol. Carbachol is known to enhance fluid secretion via calcium-induced CFTR activation (Billet et al. 2013). Figure 5(A) shows that carbachol induced swelling in the colonoids, which was inhibited by pretreatment with α,β-dehydromonacolin K in a concentration-dependent manner, with ∼ 90% inhibition being observed at 20 µM (Figure 5(B)). GlyH-101 was used as a positive control. These findings suggested that α,β-dehydromonacolin K exerted an anti-secretory effect on human colonoids under conditions of CFTR-mediated fluid hypersecretion.

Identification of potential targets of α,β-dehydromonacolin K using proteomics analyses in a 3D human colonoid model

To examine the differential protein expression resulted from treatment with α,β-dehydromonacolin K in a 3D human colonoid model, samples were collected following the completion of previous experiments and SWATH proteomics was employed to quantify the levels of protein expression. Thirty-three proteins showed significant differential expressions (Supplementary Table S1). The distribution and alterations in the levels of these proteins are depicted in the principal component analysis and heatmap as shown in Figure 6(A). Furthermore, the analysis of protein interactions and the Reactome pathways for the 33 differentially expressed proteins in α,β-dehydromonacolin K-treated human colonoids is illustrated in Figure 6(B). To elucidate the functional connections among the 33 differentially expressed proteins, we constructed a protein-protein interaction network using the STRING database. This analysis revealed a prominent cluster of interacting proteins (Figure 6(B), left panel, highlighted in the red frame). Subsequent pathway enrichment analysis of this protein cluster using the Reactome database identified the nonsense-mediated decay (NMD) pathway as a significantly enriched pathway (Figure 6(B), right panel, highlighted in the blue frames). Taken together, these results indicate that α,β-dehydromonacolin K modulates proteins within the NMD pathway, which is crucial for post-transcriptional mRNA quality control (Baker and Parker 2004; Rehwinkel et al. 2005; Hug et al. 2016).

Functional validation of NMD pathways in mediating the anti-secretory effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K

Since inhibition of NMD pathways has been shown to lead to upregulation of CFTR expression (Sharma et al. 2018; McHugh et al. 2020). We speculated that α,β-dehydromonacolin K might inhibit CFTR-mediated fluid secretion through an NMD pathway. Specifically, since a serine/threonine-protein kinase SMG1 is a key protein that has been shown to play an important role in regulating CFTR mRNA via an NMD pathway (Sanderlin et al. 2022). We hypothesized that α,β-dehydromonacolin K may inhibit CFTR-mediated chloride secretion and fluid secretion via SMG1. To test this hypothesis, T84 cells and human colonoids were preincubated with an SMG1 inhibitor (SMG1i, 1 µM) before evaluating the effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K on cAMP-induced chloride secretion and fluid secretion, respectively. As shown in Figure 7(A,B), the inhibitory effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K on forskolin-induced chloride secretion in T84 cells was completely abolished after pretreatment with SMG1i. To investigate the effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K on CFTR expression in T84 cells, CFTR protein expression was evaluated after 2-h incubation with α,β-dehydromonacolin K. As shown in Figure 7(C,D), there was no change in CFTR protein expression following the incubation with α,β-dehydromonacolin K, suggesting that α,β-dehydromonacolin K did not modulate CFTR protein expression. Furthermore, the inhibitory effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K, in the presence of the SMG1 inhibitor (SMG1i), on forskolin-induced fluid secretion was significantly diminished (Figure 7(E,F)). These results suggested that the anti-secretory effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K was mediated, at least in part, through SMG1 in the NMD pathway.

Discussion

In this study, we demonstrated the anti-secretory effects of α,β-dehydromonacolin K. Our results indicate that this compound inhibits cAMP-induced chloride efflux in intestinal epithelial cell lines without affecting potassium channel activities or negative regulators of CFTR, including protein phosphatases and AMPK. This compound was also effective in inhibiting forskolin-induced and carbachol-induced swelling of 3D human colonoids. Using proteomic approaches, we identified the NMD pathway as a potential target of α,β-dehydromonacolin K. Importantly, we demonstrated that this compound inhibits cAMP-induced chloride secretion in intestinal epithelial cell lines and cAMP-induced intestinal fluid secretion in 3D human colonoids through the SMG1 protein in the NMD pathway. This study revealed SMG1 as a novel target of α,β-dehydromonacolin K, which may be useful for the treatment of secretory diarrheas. In addition, innovation of this study includes the discovery of SMG1 as a novel regulator of CFTR-mediated chloride and fluid secretion.

α,β-Dehydromonacolin K is synthesized from lovastatin, a 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-coenzyme A reductase inhibitor (Frishman and Rapier 1989). However, treatment with lovastatin is associated with several toxicities including diarrheas in cancer patients (Thibault et al. 1996). In addition, high doses of simvastatin, a derivative of lovastatin, have been linked to side effects such as diarrheas in phase I and phase II clinical trials (Krymskaya et al. 2020; Cash et al. 2023). In contrast, α,β-dehydromonacolin K, a derivative of lovastatin, exhibited an inhibitory effect on cAMP-induced chloride secretion with an IC50 of ∼0.85 µM. Collectively, these findings suggest that lovastatin derivatives represent a group of fungus-derived compounds that effectively inhibit cAMP-induced chloride secretion in intestinal epithelia.

The inhibitory effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K was not associated with phosphodiesterase (PDE) or protein phosphatase activities. However, it exhibits minor involvement in the AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) and intracellular signaling pathways. This finding aligns with the observations of other compounds derived from anti-diarrheal fungi. For example, arthropsolide A inhibits CFTR by modulating AMPK activity (Akrimajirachoote et al. 2020). Furthermore, the inhibitory effect of α,β-dehydromonacolin K on chloride secretion did not involve potassium efflux via either Kca3.1 or KCNQ4. Several other natural compounds identified as Kca3.1 inhibitors have been shown to effectively inhibit chloride secretion in human intestinal epithelial cells (Jakakul et al. 2021). Beyond barium chloride treatment, current KCNQ4 inhibitors include linopirdine and XE991 (Sogaard et al. 2001; Li et al. 2021). Linopirdine inhibits KCNQ4 by binding beneath the inner gate of the channel (Li et al. 2021), while XE991 functions as a negative modulator of KCNQ4 (Rode et al. 2010). These results indicate that compounds derived from fungi have distinct targets compared with compounds derived from other sources including natural and synthetic compounds.

A forskolin-induced swelling assay was established to evaluate potential drugs for the treatment of cystic fibrosis (Dekkers et al. 2013). Our laboratory has successfully used this assay with enteroids to assess the antisecretory effect of piperine (Satitsri et al. 2023). The current study showed that α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibited both forskolin-induced and carbachol-induced fluid secretion, which are known to be driven by CFTR-mediated chloride secretion in human enteroids (Billet and Hanrahan 2013; Cil et al. 2017). This suggests that α,β-dehydromonacolin K may have an anti-diarrheal effect through the inhibition of CFTR-mediated chloride secretion. However, it is essential to note that this model displays a basal-out appearance, which is a limitation of this approach. To validate our findings regarding CFTR inhibition, we used monolayers of intestinal epithelial cell lines, considering that α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibits chloride-driven fluid secretion in this model. Future studies should explore how this compound penetrates the intestinal cells and whether it utilizes specific transporters for absorption. To evaluate anti-diarrheal efficacy α,β-dehydromonacolin K, further studies are required to perform using in vivo models of secretory diarrheas. To achieve this objective, a closed loop mouse model instilled with bacterial enterotoxins (e.g., cholera toxins) with or without the compound will be performed and weight-to-length ratios of intestinal loops will be analyzed to determine anti-diarrheal efficacy of the compound (Cil et al. 2017; Duan et al. 2019). This experimental design is anticipated to provide a useful insight into the anti-diarrheal efficacy of α,β-dehydromonacolin K.

In this study, α,β-dehydromonacolin K was identified as a modulator of various proteins, particularly those associated with the non-sense-mediated decay (NMD) pathway, based on proteomic analyses. NMD generally regulates mRNA that contains premature stop codons by facilitating their elimination and also plays a role in post-translational modifications (Huang and Wilkinson 2012). Previous studies have highlighted the significance of NMD in regulating CFTR, particularly in cystic fibrosis. For instance, inhibiting NMD through SMG1 inhibition in conjunction with CFTR modulators effectively restored CFTR function in intestinal organoids derived from patients with cystic fibrosis (de Poel et al. 2022). Furthermore, a specific mutation in CFTR genes results in reduced CFTR mRNA expression, which can be restored using an NMD inhibitor (Kim et al. 2022). In our study, the inhibition of the NMD pathway using an inhibitor of SMG1, a key protein in NMD pathway, abrogated the anti-diarrheal effects of α,β-dehydromonacolin K. This finding suggested that α,β-dehydromonacolin K may serve as an NMD activator that inhibits CFTR activity via an SMG1-dependent mechanism. However, the precise mechanism by which SMG1 or NMD activation inhibits CFTR activity remains unclear and warrants further investigation.

Conclusions

In conclusion, α,β-dehydromonacolin K inhibits cAMP-induced chloride and fluid secretion in human intestinal epithelial cells through an SMG1-dependent NMD pathway (Figure 8). These findings improve our understanding of chloride and fluid secretion mechanisms and highlight a novel potential therapeutic target for secretory diarrheas. Further exploration of this pathway may lead to new strategies for enhancing patient care and improving outcomes in patients with gastrointestinal disorders. Our findings provide a foundation for the development of more effective treatments for secretory diarrhea.
