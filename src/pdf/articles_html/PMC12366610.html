Abstract

Background

Despite progress made in improving family planning (FP) services in Jordan, significant barriers remain. This study aimed to assess the availability of FP services in primary care settings and explore women’s knowledge and attitudes toward FP to support the development of more person-centred FP services and interventions.

Methods

The Service Availability and Readiness Assessment (SARA) was conducted in 2022 in six governorates to assess FP service availability in Jordan. Also, a cross-sectional study was conducted among women who attended primary health centres (PHCs) for postpartum care or immunisation of newborns to assess their knowledge, attitude and past experience of using FP, and family support.

Results

SARA results showed limited healthcare capacity for FP services with inadequate FP training mainly in PHCs and village centres, and a lack of reproductive health training in most centres. Among 972 women, 6% had never heard of FP, and only 47.1% received FP counselling before their last delivery. About 76% considered contraceptives expensive, despite being free for Jordanians and refugees. Only 22.7% discussed contraceptive use with their husbands. The proportion of women who visited PHC for antenatal care ≤8 times was 56.8% among Syrian women and 46.3% among Jordanian women, while it was 68.7% among women living in Mafraq governorate and 36.8% in Irbid governorate.

Conclusion

Barriers to FP services include limited availability in rural areas, insufficient knowledge and negative attitudes, with evidence of disparities in antenatal care access across demographics and regions. Awareness programmes and enhanced staff capacity are recommended to improve FP service access and utilisation.

Keywords: Primary Prevention, Community Health, Health Services Accessibility

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

  • This study reveals gaps in the provision of family planning methods at primary health centres and village centres (which offer fewer services compared with comprehensive primary health centres), as well as gaps in the capacity to provide family planning services across all three types of centres. It also highlights inadequate adolescent-focused services and variations in training and supply readiness across health centre types. Additionally, the study documents persistently low awareness of several contraceptive methods among women, widespread misconceptions about cost and safety, limited counselling coverage and limited support from husbands.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY

  • These findings highlight the need for targeted provider training, person-centred interventions and policy reforms to improve family planning services, especially in rural and underserved areas of Jordan.

Introduction

Family planning is crucial for improving maternal health and child survival by preventing the social, economic and health consequences of unintended pregnancies and ensuring healthy timing and spacing of births.1 Family planning is called for in Sustainable Development Goal target 3.7: “By 2030, ensure universal access to sexual and reproductive health-care services, including for family planning, information and education, and the integration of reproductive health into national strategies and programs”.2 Despite global progress in improving the use of family planning methods and reducing fertility rates, there remains a relatively high unmet need for family planning,3 4 particularly in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), ranging from 20% to 58% in most studies.4 The unmet need for contraception during the first 12 months post partum in LMICs was also found to be high, with a pooled prevalence of 48.5% according to a systematic review and meta-analysis study of postpartum contraceptive use among women in LMICs.5

Jordan made notable progress in family planning between 1990 and 2012, with the contraceptive prevalence rate (CPR) increasing from 40% to 61%.6 However, CPR has since stagnated, even as the population grew rapidly; from 5.6 million in 2004 to 11.7 million in 2024, driven in part by migration, with one-third of the population now consisting of non-Jordanians.7 Jordan’s total fertility rate, a key driver of population growth, declined steadily from 1990 to 2002, plateaued between 3.5 and 3.7 from 2002 to 2012 and then dropped to 2.7 in 2017–2018, reaching 2.6 in 2023.6 Also, the use of modern family planning methods among currently married women remained stable between 2002 and 2012 (41%–42%) but declined to 37% in 2017–2018 and slightly increased to 38% in 2023. In contrast, the traditional method use rose from 13% in 1990 to 19% in 2012, dropped to 14% in 2017–2018 and then increased to 22% by 2023.6 Despite overall progress, there remains variation in the use of modern contraceptive methods. Women in rural areas, those with lower incomes and non-Jordanian women—particularly Syrians—have lower rates of using modern and effective methods than their counterparts. In 2023, the use of modern family planning methods was more common among urban married women (39%) than rural married women (35%).6

Studies have shown that family planning services use and contraceptive uptake are determined by an interplay of factors, including knowledge, attitudes, practices and perceptions of women themselves, their husbands and service providers towards family planning.8 9 Socio-cultural, gender norms and religious values significantly influence the decision to use family planning methods in Jordan, primarily acting as barriers to the use of family planning services.10 11 Traditional cultural beliefs and attitudes toward fertility and family size were also found to reduce family planning uptake, especially among younger Syrian refugee women.12

While adequate family planning services are available for both Jordanians and non-Jordanians, significant misconceptions are the most dominant barrier to family planning use, especially among younger women.12,17 Younger couples in Jordan do not have full information about family planning services available for youth, which is coupled with concerns over the quality of care and contraceptives’ effectiveness.12 17 Significant portions of both Jordanian women and Syrian refugees and their husbands believe that modern family planning methods have negative side effects and complications on their health and/or their unborn children.10 14 Jordanian women believe that the use of oral contraceptives (OCs) is related to unpleasant side effects, such as mood swings, headaches, weight gain and back pain.18 Syrian refugee women believe that using OCs could potentially lead to hormonal disturbances and infertility.19

Another important barrier to family planning uptake is related to institutional and health system factors. These factors include low access to contraception and family planning services, lack of a youth-friendly environment for the delivery of sexual and reproductive services, and negative provider behaviour.20 Further, women in Jordan have concerns about the lack of female providers, the quality of care available in their local facilities, poor counselling, providers’ bias and missed opportunities for family planning services during the provision of antenatal and postnatal care, as well as lack of coordination and cooperation between service providers.21 22 The problem of negative providers’ attitudes is more evident in younger women who are concerned about long waiting times, poorly staffed clinics and poor quality of care.23 A cross-sectional study examining family planning counselling services in governmental and private clinics in northern Jordan found that counselling in governmental clinics was significantly less adequate, with most sessions rated as half adequate or inadequate at 44.9% and 50.8%, respectively.24

Structural factors have also been identified in the literature as major barriers to using family planning. A recent study conducted in Jordan investigating the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on reproductive health reported significantly lower use of contraception during the pandemic, mainly due to the lockdown of the maternity or health centres and decreased supply of family planning products.25 Other structural barriers include a lack of awareness about family planning services. While adequate family planning services are available for both Jordanians and non-Jordanians in Jordan, significant poor knowledge of available services is the most dominant barrier to family planning use, especially among younger women.12,17

Many policy constraints affect access to family planning services by young people.17 It has been argued that existing policies that prohibit young females from accessing reproductive health services without parents’ or other legal guardians’ companions are one of the policy-based obstacles that young people face in accessing reproductive and family planning health services.17 A study conducted among Syrian refugees in urban areas revealed that young Syrians reported that a prerequisite to having a security card and stringent restrictions are among the greatest policy-based obstacles that substantially limit their ability to access reproductive health services.26

Despite all programmatic efforts in Jordan to increase the demand and use of family planning services, some major factors need to be explored.14 27 This study aimed to assess the availability of family planning services in primary care settings and explore women’s knowledge and attitudes toward family planning to support the development of more person-centred family planning services and interventions.

Methods

Study design and settings

To assess the availability of family planning services in primary care, data related to family planning were analysed using the Service Availability and Readiness Assessment (SARA) tool, which was developed by the WHO. SARA was conducted in six governorates in Jordan. In order to assess women’s knowledge, attitudes and past experience of using family planning and family support, a cross-sectional study was conducted in two governorates in the north of Jordan, namely Irbid and Mafraq governorates. The selection of the two governorates was based on criteria related to the presence of Syrian refugees in host communities and adequate representation of women belonging to the lower two income quintiles.

Data collection

Service Availability and Readiness Assessment

Facility selection followed a purposive sampling strategy to ensure geographic and service-level representation across four governorates. Within each governorate, the process involved selecting one comprehensive centre (CHC) located in the main city, two primary health centres (PHCs) under the catchment area of the selected CHC, and two village health clinics (VHCs) within the catchment area of the two selected PHCs. In total, 40 facilities were selected and assessed. The selection aimed to reflect the actual structure of service delivery across urban and rural settings. Inclusion was based on facility type, geographic location and service role; exclusion criteria involved facilities outside the defined service catchment areas or those not functioning during the study period. In Jordan, primary healthcare services are delivered through a network of CHC, PHC or VHC, with CHCs offering the widest range of services, including preventive, curative, diagnostic and some specialised care. The PHCs provide a more limited range of outpatient and preventive services and are usually situated in smaller towns. VHCs represent the most basic level of service delivery and are typically found in rural communities, offering only essential primary care services.28 The SARA tool was used to assess and monitor the service availability and readiness, including family planning services, of the selected health centres. The standard SARA manual was followed to prepare and conduct the data collection. Data were collected over 3 months in 2022, adding one more week for tool testing and training of data collectors. A team of 12 data collectors with experience in assessments and who are familiar with the health system was recruited and trained on the use of the assessment tool. (The form used is provided in online supplemental file 1).

Women’s knowledge, attitudes and past experience of using family planning

As for the primary data collection, the study was conducted among women who attended PHCs for postpartum care or immunisation of newborns during the first month after birth. A list of health centres in the selected districts was obtained from the Directorate of Primary Health Care at the Ministry of Health. Three centres were selected using a simple random sampling method from each governorate. The study included all consecutive women who visited the maternal and childhood units in the selected centres during the period March to June 2022. Women were enrolled consecutively in the study if they met the inclusion criteria: postpartum married women of reproductive age, residing in the selected two governorates, visiting the comprehensive health centres for their child’s (4 weeks of age or younger) routine immunisation services or postnatal care, and agreeing to give consent to participate in the study.

The questionnaire was developed based on the review of previous studies’ research and structured into different sections (the form used is provided in online supplemental file 2). The final version of the questionnaire was reviewed and commented on by a group of researchers, clinicians and laypeople to ensure face and construct validity. To test the instrument and the procedure, a pilot study was conducted on 30 women in each group. Based on feedback from the women, the questionnaire was rephrased and modified. The questionnaire included closed-ended questions with predefined responses on the mother’s identifying information, socioeconomic status and clinical characteristics, newborn’s information (including gender, birth weight and gestational age), awareness/knowledge of modern family planning methods, attitude towards contraceptives, past experience of contraceptive use and husband’s support. After obtaining ethical and administrative approvals, two midwives from each selected health centre were trained on the study procedures and filled out the questionnaire. Midwives verified the inclusion criteria, explained the study to participants, obtained written consent and filled out the baseline questionnaire before counselling using a paper form.

Outcomes

For the SARA, the outcomes focused on the availability and readiness of family planning services across different levels of health facilities. These outcomes included whether family planning services were offered at each facility, the presence of trained staff and service delivery guidelines, the availability of contraceptive commodities and the readiness of infrastructure and basic equipment required to deliver such services. For the survey among postpartum women, outcomes were designed to capture individual-level knowledge, attitudes and experiences related to family planning. Additional outcomes included past experience with contraceptive use and the perceived level of husband’s support for family planning decisions.

Data analysis

The paper responses were initially input into Excel sheets. Statistical analysis was performed using SPSS IBM (V.29). Data were described as frequencies and percentages for categorical variables.

Patient and public involvement

Patients and/or the public were not directly involved in the design, conduct, reporting or dissemination plans of this research.

Results

Availability of family planning services in primary care settings

A total of 40 facilities (6 CHCs, 11 PHCs and 23 VHCs) were assessed in Jordan. Nine facilities (22.5%) were located in urban areas, and 31 (77.5 %) were located in rural areas. Four CHCs (66.7%) had four full-time general practitioners (GPs), one CHC had three GPs and one CHC had two GPs. In addition, two CHCs had part-time GPs. All centres had one to three midwifery professionals. Table 1 shows the availability of family planning services in healthcare centres. All of the CHCs and PHCs, and nine (39.1%) VHCs provide family planning services. Combined oestrogen-progesterone OC pills and progestin-only contraceptive pills, and condoms are offered in all CHCs and PHCs, and eight (88.9%) of the nine VHCs that provide these services. Injectable contraceptives are offered to a lesser extent than pills. Intrauterine contraceptive devices (IUCDs), emergency pills and implants are offered to an even lesser extent. None of the CHCs, PHCs and VHCs provide family planning services for unmarried adolescents.

Family planning: awareness, attitude, past experience and support

Our primary data were collected from 972 women, 854 (87.9%) Jordanians and 118 (12.1%) Syrians. Almost 60.1% of them were aged between 21 and 30 years, 62.2% had a high school education or less and 90.2% were housewives. Almost one-fifth (19.7%) had anaemia. The majority (94.0%) attended the PHC for newborns’ immunisation, 91.4% delivered full term and 90.9% gave birth to a baby with normal weight. During the first month of birth, 63.7% of them breastfed the baby exclusively. Almost 12.3% of women did not attend the PHC for antenatal care, and 47.5% had ≤8 antenatal visits. The proportion of women who visited PHC for antenatal care ≤8 times was 56.8% among Syrian women and 46.3% among Jordanian women. As for governorates, the proportion of women who visited PHC for antenatal care ≤8 times was 68.7% among women living in Mafraq governorate and 36.8% among women living in Irbid governorate.

Postpartum women’s awareness of family planning methods

Of all women, 6% never heard about family planning. Syrian women, those living in Mafraq governorate, compared with women living in Irbid governorate, women with low education level, women with low income and women 20 years old or younger, were less aware of family planning. The majority of women (>85%) know oral pills and IUCDs. However, the majority are not fully aware of other modern family planning methods (table 3). The most commonly reported source of information on family planning was healthcare workers, cited by 57.6% of women. This was followed by relatives (51.7%) and the internet (37.2%). Television was also a notable source (26.7%), while fewer women reported learning about family planning from friends (13.8%), radio (11.5%) or newspapers/magazines (11.4%). Only 5.8% of women identified their spouse as a source of information. Almost 70.2% of women were aware of the family planning methods that are available at the clinic for postpartum mothers. Only 47.1% of women had received any counselling about family planning services before delivery of the last child, and 45.4% of women had received any counselling about family planning services in the hospital after delivery at discharge.

Postpartum women’s attitude towards contraceptives

The majority (83.2%) of women were not using contraceptives at the time of conception, and 6.8% were using contraceptives irregularly. About 76% of postpartum women thought that contraceptives are expensive, although contraceptives are provided free for Jordanians as well as refugees. One-fifth (20%) of postpartum women thought that contraceptive use leads to infertility, 20% believe that contraceptives are not effective in avoiding pregnancy, and 25% reported that contraceptives are not safe.

Past experience with contraceptive use

A total of 485 (49.9%) of postpartum women had ever used any contraception. The most commonly reported reason for using contraception was spacing (90.2%). For women who never used contraception, the main reason was the desire to have a child (74.7%), followed by fear of side effects (11.3%). About 3.5% of them reported that they did not use contraceptives because their husband or mother-in-law objected to the use.

Husband’s support

Only 22.7% of women had discussed the use of contraceptives with their husbands. The majority (88.3%) of women had received their husband’s support in issues related to family planning services. The majority (83.5%) of women reported that their husband approves of the use of family planning methods soon after delivery. Almost 7.6% of women reported that their husband is the one who makes the final decision about using family planning.

Discussion

Family planning is a vital component of women’s health services, contributing to improved health outcomes and broader societal benefits. This manuscript presents findings from two complementary data sources to offer insights into the current availability and use of family planning services in selected areas of Jordan. The SARA, conducted across 40 health facilities in six governorates, showed that family planning services are consistently available in CHCs and PHCs, but much less so in VHCs.

One of the key findings was the limited capacity of PHCs and VHCs to deliver comprehensive family planning services, primarily due to inadequate training of healthcare providers. Sexual and reproductive health training was lacking across most facility types. This aligns with findings from Jordan’s National Strategy for Reproductive and Sexual Health (2020–2030), which highlights weak integration of sexual and reproductive health into primary care services.29 Strengthening workforce capacity is essential for achieving service integration and improving access to quality care.

A second major gap identified was the limited availability of family planning services in rural VHCs. Similar challenges are reported in other LMICs where rural populations often face geographic and resource-based barriers to family planning access.30 31

From the client’s perspective, the primary data revealed that many postpartum women were only familiar with OCs and IUDs, with limited knowledge of other methods. Additionally, women reported inadequate counselling during facility visits. Evidence suggests that improving knowledge and correcting misconceptions can empower women to make informed family planning decisions.32 In our study, fear of side effects was the second most common reason for not using contraception, with 20% of women believing that contraceptive use causes infertility and 25% expressing concerns about safety. These findings are consistent with other studies in similar settings, where negative beliefs and misinformation significantly reduce contraceptive uptake.24

Importantly, 57.6% of participants identified healthcare workers as their main source of information on family planning, underscoring the critical role of provider counselling. Strengthening the counselling skills of frontline providers could help dispel misconceptions and improve trust in modern methods. Similar strategies have been effective in other countries, where provider-led communication improved both acceptance and sustained use of family planning services.33 34

Social norms and gender norms play a significant role in the use of family planning services.35 36 Husbands have a big influence on the use and selection of contraceptive methods in Jordan. Our study showed that only one-fifth of women had discussed the use of contraceptives with their husbands. Previous studies showed that the role of the husband in the process of family planning decision-making depends on several factors, including socioeconomic status as well as knowledge of various family planning methods.37,39 It is important to develop equitable gender dynamics for family planning interventions that actively include men29 and develop appropriate interventions that tackle the role of gender inequities.40 Such approaches have shown positive impacts in increasing contraceptive uptake and shared decision-making in other regions.32

Overall, national efforts should focus on underserved areas and reduce barriers to healthcare access and utilisation, such as distance, health provider availability and health centre availability. Expanding the availability of family planning services in VHCs is expected to overcome access barriers in rural areas. Enhancing women’s knowledge and awareness while promoting better engagement of husbands is also needed. Currently, the use of digital health tools appears to be one way to spread knowledge and awareness about family planning methods,41 with consideration also being given to the use of telecommunications to provide virtual family planning services to underserved areas in villages.42 Institutional and health system factors, such as lack of a youth-friendly environment for the delivery of sexual and reproductive services, negative provider behaviour, lack of female providers and poor counselling, are well-documented barriers. A study in Jordan43 showed that only 28.2% of women perceived counselling as appropriate, 58.9% of women reported no privacy during counselling and 74.2% did not provide counselling about possible side effects. Therefore, continuous efforts to strengthen the healthcare system are needed to guarantee that hospitals and maternal and child healthcare clinics have enough resources and supplies to meet women’s maternal healthcare needs, thus promoting access and utilisation of free services.40

One of the limitations of the study is that not all of Jordan’s governorates were included in the study, which may limit its generalisability to other regions in Jordan. Additionally, the use of a cross-sectional design restricts the ability to draw causal inferences. Also, reliance on self-reported measures may introduce social desirability bias; however, we mitigated this by validating responses through visual inspections of some items, such as the availability of contraceptives. Additionally, having midwives collect the data may have introduced social desirability bias. Future studies will need to be conducted on a larger scale to increase the representativeness of the results.

Conclusion

Barriers to accessing family planning services include the limited availability of family planning services in village health centres in rural areas, along with insufficient knowledge and negative attitudes among women. Also, there are evident disparities in access and utilisation of antenatal care services among various demographic groups and regions. Thus, there is a need to implement programmes to raise family planning and antenatal care awareness among women, families and communities of all nationalities across Jordan. It is also important to build the capacity of the staff in all centres to offer family planning services and ensure the availability of updated guidelines and job aids in health facilities.
