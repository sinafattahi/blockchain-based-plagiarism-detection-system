Are orthopaedic trauma surgeons at risk of occupational noise-induced hearing loss?

Abstract

Objectives:

Orthopaedic surgeons are at risk of occupational, noise-induced hearing loss due to exposure to instruments in the operating room. The primary objective of this study was to determine whether orthopaedic trauma procedures exceed recommended decibel (dB) limits. The secondary objective was to demonstrate which actions during a case create the highest sound levels.

Methods:

Intraoperative recordings were taken during orthopaedic trauma surgeries and classified into open reduction internal fixation (ORIF), intramedullary nailing, closed reduction percutaneous pinning, external fixation, soft tissue procedures, or a combination thereof. Recordings were taken of drilling, screw insertion, suctioning, saw use, and mallet striking. Decibel levels were reported as maximum dB level (MDL), LAeq, LCpeak, and time weighted average (TWA). Percentage of allowable daily noise was reported as dose, while the dose predicted for an 8-hour period was reported as projected dose.

Results:

A total of 89 recordings were collected, comprising 46 baseline and 43 trauma case recordings. All procedures had significantly higher dB levels compared with controls for all variables (P < 0.001). In all cases, the MDL was greater than 85 dB. ORIF had the highest average MDL (108.4 dB) and TWA (70.3 dB). Overall, no procedures exceeded the maximum allowable daily noise dose. However, the soft tissue group had the highest projected dose (18.8%). Suctioning against soft tissue had the highest dose and projected dose.

Conclusion:

Orthopaedic trauma procedures stayed within noise limits, but average MDL exceeded 85 dB. ORIF and suctioning produced some of the highest sound levels among procedures and recorded steps.

Keywords: orthopedic, trauma, hearing loss, occupational, noise-induced

1. Introduction

Several occupational hazards in the operating room (OR) have been well characterized and strict guidelines are followed to mitigate risks to OR personnel (Table 1).1–4 For example, dosimeter badges are worn by those exposed to radiation from fluoroscopy, and there are specific limits that cannot be exceeded.5,6 Comparatively, the risk that occupational noise exposure in the OR poses to hearing has received minimal attention despite clear guidelines from the National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH) regarding acceptable noise limits.7 Existing literature conflicts regarding the risk of developing noise-induced hearing loss (NIHL) because of occupational exposures in the OR.1,8–14

Currently, the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) and NIOSH recommend that individuals be exposed to no more than 90 decibels (dB) or 85 dB, respectively, over an 8-hour time weighted average (TWA).1,7 However, hearing protection is recommended for any noise exceeding 85 dB.15 Previously, orthopaedic procedures were found to regularly exceed 85 dB.14 Orthopaedic trauma surgeons regularly use instruments (saws, drills, and mallets) that have been show to generate noise levels greater than 85 dB for complex surgical procedures.9,16 Prolonged exposure to such noise levels over their careers may place orthopedic surgeons at risk of developing NIHL compared with unexposed peers.15

Orthopaedic trauma surgeries are heterogeneous. Procedures can involve multiple limbs, a multidisciplinary team, and a variety instruments.17 As such, the level of noise generated during procedures may vary widely compared with other orthopaedic subspecialties. The primary objective of this study was to assess whether orthopaedic trauma surgeons are exposed to harmful noise levels in the OR and which type of procedures put them at a higher risk. Our secondary objective is to identify what parts of an orthopaedic trauma procedure create the most noise. The findings of this study could lead to the implementation of measures aimed at mitigating the risk of NIHL among orthopaedic trauma surgeons in the future.

2. Methods

After Institutional Review Board waiver, a prospective, single-surgeon, single-institution review was conducted using intraoperative recordings during orthopaedic trauma procedures. These procedures were classified into open reduction internal fixation (ORIF), intramedullary nailing (IMN), closed reduction percutaneous pinning (CRPP), external fixation, soft tissue procedures, or a combination thereof.

The Sound Level Meter (SLM) application (NIOSH, Washington, DC) was used to record noise exposure data with an iMM-6 (Dayton Audio, Springboro, OH) calibrated external microphone connected to an iOS device (Apple, Cupertino, CA). Baseline noise levels were established by recording 30 seconds intervals in the OR in the absence of OR personnel, music, forced warming units, and suction systems. However, the anesthesia monitors and ventilation systems remained on for all recordings. Surgical recordings were performed by a single, orthopaedic surgery resident who wore the microphone at the level of their collar to replicate the surgeon's hearing zone. Recordings were initiated before scrubbing for the surgery and stopped when the individual removed their surgical gown.

In a nonsterile environment, 30-second recordings were captured for various surgical actions using the SLM application. These included continuous power drilling, screw insertion with a powered driver, baseline suction, suctioning against soft tissue, suctioning fluid against a surface, saw use, and malleting. Power drilling, screw insertion, and sawing were performed on saw bones using the Unium system (Synthes, West Chester, PA). Suctioning was carried out with a standard Yankauer tip at maximum suction using the Stryker Neptune 3 system (Stryker, Portage, MI). Malleting was simulated by striking a metal surface using a metal hammer at a steady pace.

Multiple parameters were recorded intraoperatively and during simulated case actions, maximum dB level (MDL; highest sound pressure level), TWA (average dB level projected over an 8-hour period), dose (percentage of maximum allowable daily noise dose), projected dose (measured noise dose projected forward over 8 hours), LAeq (equivalent continuous sound level over a given period of time that has the same energy as fluctuating noise), and LCPeak (maximum value of sound pressure at any instant during the measurement, measured with “C” frequency weighting). “C” frequency weighting is defined by the inclusion of low frequency sounds that contribute to noise but are not perceived by human hearing. “A” frequency weighting is the standard weighting of audible frequencies for human hearing (Table 2).

All surgical recordings were analyzed and classified by procedure. Descriptive statistics were performed first by comparing baseline with surgery. Continuous data are presented as mean (SD), and categorical data are presented as cell count (%). T-tests or Mann-Whitney U tests were used to compare continuous data. Following this unadjusted and adjusted regressions were analyzed looking at TWA as the dependent outcome. Data from procedures that fulfilled a single classification were then averaged and compared. Significance was determined at P < 0.05. This study was deemed exempt from Institutional Review Board and Animal Use Committee Review.

3. Results

A total of 89 recordings were collected and analyzed. There were 46 baseline and 43 trauma recordings. Of the trauma recordings, there were 16 (37.2%) ORIF, 10 (23.3%) IMN, 4 (9.3%) CRPP, 0 (0.0%) external fixation, 1 (2.3%) removal of hardware (ROH), 2 (4.7%) soft tissue procedures, and 10 (23.3%) combined recordings. Length of surgery ranged from about 17 to 420 minutes with the greatest duration being a revision pilon and fibular shaft ORIF case.

The surgery group exhibited a significantly greater MDL (107.0 dB; 95% CI 105.0–108.0) compared with the baseline MDL (84.8 dB; 95% CI 81.7–87.9; P < 0.001; Tables 3 and 4). The average TWA did not exceed the threshold of 85 dB for either group. However, the average TWA was significantly higher for the surgical group (68.8 dB; 95% CI 67.6–70.1) compared with baseline (33.4 dB; 95% CI 31.7–35.0; P < 0.001). Baseline recordings had an average dose of 0.0% (95% CI 0.0–0.0), while the surgical recordings had a significantly greater dose of 0.04% (95% CI 0.02–0.05; P < 0.001). The projected dose was 0.01% for baseline recordings (95% CI 0.0–0.01). This was significantly lower than the projected dose of 0.15% for surgical recordings (95% CI 0.12–0.17; P < 0.001).

Among the different surgical recordings, ORIF had the highest average MDL (108.4 dB), TWA (70.3 dB), and dose (5.1%), followed by IMN (107.0 dB; 68.7 dB; 2.7%; Table 5). Soft tissue procedures had the highest average projected dose at 18.8%, followed by ORIF (17.2%). Of all the procedures, a revision pilon and fibular shaft ORIF had the highest recorded dose (32.0%) and projected dose (38.0%). The TWA of that particular case was 80 dB, nearing the NIOSH threshold of 85 dB.

4. Discussion

In the United States, hearing loss affects around 15% of adults.18–21 Occupational noise exposure contributes to hearing impairment in at least 22 million workers in the United States and nearly 45 million people worldwide.7,22–25 Most notably, occupational hearing loss is one of the most commonly reported work-related injuries.7,22,23 According to reports from OSHA and NIOSH, exposure to over 85 dB TWA over 8-hour workday increases the risk of occupational NIHL by 8%. However, even levels below the recommended 85 dB have been shown in the literature to still increase the risk of NIHL.7,26,27 As noise levels surpass 85 dB, the duration of allowable daily exposure decreases, further escalating concerns of potential hearing damage for the surgeon, OR staff, and patients.7,26 Specifically, Willet et al12 found that orthopaedic OR staff exhibited a 50% prevalence of NIHL, which rose with increasing years of exposure.

The major contributors to excessive noise seen in the orthopaedic OR seem to be loud powered surgical instruments and suctioning. These instruments, including saws, drills, and suction have been seen to generate dB levels of 85–142.7,9,16,26,28 Mullet et al28 highlighted various orthopaedic powered instruments including saws, drills, and hammers that consistently exceeded safety thresholds, reaching maximum levels of up to 142 dB. Furthermore, noise levels increased as the distance to the source of the noise decreases.28

Previous studies have mainly focused on the risk of noise-induced hearing loss (NIHL) in other orthopaedic subspecialties.14 To our knowledge, this is the first study to specifically assess noise exposure and the potential risk of hearing loss within the orthopaedic trauma operating room. Using the SLM application, we were able to capture not only MDL but also a range of additional metrics, including dose and projected dose. This allowed us to analyze how individual steps in the procedure—when extrapolated over an 8-hour work period—could contribute to harmful noise exposure.

Open reduction internal fixation was the procedure type that produced the loudest dBs and the highest TWA. We speculate this is due to the higher use of saws, drills, and suctioning during these particular cases. Second was IMN, which may be related to the less soft tissue dissection and use of powered instruments when compared with ORIF. For MDL, TWA, and dose, our data indicated that noise generated from highest to lowest was ORIF, IMN, CRPP, then finally ROH. These trends are likely reflective of the percentage of instrument use and suctioning during each case. Soft tissue procedures, interestingly, were an outlier with an average MDL of 105.7 dB, TWA of 66.8 dB, and dose of 1.9% making it third highest in those categories, respectively. Moreover, soft tissue procedures had the highest projected dose out of all procedures, which may be due to suction use. Previous studies demonstrated that suction can reach a level up to 120 dB.29

Unsurprisingly, our simulated recordings revealed that suctioning against a soft tissue surface produced the highest MDL, dose, and projected dose. This even exceeded malleting or use of power instruments. A study by Walch et al used an anatomic silicon ear model to measure suction-generated noise during microsuction aural toilet. They described a “clarinet phenomenon,” in which partial obstruction of the suction tip creates sharp, high-frequency noise peaks.30 Our study recreated this effect, using soft tissue and fluid to partially obstruct the suction tip.

Limitations of our study include it being a single-surgeon, single-institution study, thereby limiting the generalizability of our findings. Considering the prolonged utilization of noise generating instruments, however, our findings support the expectation that ORIF would exhibit the highest MDL, TWA, and dose. Second, there is substantial heterogeneity among musculoskeletal procedures in orthopaedic trauma surgery and not all were included in our study, but we included the most commonly performed procedures among orthopaedic trauma surgeons. Third, our study used continuous recordings. Therefore, we were unable to distinguish what the primary noise generator was during combination procedures. For example, if a procedure included both ROH and ORIF. Fourth, we were not adequately powered to compare procedure types directly and thus cannot draw any significant conclusions from this study. Fifth, we are unable to account for variability in individual recorder's distance from the operating field and equipment, height, and approach. However, we attempted to limit this by having the same individual perform all recordings.

In conclusion, the study provides evidence that orthopedic trauma surgeons are not exceeding daily recommended accumulative noise levels but are still regularly exposed to hazardous noise levels of above 85 dB. Orthopaedic trauma surgeons should consider using hearing protection in certain high-risk procedures where frequent use of instrumentation or suction is expected. Implementation of suction tips with an on/off switch can offer an additional way to mitigate the risk of NIHL. Surgeons can also factor in case selection to help decrease the risk of exceeding daily limits. Finally, orthopaedic implant companies should keep sound levels in mind when developing new orthopaedic equipment. These instruments should subsequently be used to replace noisier, older technology.
