Abstract

Background

Polygonatum cyrtonema Hua, a valuable medicinal and edible plant in China, has been increasingly cultivated to produce nutritional and health products to meet market demand. However, the absence of the mitochondrial genome hinders the research of evolutionary analysis, artificial cultivation, and medicinal resource development. This study aimed to systematically characterize the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema and perform a preliminary bioinformatic analysis.

Result

In this study, the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema was assembled and annotated by combining Illumina reads with long-read Oxford Nanopore Technologies, which formed a complex non-circular structure covering 664,991 bp in length, with 33 protein-coding genes, 20 tRNAs, and 3 rRNAs. Overall, a total of 194 simple repeats, 24 tandem repeats, and 294 dispersed repeats were identified, and 599 RNA editing sites were predicted, all of which were C-to-T types. In particular, 29 homologous fragments between the chloroplast and mitochondrial genome were detected and accounted for 2.4% of the mitochondrial genome. In addition, codon usage analysis, nucleotide diversity analysis, and Ka/Ks analysis suggested a slower rate of evolution and a relatively conserved structure of the mitochondrial genome. Phylogenetic relationships based on 33 species demonstrated that P. cyrtonema was most closely related to Polygonatum sibiricum.

Conclusion

Our results provide comprehensive information on the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema, and demonstrate the availability of mitochondrial genome-based taxonomic classification for Polygonatum Mill.. Moreover, it offers a valuable foundation for future research in the cultivation and pharmacological development within Polygonatum species.

Background

Polygonatum cyrtonema Hua (P. cyrtonema), a perennial herb of the Asparagaceae family, grows in forests, scrublands, and shaded hillsides [1], and is primarily distributed in the middle and lower reaches of the Yangtze River, including the provinces of Guizhou, Zhejiang, Hunan, Hubei, Anhui, and Sichuan [2]. P. cyrtonema is rich in active pharmaceutical ingredients, such as polysaccharides, flavonoids, and steroidal saponins, which is one of the official Chinese Pharmacopoeia Polygonatum species (Edition 2020) [3–5]. Modern pharmacological studies revealed that P. cyrtonema exerted various physiological effects, including the enhancement of fatigue resistance, antioxidant activity, immune system regulation, modulation of the intestinal microbiome, and anti-cancer properties [6–11]. As a result, P. cyrtonema is widely used in healthcare, food, and medicine with its growing demand. At present, P. cyrtonema sourced from their native wild habitat is gradually being depleted or destroyed, and artificial cultivation is an effective means to meet market demand.

Chloroplasts and mitochondria are important organelles in plants. The former are responsible for the process of photosynthesis, which involve the conversion of solar energy into stable chemical energy, and the storage of organic matter to sustain life on Earth [12]. The latter function as a source of energy, facilitating the decomposition of organic matter through respiration, a process that releases carbon dioxide and heat energy [13], which are essential for regulating the growth and circulation of plant cells [14]. Because of their maternal inheritance, both chloroplasts and mitochondria genomes are widely used in phylogenetics and evolutionary biology [15–17], which is important for plant cultivation [13, 18, 19]. However, the assembly of mitochondrial genomes has lagged behind that of chloroplast genomes because of their highly conserved and complex structure [20, 21]. Mitochondrial genomes are widely used in biogeographic and population genetic investigation due to its slowly evolution [22–24]. Moreover, owing to the development of improved sequencing and assembly technologies, a variety of plant mitochondrial genomes have been published, providing new insights into the evolution of species. P. cyrtonema, Polygonatum filipes, and Polygonatum odoratum are assigned to the sect. Polygonatum clade based on chloroplast genome sequences [25, 26]. Unfortunately, the current molecular markers and morphological characteristics don’t satisfy the demands for identifying Polygonatum Mill., possibly due to the limited number of molecular markers and their similar phenotypes. Moreover, only four mitochondrial genomes of Asparagus officinalis [27], Chlorophytum comosum [28], Polygonatum sibiricum [29] and Polygonatum kingianum (PQ374235.1) in the Asparagaceae family were published, which might impede to investigate systematic evolution of such family. Therefore, it is necessary to characterize the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema to classify Polygonatum Mill. and understand the phylogeny of Asparagaceae.

In this study, the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema was assembled using the second and third-generation sequencing strategy. The characteristics of the its mitochondrial genome were analyzed by repeat sequences, RNA editing, codon preference, and so on. The phylogenetic relationships of P. cyrtonema and other species were inferred using mitochondrial protein-coding genes (PCGs). Overall, this study provides mitochondrial genome information for P. cyrtonema to artificial cultivation and germplasm identification.

Materials and methods

Plant materials, DNA extraction and sequencing

P. cyrtonema collected from Jiuhuashan, Qingyang County, Chizhou City, Anhui Province, China, were cultivated in Yuhang District, Hangzhou City, Zhejiang Province, China. The well-grown young leaves of P. cyrtonema were harvested and immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen for total genomic DNA extraction.

Total genomic DNA was extracted using the Plant Genomic DNA Kit (DP305, Tiangen, Beijing, China), and its concentration and quality was evaluated by electrophoresis and NanoDrop. After successful construction of the library, high-quality genomic DNA was sequenced using Illumina Novaseq6000 (Illumina, San Diego, CA, USA) and the Oxford Nanopore PromethION sequencer (Oxford Nanopore Technologies, Oxford, UK) by Nanjing Genepioneer Biotechnologies Inc. (Nanjing, China). The Illumina and Nanopore sequencing data were filtered using fastp software (v0.20.0) (https://github.com/OpenGene/fastp) [30] and filtlong software (v0.2.1) (https://link.zhihu.com/?target=https%3A//github.com/rrwick/Filtlong).

Mitochondrial genome assembly and annotation

The mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema was assembled using a combination of the filtered Illumina and Nanopore strategies. Firstly, the Nanopore sequences were aligned to the references including coding sequences (CDS) and rRNA from published plant mitochondrial genomes using the three-generation comparison software minimap2 (v2.1) (https://github.com/xul962464/plant_mt_ref_gene) [31, 32]. And the aligned sequences with a comparison length greater than 50 bp were selected as candidate seed sequences. After the Nanopore sequences were aligned with the candidate seed sequences using minimap2 (v2.1) [31], the seed sequences were selected with an overlap greater than 1 kb and a similarity greater than 70%. The Nanopore sequences were iteratively compared to the seed sequences to obtain all the Nanopore sequences of the mitochondrial genome, which were corrected using the third-generation assembly software Canu (v1.5) [33]. Bowtie2 (v2.3.5.1) [34] was employed to align the Illumina sequences with the corrected Nanopore sequences. Subsequently, Unicycler (v0.4.8) [35] with default parameters was used to concatenate the above mentioned second-generation data with the corrected third-generation data, and minimap2 (v2.1) [31] was used to compare the corrected third-generation sequencing data with the contig obtained in the second step of Unicycler (v0.4.8) [35]. Finally, the above Illumina sequences and the corrected Nanopore sequences were concatenated using Unicycler (v0.4.8) [35] with default parameters. The corrected Nanopore sequences were compared to the contigs obtained in the second step of Unicycler (v0.4.8) [35] by using minimap2 (v2.1) [31]. Branch orientation was determined manually to obtain the final assembly result.

The assembled mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema was annotated as follows: (1) PCGs and rRNAs were compared with the published plant mitochondrial sequences using default parameter by blast (v2.14.0) [36], (2) tRNA and ORF were identified using the program tRNAscan-SE [37] and the OpenReading Frame Finder tool with default parameter (http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/gorf/gorf.html). The assembled mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema was mapped and visualized using OrganellarGenomeDraw (https://chlorobox.mpimp-golm.mpg.de/OGDraw.html) [38].

Repeat sequence analysis

Simple sequence repeats (SSRs) were detected using the MISA tool (v1.0) [39] with the parameters: 1 bp-10 repetitions, 2 bp-5 repetitions, 3 bp-4 repetitions, 4 bp-3 repetitions, 5 bp-3 repetitions, and 6 bp-3 repetitions. Tandem repeats were identified using the TRF tool (v4.10.0) [40] with the parameters: Match = 2, Mismatch = 7, Delta = 7, PM = 80, PI = 10, Minscore = 50, Maxperiod = 2000. Dispersed repeats were identified using the blastn tool (v2.14.0) [36] with the parameters: word_size = 7; E-value = 1e-5. The final result was visualized using the Circos tool (v0.69–5) [41].

Analysis of RNA editing site and homologous sequence among organelle genomes

The potential RNA editing sites of P. cyrtonema PCGs were predicted using a tool PmtREP (http://112.86.217.82:9919/#/tool/alltool/detail/336). Blast (v2.14.0) [36] was used to find the homologous sequences between the mitochondrial and chloroplast genome (OL436258) [42]. The E-value was set to 1e-5 with the similarity more than 70%. The results of homologous sequence results were visualized by Circos (v0.69–5) [41].

Analysis of RSCU and nucleotide diversity

The RSCU of the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema was calculated using a self-written Perl script (http://cloud.genepioneer.com/) based on the PCGs. Homologous genes between P. cyrtonema and five other species (Phoenix dactylifera, Asparagus officinalis, Allium cepa, Chlorophytum comosum, Crocus sativus) were obtained using the comparison rate score of blast and were multiply-aligned by MAFFT software (v7.427) [43]. Pi values of each genes were calculated using DnaSP5 [44].

Analysis of phylogenetic tree

Maximum likelihood phylogenetic trees (ML) were constructed using mitochondrial genome PCGs of P. cyrtonema and other 32 selected species (Table S1). Multiple sequence alignment was performed with MAFFT software (v7.427, auto mode) [43]. Then, the aligned sequences were connected end to end and trimmed by TrimAI (v1.4.rev15) [45]. The ML tree was generated using the RAxML software (v8.2.10) [46] with the best-fit model (GTR-GAMMA) determined by Jmodeltest (2.1.10) [47] at 1000 bootstrap replicas.

Analysis of selective pressure

Multiple alignment of PCGs of P. cyrtonema and five other species (Phoenix dactylifera, Asparagus officinalis, Allium cepa, Chlorophytum comosum, Crocus sativus) were performed by MAFFT software (v7.427) [43]. KaKs_Calculator v2.0 [48] software was employed to calculate the ratio of non-synonymous to synonymous substitutions (Ka/Ks) using the MLWL method with default parameter.

Results

Mitochondrial genome characteristics

The complete mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema was visualized as a complex and non-circular DNA molecule with 21 contigs by Bandage [49] (Fig. 1), totaling 664,991 bp with an average GC content of 46.30%. The length of contigs ranged from 209 bp to 125,074 bp with similar depth of coverage. Subsequently, a total of 56 functionally annotated genes were identified in the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema, including 33 protein-coding genes (PCGs), 20 tRNAs, and 3 rRNAs (Fig. 2). The PCGs could be divided into 9 different functional groups, which were described as follows: ATP synthase (5 genes), cytochrome c biogenesis (4 genes), ubiquinol cytochrome c reductase (1 gene), cytochrome c oxidase (3 genes), maturase (1 gene), transport membrane protein (1 gene), NADH dehydrogenase (9 genes), ribosomal protein (LSU) (2 genes), and ribosomal protein (SSU) (7 genes) (Table 1). The total length of the PCGs was 43,620 bp, accounting for 6.56% of the whole mitochondrial genome. In particular, we found that ccmFc and rps3 contained one intron; cox2 and atp1 had two introns; nad4 contained three introns; nad1, nad2, nad5, and nad7 had four introns. Interestingly, the cob gene was devoid of intron and was dispersed in contig 9 and contig 20, which encompassed five genes: cox2, atp1, nad1, nad2, and nad5 (Table S2). In addition, there was a large set of 20 tRNAs in the P. cyrtonema mitochondrial genome, among which, trnA-TGC, trnI-GAT, and trnN-GTT participated in protein translation after RNA splicing. In addition, only trnM-CAT had four copies, and the others were single copy (Table 1 and Table S2).

Repeat sequence authentication

According to sequence structure characteristics, repeat sequences could be divided into simple sequence, tandem, and dispersed repeats [50–52]. Therefore, three types of the repetitive sequence were analyzed in the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema. The results showed that 194 SSRs were detected and the most common type was tetranucleotide, accounting for 35.6% (n = 69), followed by mononucleotide (25.8%, n = 50), dinucleotide (23.2%, n = 45), trinucleotide (12.4%, n = 69), pentanucleotide (2.6%, n = 5), and hexanucleotide (0.5%, n = 1) (Figs. 3 and 4). A/T (n = 49) was the most frequent repeat motif, which represented 25.3% of the identified SSRs, secondly for AG/CT (n = 16, 8.2%), GA/TC (n = 16, 8.2%), and AAAG/CTTT (n = 10, 5.2%). In particular, contig1 had the highest number of SSRs among them (Table S3).

A total of 24 tandem repeats were obtained in the P. cyrtonema mitochondrial genome, with a length range of 14–43 bp and a matching degree ranging from 69 to 100% (Fig. 3 and Table S4). Five sequences (20.8%) had a matching degree of 100%. The copy number of the tandem repeats exhibited a range of 1.9 to 4.4, indicating that a significant proportion of the tandem repeats were incomplete copies. Similar to the results of SSRs, contig1 also contained the largest number of tandem repeats (n = 8, 33.3%).

Dispersed repeats are a kind of scattered repeats distributed in the genome, and have four different patterns of repeats, including forward repeats, reverse repeats, complement repeats and palindromic repeat. A total of 294 dispersed repeats with a mean length of approximately 64 bp were detected in the P. cyrtonema mitochondrial genome, including 153 palindromic repeats and 141 forward repeats, whereas no reverse or complement repeats were identified (Fig. 3 and Table S5). The majority of dispersed repeats ranged from 30 to 69 bp in length. In particular, the number of forward and palindromic repeats ranging from 30 to 39 bp was identical (forward: 33, palindromic: 33; 22.4%) (Fig. 5).

RNA editing sites prediction

A total of 599 effective RNA editing sites were identified in 56 PCGs of the P. cyrtonema mitochondrial genome (Fig. 6). It was worth noting that nad4 had the greatest number of RNA editing sites (n = 53), while atp8 and rps1 had the fewest (n = 2). Among the RNA editing sites, the properties of 42.73% (n = 256) amino acids remained unchanged, 47.58% (n = 285) of the hydrophilic amino acids turned into hydrophobic amino acids, 9.18% (n = 55) of the total sites changed from hydrophobic to hydrophilic amino acids, and 0.50% (n = 3) of amino acid coding sites resulted in stop codons, located in the atp9 and ccmFc genes (CGA-TGA), and atp6 gene (CAA-TAA), respectively (Table 2). RNA-editing machinery mainly targeted the second base of codon (n = 378, 63%), followed by the first base of codon (n = 201, 34%). In addition, there were two particular editing cases in which both the first and second bases of the triplet codon were edited, resulting in the conversion of Pro (CCC, CCT) to Phe (TTC, TTT) (n = 20, 3%). Interestingly, the amino acids generated from editing codons were most frequently converted to leucine (n = 248, 41%) after RNA editing, whereas the least were converted to methionine (n = 8, 1%).

Chloroplast-derived mitochondrial genome sequences

The mitochondrial and chloroplast genomes of P. cyrtonema were compared to provide evidence of sequence transfer (Fig. 7). Overall, the mitochondrial genome (664,991 bp) of P. cyrtonema was much bigger than the chloroplast genome (155,205 bp) [42]. There were 29 transferred fragments between them with a total length of 15,849 bp, covering 2.4% of the mitochondrial genome (Table S6). Interestingly, these transfer fragments mainly located the tRNA (n = 8) and rRNA (n = 4) genes. Among these homologous fragments, only three incomplete tRNA genes (trnN-GTT, trnM-CAT, and trnH-GTG) also appeared in the chloroplast genome synchronously (trnN-GUU, trnM-CAU, and trnH-GUG). Additionally, two chloroplast gene fragments, specifically rrn16 and rrn23, were homologous to rrn18 and rrn26 derived from mitochondrial genome (Table S6).

Codon usage analysis of PCGs

All the PCGs contained 10,056 codons in the P. cyrtonema mitochondrial genome (Table S7). The most frequent amino acids were Leu (10.3%), Ser (9.6%), and Ile (7.7%), while the expression frequency of other amino acids was more than 100. Therefore, all 64 types of codons were evenly used in the P. cyrtonema mitochondrial genome, and half of them had RSCU values of more than 1.0. Only Trp with 1.0 RSCU value showed no bias in codon usage (Fig. 8). Met (AUG) with RSCU value of 3, was the only initiator. Similarly, as for stop codons, UAA was the most frequently employed, whose RSCU values = 1.72. Because of the degeneracy of codons, there were different preferences for the codons used in the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema. For example, among the four codons (GCU, GCC, GCA, and GCG) encoded Ala, GCU (RSCU = 1.52) was the most frequently used codon.

Nucleotide diversity

To further investigate the selection variation of genes under different selective pressures in the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema, nucleotide diversity was conducted on PCGs and rRNAs. The results showed that Pi values with an average of 0.0328 ranged from 0.0079 (nad7) to 0.1642 (atp8) with a huge gap of 21 times (Fig. 9). Notably, nearly half of the PCGs (n = 16) and all rRNAs (n = 3) showed high rate of nucleotide diversity (> 0.03). Except for the gene atp8, all PCGs and rRNAs had stable fluctuations in the range of variation. From the perspective of functional enrichment, the ATP synthase function of PCGs exhibited considerable variability, while the others demonstrated lower performance.

Phylogenetic inference and substitution rates of PCGs

To investigate the evolutionary status of the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema, a phylogenetic tree was constructed on P. cyrtonema, together with 32 other species, using the maximum likelihood (ML) method (Table S1). The results showed that taxa from 22 families in the phylogenetic tree were well clustered (Fig. 10). In addition, Asparagaceae family clustered together and P. cyrtonema had the closest genetic relationship with Polygonatum sibiricum.

In order to estimate the selection pressure of P. cyrtonema mitochondrial PCGs during the evolution process, all of them were employed to compute the Ka/Ks ratio among the mitochondrial genomes of Phoenix dactylifera, Asparagus officinalis, Chlorophytum comosum, Allium cepa, Crocus sativus, and Epigeneium amplum. From the results, with the exception of the rpl2 gene, all Ka/Ks ratios of PCGs were less than 1.0 (Fig. 11).

Discussion

Structural features of the mitochondrial genome in P. cyrtonema

Most plant mitochondrial genomes were typically assembled and presented as circular maps, as shown in studies on Asparagus officinalis [27], Polygonatum kingianum, and Polygonatum sibiricum [29], indicating that circular genome-sized molecules were the primary form of plant mitochondrial DNA [53–55]. However, the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema constructed in this study was a complex non-circular form, indicating that the true structure of mitochondria is characterized by complexity and dynamics, consistent with findings from prior investigations in Quercus acutissima, Indigofera amblyantha, and Abies alba [56–59]. It was reported that the size of the plant mitochondrial genome was incredibly diverse ranging from 200 kb (Brassica napus) to over 10 Mb (Larix sibirica) [60, 61]. Overall, the published mitochondrial genome sizes of the Asparagaceae family were not prominent (< 1 Mb) (Table S1), indicating relatively conservative evolutionary dynamics. Interestingly, the mitochondrial genome sizes of P. cyrtonema were strikingly similar to other Polygonatum species (P. kingianum and P. sibiricum), whereas their estimated nuclear genome sizes differed significantly by flow cytometry [62], suggesting that interspecific variation within Polygonatum Mill. resides predominantly in nuclear genes.

Although the plant mitochondrial genomes possessed longer lengths and more complex variations relative to their animal mitochondrial counterparts, their PCGs were highly conserved [63–65]. According to previous studies, 41 PCGs were inferred to exist in the ancestral mitogenome of angiosperms [66]. In contrast, 33 PCGs were found in the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema. It seemed that some of them were lost or transfered during evolution [67, 68]. Through comparative analysis mitochondrial PCGs in P. cyrtonema and 32 additional species, several notable patterns were identified (Table S1). The rps2, rps11, and rpl10 genes were lost during the early evolution in most eukaryotic plants, which was predicted by Adams and Kubo [69, 70]. Asparagaceae species exhibited a higher frequency of rpl2 and sdh3 genes transfer events from mitochondrial to nuclear genomes throughout evolutionary history [71, 72]. In Polygonatum Mill., the missing rps7 gene was possibly transferred and integrated into the nuclear genomes [73]. Additionally, given that P. cyrtonema exhibited the lowest number of PCGs among the published Polygonatum species, it could deduce that P. cyrtonema had drastic genetic changes during evolution from a common ancestor to better adapt to their environment.

Variation and diversity in the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema

Abundant repetitive sequences played an important role in plant mitochondrial genome evolution and significantly promoted gene recombination within mitochondrial genome [74, 75]. In this study, a number of 512 repeat sequences were identified in the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema, which not only furnished a substantial number of reference sites for genetic evolution, but also provide a foundation for developing molecular markers for germplasm identification within Polygonatum Mill. [76–78]. Furthermore, ultralong repetitive sequences were of particular interest, as they not only leaded to isomerization, but were more likely to interact with structural changes in genes to promote evolution [67, 79, 80]. Within the published mitochondrial genome of the Asparagaceae family, ultralong repetitive sequence (> 1000 bp) resulted in the duplication of genes, such as trnM-CAT. Therefore, ultralong repetitive sequences might not only drive the duplication of the trnM-CAT gene into four copies, but also contribute to the structural complexity of the P. cyrtonema mitochondrial genome.

RNA editing, a post-transcriptional process in plant mitochondrial and chloroplast genomes, modified nucleotide sequences in mRNA level, which enabled more efficient protein folding [81, 82]. All of 599 RNA editing sites were found in the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema, which was more than Asparagus officinalis (510) [27] and less than Polygonatum sibiricum (609) [29]. The main type of editing change was a transition from C to T in P. cyrtonema RNA editing sites, which was also a general type of RNA editing change in other plants [73]. At the same time, the RNA editing of P. cyrtonema mainly happened at the first and second base positions, which was also reported in most plants [32, 83]. In particular, P. cyrtonema RNA editing greatly increased the proportion of hydrophobic amino acids, which was similar to Polygonatum sibiricum [29]. The increasing proportion of hydrophobic amino acids contributed to the stability of protein structure [84]. Furthermore, plant mitochondria mitigated stress-induced damage by modulating RNA editing events in specific PCGs under environmental perturbations [85–89]. By combining RNA editing site prediction results with previous studies in Polygonatum sibiricum, cytochrome c biogenesis and NADH dehydrogenase-related PCGs were preferentially edited [29]. It could hypothesize that they experienced comparable environmental pressures throughout evolutionary history.

Evolutionary trends of mitochondrial genes in P. cyrtonema

Codon usage preference might result from species mutation, genetic drift, and translational selection to some extent [90–92]. In the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema, the most preference codon was TTT (phenylalanine), followed by ATT (isoleucine), TTC (phenylalanine), GAA (glutamic acid), ATG (methionine), which was also described in Polygonatum sibiricum [29]. Intriguingly, like most resolved plant mitochondria, P. cyrtonema exhibited a general preference for Leu and Ser in amino acid utilization, while demonstrating the lowest usage of Trp and Cys. Furthermore, the number of codons ending with A/T outnumbered those ending with C/G (Table S7), which was reported in most plant species [93, 94], indicating that the mitochondrial genes of Polygonatum Mill. had similar preferences for base changes.

The construction of a phylogenetic evolutionary tree not only determined the evolutionary position of the species but also elucidated the intrageneric and interspecific phylogeny of the family to which the species belongs [95, 96]. In this study, P. cyrtonema was most closely related to Polygonatum sibiricum, followed by Polygonatum kingianum. The similar CG content among Polygonatum Mill. corroborated their distribution in phylogenetic trees, which was consistent with the results of the evolutionary tree based on chloroplast genomes [25]. This indicated that classification based on PCGs in the mitochondrial genome was effective for plant classification and confirmed the evolutionary position of P. cyrtonema within Polygonatum Mill.. Additionally, it also provided other references for exploring phylogenetic relationships in Asparagaceae. In conclusion, although significant progress was made in phylogenetic and evolutionary analyses of the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema, more detailed biogeographic studies of related species will be necessary to elucidate their evolutionary history further.

Conclusions

In this study, the complete P. cyrtonema mitochondrial genome was sequenced and assembled based on a combination of long-read Nanopore and Illumina sequencing technologies, which was a complex non-circular form with a length of 664,991 bp, including 56 genes (33 protein-coding genes, 20 tRNA genes, and 3 rRNA genes). Subsequently, the diversity of DNA fragments in the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema was elucidated by analyzing repeat sequence identification, RNA editing site prediction, and gene transfer between chloroplasts and mitochondria. Finally, the genetic status and evolutionary trend of the mitochondrial genome of P. cyrtonema were addressed due to codon bias, synonym and non-synonym substitution rate, nucleotide diversity, and phylogenetic tree construction. In conclusion, this study enhances our understanding of the phylogeny and genetic evolution of P. cyrtonema through its mitochondrial genome, fills a critical gap in its genomic research, and provides a valuable theoretical foundation for future studies, as well as the development and utilization of this important species.
