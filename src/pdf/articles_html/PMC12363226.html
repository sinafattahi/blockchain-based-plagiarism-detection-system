Abstract

Exercise is increasingly recognized by patients, clinicians, and allied health professionals globally as an important component of cancer care. In this paper, we provide a viewpoint on developments in exercise oncology over the past 4 decades leading up to the creation of the International Society of Exercise Oncology (ISEO). We briefly review research in adult and pediatric cancers from early foundation studies to larger randomized controlled trials published in mainstream oncology journals alongside critical work undertaken in exercise and cancer biological mechanisms. We also discuss potential strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats facing ISEO in becoming a global forum for exercise oncology. Building on the foundational work undertaken over the past 4 decades by researchers, clinicians, and practitioners, ISEO provides an opportunity to support research, leverage collaborations and partnerships, facilitate education and training, increase awareness of exercise oncology, and support translation of research to clinical practice, ultimately improving the quality and quantity of life for people with cancer.

Introduction

It is important to retrospectively examine past milestones in exercise and cancer to inform future directions and continuing progress in a field that is increasingly recognized by patients, clinicians, and allied health professionals globally as a critical component of cancer care. Here we provide a viewpoint on developments in this exciting field of exercise medicine for cancer, termed exercise oncology, which continues to mature and gain recognition with strong evidence of improved patient outcomes, and endorsement by oncology and exercise science organizations internationally. In undertaking this endeavor, we selected a few key seminal events acknowledging that there are many contributions to the field that will have been missed as this was not a systematic attempt to highlight all events.

To facilitate the progression of the field of exercise oncology, the International Society of Exercise Oncology (ISEO) was proposed in 2024. The ISEO mission statement is provided in Box 1.

Box 1.

International Society of Exercise Oncology (ISEO) mission statement. “The International Society of Exercise Oncology or ISEO aims to increase awareness of the critical role that exercise plays in cancer prevention, treatment and survivorship. ISEO’s highest priority is changing the standard of care in oncology to include exercise throughout the cancer patient’s journey—beginning in prehabilitation and rehabilitation as well as in long term survivorship. ISEO is the first organization of its kind to bring together a membership that extends across a wide array of medical, fitness and scientific disciplines from research and clinical practice to workforce development.”

We also discuss potential strengths and opportunities for ISEO to become a global forum for exercise oncology to support research, leverage international collaborations and partnerships, mentor students and early midcareer researchers, provide training and translation of research outcomes to practice, promote exercise oncology to other health and medical organization, and governments, ultimately improving the quality and quantity of life for people with cancer. Much has been accomplished since the mid-1980s, and further critical work is underway for an increasing population of people with cancer who will receive earlier and longer oncological treatments. ISEO is well placed to make a difference in this rapidly advancing field.

Early discoveries and observations

The first report of an association between muscular activity and cancer incidence goes back more than a century. In 1921, Siversten and Dahlstrom1 reported an observation that muscular work associated with occupation was inversely related to cancer mortality.

In 1944, one of the first preclinical experiments by Rusch and Kline2 in mice undergoing forced exercise and inoculated with cancer resulted in lower tumor (fibrosarcoma) size (by approximately 25%-34%) compared with controls. This work was replicated in subsequent decades forming the genesis of preclinical research in exercise suppression of cancer.

Exercise oncology in the 1980s and 1990s

In one of the earliest reports of exercise in cancer patients, Buettner and Gavron3 in 1981 presented at the Third International Symposium of Adapted Physical Activity that objective and self-reported outcome measures from patients with cancer were improved after 8 weeks of aerobic exercise. In 1983, Schüle4 evaluated group exercise for patients with breast cancer and reported that the intervention did not harm and provided benefits for the participants. Subsequently, Winningham et al.5 published in 1986 in the Physician and Sports Medicine an opinion review that exercise was initially proposed as a potential restorative technique for cancer patients but considered a novel concept. The basis of this work was a 10-week aerobic exercise intervention in women with breast cancer receiving chemotherapy where the exercise intervention group reported improved nausea compared with the usual care group6 and the earlier study from Buettner and Gavron.3 A timeline of key subsequent events is presented in Figure 1.

In the mid-1990s, Friendenreich and Courneya7 advocated the critical need for additional studies in exercise and cancer after reviewing 7 published studies specifically in breast cancer. Preliminary findings at the time were improvements in functional capacity and lean mass; reduction of fat mass, nausea, and fatigue, and enhancement in quality of life. Their recommendations for future research included (1) larger number of patients from a defined population (same cancer site and treatment period) and those with high survival and with comorbidities whereby exercise would have potential to improve outcomes; (2) improvements to study design and incorporation of randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and considerations of a placebo exercise group; (3) longer length of exercise interventions (greater than 12 weeks), timing of exercise interventions in relation to cancer treatment, variation of key components of exercise prescription, individual vs group exercise, home vs clinic-based programs, cancer-specific barriers to exercise, and the addition of objective measures of exercise and physical activity; (4) attention to physiological and psychological measurements (correct tools and techniques and repeat assessments over time); and (5) attention to logistics of recruitment and exercise adherence for people with cancer to inform clinical recommendations. Many of these research questions and recommendations have been addressed throughout numerous clinical trials over the subsequent 3 decades of research and clinical practice with notable advancements achieved in several areas of exercise oncology.

Other advancements in the late 1990s comprised a series of studies from Dimeo and colleagues8-10 investigating the effect of aerobic exercise during chemotherapy treatment and in patients followed by autologous stem cell transplantation. Overall findings were preservation of physical function during hospitalization, reduction in fatigue and improvements in physiological parameters such as duration of neutropenia, and reduced thrombocytopenia after exercise intervention.

Another exciting field is exercise in the context of pediatric and adolescent cancer. Indeed, childhood tumors usually show quite distinct biological characteristics compared with adult cancers and develop in a moment of life when the immune system might not be totally mature. Furthermore, treatments are typically more aggressive than in adults, leading to a myriad of sequalae, often lasting decades after treatment. The first study showing a positive association between leisure-time physical activity during adolescent cancer and health benefits—such as an improved psychosocial well-being—was published in 1999.11 In the same year, the effects of a tailored 12-week exercise intervention in adolescents undergoing treatment for different types of cancer resulted in improved health markers, such as peak oxygen uptake (VO2peak) and mental health.12

Exercise oncology in the 2000s

An instrumental shift in exercise oncology research publications occurred in the 2000s when original research with stronger study designs and review papers were published in highly prestigious mainstream oncology journals (previously appearing only in exercise science and nursing discipline journals), such as the Lancet Oncology13 and the Journal of Clinical Oncology (JCO)14-16 and the first systematic review with meta-analysis published in Cancer Epidemiology, Biomarkers and Prevention.17 This work led to larger visibility of exercise oncology research influencing clinical recommendations globally, creating a new platform (exercise studies appearing in mainstream oncology journals) where oncology clinicians could access this innovative work in oncology and subsequently support and contribute with their own perspectives, collaborating and leading clinical trials. In 2001, the Physical Exercise Across the Cancer Experience framework18 provided the first attempt to organize the emerging field of exercise oncology by proposing cancer-related time periods and cancer control categories. In 2007, the framework was updated to the Physical Activity and Cancer Control framework (Figure 2).19

In Lancet Oncology, Lucia et al.13 offered a unique perspective on exercise being “very useful” to oncologists, by combating cancer-related fatigue resulting from radiation and chemotherapy and making an analogy to cardiovascular disease and the long-abandoned recommendation “to seek periods of rest and to reduce their amount of physical activity.” Critically, much of the research that followed demonstrated exercise to be critical in ameliorating cancer-related fatigue.20

In JCO, pivotal original research was published in 2001 and 2003 by Segal et al.14 and Courneya et al.,15 respectively, in patients and survivors of breast cancer and in patients with prostate cancer in 200321 with an accompanying editorial22 entitled “Exercise and Cancer: No Pain, Some Gain?” highlighting that studies in exercise and cancer addressing direct concerns identified by patients would only increase. Subsequently, Galvão and Newton16 in JCO reviewed exercise intervention studies during and following cancer treatments reporting on preliminary evidence of physiological and psychological benefits. There were more than 20 studies reported at the time and a recommendation was proposed to include other cancer groups in addition to those primarily with breast cancer to investigate resistance training as an exercise mode to counteract treatment-related side effects and to examine training dose–response to support patients in improving physical function and quality of life. In 2007, the highly cited Supervised Trial of Aerobic vs Resistance Training on the effects of aerobic vs resistance exercise in breast cancer patients receiving adjuvant chemotherapy was the first study to show that exercise may improve chemotherapy completion.23

The first systematic review and meta-analysis on exercise and cancer by Schmitz et al. published in 2005 in Cancer Epidemiology, Biomarkers and Prevention17 provided pooled quantitative data showing small to moderate positive effects of exercise on cardiorespiratory fitness, selected physiologic outcomes, and symptoms and side effects during cancer treatment and large positive effects on cardiorespiratory fitness and vitality following treatment completion. At that point, exercise oncology research continued to flourish, and the field expanded to other cancer groups and the examination of exercise modes (second generation of studies) on patient-reported and surrogates of treatment outcomes (eg, chemotherapy completion)23 and specific treatment toxicities by cancer (eg, lymphedema (breast),24 androgen deprivation, and musculoskeletal health (prostate)25 with the evolution of this work appearing in mainstream medical journals (Journal of the American Medical Association and New England Journal of Medicine).26,27 A first RCT published in JCO focused on patients with lymphoma also suggested that aerobic exercise may improve chemotherapy efficacy.28

Simultaneously, initial epidemiological studies showed an association between higher levels of exercise and increased survival in breast29 and colon30,31 cancer; a new narrative with increased clinical depth and relevance started to evolve and raised the question, does exercise have an impact on cancer progression and patient survival? An increasing number of epidemiological studies showed an association between higher levels of exercise in patients with cancer and increased survival.

Consequently, investigations into the potential biological mechanisms driving these associations between exercise and increased survival began to appear. Jones et al.32,33 published a series of key papers on the effect of exercise on tumor biology—some of this preclinical work addressed the potential sensitivity and efficacy of chemotherapy and vascular normalization of the tumor induced by exercise training.34

In 2009, Exercise and Sports Science Australia (ESSA) produced the first national position statement on exercise and cancer: optimizing cancer outcomes through exercise.35 In this publication, exercise prescription guidelines (aerobic and resistance exercise), considerations, and contraindications were presented with a summary of potential benefits from exercise. Conclusions were that exercise should be seen as “adjuvant therapy” with the aim to maintain and/or improve function, alleviate symptoms, and assist with recovery.

Data in pediatric cancer were comparatively scarcer, largely owing to the rarity of this condition—and the subsequent recruitment challenges—compared with adult cancer in general. Yet, pioneering studies in the first decade of this century showed that not only prepubertal but also very young children (starting from the age of 4 years) receiving maintenance therapy for acute lymphoblastic leukemia could perform, and favorably adapt to, tailored weight training exercise, whether performed in the hospital setting36 or at home.37

Exercise oncology in the 2010s

American College of Sports Medicine Roundtable of Exercise and Cancer 2010: a landmark contribution placing exercise oncology on the global stage

The effects of exercise on breast, prostate, colon, hematological, and gynecologic cancers were jointly presented for the first time with clinical evidence on safety and efficacy weighted and assessed from original data and expert opinion by a team of researchers and clinicians from the United States, Canada, Australia, and Spain.38 The overall outcome was rated as level of evidence B (determined as “few RCTs exist, or they are small and results are inconsistent”), and general recommendations were to avoid rest and adhere to the physical activity guidelines for healthy adults, with specific exercise programming adaptations based on disease and treatment-related adverse effects—this was a considerable shift in clinical recommendation as clinicians at the time would advise patients with cancer to rest and avoid exercise. The global reach of this document was enormous with the work being cited in more than 70 countries (Web of Science).

Preclinical exercise studies showed the potential of exercise to attenuate the growth of some types of tumors. One key mechanistic preclinical study by a Danish group led by Dr Pernille Hojman related exercise, adrenaline, and interleukin (IL)-6 to natural killer (NK) cell mobilization and infiltration into tumors, with a subsequent reduction in the growth rate of several types of tumor (eg, melanoma, lung, liver) (Figure 3).39 Two of the main cytokines released from skeletal muscle (myokines) with a higher potential to improve anticancer immune function (mainly through their positive effects on T-cell homeostasis) are IL-7 and IL-15,40 with muscle-released IL-15 driving CD8+ T-cell homing41 and alterations in the abundance of hundreds of intratumoral proteins and molecular pathways, particularly those involved in energy metabolism, apoptosis, and tumor suppression.42-44

Of interest, in 2017 it was reported in JAMA Oncology45 that exercise was the most effective treatment for the management of fatigue during and after completion of primary cancer treatments. In 2019, the largest RCT in patients with bone metastatic disease reported improvements in physical function with no skeletal complications or increased bone pain.46 Increased work in this setting has followed with larger international clinical trials conducted in patients with metastatic disease.47,48

An update and evolution American College of Sports Medicine Roundtable 2019: consensus statement from an international multidisciplinary roundtable

In a follow-up from American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) 2010, 40 clinicians, researchers, and consumers from 20 national and international organizations participated in a second ACSM roundtable to advance and update exercise recommendations for patients with cancer. Three documents were produced from this roundtable: (1) physical activity, sedentary behavior, and cancer prevention and control discusses biological and epidemiologic evidence for the role of exercise in cancer prevention and survival;49 (2) exercise guidelines for cancer survivors, consensus statement, discusses exercise recommendations extending beyond public health guidelines toward prescriptive programs based on cancer type, treatments, and/or outcomes;50 and (3) exercise is medicine in oncology discusses engaging clinicians to help patients move through cancer focuses on exercise programming and opportunities for exercise referrals by oncology clinicians and provides summary and key points of the 2 previous papers.51 This was a important effort, and as of December 2024, these 3 publications collectively attracted more than 2500 citations (Scopus) demonstrating a substantial interest, growth, and impact of exercise oncology globally.

In 2019, ESSA produced its second position statement—exercise medicine in cancer management.52 A comprehensive exercise prescription framework of cancer-specific considerations for individualization, specificity, safety, feasibility, and progression was developed alongside considerations for treatment-related concerns, including hematological, musculoskeletal, systemic, cardiovascular, lymphatic, gastrointestinal, genitourinary, and neurological disease, and presence of comorbid chronic disease. The concept of “targeted exercise prescription” for specific clinical needs, which includes the provision of behavior change advice and support, was provided for the greatest short- and long-term benefit. Alongside ACSM, these updated statements provided more advanced exercise prescription and specific cancer considerations for patients. Other organizations developed similar guidelines for exercise and physical activity for patients with cancer and survivors including the American Cancer Society,53 American Society of Clinical Oncology,54 the Clinical Oncology Society of Australia,55 and the Spanish Society of Medical Oncology.56 Importantly, in 2024 the American Society of Clinical Oncology highlighted exercise as the recommended gold standard first-line therapy for treating the major toxicity of fatigue.57

In addition, the field of pediatric exercise oncology has considerably grown in the last 2 decades. As such, there is now RCT-derived evidence that exercise intervention starting during pediatric cancer treatment has beneficial effects on muscle strength58 and functional mobility,59 with prospective controlled studies also showing benefits on echocardiography-derived cardiac function (ie, attenuation of cardiotoxicity).60

A growing number of mechanistic studies were subsequently published, however, the translation of this work into clinical trials is still ongoing. It has been demonstrated that exercise training by patients with advanced prostate cancer over several months increases the levels of cancer-suppressive myokines released from muscle and that exposing prostate cancer cells in vitro to serum from these patients substantially suppressed their growth.61 Further, additional acute bouts of exercise drive a further “dose” of these myokines and even greater growth suppression.62 Of note, however, some caution is needed because isolated in vitro models are unable to mimic in vivo conditions, where all the tissular, circulating, and immune players—whether pro or antitumorigenic—interact with each other.63

One example of a proof-of-concept RCT64 as the first human study examining the effects of an exercise intervention on NK cell infiltration was published in the setting of localized prostate cancer—with tumor tissue comparisons and NK infiltration compared prior to and after chronic exercise. The outcomes were not consistent with NK infiltration responses occurring based on the type of analyses (intention-to-treat and per-protocol analyses).65 Another exploratory analysis66 from the Supervised Trial of Aerobic vs Resistance Training examining exercise modes during adjuvant chemotherapy for breast cancer showed a nonsignificant disease-free survival advantage (82.7% for exercise vs 75.6% controls) at a median follow-up of 89 months, with the exercise (resistance training) group having improved chemotherapy completion rates in the original report.23 This finding may be due to enhanced chemotherapy sensitivity and efficacy, but more research is required to elucidate the underlying mechanisms and generalizability to other tumor types, chemotherapy regimens, and other anticancer therapies. In addition, others have shown potential associations between exercise and survival in patients with lymphoma,67 allogeneic stem cell transplant,68 or breast cancer.69 At present, data are maturing for exercise trials on clinical cancer outcomes in Colon Health and LifeLong Exercise Change (CHALLENGE) trial co.70 and Intense Exercise for Survival among Men with Metastatic Castrate-Resistant Prostate Cancer (INTERVAL-GAP4).47

Understanding the effects of exercise on cancer biology is a particularly exciting avenue of investigation as this will pave the way for more precision exercise oncology prescription and enhanced patient outcomes and potentially inform new drug development and treatments. If mechanistic proof of exercise increasing cancer-specific survival is realized, then strong clinical and government support will ensue. However, larger and more sophisticated trials are required to translate biological mechanisms into clinical trials and practice, and ISEO could be a platform to facilitate these activities.

Forming the ISEO: strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats

A strategic tool Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats (SWOT) was used to guide discussions on potential merits for ISEO.

Overall strengths relate to the following 4 areas.

  1. The creation of a global forum for exercise oncology: ISEO to become a unique ecosystem where researchers and clinicians globally could come together to advance education, research, and clinical practice of exercise oncology.

  2. People and level of expertise: The exercise oncology community has people across multidisciplinary areas with high levels of expertise in clinical exercise science, oncology, behavioral science, cancer biology, clinical trials, epidemiology, and imaging. This existent capacity can be leveraged extensively.

  3. Build on established international networks: There are numerous opportunities to increase collaborations and partnerships internationally to advance the field, and ISEO can facilitate this process.

  4. Well established research foundation: As presented above, exercise oncology has a large body of research developed over the past 4 decades with multiple trials published in mainstream oncology journals and recommendations endorsed by professional national and international organizations.

Weaknesses identified in discussions relate to the following 3 areas.

  1. Lack of processes and/or disparity of skill levels and/or experience in research and clinical practice: Translation from research to clinical practice has not been widely implemented. More work is required to translate new knowledge from research into practice, and ISEO can contribute to these efforts.

  2. International clinical trials in exercise oncology have been challenging: A limited number of large-scale multinational clinical trials have been successfully completed in exercise oncology, and there is an opportunity for ISEO to support some of these efforts.

  3. Lack of trained exercise professionals: Overall, there is a lack of trained exercise oncology professionals globally. Although initial efforts in addressing these gaps have been proposed, more work is required to address this shortfall in capacity. Differences exist across countries and continents with specific needs related to geographical areas.

Potential ISEO opportunities relate to the following 6 areas.

  1. Recognition by oncology clinicians: Although gaining acceptance in medical oncology, exercise is not universally recognized by all clinicians, hospitals, and practices, and as such, continuing work is required to further improve collaborative clinical care, research, and access to this potentially beneficial therapy.

  2. Global accreditation in exercise oncology: There is a need for training professionals in exercise oncology and ensuring quality control. ISEO could contribute to an accreditation or level of quality control with continuing education points such as undertaken by other professional organizations.

  3. Further networks and collaborations: As a global forum for exercise oncology, ISEO would bring individuals and teams together (virtually and in person) creating an opportunity for collaborations and development of new partnerships nationally and internationally.

  4. Mentoring of trainees, early and midcareer researchers: A structured plan for mentorship could be developed by ISEO to further support and develop the next generation of researchers and clinicians.

  5. Consistent communication on exercise oncology: ISEO could develop and support a clear and concise channel for information on exercise oncology globally. This would require an online presence with a dedicated ISEO website and social media channels.

  6. Research and multinational clinical trials: There could be opportunities in multinational clinical trials to further improve the logistics, promotion, and recruitment of large phase III trials via ISEO and avoid research duplication.

There were 5 primary threats considered.

  1. Integration of research findings into clinical care: RCTs and their lack of integration with clinical practice is recognized in all areas of medicine as clinical trials and care delivery can function as separate entities,71 and this is no different in exercise oncology research—this is a considerable threat to ISEO.

  2. Funding for exercise oncology clinical care: Lack of funding for implementation of exercise oncology into clinical practice is also a major risk.

  3. Other established societies and meetings: How will ISEO fit in the presence of existing societies and meetings? There are opportunities for ISEO meetings to be conducted independently or jointly with other international societies (eg, ACSM and ESSA).

  4. Initial and continued funding: How will ISEO be funded, and what level is required to develop and maintain this initiative?

  5. Long-term sustainability of ISEO: Long-term sustainability will require efforts from members and a sound governance structure to oversee activities and foster growth.

Conclusions

Building on the foundational work undertaken over the past 4 decades by researchers, clinicians, and practitioners, ISEO provides an opportunity for a global forum for exercise oncology to support research, leverage collaborations and partnerships, facilitate education and training, increase awareness of the field, and support translation of research to clinical practice. Europe 2026 is planned for the first ISEO meeting where researchers, exercise professionals, clinicians, and stakeholders can further enhance the field of exercise oncology.
