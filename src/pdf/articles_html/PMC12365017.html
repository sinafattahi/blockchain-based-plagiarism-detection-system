Abstract

This work uses the Darcy–Forchheimer model in porous media to study the thermal and flow behaviour of such hybrid nanofluids, monitoring the combined impacts of viscous dissipation, porosity, Forchheimer number, Eckert number, and changing water temperatures. Velocity, temperature, and heat transfer profiles are examined using the BVP4C numerical scheme, which has better accuracy in solving nonlinear boundary value problems. Response Surface Methodology (RSM) is used to optimize system performance, including analysing multi-parameter interactions and how these factors affect the effectiveness of heat transfer to complement the numerical approach. Certain parameter settings resulted in a notable improvement in the hybrid nanofluids’ thermal performance. The results indicate that the increasing values of porosity and inertial resistance, instead of accelerating the flow velocity, slow it down, but at the same time, their effect enhances the thermal distribution profile. Viscous dissipation significantly affects thermal energy, and the Eckert number () is essential for figuring out the temperature profile of fluid flows. The increase in kinetic energy to thermal energy, with increasing Eckert number, is also evident, as demonstrated by the increased fluid temperatures. Optimal configurations delineated through RSM reveal improvements in the Nusselt number contingent on specific parametric combinations. Moreover, the study fills the gap in knowing the thermal behaviour of hybrid nanofluids under varied temperature conditions in porous structures. The results provided in this article offer valuable guidelines for designing and operating thermal systems in applications that include advanced cooling technologies, heat exchangers, biomedical devices, and renewable energy systems. A mathematical approach that integrates numerical and statistical methodologies offers a robust framework for advancing the field of hybrid nanofluid research and its emerging applications.

Keywords: Forchheimer effect, Porous effect, Viscous dissipation, Response surface methodology, Hybrid nanofluid and water at different temperatures

Subject terms: Engineering, Mathematics and computing

Introduction

Recent studies have focused on enhancing thermal transfer efficiency using hybrid nanofluids within numerous thermal systems. Nanofluids are made through the integration of a single nanoparticle with a base fluid. Hybrid nanofluids are synthesised through the combination of two different nanoparticles in a base fluid.The thermal conductivity of these diminutive nanoparticles is exceptional. Augmentation of fluid thermal transport capabilities, through their distribution within a base fluid, is significant. Due to their improved thermal properties, hybrid nanofluids are now being investigated and applied in various industrial and scientific fields. Göksu et al.1 also realised that in the case of heat sinks, the single-phase nanofluids in water showed better cooling performance than the two-phase nanofluids in water. Haque et al.2 improved the heat transfer coefficient when contrasted with the single-phase water and -Water nanofluids within the recto trapezoidal cavity. Gaur et al.3 depicted that the Brick-Blade hybrid nanofluid in the heat exchanger with three concentric tubes yields thermal performance factors comparable to different nanoparticle shapes. In addition, Azizi et al.4 established that in the shell-and-conical coil heat exchanger, the /Water hybrid nanofluid outperformed other hybrid nanofluids, where higher concentrations were generally linked to better thermal yields. The Darcy-Forchheimer model was examined with concern for numerous fluid flow situations. Managing porous media is considered a space to address heat transfer and control the fluid velocity during different manufacturing processes (such as hydrocarbon extraction and management of radioactive waste). Although the modified Darcy–Forchheimer model addresses issues with high-velocity flow, the basic Darcy’s rule is still employed to define the features of this medium. Nihaal et al.5 showed that the model effectively predicts the flow scenario in porous media and explored when it is valid, such as in heat exchange and filtration procedures. Zhu et al.6 also assessed the influence of rough fractures on the fluid flow when in shear conditions; the Forchheimer equation is shown to incorporate nonlinearity. For the flow types occurring in architectures with high Reynolds numbers in both SRM and LSM arrangements, an extended Darcy-Forchheimer model, which imitates the inertial flow distortion, was studied by Aghaei-Jouybari et al.7. In studying MHD hybrid nanofluid flows, Ramana et al.8 found that the Forchheimer number reduced velocity in the x and y direction with an enhancement in the concentration profile of hybrid nanofluids. The Darcy-Forchheimer model for micropolar nanofluid flow over a flexible surface incorporating bioconvective and chemical reaction effects is studied by Khan et al.9. Collectively, these investigations highlight the versatility and importance of the Forchheimer effect when employed in broadening our understanding of complex flow phenomena at substantially large scales of interest for a broad range of applications and scenarios. When flows are moving at high speeds, viscous dissipation is important in the field of convective heat transfer, the fluid viscosity is high, or the Prandtl number is high. In situations where the thermal gradients between the wall and the fluid are small or where the heat fluxes at the walls are small, however, this phenomenon becomes much more important, influencing the energy exchange within the fluid and, consequently, the thermal distribution in the fluid region. To accurately predict heat transfer behaviour in a wide range of fluid flow scenarios, a thorough understanding of the ramifications of viscous dissipation is essential. Recent publications have focused on exploring the diverse impacts of viscous dissipation under varied flow circumstances.

In the simulations of rotating, layered convection in a stratified plane, Lance et al.10 found that viscous dissipation is not contingent on viscosity or thermal diffusivity whenever convection is strong enough. Khedher et al.11 measured the fluid flow over a horizontal circular cylinder under the magnetic field effect and pointed out that viscous dissipation strongly affects it. Sahu Vocale et al.12 noted that at low Knudsen numbers and steep angles, the convective heat transfer coefficient is strongly influenced by the viscous forces, acute angles in the analysis of the rhombic microchannels. Furthermore, Zhang et al.13 studied foil journal bearings and analysed that thermal influence due to viscous dissipation changed the shear flow aerodynamics and dynamic characteristics of the elastic foils at higher speeds of rotation and eccentricity ratios. The morphological effects and hall current on non-Newtonian fluid are examined by Rauf et al.14. Sohail et al.15 examined the use of ternary hybrid nanoparticles in a porous sheet to increase the temperature of a pseudo-plastic liquid.The unsteady magnetic-driven convective transport in porous materials of a binary fluid with micropolar characteristics over an oblique surface, investigating the impact of viscous dissipation by Hossain et al.16. In many thermal exchange processes, heat transfer optimisation is of paramount importance. Mathematically, one can realise this objective through various methodologies. Response Surface Methodology (RSM), on the other hand, is one such technique that combines mathematical principles and statistical analysis for the design, improvement, and optimisation of processes. The optimisation of nanofluid flow systems has been the focus of recent research, which utilises sensitivity analysis and response surface methodology (RSM). RSM was used by Ajaykumar et al.17 to examine entropy optimisation in Casson-Williamson nanofluid movement along a curved sheet, and by Basit et al.18 to study bioconvective nanofluid flow over wavy cylinders. This study shows that RSM can model complex fluid dynamics and optimise system parameters. Furthermore, Farouk et al.19 modelled solar stills’ thermal efficiency, incorporating nanoparticle-added phase change materials using the RSM approach. Apart from RSM, a study in which Nasir et al.20 integrated computational intelligence techniques, including neural networks, with numerical methods for analysing trihybrid nanofluid flow in porous media is also reported. Response surface methodology was applied by Shao et al.21 to improve the thermal transfer rate of flow through a pharmaceutical delivery system.

Bikash and Ahmed22 used a Darcy-Forchheimer porous medium to investigate the effects of single-walled carbon nanotube (SNT) nanoparticles in the base fluid, ethylene glycol, through hydrodynamic steady exchanges and thermal transfers of the overstretching plate. Das et al.23 developed a comprehensive mathematical model to determine how essential parameters affect the temperature field and the dynamical characteristics of a nanofluid’s flow across an inclined cylinder in a non-Darcy porous constrained situation. The Lewis number, Peclet number, volume fraction, and angle of inclination are some of these factors. Sahin and Das24 investigated the kinematics of molecules in a Williamson micropolar fluid through a stretchy sheet in a porous field, accounting for microrotation in a magnetic field and the Darcy-Forchheimer premises.

The current study’s novelty.

  • combines the Darcy–Forchheimer porous model with hybrid nanofluids at different base fluid temperatures, which is not often done.

  • combines the effects of Eckert numbers and viscous dissipation to capture the conversion of kinetic energy into thermal energy.

  • Uses a hybrid approach that combines Response Surface Methodology (RSM) and BVP4C numerical simulation for accuracy and optimisation.

  • Assists in the design of heat exchangers, biomedical devices, and renewable energy systems by offering application-specific thermal insights.

Objectives of the Present Study.

  • To simulate and examine the effects of variations in water temperature, porosity, Forchheimer number, and viscous dissipation on the flow and heat transmission of hybrid nanofluids.

  • To use BVP4C to solve nonlinear boundary value problems with temperature and flow fields.

  • To use RSM to find the best pair of parameters for improved thermal performance.

  • To use kinetic-to-thermal energy conversion to examine the effects of Eckert number on temperature profiles.

Research Problem and Its Motivation.

  • There aren’t many investigations on hybrid nanofluids in porous media that contain base fluids that change temperature.

  • It’s crucial for system design to comprehend how various physical effects—such as porosity, viscous dissipation, and inertia—interact to affect heat transmission.

  • The ineffective performance of thermal systems is caused by the’ lack of multi-parameter optimisation in traditional methods.

  • A combined numerical-statistical framework must be created in order to maximise thermal systems and increase accuracy.

Research Questions.

  • How do Forchheimer effects and porosity affect the hybrid nanofluids’ ability to boost heat and slow flow?

  • What changes in temperature profiles does viscous dissipation, as indicated by the Eckert number, bring about in porous media?

  • Which parameter combinations, when employing RSM, maximise the Nusselt number?

  • For hybrid nanofluid applications, can combining BVP4C with RSM offer a more reliable prediction and optimisation approach?

Response surface methodology

When modeling and optimizing a process with numerous interacting factors, Response Surface Methodology (RSM), a very advanced statistical technique, is employed.Such a system provides a systematic structure that helps design experiments, develop predictive models, and study the relationship between the input variables and the corresponding resultant outcomes. The importance of RSM in fluid dynamics is articulated by its use in optimising complex phenomena, optimising both heat transfer and drag reduction and the refinement of flow characteristics. The RSM successfully envelops the nonlinear interdependency amongst parameters of Reynolds number, flow velocity, and geometric characteristics of the shrinking sheet, employing second-order polynomial equations. This methodology eliminates the need for extensive experimental or computational work to identify ideal conditions of system performance. The reason for the attractiveness of RSM is its efficacy, mainly when the traditional trial and error approaches are unsuitable because of too much cost or complexity.

Conclusion

The Darcy–Forchheimer paradigm is used in this work to analyse the flow and thermal behaviour of hybrid nanofluids moving through porous media. In this study, the BVP4c numerical scheme and the Response Surface Methodology (RSM) are used in tandem to model and optimise the effects of critical parameters on velocity, temperature, and heat transfer profiles, including porosity, Forchheimer number, Eckert number, and water temperature variation. Advanced cooling systems, heat exchangers, medicinal devices, and renewable energy technologies can all benefit from these discoveries. By combining numerical and statistical approaches, a strong framework for future study in this area is provided, offering precise prediction and performance optimization for hybrid nanofluid systems.

Key findings of the current study:

  1. The porosity parameter () affected the velocity profile oppositely in that a higher porosity level leads to a lower fluid velocity. Still, it improved the temperature profile because of reduced convection heat transfer and increased heat transfer conduction.

  2. An increase in the Forchheimer parameter () lowered the velocity profile due to higher inertial resistance and enhanced the thermal profile by facilitating a transition in heat transfer dominance from convection to conduction.

  3. It was determined how viscous dissipation affected the temperature profile.as the Eckert number () increased, and the temperature profile increased by converting kinetic energy into thermal energy, reducing heat transport effectiveness and thickening the thermal boundary layer.

  4. The unsteadiness parameter (A) decreased temperature and velocity profiles at higher values due to the system’s enhanced energy dissipation and temporal instability.

  5. Fluctuation in water temperature illustrated that high temperatures resulted in better heat transfer and velocity profiles because of the thermal dependency of the hybrid nanofluid.

  6. The numerical analysis was supported by Response Surface Methodology (RSM)and justified system performance enhancement by determining the significant parameters of heat transfer efficiency.

Future work: Further research will then extend the numerical model to non-Newtonian hybrid nanofluids using a scale porous-medium system to confirm results in the setting of a magnetic field, electric field, transient flow, and pulsatile flow through porous medium and apply multi-objective surrogate-based machine-learning techniques to optimise thermal performance and minimise energy consumption.

Limitations: The study assumes a porous medium with constant porosity and permeability, ignores the adoption of nanoparticle agglomeration and thermophoretic migration at high concentration levels, and limits to two-dimensional flow without considering radiative heat transfer or change in the pressure-dependent gradient.
