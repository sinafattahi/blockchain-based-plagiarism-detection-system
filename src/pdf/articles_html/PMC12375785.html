Abstract

The structural asymmetry of Janus transition metal dichalcogenides gives rise to physical properties distinct from those of their symmetric counterparts. When Janus transition metal dichalcogenides are vertically stacked, their resulting heterostructure exhibits novel and enhanced electronic properties due to interactions between interfacial atoms, changes in electric dipole moments, and van der Waals interlayer interactions. In this study, we employ first-principles calculations based on density functional theory using the Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package to investigate the electronic properties of MoSSe-WSSe vertical Janus transition metal dichalcogenides heterostructures. By analyzing the density of states, charge density distribution, formation energy, bandgap, band alignment, and dipole moment, we identify key factors governing the electronic behavior of these heterostructures. Our calculation identifies the most favorable interfacial configuration and stacking type for synthesis. Furthermore, while the bandgap type is primarily dictated by the composition of interfacial atoms irrespective of the stacking type, its magnitude is influenced by both the interfacial atomic composition and stacking configuration. Notably, the total dipole moment is not merely the arithmetic sum of the dipole moments of the individual layers; rather, it is influenced by the interlayer coupling between the two layers. This trend can be explained by variations in the charge density distribution as a function of interlayer spacing. These findings suggest that the electronic properties of Janus heterostructures can be effectively tuned by modifying the interfacial atomic composition, stacking configuration, and interlayer distance, highlighting their potential for next-generation nanoelectronic applications.

Introduction

Transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs), such as , were initially explored for their lubricating properties and catalytic applications from the 1960 s to the 1980s. However, interest in TMDs was amplified significantly after the discovery and isolation of graphene in 20041,2, which highlighted the potential of other two-dimensional (2D) materials for a range of novel applications. TMDs have an X-M-X sandwich structure, where M represents a transition metal atom (such as Mo, W, Zr, or Hf) ordered on a triangular lattice, and X is a chalcogen atom (S, Se, or Te) arranged on a triangular lattice of alternating hollow sites. This structure has been widely investigated owing to its peculiar electronic, optical, and mechanical properties. Unlike graphene, which is a zero bandgap material, TMD monolayers have direct bandgaps within the optical range of the energy spectrum and large spin-orbit interactions. These properties provide a possible path to device applications such as light emitting diodes, photodetectors, photovoltaic cells, gas sensors, and low-power, high-performance transistors3–12. Research has also led to heterostructures designed by stacking TMDs either with themselves or with other 2D materials, providing improved control over their electronic properties13–17.

Since the first Janus TMD, MoSSe, was experimentally synthesized in 2017, research on Janus materials has expanded rapidly. With the successful synthesis and characterization of monolayer Janus MoSSe18–21, studies have begun to reveal the unique properties of Janus TMDs, such as their built-in out-of-plane dipole22–24, enhanced catalytic properties for reactions such as hydrogen evolution reaction (HER)25–27, and distinctive optical and electronic behavior28–31. As the name suggests, Janus TMDs (with an X-M-Y sandwich structure) are a special class of TMDs in which the two chalcogen layers (typically X,Y = S, Se, or Te) on either side of the transition metal layer (such as M = Mo or W) differ (XY). Due to the different physicochemical properties on each side, an intrinsic electrostatic dipole is created in the out-of-plane direction22–24 and Rashba effects appear, which are absent in symmetric TMDs31–35. Janus TMDs have been extensively studied for applications in practical devices such as photodetectors, transistors, and catalysts. Their built-in out-of-plane dipole, large Rashba effect, and unique optical responses make them ideal for next-generation nanoelectronics, optoelectronics, and energy storage. Similarly to TMDs, research on stacked heterostructures22,28,29,36–40 and twisted heterostructures41–43 in Janus TMDs is also of great interest.

This paper focuses on Janus TMD heterostructures in which the layers are individually synthesized, exfoliated, and then vertically stacked. The interfacial atoms can be precisely controlled by flipping layers during the stacking process. Furthermore, the distance between two layers and the strength of the binding can be adjusted more flexibly than by applying pressure44, allowing exploration of various types of stacking and expanding the scope of potential research. Because the Janus heterostructure is tuned by interlayer coupling and interfacial atoms, we investigated the effects of interfacial atoms and stacking types on its electronic properties, including the formation energy (), bandgap (), and dipole moment (P) in WSSe-MoSSe heterostructures, which are formed by vertically stacking two distinct mechanically exfoliated Janus TMDs. Among the various Janus TMDs, WSSe and MoSSe were chosen because their lattice mismatch is less than a few percent, according to previous studies40.

Effects of the stacking type and interfacial atomic combination

In the vertical Janus TMD MoSSe-WSSe heterostructures, the interfacial atomic combinations have three possible patterns, denoted as S–Se, Se–Se, and S–S structures. The Se-Se atomic combination appears to correspond to SWSe/SeMoS (lower/upper) and SMoSe/SeWS. The S-S atomic combination case corresponds to the SeWS/SMoSe and SeMoS/SWSe types. However, because of the mirror symmetry of chalcogen atoms along the xy plane, each pair of types can be treated as a single distinct case. The cases of SMoSe/SWSe and SWSe/SMoSe appear to involve the same S-Se atomic combination. However, they can be considered distinct because they break the mirror symmetry. We analyze the effects of interfacial atoms on their electronic properties by categorizing them into four groups: I: SMoSe/SWSe, II: SWSe/SMoSe, III: Se-Se, and VI: S-S. Hereafter, we use the notation System I, System II, etc. to represent each interfacial atomic combination when necessary to avoid the repetitive use of long expressions.

In Fig. 2, we show the side and top views of three stacking types: (a) AA, (b) , and (c) AB. Among the four interfacial atomic combinations, system I: SMoSe/SWSe is shown for demonstration purposes. Depending on the interfacial atomic combinations, the visible atoms in the top view change, but the overall lattice structure remains unchanged. The black arrow, labeled d, represents the relaxed distance between layers in the z direction. The value d varies depending on both the stacking types and the interfacial atomic combinations. In the case of AA stacking, the upper- and lower-layer atoms are aligned, while they are misaligned in other cases. For and AB stacking, we predict that the value of d may be reduced as a result of diagonal accumulation in a stable structure. For , the S and Se atoms are not aligned, but considering the lattice size and the atomic size of the transition metal in the upper layer, the top view appears to have a honeycomb structure. W and Se atoms are seen in the top view. However, the actual constituent atoms are rotated by 60 compared to AA stacking.

In Fig. 3, we show the detailed band structures for 12 cases, combining four interfacial atomic combinations with three stacking types. The first row presents the AA stacking band structures for interfacial atomic combinations (a) I: SMoSe/SWSe, (b) II: SWSe/SMoSe, (c) III: Se-Se, and (d) VI: S-S. The second and third rows correspond to the and AB stacking types, respectively. Compared to the direct bandgaps of both layers, with =1.457 eV (MoSSe) and 1.377 eV (WSSe), only II (the second column) and III (the third column) exhibit a direct bandgap, while I and IV have indirect bandgaps. System II has the smallest among all stacking types. The blue (red) represents the spin-up (spin-down) state, while purple indicates a mixture of both spin states. When we investigate spin states in TMD materials, there are two major aspects to consider. First, the spin states in the K and valleys are reversed because of time-reversal symmetry. This is observed in all 12 cases. Second, the SOC constant is larger in the valence band than in the conduction band, resulting in noticeable spin splitting in the valence band. In the conduction band, the splitting is almost negligible, making the spin-up and spin-down states appear nearly degenerate. This trend is also observed in all 12 cases.

Systems I:SMoSe/SWSe and II: SWSe/SMoSe have the same interfacial atomic combination, Se/S, but are treated differently because of the breaking of mirror symmetry. As expected, these two cases show significantly different energy band structures. Apart from the type of bandgap, they also exhibit different spin splitting. Compared to system I, system II exhibits more significant spin splitting in the valence band and less spin splitting in the conduction band.

In the stacking case (second row), the spin behavior differs from that of AA and AB stacking. In particular, for the III: Se-Se and IV: S-S interfacial combinations, the spin states in the K and valleys of the VBM are inverted. Meanwhile, in the (systems I and II, the spin states in the K and valleys below the VBM are converted. The spin inversion of the K and valleys can be explained by the fact that the K and valleys are swapped as the honeycomb shape rotates by 60, as mentioned previously. In Fig. 4, we analyze the projected density of states of the transition metals and examine the spin-resolved energy band structure of Mo and W to determine which transition metal contributes to the spin states of the VBM and CBM at each symmetric point in the heterostructure’s energy band. It is evident that the spin state of the transition metal that contributes most near the VBM determines the overall spin states near the VBM of the heterostructure. Here, we focus on the properties at the K point near the VBM, where spin splitting is most pronounced. The CBM region and other symmetric points can be analyzed using the same reasoning applied to the K point. The first column shows the energy band of the heterostructure between the M and points, while the second column lists the projected density of states for Mo and W atoms. The third and fourth columns provide the energy bands of the Mo and W atoms, respectively. In system I, the Mo atom determines the spin state of the VBM, while in systems II, III, and IV, it is determined by the W atom. This is indicated by the blue circle in Fig. 4(a). The spin conversion below the VBM for system I is determined by the W atom, whereas for systems II, III, and IV, it is determined by the Mo atom. This is indicated by the red circles in Figs. 4(b)–(d). These findings match the projected density of states of the transition metals in the second column well. It is not easy to intuitively determine which atom, Mo or W, contributes more to the VBM or CBM before calculating the projected density of states of the transition atoms. The result is influenced by a combination of factors, including the intrinsic dipole between the upper and lower layers due to the atomic interface and the stacking type, the electric field generated by the dipole, and the interactions between the atoms constituting the upper and lower layers within the electric field.

The stacking type that produces the maximum for any interfacial combination varies case-by-case. Among the 12 cases, the stacking in system III has the maximum , and the bandgap is direct. Here, quantitatively, we investigate the effect of the stacking type on within a given category of interfacial atoms. The largest change in due to the stacking type occurs in system IV, with a 31  change, while the smallest change is seen in system III, with only a 3  change. When the interface atoms are the same, and the dipoles of the lower and upper layers face outward, the bandgap is more affected by the stacking type than when dipoles are either aligned or facing inward. The bandgap type remains consistent as long as the interfacial combination is the same, even when the stacking type is changed. However, this conclusion is limited to the case where d is a relaxed d value. The bandgap type can vary even for the same interfacial atom combination, depending on the distance between the upper and lower layers. This will be discussed in detail in the next section.

Effects of interlayer distance

In our investigation, the layers are individually synthesized, exfoliated, and then vertically stacked. In addition to controlling the interfacial atoms by flipping layers during stacking, the interlayer distance and binding strength can be easily tuned. In this section, we study the effect of the distance between layers on and P. Among the three stacking types, we choose , which has the smallest and the biggest in system III: Se-Se. This type of stacking showed spin state inversion between K and valleys in its energy band structures.

In Fig. 5, we show the interlayer distance d-dependence of and P and the charge distribution difference . Figure 5(a) illustrates the dependence of on the distance between layers d. Here, d no longer represents the relaxed layer distance but instead is mechanically adjusted to shift the upper layer downward or upward. In systems I: SMoSe/SWSe and IV: S-S, increases as d increases, whereas in systems II: SWSe/SMoSe and III: Se-Se, reaches its maximum near the relaxed distance between layers (marked by a red boundary line) and decreases as d increases. From the calculation of the energy band structure, we confirm that systems I: SMoSe/SWSe and IV: S-S have an indirect bandgap (solid line) in all d-value ranges shown in the graph, while for systems II: SWSe/SMoSe and III: Se-Se, the type of bandgap changes from indirect (solid line) to direct (dashed line) when d becomes close to the relaxed distance. As the d value changes, the interatomic interactions are modified, leading to energy changes at the symmetric point .

Figure 5(b) illustrates the dependence of P on the distance between layers, d. The dipole is formed along the z direction. The upper panel shows the results for systems I and II, where the dipoles of the lower and upper layers align in the same direction, causing the total dipole to increase as d increases. This behavior is expected because the dipole moment is proportional to the separation between the positive and negative charge distributions, resulting in a net increase in the total dipole. However, when d is large enough to have no interlayer interaction, the total dipole saturates to 73.8 meÅ, which is the sum of the dipoles from the upper and lower layers. The lower panel presents systems III: Se-Se and IV: S-S, where the dipoles from the lower and upper layers are aligned in the opposite direction, causing the total dipole to decrease as d increases. The dipole of III converges to 0.1 meÅ. In system IV, the dipole values of the upper and lower layers were expected to algebraically add up to a negative value. However, in the results, a positive value is observed for small d due to interlayer interactions. When the distance between layers increases, a negative value is obtained and is expected to converge to  meÅ.

Conclusion

We have investigated the effects of interfacial atoms and different stacking configurations for vertically stacked WSSe-MoSSe heterostructures. Unlike previous studies that primarily focused on optical properties or general trends across various Janus or conventional TMD heterostructures, our work provides a detailed systematic understanding of how interfacial atomic configurations and stacking types govern key electronic properties, including band gap, spin-splitting behavior, interfacial charge redistribution, and dipole variation. Using a first-principles calculation, the bandgaps of heterostructures with four different interfacial atomic combinations (I: SMoSe/SWSe, II: SWSe/SMoSe, III: Se-Se, and VI: S-S), three stacking types (AA, , AB), and different interlayer distances d were investigated. Our results indicate that the Se-Se interfacial combination with the stacking type is relatively easy to synthesize due to its minimal . For the stacking type, due to the 60 rotation of the top layer (see the lattice), the spin states in the K and valleys in the VBM are inverted in III: Se-Se and VI: S-S interfacial atoms. And the spin states in the K and valleys lower than VBM are inverted in I: SMoSe/SWSe, II: SWSe/SMoSe interfacial atomic combinations. The bandgap and band alignment are tunable depending on interlayer distance, interfacial atoms, and stacking types. However, the type of bandgap remains consistent as long as the interfacial combination is the same with the relaxed interlayer distance, even when the stacking type is changed.

The built-in out-of-plane dipole moments in each layer can align or oppose each other. However, the total dipole moment is not simply the sum or difference of these individual values. Instead, the total P strongly depends on the interlayer distance due to the strength of the interlayer coupling. The largest P appears in system I with SMoSe/SWSe and the AA stacking type. The enhanced dipole moment of the Janus TMD heterostructure can further enrich the application of next-generation nanoelectronics.

Methods

In these simulations, all first-principle calculations are performed by density functional theory (DFT) using the commercially available Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package (VASP)45. The generalized gradient approximation (GGA) parameterized by the Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof (PBE) functional is used to describe the exchange-correlation function46. The projector augmented wave (PAW) potentials47 generated based on the PBE type of exchange-correlation functional, are used to treat valence electrons with a cutoff energy of 550 eV. For the monolayer Janus structure and the vertical Janus heterostructure with its z direction perpendicular to the xy plane, a vacuum region of more than 25 Å is used to avoid the effects generated by the periodic boundary conditions. The Brillouin zone is sampled with a Monkhorst mesh of 23 23 1 k-points48. The ionic relaxation stops when the residual force on each atom is less than 0.01 eV/Å, and the electronic optimization stops when the total energy difference is smaller than 10 eV. A Gaussian smearing method with a width of 0.05 eV is employed. The formation energy () is calculated as , where , , and represent the total energies of the MoSSe-WSSe heterostructures and monolayer MoSSe and monolayer WSSe, respectively.

Although the PBE functional has well-known limitations, such as underestimating bandgaps and not including non-local interactions, it is widely used because it allows efficient calculations and still captures the overall trends in electronic properties. Considering the van der Waals (vdW) interactions in Janus heterostructures, we include the Grimme DFT-D2 correction with the GGA-PBE functional for atomic relaxation49. To investigate the Rashba effect and determine the spin distribution, spin-orbit coupling (SOC) calculations are performed.
