Abstract

This paper introduces the chaotic enhanced leader slime mold algorithm (CELSMA), an advanced bio-inspired optimization technique aimed at addressing high-dimensional engineering challenges. Building on the traditional slime mold algorithm (SMA), CELSMA implements a multi-leader strategy that utilizes three candidate leaders to enhance both exploration and exploitation capabilities. Additionally, CELSMA harnesses the ergodic and non-repetitive characteristics of chaotic maps to improve global search behavior and reduce the risk of premature convergence to local optima. The proposed algorithm is applied to the size optimization of truss structures under frequency constraints, a computationally intensive task due to the repeated evaluation of structural eigenvalues. To tackle this issue, the largest eigenvalues of sparse matrix (LESM) technique is employed to significantly decrease computational time, facilitating the practical optimization of large-scale truss systems. Comprehensive numerical experiments were conducted on large-scale dome truss structures and benchmarked against established metaheuristic algorithms. The results clearly indicate that the CELSMA-LESM approach achieves superior accuracy and convergence speed, consistently yielding optimal solutions with fewer iterations. The CELSMA source code is publicly available at: https://github.com/nut123456/CELSMA.git.

Introduction

Achieving robust dynamic behavior in structural systems often requires careful consideration of natural frequency constraints, especially under low frequency excitations1. A primary objective in such designs concern is avoiding resonance within the operational bandwidth, as the intrinsic vibrational characteristics, governed by the structure’s natural frequencies and corresponding mode shapes significantly affect performance, especially through fundamental modes. These modal characteristics, particularly the lower modes, are essential for suppressing resonance effects. Consequently, extensive research has addressed simultaneous optimization of structural size and geometry, especially for truss and frame structures subjected to frequency-based constraints. However, such optimization problems remain challenging due to their nonlinear, non-convex nature, and the trade-off between minimizing structural weight and satisfying dynamic requirements. Conventional gradient-based solvers often suffer from poor convergence and high computational cost. To overcome these issues, metaheuristic algorithms, known for their gradient-free nature and global search capabilities—have emerged as powerful alternatives2.

Notably, Lingyun et al.3 introduced genetic algorithms for truss optimization under dynamic constraints, prompting a surge in algorithmic approaches for frequency-constrained design. especially for dynamic load conditions like wind and earthquakes4. Such constraints suppress excessive vibration and prevent resonance5; however, challenges remain due to potential mode switching and the computational burden of repeated eigenvalue evaluations6. To address these complexities, many researchers employ methods capable of efficiently navigating high-dimensional and multimodal search spaces. These algorithms often require multiple structural analyses and eigenvalue computations, making them suitable for large-scale optimization tasks7.

In recent years, nature-inspired algorithms—drawing from biological systems, physical principles, and social behavior8. For example, the niche genetic hybrid algorithm (NGHA) merges genetic algorithms with simplex search, while the cyclical parthenogenesis algorithm (CPA) mimics aphid reproduction9. Other examples include the charged system search (CSS)10 the colliding bodies optimization (CBO)11 and the big bang-big crunch (BB-BC) algorithm12. Social- and human-inspired methods such as harmony search (HS)13 teaching-learning-based optimization (TLBO)14 and forensic-based investigation (FBI)15 have also been proposed. Animal-inspired approaches, like the crow search algorithm (CSA)16 and enhanced colliding bodies optimization (ECBO)17 offer additional diversity. Probabilistic models such as the generalized normal distribution optimization (GNDO)18 further broaden the landscape of available tools. Among these, swarm intelligence algorithms such as particle swarm optimization (PSO)19 are widely recognized for their simplicity and effectiveness. While powerful, standard PSO is known to suffer from premature convergence. This has led to many enhanced variants, such as democratic PSO (DPSO)20 harmony search-based aging leader and challengers PSO (HALC-PSO)21 and phasor PSO (PPSO)22 which integrates sine and cosine dynamics into velocity updates. Other notable enhancements include comprehensive learning PSO (CLPSO)23,24 its chaotic variant C-HCLPSO25 fully informed PSO (FIPSO)26 and cooperative PSO (CPSO)27.

More recently, Li et al.28 proposed the slime mold algorithm (SMA), which simulates the adaptive foraging behavior of slime molds. While promising, SMA faces the common challenge of balancing exploration and exploitation, a balance critical for achieving global convergence while avoiding local optima. The No Free Lunch theorem29,30 underscores the importance of ongoing research into algorithmic improvements, including the integration of chaotic dynamics for enhanced performance.

To overcome these challenges, we propose the chaotic enhanced leader slime mold algorithm (CELSMA), which augments the standard SMA using a triple-leader strategy combined with chaotic dynamics. In addition, the sparse matrix eigenvalue (LESM) technique is integrated to address the computational bottleneck of repeated eigenvalue extraction. Extensive numerical studies on large-scale dome truss structures validate the efficiency, scalability, and robustness of the proposed CELSMA-LESM framework.

The motivation for developing this framework stems from two persistent challenges in structural optimization: (1) the difficulty of maintaining global search capability in the presence of complex, multi-modal landscapes, and (2) the heavy computational load associated with solving high-dimensional eigenvalue problems repeatedly. As engineering applications scale in both size and complexity particularly in structures subjected to dynamic loads such as earthquakes and wind the need for algorithms that are both efficient and robust become increasingly urgent. Although many methods have achieved success, they often suffer from limited scalability and a propensity for premature convergence when tackling complex dynamic design problems.

CELSMA addresses a significant methodological gap by integrating the adaptive intelligence of slime mold behavior with the ergodic properties of chaotic systems and the numerical efficiency of sparse matrix eigenvalue extraction. The combined CELSMA-LESM framework effectively tackles real-world, high-dimensional problems that would otherwise be computationally intractable or prone to suboptimal convergence. Extensive experiments conducted on dome truss structures of varying scales showcase the framework’s robustness, scalability, and enhanced accuracy in solving complex structural optimization problems under frequency constraints.

Slime mold algorithm (SMA)

SMA is a population-based metaheuristic algorithm inspired by the foraging behavior of Physarum polycephalum35 a true slime mold known for its decentralized decision-making and efficient nutrient path finding abilities. In its natural environment, the organism forms vein-like networks to explore and exploit nutrient sources, adapting its search based on cytoplasmic oscillations regulated through chemotactic feedback. From a computational perspective, SMA emulates this adaptive mechanism to navigate complex search spaces. Individuals (slime molds) adjust their positions based on internal weight factors that reflect their fitness and the distribution of global population. The transition between exploration and exploitation is dynamically controlled by a bio-oscillator model that simulates the variation of cytoplasmic flow under fluctuating environmental conditions.

Chaotic enhanced leader slime mold algorithm (CELSMA)

CELSMA is developed by embedding chaotic dynamics into the LSMA framework to mitigate premature convergence and improve global search performance. Although LSMA benefits from multi-leader guidance, its performance in high-dimensional, multimodal landscapes can still suffer from search stagnation. To address this, CELSMA leverages the properties of chaotic systems—deterministic yet non-repetitive and ergodic—to introduce structured diversity during the optimization process39,40. Each map recursively generates a sequence {x1, x2, …, xT} from an initial seed x0 ∈ (0, 1), governed by its nonlinear recurrence relation. The implemented maps include Logistic, Tent, Chebyshev, Singer, Sine, Piecewise, Sinusoidal, Circle, Iterative, and Gauss maps, each offering distinct dynamic behaviors41–43.

These maps are encapsulated within a modular chaos function, which returns rchaos(t) given a map index and iteration number. To promote behavioral diversity, CELSMA can operate in two modes: (1) Fixed-map mode: A single chaotic map is selected at the start and used throughout. (2) Random-chaos mode: A different map is randomly selected at each iteration.

To systematically investigate the role of chaotic dynamics, ten algorithmic variants—CELSMA1 through CELSMA10—are constructed. Each variant uses a fixed chaotic map for the entire optimization run. For instance, CELSMA1 uses the Chebyshev map, CELSMA2 uses the Circle map, and CELSMA10 uses the Tent map. These variants are benchmarked under identical conditions to evaluate their effects on solution quality, convergence stability, and robustness. Additionally, the random-chaos variant allows map switching at each iteration. This dynamic switching enhances ergodicity and introduces temporal variation into the search process, leading to improved global performance.

Computational complexity analysis of CELSMA

CELSMA enhances the original SMA by incorporating chaotic dynamics to improve both exploration and convergence. The computational complexity of CELSMA is governed by the population size N, problem dimension D, and maximum number of iterations Itermax. The initialization phase requires O(N.D). In each iteration, boundary checking and fitness evaluation cost O(N.D), sorting takes O(NlogN), chaotic weight update and position update contribute O(N.D) each. Therefore, the total per iteration complexity is O(N.D + N.logN), and the overall time complexity is O(Itermax N.(D + logN)).

Sensitivity analysis

To examine the effect of key control parameters on algorithm performance, a sensitivity analysis was conducted for both CESMA and CELSMA with respect to Np and Itermax44.

The number of agents varied over the set Np={2, 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30}. As shown in Fig. 4 (a)–(b), increasing Np enhances convergence speed and solution quality by introducing greater population diversity. However, beyond Np=20, the improvement becomes marginal, indicating that larger populations offer diminishing returns in terms of optimization gain. Therefore, Np=20 can be considered an optimal trade-off between exploration capacity and computational cost.

Similarly, the effect of Itermax = {250, 500, 750, 1000} was evaluated. Figure 5(a)–(b) demonstrates that higher iteration counts yield progressively better solutions, but performance gains tend to plateau beyond Itermax = 500. This suggests that 500 iterations strike a good trade-off between computational time and solution refinement.

Effect of chaotic map strategies

To enhance the exploration capability of CESMA and CELSMA, ten distinct chaotic maps were integrated into the initialization and position update mechanisms. Additionally, a random-chaos strategy—in which a different chaotic map is selected at each iteration—was tested as a dynamic baseline45. The convergence trajectories, shown in Fig. 6(a–b), reveal that incorporating chaos maps accelerates convergence during early iterations by promoting ergodic and diverse sampling behavior. Among all variants, the random-chaos approach consistently achieved the lowest structural weight and the most stable convergence profile, indicating superior exploration–exploitation trade-offs. This dynamic alternation of chaotic maps per iteration allows the algorithm to adaptively escape local optima and adjust search intensity based on progress. Consequently, the random-chaos strategy is recommended over fixed-map variants across all benchmark cases.

Optimization was performed using SMA, LSMA, CESMA and the proposed CELSMA algorithm, with a population size of Np =20 and Itermax = 500. As reported in Table 3 and visualized in Fig. 7, the proposed CELSMA algorithm achieved the lowest final truss weight (199.59 kg), outperforming CESMA (199.85 kg), LSMA (201.77 kg), and SMA (202.06 kg). All three slime mold-based methods (CESMA, LSMA, CELSMA) significantly outperformed conventional metaheuristics such as HS32FA32and MPA39confirming their suitability for frequency-constrained structural optimization. From a computational efficiency standpoint, CELSMA maintained a solution time comparable to SMA, LSMA, and CESMA, ranging from 7.25 to 7.45 s, confirming that the introduction of chaotic dynamics does not impose any notable overhead in low-dimensional cases. Although the LESM technique was not applied in this scenario due to the small size of the eigenvalue problem the results suggest that CELSMA can deliver accurate and consistent solutions even without specialized eigenvalue solvers.

The third benchmark problem involves a large-scale structural optimization of a 600-bar single-layer dome truss structure, composed of 216 nodes and constructed from 24 identical substructures. Discussion

Figure 16 summarizes the superior performance of the proposed CELSMA algorithm across five benchmark dome truss optimization problems. Based on the statistical distribution of structural weight obtained from 50 independent runs, CELSMA consistently outperformed SMA, LSMA and CESMA. It achieved lower median and mean weight values, along with reduced standard deviation particularly in large-scale, high-dimensional cases (Examples 3–5) indicating enhanced robustness and more stable convergence.

In these complex scenarios, CELSMA’s results featured narrower interquartile ranges and minimal presence of outliers, reflecting its strong ability to explore multimodal design spaces effectively. This performance is attributed to the hybrid framework of CELSMA, which integrates chaotic dynamics, multi-leader guidance, and the LESM technique to maintain search diversity while satisfying frequency constraints efficiently. Conversely, in lower-dimensional problems (Examples 1–2), the performance of CELSMA, CESMA, LSMA, and SMA was relatively similar, suggesting that the benefits of chaos-enhanced exploration and structural decomposition become more significant as the problem dimensionality and constraint intensity increase.

When compared with conventional metaheuristic algorithms reported in the literature (e.g., HS, FA, MPA, DPSO, GNDO, CLPPSO, CPA, CRPSO), the proposed CELSMA consistently achieved lower optimal structural weights across all five benchmark problems, as shown in Tables 3, 4, 6, 8 and 10. More importantly, it exhibited superior statistical robustness, evidenced by its smaller standard deviations and tighter distributions in the boxplots (see Fig. 16). These outcomes confirm that CELSMA not only improves solution quality but also enhances consistency across independent trials. Such performance gains are attributed to the integration of three key components: bio-inspired multi-leader coordination, chaotic dynamic perturbation, and sparse matrix acceleration via the LESM technique. These elements work synergistically to improve both global exploration and local refinement while maintaining computational efficiency, especially in high-dimensional and frequency-constrained design problems.

Overall, the results validate CELSMA as a scalable and reliable optimization tool for complex structural engineering tasks. Future work may consider integrating surrogate modeling approaches—such as Kriging or Gaussian Process Regression—to further reduce computational overhead, as well as employing adaptive chaotic switching strategies or dynamic population control to enhance algorithmic adaptability in highly nonlinear optimization settings.

Conclusions

This study introduces a novel metaheuristic framework CELSMA combined with the LESM technique for the optimal size and shape design of truss dome structures under natural frequency constraints. The algorithm is inspired by bio-swarm intelligence and enhances the original SMA by embedding leader-based guidance, adaptive learning, and chaotic tent mapping. These modifications significantly improve the balance between global exploration and local exploitation. To accelerate the frequency evaluation process during optimization, the LESM technique is employed to efficiently approximate the dominant eigenvalues from sparse finite element matrices. This not only reduces computational time but also enables the practical application of the proposed algorithm to large-scale design problems. Extensive validation has been conducted on five benchmark truss dome structures, ranging from 52-bar to 1410-bar truss dome. The CELSMA-LESM algorithm consistently outperforms conventional methods in terms of minimizing structural weight while satisfying frequency constraints. Statistical analysis over 50 independent runs reveals narrow standard deviations, indicating the reliability and robustness of the proposed method. Due to its generality, scalability, and efficiency, the proposed CELSMA-LESM framework can be readily extended to other structural optimization problems such as frame systems, shell structures, and frequency-based reliability designs. Future research may incorporate stress-based constraints, discrete cross-sectional sizing, and parallel implementation to further broaden its applicability in engineering practice.

Code B

Chaotic enhanced leader slime mold algorithm.

function [Destination_fitness, bestPositions, Convergence_curve] =.

CELSMA (N, Max_ iter, lb, ub, dim, fobj, chaos_index).

bestPositions = zeros(1,dim);

Destination_fitness = inf;%change this to -inf for maximization problems.

AllFitness = inf*ones(N,1);%record the fitness of all slime mold.

weight = ones(N, dim);%fitness weight of each slime mold.

X = initialization(N, dim, ub, lb);

Convergence_curve = zeros(1,Max_iter); it = 1; %Number of iterations.

lb = ones(1,dim).*lb; % lower boundary.

ub = ones(1,dim).*ub; % upper boundary.

z = 0.03; % parameter.

time_per_iteration = zeros(1, Max_iter);

% Main loop.

while it < = Max_iter.

tic; % Start timer for iteration.

for i = 1:N.

Flag4ub = X(i,:) > ub;

Flag4lb = X(i,:) < lb;

X(i,:)=(X(i,:).*(~(Flag4ub + Flag4lb))) + ub.*Flag4ub + lb.*Flag4lb;

AllFitness(i) = fobj(X(i,:));

end.

[SmellOrder, SmellIndex] = sort(AllFitness);

worstFitness = SmellOrder(N);

bestFitness = SmellOrder(1); %Leader 1.

bestPositions2 = X(SmellIndex(2),:);%Leader 2.

bestPositions3 = X(SmellIndex(3),:);%Leader 3.

S = bestFitness-worstFitness + eps;% plus eps to avoid denominator zero.

for i = 1:N.

chaos_value = chaos(chaos_index, it, Max_iter,1);%Generate chaotic.

for j = 1:dim.

if i <= (N/2) %Eq. (7)

weight(SmellIndex(i), j) = 1 + chaos_value * log10((bestFitness - SmellOrder(i))/S + 1);

else.

weight(SmellIndex(i), j) = 1-chaos_value*log10((bestFitness - SmellOrder(i))/S + 1);

end.

end.

end.

if bestFitness < Destination_fitness.

bestPositions = X(SmellIndex(1),:); %Leader 1.

Destination_fitness = bestFitness;

end.

a = atanh(-(it/Max_iter) + 1); b = 1-it/Max_iter;

for i = 1:N.

if rand < z.

chaos_value = chaos(chaos_index, it, Max_iter, 1);

X(i,:) = (ub-lb)*chaos_value + lb;

else.

p = tanh(abs(AllFitness(i)-Destination_fitness));

vb = unifrnd(-a, a,1,dim); vc = unifrnd(-b, b,1,dim);

A = randi([1,N]);%two positions randomly selected from Np.

B = randi([1,N]); r1 = chaos(chaos_index, it, Max_iter, 1);

for j = 1:dim.

if r1 < p.

X(i, j) = bestPositions(j) + vb(j)*(((weight(i, j)*bestPositions2(j)-X(A, j))+(weight(i, j)*bestPositions3(j)-X(B, j))));

else.

X(i, j) = vc(j)*X(i, j);

end end end end.

Convergence_curve(it) = Destination_fitness; time_per_iteration(it) = toc;

disp([‘Iteration’num2str(it) ‘: Best Cost ='num2str(Convergence_curve(it)) ‘: Time =’ num2str(time_per_iteration(it))]); it = it + 1;

end.
