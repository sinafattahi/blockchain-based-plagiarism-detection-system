Abstract

Context

Unhealthy and unsustainable diets are common worldwide. Health and sustainability need to be balanced because environmentally sustainable diets may lack macro- and/or micronutrients, resulting in nutrient deficiencies.

Objective

A systematic review was conducted following Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-analysis guidelines to analyze the environmental impact of current diets and alternative dietary scenarios worldwide based on food-group consumption.

Data Sources

The Scopus, Web of Sciences, and PubMed literature databases were searched. Only studies quantifying the food composition of diets per food group and their environmental impact were eligible.

Data Extraction

Data were extracted for food groups as averages, means, or medians, depending on how they were reported in the original publication, for both environmental impact indicators and grams consumed.

Data Analysis

A total of 120 original articles from 41 countries were included and 703 diets were analyzed. Current diets were the most prevalent (42%). Among the environmental indicators, carbon footprint (CF) was the most reported (86% of diets), followed by land (36%), total freshwater (22%), blue water (15%), and cumulative energy use (14%). Data about food consumption and environmental impact were available mainly for European countries (n = 471 diets; 67%), and Africa was the most underrepresented continent, with data from only 2 countries.

Conclusion

The environmental impact of food consumption varied widely among diet types and continents, due to methodology heterogeneity of dietary assessment and different definitions of diets and food groups. Diets enhancing healthiness, such as the Mediterranean diet and food-based dietary guidelines, were associated with a higher CF than diets reducing meat consumption. Furthermore, diets including meat substitutes were associated with a higher CF than other plant-based diets that did not include them. Affordable diets involved more freshwater use, whereas acceptable diets that include high amounts of meat intake had a high CF. Finally, extensive research on dietary intake and environmental impact in low- and middle-income countries is required due to lack of available studies and data in these areas.

Keywords: dietary scenarios, food sustainability, environmental impact, alternative diets

INTRODUCTION

The technological innovations in the food and agricultural sectors have contributed significantly to the increase of food availability and security and, consequently, the reduction of hunger, undernutrition, poverty, and death worldwide.1,2 However, food production contributes to around one-third of global greenhouse gas emissions (GHGEs), two-thirds of freshwater use, and one-third of land occupation caused by human activities.2–5 Diets characterized by a relatively high caloric and animal-based protein content have a negative impact both on health and the environment.1,6,7 Specifically, animal-based foods seem to be responsible for the double amount of GHGEs compared to plant foods, such as whole vegetables, fruits, and legumes.8 The nutrition and economic transition in low- and middle-income countries due to urbanization, resulting in the adoption of a Western diet is 1 of the main factors of the double burden of malnutrition and high environmental impact of diets in these countries.9–12 Hence, a dietary shift to more sustainable dietary sources is necessary.

Multiple studies have been conducted with the aim of defining sustainable diets, taking into consideration a variety of factors, other than environmental and health ones, such as food affordability and acceptability. According to the Food Agricultural Organization (FAO), “Sustainable Diets are those diets with low environmental impacts which contribute to food and nutrition security and to healthy life for present and future generations.”13 The EAT-Lancet Commission defined the planetary health diet based on nutrient requirements and environmental impact constraints that would keep temperature increases to 1.5°C.1,14 Although the planetary health diet could have a positive effect on both health and the environment, it does not take into account the eating habits, traditions, and food availability of each country, separately. Additionally, Springmann et al15 created optimized diet scenarios worldwide by using the FAO Statistical Database (FAOSTAT) Food Balance Sheets (FBSs), reducing animal-based proteins; adjusting for health factors, such as death; and considering 4 energy-balanced dietary patterns (ie, flexitarian, pescatarian, vegetarian, vegan). It was concluded that even though the GHGEs and mortality rates may have been decreased globally, the resource use (eg, water footprint) of the diets was increased at regional levels, mainly in low-income countries, requiring a context-specific approach to sustainable diets.16 Due to the emerging need of adapting sustainable diets, increasing healthiness of diets, and consideration for environmental health, industry plays a crucial role in contributing to the sustainable dietary shift by developing and producing plant-based products labelled vegan or vegetarian.17–19

Previous and more recent systematic reviews analyzed the available literature by selecting specific geographic areas and/or only considering certain dietary patterns, such as priori diets20 or “sustainable diets” rich in plant-sourced and poor in animal-sourced foods,21 or reporting in a general way diet categories without focusing on their food components (food groups).22,23 To our knowledge, no systematic review has been conducted until now that extensively evaluated the contemporary food consumption habits per food group, and all the available environmental impact indicators globally, in a long of time. Therefore, the aim of this systematic review was to systematically analyze the available data about the environmental impact characterizing current diets and alternative dietary scenarios based on food-group intake worldwide. Diets of any type including the actual food consumption of populations, optimized diets, and a priori–defined dietary patterns were evaluated according to the consumption of food groups and their environmental impact, considering any available environmental impact indicator from GHGEs to eutrophication and biodiversity loss. To allow comparisons, the outcomes were reported by diet type and by continent and continental regions.

METHODS

Search Strategy and Inclusion Criteria

This systematic review was conducted according to the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-analysis (PRISMA) guidelines.24 The Web of Science, Scopus, and PubMed literature databases were searched for pertinent articles from October 2021 until January 2022. Key strings were adjusted depending on the database searched and can be found in Table S1. The general search strategy included environmental impact indicators (eg, “carbon dioxide emission*” OR “nitrogen use” OR “energy use”) AND diet-related keywords (eg, “diet” OR “diet* change” OR “food choice”) AND keywords related to energy and nutrient composition of the diets (eg, “nutrient” OR “energy” OR “nutrition quality”). After removing duplicates, articles were screened and selected for eligibility initially by title and abstract and subsequently by full text. Two researchers screened all articles independently and conflicts were solved after discussion.

The final data set was composed of full-length original studies in the English language and conducted from 2000 onward. Other types of publications, such as letters, short communications, brief reports, and reviews, were excluded. Observational, intervention, and modelling studies were eligible. Only studies quantifying both environmental impact indicators and daily dietary intakes per food group (eg, vegetables, fruit, red meat, dairy, pulses, nuts and seeds, fish and seafood) were included. Studies reporting on a single food group or product were excluded. Studies conducted of general, free-living adult populations (ie, aged ≥ 18 years) were included; studies of diseased populations or populations with special food consumption (eg, athletes, pregnant, lactating women) were excluded. When dietary data on current diets (CDs) was reported, studies or surveys had to be conducted as of January 2000 to retrieve an adequate picture of modern food consumption habits. Data Extraction

Data were extracted for food groups as averages, means, or medians, depending on how they were reported in the original publication, for both environmental impact indicators and grams consumed. The main food groups considered were Cereals and starchy roots; fruits and vegetables; fruits; vegetables; legumes and pulses; nuts and seeds; oils and fats; fish and seafood; total meat; white meat; red meat; dairy products; eggs; sugar, spreads, sweeteners, sweets, and desserts; and total beverages. If studies reported food groups not according to the definitions used in this review, the data were added to the food group considered by the authors and noted (eg, in some studies, legumes were included in the vegetable group; therefore, if it was not possible to separate legumes and vegetables were reported all together under the vegetable group), or they were not considered for the calculation of the average consumption per food group per continent or continent region (eg, if fish and meat consumption were reported together in the included studies). Additional information about food groups and subgroups categorization can be found in Tables S2 and S3.

Basic characteristics of the studied population, such as sex, age, sample size, type of study, and territory were extracted. Specifically, countries were divided into continents and continent regions based on the United Nations Geoscheme by the United Nations Statistics Division25 and WorldAtlas.26 Additional information, such as year the study was conducted, national representativeness of the surveys or studies, year of publication, and type of diets and dietary scenarios were also extracted. Macro- and micronutrient composition of the diets was retrieved when available.

Dietary Scenarios

After data extraction, diets were described either as CDs or non-CDs (ie, alternative dietary scenarios) and were described in terms of consumption of food groups, environmental impact performance, healthiness, or other sustainability-related aspects (eg, cultural acceptability, affordability) singularly or simultaneously applied. Current diets were defined as the actual food consumption of the populations deriving either from a nationally representative population or not. A study was considered nationally representative when so stated by the authors. If not explicitly declared, national representativeness was evaluated based on the applied methodology and sample stratification (eg, sex, age, geographic distribution) of the studied population, as already reported by Kalmpourtzidou et al.9

Diets not classified as CDs were (1) diets defined a priori based on already established dietary patterns, such as diets compliant with food-based dietary guidelines (FBDGs), traditional diets and the New Nordic Diet; and (2) optimized diets based on mathematical optimization models, which seek to find an optimal solution regarding food consumption by applying an objective function and taking into consideration constraints such as environmental impact indicators, healthiness, nutrient adequacy, and food acceptability. The objective of the optimization models and constraints applied for diet optimization were categorized based on their frequency of use in 6 general classes: meat and/or animal restriction; nutritional adequacy; diet healthiness; lower environmental impact; affordability; acceptability; and “other.” When a diet belonged to more than 1 category, either a new main diet category was created, if the type of dietary scenario was common among studies (≥3 dietary scenarios) or it was categorized based on the characteristic that was considered dominant. For example, if a dietary scenario was described as omnivorous with a minimum water footprint, the dominant characteristic of the diet is the minimum water footprint.

Based on data availability, diets other than CDs (ie, a priori and optimized diets) were divided into 30 main categories (Table S4), as follows: acceptable diet; affluent/transition diet; affordable diet; Atlantic diet; diet compliant with FBDGs; diet including or excluding specific meat or other animal products; diet with a lower environmental impact considering specific indicator(s); diet with meat replacement; energy-oriented diet; flexitarian diet; healthy diet; healthy-acceptable diet; healthy-affordable diet; lacto-ovo vegetarian diet; Mediterranean diet; New Nordic Diet; omnivorous diet; pescatarian diet; plant-based diet; protein-oriented diet; reduced-meat diet; sustainable (eco-friendly) diet; sustainable-acceptable diet; sustainable-healthy diet; sustainable-healthy-acceptable diet; sustainable-healthy-affordable diet; traditional diet; vegan diet; Western diet; and other diets (eg, ketogenic, high-income, vitamin D-oriented). Additional information about diet categorization can be found in Table S3.

Data Analysis

Descriptive statistics were used to report food consumption and environmental impact data. Data were analyzed in Microsoft Excel (Redmond, WA) and IBM SPSS Statistics (Armonk, NY) and reported as means and SDs per continent region. When alternatively reported, dietary intake was converted to grams per capita per day. When food consumption data were expressed in kilocalories or servings and no information was provided by the researchers regarding the conversion in grams, the food equivalents list defined by the American Dietetic and Diabetes Associations was used.27 When data were presented for the total population, the number of inhabitants estimated for the specific year, provided by the World Bank, was researched.28 Specifically, to express data as grams of food group consumed per capita per day, the annual food group consumption (in grams) was divided by the number of the total population and by 365 days.

The following environmental indicators and units of measurement were included in the analysis: GHGEs (kg CO2 equivalent [eq] capita–1 d–1); water use (L capita–1 d–1); land use (m2 capita–1 d–1); energy use (MJ capita–1 d–1); acidification (g SO2 eq capita–1 d–1); eutrophication (g PO4 eq capita–1 d–1); biodiversity loss (potentially disappeared fraction of species [PDF] × y capita–1 d–1); nitrogen application (g N capita–1 d–1), and phosphorus application (g P capita–1 d–1).

Concerning eutrophication and acidification, when data were not expressed in g PO4 and g SO2, respectively, the following chemical equations were used for their conversion:

  • 1 kg NH3 = 0.35 kg PO4 eq

  • 1 kg N = 0.42 kg PO4 eq

  • 1 kg P = 3.07 kg PO4 eq

  • 1 kg NH3 = 0.93 SO229

  • 1 kg SO2 = 1000/32 mol H+ eq →1 g SO2 = 1/32 mol H+→1 mol H+ = 32 SO2 eq30

Other, less commonly reported environmental impact indicators, such as water scarcity, fossil energy use, green and grey water use, water deprivation, cropland malnutrition, cropland scarcity, fine particulate formulation, and food loss or waste, were not included in the main analysis of this review, but the data are listed in Table S3.

The majority of studies or surveys reporting CDs were considered nationally representative (68%) (Table S3). Moreover, most of the CDs (47%) derived from national nutrition surveys (nationally representative surveys conducted to assess the health status and nutritional intake of a country’s general population), 27% derived from health and nutrition studies (studies usually not nationally representative, with specific outcomes related to health and nutrition, including food consumption data), 22% from secondary data (11% from FAOSTAT FBS, 7% from combination of studies and/or surveys, and 4% from household and/or purchase surveys), and 4% from unclear or not-reported study types.

Due to the lack of data, most of the middle- and low-income countries are not represented, and only results from Europe are shown per continent region, otherwise they are presented per continent (Figure 2). The majority of the studies (63%) and diets (67% of total) refer to Europe, whereas Africa is the most underrepresented continent because data from only 2 countries are available. Specifically, in western Europe, 36 studies were conducted providing 214 diets (46% of diets referring to Europe); northern and southern Europe followed with 107 diets (23%) from 23 studies; and 99 diets (21%) derived from 28 studies. Lastly, 6 studies, reporting on a total of 32 diets (7%), were included from eastern Europe (Figure 2). Additionally, 4 studies with 16 diets reported summed results for all of Europe or countries around Europe (Table S3). Most of the data from the Americas derive from high-income countries of North America (n = 14 studies; n = 53 dietary scenarios). Only 4 studies and 8 dietary scenarios were reported for South American countries; specifically, 6 dietary scenarios were from Argentina and 2 dietary scenarios were for all of South America.

Food Consumption and Environmental Impact

Global Results

The contribution of main food groups, excluding total beverages, was evaluated overall per CDs and a priori and optimized diets (Figure 4). The highest mean fruit (314 g d–1) and vegetable (322 g d–1) consumption was noticed for the a priori diets; the lowest mean fruit intake (197 g d–1) was noted for optimized diets and the lowest vegetable intake for CDs (226 g d–1). On the other hand, the total meat consumption was the highest for CDs (124 g d–1); for optimized diets, total meat consumption was less than half that of CDs (51 g d–1). The highest amounts of dairy consumption was found for a priori diets (364 g d–1) and the lowest was with optimized diets (212 g d–1). Fish consumption ranged from 35 g d–1 for CDs to 44 g d–1 for optimized diets. Nuts and seeds were poorly consumed in CDs (9.6 g d–1); a higher consumption of nuts occurred with optimized diets (34 g d–1).

The global environmental impact of the 3 most commonly studied environmental impact indicators (ie, CF, land use, and freshwater use) was evaluated per diet category (Figure 5A, C, and E) considering all dietary scenarios included. Figure 5B, D, and F show the same results divided per a priori and optimized diets. The lowest mean CF was reported for sustainable and acceptable diets (1.1 kg CO2 eq d–1), and the highest was found for Western diets (9.2 kg CO2 eq d–1). The CF of CDs varied globally, with a mean CF of 4.4 kg CO2 eq d–1 (range, 0.5-25.2 kg CO2 eq d–1). The Mediterranean, healthy, meat-replacement diets, and diets following FBDGs had similar CFs with CDs, ranging from 4.3 to 4.6 kg CO2 eq d–1. Diets with relatively high CFs were the acceptable (5.3 kg CO2 eq d–1), omnivorous (6.5 kg CO2 eq d–1), and protein-oriented (5.4 kg CO2 eq d–1) diets, whereas diets with relatively low CFs were the sustainable, healthy, and affordable diets (1.6 kg CO2 eq d–1), the flexitarian diets (1.5 kg CO2 eq d–1), and the affordable diets (1.6 kg CO2 eq d–1) (Figure 5A).

Affordable diets showed the highest impact on freshwater use (3030 L d–1). Flexitarian diets reported the lowest freshwater use (510 L d–1). The mean freshwater use of CDs was 1772 L d–1 and varied widely from 193 to 4368 L d–1. Most of the diet categories affected freshwater use less than did CDs, but examples of diets with higher freshwater use are the Mediterranean diet (2458 L d–1), the healthy diets (2288 L d–1), and the sustainable diets (1936 L d–1) (Figure 5C).

When land use and diet categories that studied land use in more than 1 dietary scenario were considered, sustainable and healthy diets had the highest mean land use impact (78.6 m2 d–1); however, this may be related to some extreme values found in the data. On the other hand, vegan diets and diets reducing specific environmental impact indicators (specifically land use) had the lowest land use impact (3 m2 d–1 each). The mean land use impact of CDs was 11.5 m2 d–1 (range, 3.2-190 m2 d–1) (Figure 5E).

Current Diets

The average consumption of animal products ranged from 151 g d–1 in Africa to 479 g d–1 in northern Europe, with a respective total meat intake of 59 g d–1 and 135 g d–1, which peaked in eastern Europe (190 g capita–1 d–1). Average fish consumption ranged from 13 g d–1 in eastern Europe to 64 g d–1 in southern Europe. Considering the mean consumption of fruit and vegetables, the lower and upper extremes were found in northern (347 g d–1) and southern (578 g d–1) Europe, respectively. Average consumption of legumes ranged from 8 g d–1 in eastern Europe to 47 g d–1 in Asia. Moreover, the highest and the lowest amounts of cereals and starchy roots consumption were noted in Asia (589) and Oceania (237 g d–1), respectively. Sugar, sweeteners, and desserts consumption ranged from 42 g d–1 in Asia to 94 g d–1 in northern Europe (Table 3, Table S3).

Most of the CDs analyzing GHGEs were from western Europe (n = 86). The average performance of African diets was 1.6 kg CO2 eq d–1, which was at the opposite extreme of that noticed in Oceania (10.7 kg CO2 eq d–1). Total freshwater use was mostly analyzed in southern Europe (n = 38 diets), where a mean value of 2188 L capita–1 d–1 was calculated. The highest value occurred in western Europe, accounting for 3409 L capita–1 d–1. Specifically, blue water use was mainly reported in Asian diets (n = 22) and ranged from 40 L d–1 in eastern Europe to 555 L d–1 in the Americas. Land use was mostly evaluated in western Europe (n = 46 diets) and ranged from 3.2 m2 d–1 in Asia and Oceania combined to 158 m2 d–1 in Oceania, as reported by 115 and 738–42,44,45 studies, respectively. In Africa, nitrogen and phosphorus applications corresponded to 11 g N d–1 and 2 g P d–1, respectively. These values were less than 33% of those of Asia and Oceania combined (36 g N d–1) and western Europe (8 g P d–1). Current diets had the largest energy-use impact in the Americas (32 MJ d–1); they had a small energy-use impact in Oceania (4 MJ d–1).

Countries in southern Europe were associated with the highest eutrophication values (29 g PO4 d–1), and those in northern Europe were associated with the highest acidification values (35 g SO2 d–1). Finally, biodiversity loss ranged from an average of 2 × 10–13 PDF × y d–1 in Africa to 5 × 10–12 PDF × y d–1 in the eastern, northern, and southern Europe regions (Table 3, Table S3).

Vegan, Vegetarian, Pescatarian, Flexitarian, and Other Plant-Based Diets

There was variability in the reported CF in vegan dietary scenarios, with impact ranging from 0.3 CO2 eq d–1, as reported in a study assessing dietary GHGEs globally,16 to 2.6 CO2 eq d–1 for northern Europe.74,76,79 Vegetarian diets ranged higher in CF impact than did vegan diets, from 0.6 CO2 eq d–1 in a study assessing dietary GHGEs globally16 to 6.2 CO2 eq d–1 in southern Europe,98,115,134 even though the dairy consumption in southern Europe was lower than in other continents and in European regions. Flexitarian diets had a moderate CF impact (1.1-1.6 CO2 eq d–1) in western Europe. Other plant-based diets (namely, a low-food-chain diet reported by de Pee et al64 and a green diet reported by Mogensen et al75, described in detail in Table S3) for which CF was assessed derived from only 2 studies64,75 and dietary scenarios ranging from 1 to 4.3 CO2 eq d–1 in Asia and northern Europe, respectively. Pescatarian diets assessing CF also were derived from 2 studies,16,125 and they reported values ranging from 0.6 CO2 eq d–1 globally to 2 CO2 eq d–1 in western Europe. Total freshwater use varied from 517 globally to 2258 L d–1 in Oceania for vegan diets, from 445 L d–1 globally to 2584 L d–1 in Europe for vegetarian diets, from 430 L d–1 globally to 590 L d–1 in western Europe for flexitarian diets, and from 434 L d–1 globally to 2401 L d–1 in Asia for pescatarian diets. Consequently, total freshwater use may not differ significantly among the specific diets, and moderate consumption of fish, meat, and animal products may not play the most important role—the food supply may be the key (Table 3, Table S3).

FBDGs, Acceptable, Healthy, Healthy-Acceptable, and Traditional Diets

The highest average meat consumption occurred with the FBDGs of northern Europe (169 g d–1) and the lowest in Oceania (42 g d–1); the average meat consumption in healthy diets ranged from 50 g d–1 in western Europe to 110 g d–1 in Oceania. The highest average fish consumption was associated with the FBDGs of southern Europe (78 g d–1), the lowest was associated with those in Asia (15 g d–1). while the highest average fish consumption in healthy diets was reported in southern Europe (74 g d–1) and the lowest in Oceania (21 g d–1). Asian FBDGs included the highest level of fruit and vegetable consumption (746 g d–1) and the highest consumption of legumes (91 g d–1). Oceanian healthy diets included the highest level of fruit and vegetable consumption (685 g d–1) and southern European healthy diets the highest amount of legume consumption (90 g d–1). Finally, consumption of cereals and starchy roots ranged from 115 g d–1 in Asia and Oceania combined to 554 g d–1 in northern Europe.

The CF of diets following the FBDGs varied widely, from 1.7 CO2 eq d–1 in Asia and Oceania combined (n = 1)16 to 13 CO2 eq d–1 in Oceania alone.32,40 Among European regions, diets following the FBDGs of northern Europe (5.1 CO2 eq d–1) had the highest CF impact and those from southern Europe (3 CO2 eq d–1) the lowest. The CF of acceptable and healthy acceptable diets was similar among regions and varied from 4.1 CO2 eq d–1 in eastern, northern, southern, and total Europe to 5.6 CO2 eq d–1 in eastern Europe. On the other hand, the CF of healthy diets and traditional diets varied extensively from 3.6 CO2 eq d–1 in western Europe to 7.9 CO2 eq d–1 in southern Europe, and from 1.7 CO2 eq d–1 in Asia to 3.5 CO2 eq d–1 in northern Europe. Diet scenarios following the FBDGs in Asia and Oceania combined had a total freshwater use of 634 L d–1, accounting for less than one-third of the impact noticed in southern Europe (2178 L d–1). Land use of diets following the FBDGs was high in Oceania (13 m2 d–1) and low in Asia and Oceania combined (1.7 m2 d–1), whereas for healthy diets, in western Europe it was less than 3% of that in Oceania (4.7 m2 d–1 and 180 m2 d–1, respectively) (Table 3, Table S3).

Diets Including or Excluding Specific Meat or Other Animal Products, Reduced-Meat, Omnivorous, and Meat-Replacement Diets

The CF associated with diets including or excluding specific meat or animal products varied from 2 CO2 eq d–1 in the Americas to 4.4 CO2 eq d–1 in northern Europe. The diets replacing meat with vegan or vegetarian products had a limited CF variance, from 3.9 CO2 eq d–1 in southern Europe to 4.9 CO2 eq d–1 in Oceania, compared with the studies that reduced the meat consumption in comparison with the baseline diets (1.1-2.7 CO2 eq d–1). Conversely, the CF associated with omnivorous diets greatly varied, from 3.8 CO2 eq d–1 in West Asia to 9.2 CO2 eq d–1 in Americas (Table 3, Table S3).

Mediterranean, New Nordic, Atlantic, and Western Diets

The Mediterranean-style diet was also reported on in regions other than the Mediterranean—specifically, in the United States32 and in the Netherlands.126,132 The Mediterranean diet was differently defined among the regions in which this dietary pattern has been proposed. Average meat consumption was higher in western Europe (108 g d–1) and lower in southern Europe (44 g d–1), and fish consumption in western Europe (25 g d–1) accounted for less than half that in the Americas (56 g d–1). High levels of fruit and vegetable consumption were associated with the Mediterranean diet in studies from southern Europe (963 g d–1), whereas western European studies were associated with low amounts of fruit and vegetable consumption (381 g d–1). However, based on the diet analysis of these studies, the Mediterranean diet as followed in western Europe had the lowest CF impact (3.3 CO2 eq d–1), and that in the Americas had the highest (5.4 CO2 eq d–1).

The New Nordic Diet was studied only in Denmark and Iceland,47,77 where the meat and fish consumption was higher than in Mediterranean diet (100-141 g d–1 and 43-66 g d–1, respectively), and the average CF ranged from 3.8 to 4.3 CO2 eq d–1. The Atlantic diet was evaluated only in Spain,107,109,111 where the average meat and fish consumption was 113 g d–1 and 159 g d–1, respectively, and the average CF was 3.7 CO2 eq d–1. The Western diet also was analyzed only in Spain (southern Europe), where the highest average meat consumption (282 g d–1) and CF impact (9.2 CO2 eq d–1) occurred from among the ”other” group of dietary patterns (ie, Mediterranean, New Nordic, Atlantic, and Western diets) (Table 3, Table S3).

Affluent/Transition, Affordable, and Healthy-Affordable Diets

Affluent/transition diets are based more on economic growth rather than healthy guidelines, and they include a wide consumption of animal products (from 90 g d–1 in Asia to 225 g d–1 in western Europe). The available dietary scenarios of affluent/transition diets provided mainly data for land use and nitrogen application. The lowest values were found in western Europe (4.9 m2 d–1 and 6.7 g N d–1, respectively), and the highest were found in Asia (14.4 m2 d–1 and 80 g N d–1, respectively). Affordable and healthy-affordable diets suggested lower, absent, or limited meat consumption (≤ 92 g d–1); however, the average CF range was lower for affordable diets (1.2-2.3 CO2 eq d–1) than healthy-affordable diets (2.9-6.8 CO2 eq d–1) (Table 3, Table S3).

Diets Reducing Specific Environmental Impact Indicators and Diets Characterized by Multiple Sustainability Dimensions

Among all the types of sustainable diets, the lowest CF was reported in Asia for the sustainable-healthy-acceptable diet scenario (0.5 CO2 eq d–1), where the total meat consumption was relatively high (75 g d–1) and fruit and vegetable consumption was 662 g d–1. The highest CF impact among all types of sustainable diets was noticed in the dietary scenarios defined as sustainable in eastern Europe (4.9 CO2 eq d–1), where the highest level of meat consumption in the category was reported (109 g d–1).

Additionally, the minimum freshwater use was found in dietary scenarios from the Americas aimed specifically at reducing such indicator (average water footprint, 364 L d–1). The most freshwater use occurred in dietary scenarios from Asia (average reduced water footprint, 3100 L d–1), which was reported in a study from Iran, where the freshwater use of the CD was 4100 L d–1. It was reduced with the limitation of cereals, bread, pasta, and rice (Table 3, Table S3).

Dietary Scenarios Performance per Continent or Continent Region

Africa

Current African diets had a higher environmental impact in terms of CF (1.6 CO2 eq d–1) and land use (134 m3 d–1) than diets following the FBDGs in Africa (1.3 CO2 eq d–1 and 6 m2 d–1, respectively). However, fruit and vegetable consumption (285 g d–1) and cereals and starchy roots consumption (154 g d–1) recommendations of FBDGs are very low and nutrient intake needs would not be probably met (Table 3, Supplementary Material). On the other hand, diets following the FBDGs had a greater impact than CDs in terms of nitrogen and phosphorus application. Moreover, land use associated with CDs was much lower in contrast to healthy (315 m3 d–1) and sustainable healthy (142 m3 d–1) diets, whereas CDs contributed more (2 × 10–13 PDF × y d–1) to biodiversity loss than did sustainable healthy diets (1.3 × 10–13 PDF × y d–1) but less than healthy diets (2.7 × 10–13 PDF × y d–1) (Table 3, Table S3).

The Americas

In the Americas, CD had the lowest CF impact (4.8 CO2 eq d–1) than the other diets except for vegan diets (1.5 CO2 eq d–1), diets including/excluding specific meat/animal products (2.1 CO2 eq d–1), and energy-oriented diets (3.8 CO2 eq d–1). The CF in the Americas ranged from 1.5 CO2 eq d–1 for vegan diets to 9.2 CO2 eq d–1 for omnivorous diets. The greatest total freshwater use derived from vegetarian and omnivorous diets (1181 L d–1 each), and the greatest impact from blue water use was associated with the CDs across the American continent (555 L d–1) and the lowest from healthy diets (106 L d–1). Current diets performed worse than other diets in terms of land use (14.5 m2 d–1), excluding healthy diets (18.7 m2 d–1), but they resulted in the lowest energy use (32 MJ d–1) compared with other diets, except for energy-oriented diets (29 MJ d–1). Furthermore, CDs (16 gPO4 eq d–1) are responsible for more eutrophication than vegetarian (13.3 g PO4 eq d–1) and the Mediterranean (15.8 g PO4 eq d–1) diets, apart from healthy diets (19.2 g PO4 eq d–1). The FBDGs performed the best for land use (4.1 m2 d–1) and nitrogen application (22.4 g N d–1) among the other dietary scenarios analyzed in Americas (Table 3, Table S3).

Asia

Current diets in Asia were associated with a moderate to high CF (2.6 CO2 eq d–1) compared with the other extracted diets in Asia. Sustainable-healthy-acceptable diets had the best performance in Asia with respect to CF (0.5 CO2 eq d–1) and total freshwater use (470 L d–1), whereas healthy-affordable diets, those following the FBDGs (2.9 CO2 eq d–1 each) and the affordable diets performed the worst (3900 L d–1), respectively. The lowest impacts on land use (3.8 m2 d–1) and nitrogen application use (18.4 g N d–1) were reported for CDs. The highest impacts of blue water use (700 L d–1), land use (14.4 m2 d–1), and nitrogen application (80.4 g N d–1) impact were associated with affluent/transition diets. Sustainable-acceptable diets had the lowest phosphorous application impact (2.9 g P d–1), but those following the FBDGs had the highest (5.7 g P d–1) (Table 3, Table S3).

Eastern Europe

Vegan diets had the least impact on total freshwater (865 L d–1) and blue water (23 L d–1) use, whereas CDs performed the best for CF (2.6 CO2 eq d–1). The most blue water use (51 L d–1) and highest CF (5.7 CO2 eq d–1) were associated with healthy diets (Table 3, Table S3).

Northern Europe

In northern Europe, CDs had the highest CF impact (4.9 CO2 eq d–1) among the diets presented, except for acceptable diets (5.5 CO2 eq d–1), those following the FBDGs (5.1 CO2 eq d–1), and the omnivorous diets (5 CO2 eq d–1). Additionally, in terms of CF, the lowest impact was reported with affordable diets (1.8 CO2 eq d–1). Moreover, CDs had the greatest impact on land use (8.4 m2 d–1), whereas plant-based diets (4.1 m2 d–1) had the lowest (Table 3, Table S3).

Southern Europe

In southern Europe, CDs had the lowest blue water (439 L d–1) and energy (27 MJ d–1) use, whereas they had a larger CF (4.9 CO2 eq d–1) and land use impact (7.5 m2 d–1) compared with other diets in the region. The best performance related to CF impact was associated with affordable diets (1.2 CO2 eq d–1), and the worst was with Western diets (9.2 CO2 eq d–1), even though Western diets had the lowest total freshwater use (1352 L d–1). Although the lowest land use impact was related to the Mediterranean diet (5.2 m2 d–1), this diet performed the worst in the region in terms of biodiversity loss (4.5 × 10–10 PDF × y d–1). The Atlantic diet contributed the greatest blue water use impact (527 L d–1), and the healthy diet had the biggest energy-use impact (52 MJ d–1) (Table 3, Table S3).

Western Europe

Sustainable-healthy-acceptable diets performed well in western Europe by having the lowest CF impact (1.6 CO2 eq d–1) and the lowest energy-use impact (9.9 MJ d–1). Meanwhile, CDs had the largest environmental impact related to total freshwater use (4.3 CO2 eq d–1) and acidification (7 g SO2 eq d–1). Also, diets adhering to FBDGs performed the worst among the dietary scenarios evaluated in western Europe with regard to eutrophication (4.7 g PO4 eq d–1) and phosphorous application (11.5 g P d–1), whereas they had the lowest blue water use footprint (57 L d–1). Omnivorous diets had the greatest performance for total freshwater use (583 L d–1) and the poorest for nitrogen application (33 g N d–1) among the diets (Table 3, Table S3).

Oceania

Carbon footprint was evaluated for most dietary scenarios in Oceania, where sustainable-healthy-affordable diets performed the best (1.6 CO2 eq d–1), whereas diets following the FBDGs (13 CO2 eq d–1) performed the worst, followed by CDs (10.7 CO2 eq d–1). On the contrary, CDs resulted in the lowest amount of freshwater use (1944 L d–1) compared with the other available scenarios, such as vegan diets (2258 L d–1) and healthy dietary patterns (2183 L d–1) (Table 3, Table S3).

DISCUSSION

The aim of this systematic review was to gain an overall understanding and interpretation of the environmental impact of diets worldwide. To our knowledge, this is the first systematic review analyzing all available environmental impact indicators of CDs and other dietary scenarios, considering consumption per food group globally. The environmental impact and the food consumption of the different considered diets varied widely among diets, continents, and continent regions, as also reported in the systematic review by Aleksandrowicz et al.149 Even though data were harmonized to be expressed in the same units of measure, a wide variation in environmental impact within the same diet category was observed, which could be partially explained by the absence of strict definitions of diets and the different methodologies used for the construction of dietary scenarios, including the different applied assumptions and constraints, reducing the comparability among diets. Optimized diets were associated with lower dairy, total meat and eggs, and higher fish, nuts, and seeds consumption than were CDs and a priori diets.

Common constraints and objectives used to optimize diets for their sustainability and healthiness aspects might have a key role in diet composition. On the contrary, the highest total meat consumption was reported for CDs. Indeed, many a priori (with some exclusions [eg, Western diet]) and optimized diets apply healthy principles recommending low or moderate meat intake, as was reported as well in the Aleksandrowicz et al.149 systematic review. Instead, a priori diets were associated with the highest amount of fruit and vegetable consumption, but these diets usually do not consider factors such as accessibility, availability, and affordability. Overall, globally, a Western diet,115 followed by protein-based,123 omnivorous,102 and acceptable142 diets, had the highest CF, which can be considered predictable considering the high meat consumption included in these diets. On the other hand, diets mostly optimized by taking into consideration sustainability, affordability, and acceptability had the lowest CF.59,65 This could be explained because these diet categories usually consider CF constraints and decrease the consumption of food groups that have a high environmental impact. The high level of freshwater use of affordable diets, which aim to minimize food costs or range within a small percentage of CD costs,64,102 could be explained by the potentially affordable prices of staple food crops, such as rice and dairy, that are consumed in high quantities and, consequently, are dominant in local diets.150 As well, they are cheap food source in the majority of the countries.151,152 On the other hand, vegan diets reported the lowest land use, probably due to absence of meat and animal products, which highly contribute to land use impact.153

The environmental impacts of CDs differed broadly throughout the world and performed diversely based on the environmental impact indicator studied. For example, African CDs resulted in the lowest CF (1.6 kg CO2 eq d–1), in contrast to Oceania (10.7 kg CO2 eq d–1). The high CF in Oceania was unexpected because the meat and animal products consumption was much lower than in other continents or European regions; however, multiple scenarios from Oceania reported meat and fish consumption together, so they could not be included in the average meat and fish consumption separately. On the other hand, Africa had the lowest CF impact among the regions and among all the other dietary scenarios presented for the continent. African CDs were characterized by moderate consumption of cereal, starchy roots, fruits, and vegetables and low consumptions of fish, meat, and animal products. Based on Global Hunger Index 2022, the majority of African countries were in the top ranks of the classification; therefore, the low CF could be partially contributed to undernutrition of the African populations.154 Western Europe had the highest freshwater use (3409 L d–1). In the systematic review and meta-analysis conducted by Harris et al155 on water use of diets, Europe emerged as the continent with the greatest water impact. More specifically, southern Europe seemed to have a higher impact than western Europe. However, such results were based on comparatively fewer studies (about one-fourth) assessing freshwater than in the present review. Southern Europe used more land (6.5 m2 d–1) in contrast to western Europe (3.9 m2 d–1). According to Eurostat 2016, southern European countries, such as Italy, Greece, and Spain, are the main producers of fruits and vegetables in Europe.156

Diets with no or relatively low consumption of meat and/or animal products tended to perform better regarding impact on GHGEs; however, this is not an absolute finding, because several diets with low consumption of meat and animal products reported high GHGEs and vice versa. As expected, vegan diets reported very low GHGE impact (0.3-2.6 CO2 eq d–1) both for optimized (mean, 1.6 CO2 eq d–1) and a priori (mean, 1.8 CO2 eq d–1) diets; however, their healthiness and their nutrients efficiency were not analyzed, due to underreporting of nutritional composition of dietary scenarios in the studies. The availability of the nutrient composition of diets could have given a clearer idea for both the environmental and health effect of the diets. Specifically, vegan diets may be nutritionally adequate and healthy if well-planned with the consumption of fortified products or supplements for specific micronutrients. Otherwise, micronutrient deficiencies (eg, vitamins B12 and D, ω-3 fatty acids, calcium) related to consumption of no meat or animal products may be common.157–159

Interestingly, diets including meat substitutes, such as “plant-based meat,” had a relatively higher CF (4–4.9 CO2 eq d–1) than other plant-based diets that did not indicate the consumption of meat substitutes. Within optimized diets, vegan and vegetarian diets and diets including or excluding specific animal products had a lower mean CF than diets including meat substitutes (Figure 5B). In parallel, in the a priori diet category, vegan diets and diets including or excluding specific animal products had lower CFs, whereas vegetarian diets had equal mean CF to the meat replacement diets (Figure 5B). This result is in accordance with a recent Swedish study by Bunge et al,160 who found that the CF of vegan diets including whole foods was lower than that of vegan diets including plant-based alternatives. Nevertheless, the CF of these diets was lower compared with CDs and, in the case of the study by Mertens et al142, when fortified meat substitutes were used in the optimized models, CF was even lower. According to a systematic review by Lindberg et al,161 only 6 studies have measured the environmental impact of whole diets using meat replacement scenarios, implying, thereby, that the existing evidence is mostly limited in terms of comparisons between animal meat and plant-based meat substitutes and not dietary patterns. In this case, it should be noted that most of the industrial plant-based meat substitutes are considered ultra-processed foods and, potentially, as shown in this review, diets including them may have a high environmental impact. Thus, food sustainability labelling may help consumers make more conscious food choices, because ultra-processed plant-based products have also been criticized for their healthiness.162 Thus, the development of ultra-processed plant-based alternatives with similar or better nutritional composition compared with the products that they are supposed to replace should be a high priority.162 Additionally, alternative sources of traditional animal-based proteins, such as insects, microalgae, and cell-cultured meat, are being tested and slowly introduced to the market.17 Mazac et al163 found that by optimizing CDs with novel foods, global warming potential, water use, and land use could be reduced by greater than 80% while at the same time meeting nutrition and feasible food consumption goals. Although these alternative protein sources may have more desirable nutritional characteristics and, consequently, health benefits, studies on their content of toxic compounds and the potential allergic effects are scarce.164

Affordability, acceptability, accessibility, and availability of new, alternative food products need to be considered, too. In this regard, the European Economic and Social Committee recommended, in October 2022, the establishment of an evidence-based sustainable food–labelling framework to help stakeholders improve their products with respect to sustainability and consumers to have the information needed for healthy and sustainable food choices.165 In addition, providing consumers adequate purchasing and cooking skills is essential in education programs. A good and effective intervention for the enhancement of these skills are community gardens and kitchens.166–168 Studies have shown that the inclusion of children and adolescents in urban gardening or farming and cooking their produce increased their vegetable and fruit consumption.169–172 Simultaneously, urban gardening by adults, especially those with low socioeconomic status, increases the food security and access of their households and also improves their diet and general well-being as individuals.173–177

Culinary and eating habits as well as the socioeconomic status differed among populations in the articles included in this review. Therefore, the same diet category may not have the same food group and nutritional characteristics among continents and continent regions. For example, the Mediterranean diet, which originated in southern Europe, is defined differently in other world regions with possibly higher meat and animal products consumption and lower olive oil intake, according to the traditions of each country or region. The western European version of the Mediterranean diet characterized by the average values calculated in this review presents more than double the meat consumption compared with southern Europe. According to Martínez-González et al,178 in non-Mediterranean countries, and especially in the United States, there are misconceptions about the food items included in the traditional Mediterranean diet. The transferability of Mediterranean diet to non-Mediterranean countries is challenging, but it could be feasible with the assumption of certain flexibility and education of the populations.178,179 Consequently, the food acceptability, affordability, and availability should always be considered in the design of sustainable diets, especially FBDGs, by taking into account the overall sociocultural context of the populations.179,180 For example, in low- and middle-income countries where plant-based diets were part of the traditional diets, dietary habits have changed due to the nutrition transition and higher consumption of meat and animal products.181,182

Diet sustainability often implies relevant trade-offs among the multiple sustainability dimensions, with potential different performance for health and environmental impact.183 As shown in the present review, the Mediterranean diet and, especially, “healthy” diets, although being appropriate from the nutritional perspective and health-enhancing, as widely demonstrated, performed suboptimally regarding environmental impact. Moreover, diets based on FBDGs had a higher CF compared with other a priori healthy diets, as shown in Figure 5B, probably because many FBDGs do not consider the environmental impact of their recommendations.15,184

Another limitation of the studies included in this review is the methodology heterogeneity observed among studies regarding the collection of food consumption data. Because food groups were defined differently among the studies, calculations were necessary to quantify the main food groups provided in this review, thus there could be possible overestimation or underestimation of food consumption. Cases of overestimation occurred for continents or continent regions where studies included potatoes or legumes in the vegetable group. Conversely, an example of probable underestimation of meat and fish consumption is the case of Oceania, where an accurate calculation of the average consumption was prevented because data from multiple studies reporting meat and fish intake together were not included. In addition, a relatively low proportion of studies (11%) used FAOSTAT FBSs as food consumption data instead of national nutrition surveys. Consequently, an erroneous estimation may be possible, both in terms of overestimation of food intake, due to the data based only on food supply and food waste,185,186 and underestimation because of the unavailable data related to private production in rural and urban areas and in the wild.187 The latter specific underestimation may result in the underestimation of the environmental impact of private production and foraging, not only related to the common indicators. Unsustainable foraging practices such as the uprooting whole plants could lead to their absence from their natural habitat and, consequently, biodiversity loss and ecological instability,188 threating not only rural areas and forests but also urban areas, as discussed by Schunko et al189 in their study conducted in Vienna, Austria. Unsustainable rural or urban gardening could contribute to CF impact, misuse of pesticides and fertilizers, and potential introduction of alien species that are a threat to local biodiversity. On the other hand, gardens can have a beneficial effects because they could contribute to biodiversity enhancement, by the planting of diverse species and local air cooling.190 In this regard, a data set of the environmental impacts linked to different dietary patterns at the country level, detailing food groups, is freely available at the World Wildlife website.191 However, the applied food consumption data are estimated based on food availability at the retail store rather than on actual recorded food intake quantities and frequencies. Additionally, FAOSTAT FBSs do not take into account basic characteristics of the population, such as age and sex; hence, the food consumption of adults is underestimated because the elderly and children are also considered.185,186 Higher data unreliability occurs in developing countries, due to low quality of data.185 Different dietary assessment methods were used for national nutrition surveys. Food frequency questionnaires are the most commonly used tool in national nutrition surveys.9,192 A combination of methods for the accuracy and feasibility of national nutrition surveys is required.193,194 The European Food Safety Authority has developed the European Union Menu methodology for conducting national nutrition surveys that provide harmonized and high-quality food consumption data in Europe.195

An aspect characterizing multiple studies evaluating the environmental impact of diets is that the main attention is given to the analysis and reporting of the environmental impact indicators, ignoring sometimes the importance of efficiently describing the data collection methodology. Specifically, food consumption and baseline characteristics of the studied population may not be described in detail and, as a consequence, the dietary assessment methods used and/or the characteristics of the population remain unknown or not adequately understood.

Another source of heterogeneity is the constraints used for the modelling of the optimized dietary scenarios. Healthiness and nutritional adequacy of diets, followed by their environmental impact, were the constraints used most commonly, but they were defined diversely in numerous cases. For example, nutritional adequacy of a diet may have derived from FBDGs or dietary reference levels or from a diet that is commonly recognized as healthy and/or nutritionally adequate, such as the Mediterranean diet. On the other hand, different environmental impact indicators have been used, some of them set at the lowest possible level without simultaneously considering other variables for a sustainable diet. Hence, even though some optimized dietary scenarios were categorized together (eg, as vegan or vegetarian diet), their constraints may have differed, taking into consideration the cultural differences and the outcomes of interest of the researchers. Additionally, the mean CF and freshwater use of a priori vegetarian and vegan diets were higher than those of the optimized diets (Figure 5B and D), probably due to stricter constraints applied regarding the environmental impact of the latter.

Furthermore, most of the studies, especially those from Europe, were derived from high-income countries. Because we are lacking data from many African, Asian, Oceanian, and Latin American countries, we cannot have an accurate overview of the environmental impact of their CDs and potential sustainable scenarios, but results also may not be accurate to provide a figure for the macro-geographic areas when they originate from few studies per continent or continent region. Therefore, the subdivision of these continents into continent regions was not possible, even though there are differences in eating habits between, for example, North and South America.196 Moreover, the evaluation of the environmental impact of diets at a global level derives from few specific studies, which optimized the global CDs with the use of FAOSTAT FBS. The cost of collection and processing data may be high and, therefore, this may be 1 of the reasons for the lack of national nutrition surveys in mostly low-income countries.197 An important initiative in this direction is represented by the STEPwise approach to noncommunicable disease surveillance surveys initialized by World Health Organization198 and conducted by local authorities in low- and middle- income countries. However, only vegetable and fruit consumption are reported, which does not allow the total evaluation of the diet-related environmental impact.198 Additionally, we report here data on adult general populations, as more represented in studies evaluating diet-related environmental impact.15,16 Therefore other population groups, such as children and pregnant women, which have special nutritional needs and dietary patterns, are not included.15,16

Researchers based their estimations of environmental impact measurements related to diet on different literature sources, sustainability tools, protein consumption, and life cycle assessment (LCA) implementations. The results of LCA, even for the same environmental impact measurement, could vary significantly due to different calculations and assumptions.199 For this reason, it is essential that national LCA data be provided from reliable government sources for comparable and harmonized measurements per country.200 Like food consumption data, sustainability assessment tools have been used mostly in developed countries. Ideally, harmonized indicators could be used related to nutrition, health, environment, and socioeconomic status for the assessment of sustainable food systems.

To our knowledge, this is the first review providing a wide picture of the environmental impact of CDs and a priori or optimized diets collected worldwide. The findings strengthen the need for harmonized measurements in food consumption and environmental impact to allow meaningful and easier comparisons. The strength of the present systematic review is, indeed, the broad inclusion of studies, which allows us to describe the high variability of diet definitions and categorization and of methods applied to measure food consumption and diet environmental impact.

CONCLUSION

The comparison of the environmental impact and food consumption of CDs and alternative dietary scenarios is very challenging and demanding, due to the different variables and methodologies used for the definition and analysis of the various diets. Harmonized LCA and dietary assessment methods are essential. As mentioned, the composition of a diet in terms of food groups may not, itself, define its environmental impact, because other factors related to food systems, such as technology use, applied farming techniques, and farmers’ knowledge, may contribute greatly. Therefore, extensive understanding of all LCA steps is fundamental. Moreover, specific dietary scenarios performed highly regarding GHGEs but low for other environmental impact factors; thus, a holistic approach is required with the use of multiple environmental indicators to analyze the environmental performance of a diet. Diets considering healthiness, such as the Mediterranean, healthy, healthy-acceptable diets, and diets based on FBDGs, seem to have a higher CF than other diets that reduce meat and animal-based product consumption. Furthermore, diets including meat substitutes had a higher CF than other plant-based diets. The environmental impact of vegan and vegetarian diets comparing a priori and optimized diets showed that the latter had lower CF and freshwater use, possibly due to the use of stricter constraints. On the other hand, affordable diets in total seemed to have a high freshwater use, probably due to the high cultivation of staple foods and acceptable diets that report very enhanced CF due to high meat intake. Additionally, food systems transformation cannot be expected to occur through actions simultaneously in different parts of the world, because food systems and local population needs for the transition toward sustainability, including food accessibility and security, differ substantially. Considering the high diversity of food systems, cultural habits, and needs, the design of sustainable diets should also consider food types such as neglected species, novel foods, and alternative plant-based products that could harmoniously contribute to the reduction of different environmental impact indicators. Moreover, diets named identically may produce different environmental impacts, not only because of the different criteria used for their definition but also the geographic context in which the food is produced. Hence, a diet that may be sustainable in 1 region of the world may not be in another. Data about food consumption and environmental impact are available only in a small part of the world. Extensive research on dietary intake and environmental impact in low- and middle-income countries is required to draw reliable conclusions about the environmental impact of diets worldwide and to promote evidence-based changes in the food systems based on the international context and national possibilities considering population needs, habits, and feasible food supply.

Figure 2 shows the countries and the different diets considered in the review. Figure 3 shows the distribution of diet categories among CDs, a priori diets, and optimized diets. A priori and optimized diets shared multiple diet categories, whereas diets such as the Mediterranean, Western, and Atlantic were only part of a priori diets. Diets focusing on affordability, acceptability, and sustainability of dietary scenarios were mostly described by optimized models. From a total of 703 diets extracted (Table 3), 294 were CDs (42%) and 206 were diets defined a priori (29%). Among optimized diets (n = 203; 29%), healthiness of diets (51%) and nutritional adequacy (50%) were the objectives or constraints mostly reported, followed by lower environmental impact (36%), meat and/or animal products reduction (23%), acceptability (21%), affordability (18%), and then the “others” group (42%; eg, nutrient-oriented constraints, organic production and bioavailability). Among the environmental indicators, the carbon footprint (CF) was the most reported (86% of dietary scenarios), followed by land use (36%), total freshwater use (22%), blue water use (15%), energy use (14%), nitrogen and phosphorus application (10% each), biodiversity loss (7%), eutrophication (4%), and acidification (1%) (Table S3).

The majority of studies or surveys reporting CDs were considered nationally representative (68%) (Table S3). Moreover, most of the CDs (47%) derived from national nutrition surveys (nationally representative surveys conducted to assess the health status and nutritional intake of a country’s general population), 27% derived from health and nutrition studies (studies usually not nationally representative, with specific outcomes related to health and nutrition, including food consumption data), 22% from secondary data (11% from FAOSTAT FBS, 7% from combination of studies and/or surveys, and 4% from household and/or purchase surveys), and 4% from unclear or not-reported study types.

Due to the lack of data, most of the middle- and low-income countries are not represented, and only results from Europe are shown per continent region, otherwise they are presented per continent (Figure 2). The majority of the studies (63%) and diets (67% of total) refer to Europe, whereas Africa is the most underrepresented continent because data from only 2 countries are available. Specifically, in western Europe, 36 studies were conducted providing 214 diets (46% of diets referring to Europe); northern and southern Europe followed with 107 diets (23%) from 23 studies; and 99 diets (21%) derived from 28 studies. Lastly, 6 studies, reporting on a total of 32 diets (7%), were included from eastern Europe (Figure 2). Additionally, 4 studies with 16 diets reported summed results for all of Europe or countries around Europe (Table S3). Most of the data from the Americas derive from high-income countries of North America (n = 14 studies; n = 53 dietary scenarios). Only 4 studies and 8 dietary scenarios were reported for South American countries; specifically, 6 dietary scenarios were from Argentina and 2 dietary scenarios were for all of South America.
