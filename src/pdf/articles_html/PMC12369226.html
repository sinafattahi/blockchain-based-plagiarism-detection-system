Computer identification of Notch3 in the neurogenic progenitor cells of mammalian early optic vesicles

Abstract

Objective

The developing mammalian retina initially contains undifferentiated cells, providing a model for investigating the mechanisms of differentiation. Notch signaling, mediated by four Notch receptors (Notch 1–4) in mammals, has been studied in the differentiation of neural progenitor cells. Among the four Notch receptors, the frequency, rather than the peak level, of Notch1-mediated signaling has been suggested to promote the activation of neural progenitor cells. In contrast to Notch1, the involvement of Notch3 in this process is poorly documented, although Notch3 is known for its role in vascular integrity.

Results

By re-analyzing publicly available single-cell RNA-seq data from one mouse retinal dataset, two human retinal organoid datasets and two human embryonic retinal datasets, we found that, along with Notch1, Notch3 is expressed in neural progenitor cells in the retina. In addition, the results of the co-expression profile analyses varied among the datasets, leaving uncertainty regarding the regulatory mechanisms of Notch1 and Notch3. Our findings shed light on Notch3 in neurogenic progenitor cells of the developing mammalian retina. Since Notch3 has been suggested to cause ligand-independent signaling, Notch3 expression might antagonize Notch1-mediated signaling oscillations, maintaining the quiescent state of neurogenic progenitor cells.

Keywords: Hes1, Notch signaling, Oscillation, Quiescence, Retina, Retinal progenitor cell, Single-cell RNA-seq

Introduction

The development of the retina begins with the accumulation of undifferentiated cells (retinal progenitor cells [RPCs] ) in the forebrain, called optic vesicles. Proper activation of quiescent RPCs is key to optic vesicle maturation, which occurs from the 12-somite stage to the 26-somite stage (approximately embryonic day 9) in mice [1]. Terminally differentiated neuronal or glial cells originating from RPCs appear after the optic vesicle becomes the optic cup. Both the maintenance of RPCs and their terminal differentiation are mediated, in part, by Notch signaling.

Notch signaling is a well-conserved pathway involving four Notch receptors (Notch1–4) in mammals. The involvement of Notch1 and Notch3 in Notch signaling in retinal development has been studied. For example, these two Notch receptors are involved in ganglion cell fate specification [2], although Notch3 is known to be responsible for the cerebral vasculature disease CADASIL [3]. In contrast to the terminally differentiating stage of retinal development, the maintenance of RPCs in the early stage of retinal development remains unclear despite the putative involvement of Notch1 (summarized in [2]).

Notch receptors are cleaved upon binding to their ligands to induce the expression of downstream target genes such as Hes1 [4]. The oscillatory expression of Hes1, especially its frequency, rather than its amplitude, is crucial for neural stem cell activation [5]. Induction of Hes1 expression differs depending on the upstream Notch receptors. Crystallography has revealed that the autoinhibitory mechanism is loose in the NOTCH3 receptor compared to that in the NOTCH1 and NOTCH2 receptors, which may allow basal induction of downstream gene expression by the NOTCH3 receptor without ligand binding [6]. This biochemical difference prompted us to investigate the involvement of the four Notch receptors in early optic vesicles, where proper activation of quiescent RPCs occurs.

Main text

Methods

A single-cell RNA-seq dataset from mouse embryos (GSE148063) [1] was downloaded from the Gene Expression Omnibus (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/). Two single-cell RNA-seq datasets from human retinal organoids (GSE220661 [7] and GSE235585 [8]) were downloaded from the Gene Expression Omnibus. The data were processed using Seurat version 5.1.0 [9] in R version 4.4.1 on the Ubuntu 22.04.4 LTS environment. Monocle3 version 1.3.7 [10] was used to conduct the pseudotime analysis, and CSCORE version 0.0.0.9000 [11] and WGCNA version 1.72-5 [12] were used to identify co-expressed genes. To reduce computational burden, retinal organoid samples at days 20, 33, 90, and 210 from the GSE235585 dataset were selectively used. The codes used in the analyses are available at 10.5281/zenodo.15876358.

Results

Re-analysis of mouse retina dataset (GSE148063) revealed Notch3 expression in the retinal progenitor cells

We reanalyzed a published single-cell RNA-seq dataset of mouse optic vesicles. This dataset contained samples from five developmental stages (12, 16, 20, 24, and 26-somite stages). We identified 18 clusters in the UMAP analysis (Fig. 1A) that were annotated using established cell lineage markers (Fig. 1B). Of the 18 clusters, seven were annotated to the optic vesicle (OV) based on their high expression of Pax6, Six3, Rax, and Lhx2. Importantly, OV1-3 were mainly derived from the 12-somite stage sample, whereas OV5-6 were mainly derived from the 26-somite stage sample (Fig. 1C). Dot plot analysis revealed the expression of Notch1 and Notch3 in optic vesicle clusters (Fig. 1D). To further characterize the developmental timing of Notch1 and Notch3 expression in these clusters, we conducted pseudo-time analysis using Monocle3 (Fig. 1E). Pseudotime analysis suggested that Notch1 and Notch3 were expressed in the early period of retinal differentiation, which corresponds to the neural progenitor cells in the optic vesicle (Fig. 1F). To identify common regulators of Notch1 and Notch3, we hypothesized that these receptors would show similar expression profiles in the presence of such regulators and used the CSCORE pipeline to identify gene sets that showed similar expression profiles at the single-cell level. Co-expression profile analysis identified 30 gene sets and revealed a Notch3-associated gene set (Fig. 1G). Unfortunately, a Notch1-associated gene set has not yet been identified. The gene set data is available in “Additional_file_1.” In addition, characteristic gene heatmap for cell clustering and two additional pseudotime analyses were also carried out to increase the accuracy of our analysis, which are available in “Additional_file_2.”

Re-analysis of human retinal organoid dataset (GSE220661) revealed Notch3 expression in the neural progenitor cells

To focus on neurogenic progenitor cells, we reanalyzed a published single-cell RNA-seq dataset of human retinal organoids. This dataset contained samples from three stages (days 28, 78, and 185). We identified 16 clusters in the UMAP analysis (Fig. 2A) that were annotated using established cell lineage markers (Fig. 2B). Of the 16 clusters, seven were annotated to RPC based on their high expression of LHX2, PAX6, RAX, and SIX3. RPCs were mainly derived from day-28 samples, supporting the validity of our annotation strategy (Fig. 2C). In addition, characteristic gene heatmap is also shown in “Additional_file_3” to increase the accuracy of this annotation. Dot plot analysis revealed NOTCH3, NOTCH1 and NOTCH2 expression in the RPCs (Fig. 2D). We proceeded to perform pseudotime analysis (Fig. 2E) with attention to relatively differentiated RPCs, since a minor population of RPCs existed even in the day-185 sample. Specifically, we examined the expression of NOTCH1 and NOTCH3 in RPCs and found that these genes were expressed during the early period of retinal differentiation (Fig. 2F). Co-expression profile analysis identified 44 gene sets and revealed that NOTCH1 and NOTCH3 belong to the same gene set (Fig. 2G). The gene set data is available in “Additional_file_4.” Unfortunately, we were unable to carry out additional pseudotime analysis possibly owing to computational burden, although the validity of Monocle3 pseudotime analysis was supported by its consistency with developmental stage (sample)-specific UMAP analysis.

Re-analysis of human retinal organoid dataset (GSE235585) confirmed Notch3 expression in the neural progenitor cells

To confirm NOTCH3 expression, we reanalyzed another published single-cell RNA-seq dataset of human retinal organoids. Fourteen clusters were identified in UMAP analysis (Fig. 3A). The two clusters were identified as RPCs because these clusters, but not transient NRPC, expressed LHX2, PAX6, RAX, and SIX3 (Fig. 3B). In addition, characteristic gene heatmap is also shown in “Additional_file_5” to increase the accuracy of this annotation. Two RPC clusters were derived from the earliest sample (day 20) (Fig. 3C). As expected, NOTCH3 was expressed in the RPC clusters (Fig. 3D). Pseudotime analysis showed that this dataset contained a wide range of cells, from 0 to approximately 70 (Fig. 3E). In contrast to the previous organoid dataset, RPCs were concentrated within 15 pseudotimes (Fig. 3F). This dataset also showed that NOTCH3 as well as NOTCH1 was expressed in the undifferentiated cell population. In addition, the co-expression profile analysis identified 59 gene sets and revealed that NOTCH1 and NOTCH3 belonged to different gene sets (Fig. 3G). The gene set data is available in “Additional_file_6.” Unfortunately, we were unable to carry out additional pseudotime analysis possibly owing to computational burden, although the validity of Monocle3 pseudotime analysis was supported by its consistency with developmental stage (sample)-specific UMAP analysis.

To further validate the results from the two human retinal organoid datasets, we also carried out additional analyses of two public single-cell RNA-seq datasets from human embryos (GSE138002 in “Additional_file_7”) and (GSE142526 in “Additional_file_8”). In summary, these analyses also supported the expressions of NOTCH3 and NOTCH1 in early retinal progenitor cells although the latter contains a small number of NOTCH3- or NOTCH1-expressing early retinal progenitor cells which could be a potential limitation.

Discussion

The developing retina is a suitable model for analyzing differentiation via Notch signaling mediated by the four Notch receptors. Although the biochemical properties of ligand-independent basal activity differ, a detailed analysis of the contribution of specific receptors has been poorly described. By reanalyzing five single-cell RNA-seq datasets, we found that Notch3 was expressed in undifferentiated RPCs. Compared to Notch1 which has already been suggested to be involved in the maintenance of RPCs, Notch3 has a loose autoinhibitory subunit that allows ligand-independent basal Notch signaling. Pulsatile Notch1 signaling, along with the autoinhibitory expression of the Hes1 protein, supports oscillatory Notch1 signaling [13, 14], which can be dampened by basal Notch3 signaling. Notch1 and Notch3 act as the initiators and repressors of RPC activation, respectively. Another interpretation of Notch3 expression is that it ensures that a certain level of nuclear Notch intracellular domain is necessary for Hes1 oscillation [5].

One of the unique points of the present study was the pseudotime analyses that enabled more precise characterization of retinal progenitor cells than cell cluster-based analysis. Since both Notch3 and Notch1 are expressed in retinal progenitor cells, we hypothesized that the expression of these receptors is dependent on a common upstream regulator. Unfortunately, the results of co-expression profile analyses varied among the three datasets (one mouse retina dataset and two human retinal organoids dataset), contradicting our hypothesis.

The experimental difficulty of separately examining the contributions of Notch1 and Notch3 lies in the fact that these receptors share common ligands and downstream target genes. In addition, the fetal lethality of Notch1 functional knockout between mouse embryonic days 8.5 and 10.5 [15] augments this difficulty, although Notch3 knockout adult mice are viable and fertile [16]. One solution might be transgenic mice harboring chimeric Notch receptors, in which their intracellular domains are replaced with an artificial transcription factor, Gal4VP16 [17]. By crossing with a transgenic mouse harboring a Gal4VP16-responsible element, Notch1-specific signal transduction has been visualized at a cell level [18]. The present study sheds light on Notch3 in early retinogenesis and suggests the usefulness of generating chimeric Notch3 reporter mice.

Conclusion

Notch3 and Notch1 expressions were observed in retinal progenitor cells. Because the biochemical properties of signal transduction are different, this observation warrants attention to Notch3 in future studies on retinal development. Quantitative assessment on the impact of the orchestrated Notch expressions would facilitate understanding how retinal neural cell development is achieved, and hopefully, contribute to future fine-tuned regenerative medicine.

Limitations

The oscillatory expression of Hes1 in the early optic vesicles should be examined in a time-lapse study. In addition, the impact of Notch1 and Notch3 on Hes1 expression could be examined using knockdown or knockout studies.
