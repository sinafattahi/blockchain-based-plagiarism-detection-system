Abstract

Abstract

Objectives

To assess the impact of China’s medical insurance integration policy on urban-rural disparities in medical insurance benefit levels, explore its heterogeneous effects in regions with different economic development levels and provide empirical evidence for promoting health equity.

Design

Retrospective panel data analysis using a quasi-natural experiment. Causal inference was conducted using propensity score matching-staggered difference in differences (PSM-staggered DID) method with time-varying policy variables.

Setting

Provincial-level analysis covering 31 provinces in China, using administrative data from 2006 to 2021.

Participants

31 Chinese provinces with complete annual data on medical insurance and economic indicators from 2006 to 2021, yielding 496 province-year observations.

Intervention

Gradual implementation of the policy of integration of medical insurance (2006–2021), with start-up dates varying from province to province.

Primary and secondary outcome measures

The primary outcomes are natural logarithm of per capita medical insurance benefits for all residents, urban residents and rural residents. No secondary outcomes were predefined.

Results

Overall, the policy had a weak positive impact on the per capita benefits of rural residents. Subgroup analyses showed no immediate effect in provinces with a gross domestic product (GDP) per capita of more than 100 000 yuan, but an increase in benefits for rural residents 1 year after implementation; an immediate increase in rural benefits in provinces with a GDP per capita of 60 000–100 000 yuan; and a reduction in overall and rural benefits at the beginning in provinces with a GDP per capita of <60 000 yuan, followed by a restoration of rural benefits after 2 years. Mechanistically, the policy increased medical insurance coverage rates, but higher coverage was associated with short-term reductions in per capita benefits.

Conclusion

Region-specific strategies need to be developed to provide lessons for developing countries pursuing universal health coverage.

Keywords: China, HEALTH ECONOMICS, Health Equity

Strengths and limitations of this study.

  • Uses administrative data from the China Health Statistics Yearbook, ensuring standardisation and comparability of data.

  • Adopts a quasi-natural experimental design with propensity score matching-difference in differences to improve causal inference.

  • Incorporates subgroup and mediation analyses to explore heterogeneity and pathways, increasing methodological depth.

  • Relies on provincial-level data, potentially overlooking heterogeneity at the municipal or county level.

  • Unable to fully control for unmeasured confounders from differences in local insurance programme operations.

Introduction

The WHO has long emphasised universal health coverage as a core strategy for promoting equitable access to healthcare, urging countries to address the fragmentation of insurance systems to reduce disparities in financing and access to healthcare.1 2 Globally, developing countries (eg, Thailand, Mexico) and developed countries (eg, Germany, South Korea) have undertaken insurance integration reforms, which have generally expanded insurance coverage and reduced the financial burden on vulnerable groups.3 4 These experiences highlight that integrating insurance schemes is an important tool for promoting health equity, but their effectiveness often depends on the local economic and institutional context.

In China, universal health coverage has expanded dramatically over the past two decades.5,9 As of 2016, three major programmes, the Urban Employees’ Basic Medical Insurance (UEBMI), the New Cooperative Medical Scheme for Rural Residents (NCMS) and the Urban Residents’ Basic Medical Insurance (URBMI), covered more than 95% of the population.10,14 However, inherent disparities between these programmes have led to persistent urban-rural inequalities: for example, UEBMI provides more generous benefits than NCMS and URBMI, widening the welfare gap.515,18 To address this issue, China launched the Urban-Rural Resident Basic Medical Insurance Integration Program (URRMI) in 2016, unifying URBMI and NCMS to merge risk pools, standardise reimbursement and eliminate household registration-based barriers.16 Prior to 2016, integration pilots had already been carried out in a number of provinces, which provided a unique quasi-natural experiment to evaluate policy impacts (see online supplemental table A1).17 19

Existing research on URRMI has yielded mixed findings. Some studies suggest that integration improves rural benefits by expanding coverage and reducing barriers to access,20,22 while others note unintended consequences, such as the concentration of medical resource use in urban areas or limited reimbursement for low-income groups.23,25 A key gap that remains is that most studies either focus on short-term effects or ignore regional heterogeneity. For example, while studies using the difference-in-differences methods826,28 have assessed integration, few have explored how economic development levels moderate policy impacts. In addition, available data are primarily pre-2019 and lack data on post-2019 dynamics, including the impact of major health events on insurance utilisation.27 29

Against this background, this study aims to address two central questions: (1) Does URRMI programmes reduce urban-rural benefit disparities, and does this gap vary by regional economic development? (2) What are the mechanisms driving these effects? We use provincial panel data (2006–2021) from 31 provinces in China, treating the staggered implementation of URRMI as a quasi-natural experiment. Employing a time-varying difference-in-differences model combined with propensity score matching (PSM-DID), we quantify the impact of the policy on urban and rural medical insurance benefits. We further conduct subgroup analyses by per capita gross domestic product (GDP) and mechanism tests to explore economic heterogeneity and underlying pathways.

By focusing on spatial equity and economic gradients, this study contributes to the literature in three ways. First, this study extends the URRMI research by examining long-term impacts over the period 2006–2021. Second, it fills a gap in the analysis of regional heterogeneity by elucidating how economic development moderates policy impacts. Third, it links findings to the “social determinants of health” framework,30 31 emphasising that integration success depends on balancing coverage expansion and resource allocation, a concept that is relevant to developing countries pursuing universal health coverage.

Methods

Study design

This study is a retrospective panel data analysis, based on the medical insurance integration policy of 31 provinces in China from 2006 to 2021 as a natural experiment, and assesses the impact of the policy on the urban-rural medical insurance utilisation gap through the PSM-DID method.

Setting and participants

This study uses provincial-level data from 31 provinces in China, spanning the period 2006–2021, with a total of 496 province-year observations. Participants are defined as these 31 provinces, and the observations come from annual data on medical insurance, demographics and socioeconomic conditions. Online supplemental table A1 lists all 31 included provinces.

Data sources and measurement

The data are obtained from the China Health Statistical Yearbook, provincial Health Statistical Yearbooks and annual statistics of the National Bureau of Statistics.32 33 All variables are measured through statistical calculation based on the original data from these sources. Demographic statistical data are truncated to the range of 1%–99% to mitigate the influence of extreme outliers, which could bias regression estimates.

Variables

Outcome variables

Outcome variables focus on measuring integrated insurance system utilisation, including: the level of per capita benefit, calculated by dividing overall reimbursement expenditures by overall enrollees and transformed into a natural logarithm (lnpb); the per capita benefit of urban residents, calculated by dividing reimbursement expenditures of urban enrollees by urban enrollees and transformed into a natural logarithm (lnpbu); and the per capita benefit of rural residents, calculated by dividing reimbursement expenditures of rural enrollees by rural enrollees and transformed into a natural logarithm (lnpbr).

Dummy variable

The policy dummy variable (policy_implemented) is set to 1 if the policy was implemented in 2016 or earlier and 0 if it was implemented after 2016.

Covariates

Covariates are included to control for potential confounding factors and are categorised into three groups based on theoretical relevance and prior literature.5 31 Healthcare accessibility indicators include density of institutions, calculated as the number of medical institutions per 10 000 people (institution_density); density of beds, calculated as the number of beds per 1000 people (bed_density); density of technicians, calculated as the number of health technicians per 1000 people (tech_density); and urban-rural ratio of health technician density, calculated as the ratio of urban health technician density to rural health technician density (tech_ratio). Demographic indicators include the proportion of people aged 65 and above (elder), sex ratio with female as 100 (female), proportion of people with a college degree or above (educated) and proportion of the urban population (urban). Economic indicator refers to provincial per capita GDP in 2021 (pgdp).

Predictor variables

Predictor variables employ the setting of a mediating variable that responds to the mediating role between policy implementation and outcome variables. The medical insurance coverage rate (insurance_coverage) is used, calculated as the ratio of the actual number of insured individuals to the total population. This variable is chosen because it directly reflects the accessibility of the insurance system and is a core indicator of the sustainability of medical insurance, as highlighted in studies on healthcare system performance.31

Statistical analysis

Propensity score matching - staggered difference-in-differences model with time-varying variables

In the statistical analysis, a staggered difference-in-differences (staggered DID) model was used to assess the impact of the policy on the outcome variables, which is applicable to the implementation of multitemporal policies. The parallel trend test verified the key assumptions of the DID model: regression results for the policy lead/lag term showed no significant difference (p>0.1) between the treatment and control groups before policy implementation, confirming the parallel trend assumption (see figure 1).

The propensity score matching (PSM) method was used to match the treatment and control groups: the treatment group consisted of provinces that had implemented an insurance integration policy before 2016, and the control group consisted of provinces that had not implemented an insurance integration policy. PSM are implemented using Stata 17.0 and the psmatch2 command using nearest-neighbour matching (1:1 ratio) with a calliper of 0.05 to balance covariates between treatment and control groups. After matching, standardised deviations for all covariates were less than 10% and not significantly different, indicating that baseline characteristics were effectively balanced (see Tables A2 and A3). Figure 2 demonstrated that practically all covariates were closer to 0 after matching, indicating the validity of PSM application. Figure 3 showed propensity score density plots before and after matching on all nine covariates.

Based on the results of the Hausman test and the likelihood ratio (LR) test, a two-way fixed-effects model was chosen to estimate the outcome variables (see online supplemental table A4), with model specifications including immediate policy effects (year of implementation), 1-year lagged effects and 2-year lagged effects. The model was constructed as equation (1):

 ln⁡(outcome)it=β0+β1(Treati×Postt=0)+β2(Treati×Postt=1) +β3(Treati×Postt=2)+β4Zit+λt+μi+εit (1)

where ln⁡outcomeitrepresents the outcome variable of province i in year t; Treati is a dummy variable (1 indicates that province i is in the treatment group; 0 indicates the control group). Postt=0, Postt=1 and Postt=2 are time dummies for the policy implementation year, 1 year after implementation (lag1) and 2 years after implementation (lag2), respectively (taking 1 if t=T, t=T+1 and t=T+2, where T is the policy start year; 0 otherwise). Zit denotes control variables, λt and μi are year and province fixed effects, respectively, and εi,t is the error term. In this framework, β1 captures the policy’s immediate effect in the implementation year, β2 the lag 1 effect (1 year later) and β2 the lag 2 effect (2 years later).

Robustness tests

Robustness tests included a placebo test, subgroup analysis and influencing mechanism analysis. The placebo test assessed the stability of the results by randomly sampling the policy dummy variable 500 times; most of the randomised samples showed significant deviations from the baseline model coefficients, suggesting that the estimates were robust (see online supplemental figure A1).

Subgroup analyses were conducted to explore heterogeneous effects, since previous studies have shown that fiscal capacity affects the effectiveness of insurance policies.34 Provinces were categorised into three subgroups based on GDP per capita: above 100 000 yuan, between 60 000 and 100 000 yuan, and below 60 000 yuan (RMB 1 ≈ US$0.1485 at 2022 exchange rates).

Influencing mechanism analysis explored the internal pathways of policy impact by examining a mediation framework in which the sustainability of the medical insurance system (as measured by medical insurance coverage, insurance_coverage) served as the mediating variable. Specifically, we tested the hypothesised pathway that policy affected healthcare outcomes by influencing medical insurance coverage. To verify the existence of the mediating effect, the Sobel test and Bootstrap test (5000 replications) were used. The results showed that both tests were statistically significant, indicating that policy partially affects healthcare outcomes through the mediating effect of medical insurance coverage.

Patient and public involvement statement

Patients or the public WERE NOT involved in the design, or conduct, or reporting, or dissemination plans of our research.

Software

All statistical analyses are performed using Stata 17.0.

Influencing mechanism and mediation analysis

Tables A5 and 4 present the results of the mechanistic analyses. Online supplemental table A5 analyses the effect of policy on medical insurance coverage (insurance_coverage). The policy effect with a 2-year lag (policy_implemented_lag2) has a significant positive impact on insurance_coverage (coefficient=9.943, 95% CI (2.064, 17.822), **p<0.05), suggesting that medical insurance coverage increases significantly after 2 years of policy implementation.

Table 4 further tests whether insurance_coverage mediates the effect of the policy on benefit levels (lnpb, lnpbu, lnpbr). For the 1-year lagged policy effect, policy_implemented_lag1 significantly increases insurance_coverage (coefficient=12.356, 95% CI (6.901, 17.811), ***p<0.01), suggesting that coverage rises 1 year after the policy is implemented. insurance_coverage has a significant negative effect on all benefit levels: overall per capita benefit (lnpb: coefficient=−0.011, 95% CI (−0.013, –0.009), ***p<0.01), per capita benefit for urban residents (lnpbu: coefficient = −0.009, 95% CI (−0.011, –0.007), ***p<0.01) and per capita benefit for rural residents (lnpbr: coefficient=−0.011, 95% CI (−0.013, –0.009), ***p<0.01). This suggests that a policy with a 1-year lag increases coverage, which in turn reduces the benefit levels.

Discussion

Statement of principal findings

This study used the PSM-DID method to assess the impact of China’s medical insurance integration policy on urban-rural benefit disparities across 31 provinces (2006–2021). The main findings are as follows.

Overall, the policy implementation reduced the per capita benefit of urban residents (coefficient=−0.373**). Specifically, the policy had a positive effect on rural welfare with a 1-year lag in provinces with a GDP per capita of more than 100 000 yuan (coefficient=0.360**). In provinces with GDP per capita of 60 000 to 100 000 yuan, the policy immediately increased rural benefits (coefficient=0.401***). And in provinces with GDP per capita less than 60 000 yuan, the policy immediately reduces overall benefits and rural benefits (coefficient=−0.409**, −0.457**), but there was a recovery effect of the rural benefits with a 2-year lag (coefficient=0.413**).

In addition, it should be added that the impact of the policy is partly influenced by medical insurance coverage. The effect of the policy with a 1-year lag significantly increased medical insurance coverage (coefficient=12.356***), but the increase in medical insurance coverage is associated with a decrease in the level of benefits (lnpb: −0.011***, lnpbu: −0.009***, lnpbr: −0.011***), which suggests that there is a trade-off between expanding coverage and benefits in the short term.

Strengths and limitations

This study used provincial panel data (2006–2021) and a quasi-natural experiment to mitigate endogeneity, and used PSM-DID and two-way fixed effects to address sample selection bias. Multiple robustness tests (placebo, subgroup analyses and influencing mechanism analyses) improved the reliability of the results.

The study has limitations. Risk pools for integrated insurance programmes vary across provinces, and there are differences in local policy implementation strategies and resource allocation that may not be fully captured by provincial data. Besides, the mediation analysis used only medical insurance coverage as the mechanism variable and does not incorporate other potential pathways, which may limit the comprehensiveness of the mechanism explanation. Last but not least, data were derived from administrative statistics and may be affected by statistical methods and missing values, which may affect the accuracy of the findings.

Interpretation within the context of the wider literature

Our findings are consistent with and extend the existing literature on healthcare integration in China and globally. First, consistent with the WHO’s call for universal healthcare coverage1 2 and cross-country evidence (eg, Universal Coverage Scheme in Thailand, Seguro Popular in Mexico9 35 36), our study confirms that insurance integration reduces disparities in middle-income regions. Specifically, provinces with GDP per capita of 60 000–100 000 yuan show immediate improvements in rural benefits. This is in line with previous studies on China’s URRMI, which highlight the potential of integration to narrow the urban-rural gap by unifying fund pools.18 35 For example, Su et al found that integration improves rural healthcare utilisation, a pattern we replicate in middle-income provinces where fiscal capacity supports a balanced resource allocation.8

However, our study differs from those that argue that integration leads to generalised rural benefits.5 22 He (2023) argues that integration reduces healthcare disparities through standardised benefits, but our findings suggest that this is not universal: rural benefits are initially reduced in low-income provinces.5 This divergence reflects a Rawlsian conception of the social determinants of health—as Lagomarsino et al point out, insurance reforms in developing countries often struggle to strike a balance between coverage and equity without adequate fiscal support.31 Low-income provinces in China, like their counterparts in Brazil or Cuba,8 36 face constraints in maintaining benefit levels while expanding coverage, leading to short-term rural losses.

Our focus on regional economic heterogeneity also fills gaps in existing Chinese studies. Ren et a and Li et al examined integration pilot programmes but did not explore economic gradients.19 28 Our study shows that high-income provinces (eg, coastal regions) exhibit lagged positive effects on rural benefits, possibly because they are able to absorb initial adjustment costs, which is consistent with Yang et al’s study, who found that fiscal capacity moderates integration effects.24 In contrast, middle-income provinces (eg, central China) saw immediate rural gains, supporting Wang et al’s observation that integration works best in moderately resource-constrained regions.17

Notably, our findings on the coverage-benefit trade-off, that is, higher medical coverage reduces per capita benefits, complement those of Zhou et al, who note that universal healthcare in China sometimes dilutes benefit intensity.25 This trade-off is temporary in high-income provinces but persists in low-income provinces, echoing Meng et al’s warning that consolidation without targeted subsidies may widen disparities.15

Implications for policy, practice, and research

For low-income provinces, short-term fiscal transfers are needed to offset initial reductions in rural benefits. For high-income provinces, policymakers should leverage the lagged effect by optimising post-integration fund management. Local governments should formulate consolidation policies based on economic conditions. For example, middle-income provinces could prioritise immediate rural benefit expansion, while low-income provinces could gradually implement the reform through transitional subsidies. Future studies should use municipal/county-level data to capture local heterogeneity and explore the policy implementation intensity, for instance, how the fund pooling scale affects the distribution of benefits.

Conclusions

China’s medical insurance integration policy has shown different impacts on narrowing urban-rural benefit disparities, with its effectiveness closely related to the level of regional economic development. Mechanistically, the policy has promoted the expansion of medical insurance coverage, but short-term improvement in coverage has been accompanied by a trade-off with per capita benefit levels, which reflects the challenge of balancing the breadth of coverage and the intensity of benefits at the initial stage of integration.

These findings suggest that medical insurance integration is not a one-size-fits-all solution for achieving urban-rural equity. Its success depends on matching policy design with local economic capacity: middle-income regions can leverage integration to directly improve rural benefits; high-income regions need to optimise post-implementation fund management to amplify lagged effects; and low-income regions need targeted fiscal support to mitigate the initial negative impacts on rural residents.

This study highlights that, for developing countries pursuing universal health coverage, insurance integration must be combined with region-specific strategies, especially fiscal transfers and phased implementation in less developed regions, to avoid widening disparities. Ultimately, achieving spatial equity in medical insurance systems requires not only institutional integration but also adaptive support based on local conditions.
