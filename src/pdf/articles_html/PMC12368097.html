Abstract

In deregulated power markets (DPMs), transmission-line congestion has become more severe and frequent than in traditional power systems. This congestion hinders electricity markets from operating in normal competitive equilibrium. The independent system operator (ISO) is responsible for implementing appropriate measures to mitigate congestion and ensure the proper functioning of the power market. This study utilized generation rescheduling (GR) to address congestion in spot or day-ahead power markets. Rapid alleviation of congestion is essential to prevent tripping of overloaded lines. Owing to their computational inefficiency, evolutionary algorithms (EAs) are ineffective for real-time congestion management, necessitating hybrid models to deliver rapid solutions. This paper proposes a hybrid deep neural network (DNN)-based congestion management (CM) approach for real-time congestion control and reduced computation time. The proposed CM system consists of three cascaded long short-term memory (LSTM) DNNs that operate sequentially. These LSTM modules predict congestion status, violated power, and adjusted active power for rescheduling generation. The LSTM-DNN was trained using data from a grey wolf optimization (GWO). This method provides a rapid solution with approximately 98% accuracy for managing congestion in the spot power market. The suggested approach was evaluated on an IEEE 30 bus system and demonstrated to be highly effective.

Introduction

Deregulation of the power market has led to increased competition and the need for effective CM strategies. Deregulated power systems are electricity markets that have undergone liberalization and restructuring, allowing multiple independent entities to generate, transmit, and distribute electricity. This shift from traditional regulated monopolies to competitive markets aims to promote efficiency, encourage innovation, and provide consumers with more choices. Transmission congestion occurs when the system is incapable to accommodate all the essential transactions, resulting in locational price disparities and reliability issues. In deregulated power markets, where transmission, distribution, and generation are unbundled, the CM aligns with market principles, emphasizing competition. There are two primary categories of CM: preventive versus corrective, and centralized versus decentralized. Preventive methods involve proactive measures during scheduling, such as security-constrained economic dispatches. In contrast, corrective methods entail real-time adjustments, including generator redispatch and load shedding. In a centralized system, the system operator (SO) directs actions, whereas in a decentralized system, market participants respond to price signals, as seen in demand response programs. In DPM, CM within the day-ahead market (DAM) emphasizes preventive planning by utilizing forecasts to schedule generation and demand 24 h in advance. Tools such as security-constrained unit commitment (SCUC) and locational marginal pricing (LMP) are used to optimize costs while preventing transmission overloads, with financial transmission rights (FTRs) enabling participants to hedge against congestion risk. Conversely, real-time market (RTM) CM addresses the immediate grid stress resulting from forecasting errors or sudden outages. The real-time LMP is updated every 5–15 min, prompting corrective actions such as demand response, generator redispatch, or dynamic line rating (DLR) to maintain grid balance. While the DAM prioritizes cost efficiency through proactive measures, the RTM depends on rapid and often more expensive adjustments to ensure reliability. Collectively, they facilitate efficient grid operation: the DAM establishes a baseline and the RTM refines it, minimizing congestion costs while preserving stability.

Evolutionary algorithms (EAs), including genetic algorithms (GA) and particle swarm optimization (PSO), are increasingly employed in day-ahead CM within power markets to address complex nonlinear optimization challenges. These algorithms optimize the generation schedules and unit commitment while applying transmission constraints, thereby ensuring cost-effective and safe grid operations. By incorporating probabilistic forecasts of renewable generation and demand uncertainties, EAs can design robust schedules that proactively mitigate congestion risks associated with wind or solar variability. They are particularly adept at solving mixed-integer problems, such as the activation or deactivation of generators, and facilitate multi-objective trade-offs among cost, emissions, and reliability. Additionally, EAs optimize grid topology, such as line switching, to reroute power flows and alleviate congestion. Although computational demands and parameter sensitivity present challenges, their flexibility in managing real-world complexities, such as nonconvex cost curves and policy-driven constraints, render them valuable for modern grids. Advances in hybrid models (e.g., combining EAs with machine learning) and parallel computing have enhanced their scalability, positioning evolutionary algorithms as critical tools for future power systems characterized by high renewable penetration and dynamic market demands.

Effective CM is crucial to ensure smooth operation of the power system and maximize its overall efficiency. In a deregulated power system, CM is typically achieved through market mechanisms and economic principles that are necessary to control congestion in the evolving day-ahead hybrid electric market, where bid-based generation rescheduling (GR) is the most prominent1. According to the GR, power suppliers must adjust their active power generation to improve flow and reduce congestion2. Several Evolutionary Algorithms (EA) have been proposed in the literature for generation rescheduling. Panigrahi BK and Pandi VR3 proposed an adaptive bacterial foraging algorithm using Nelder-Mead for congestion mitigation. Andrea Ewerszumrode et al.4 presents an iterative methodology that separates topology optimization from redispatch and phase shift transformer optimization to effectively manage congestion. Yamazaki et al.5 propose an innovative CM planning method that integrates data from the Meso-scale Ensemble Prediction System (MEPS) and considers weather-related uncertainties. M. Girod et al.6 introduced bid filtering for CM utilizing reinforcement learning. Kausik Paul et al. have been proposed A Novel Improved Crow Search based Algorithm to reduce congestion in transmission lines7, Verma et al. proposed the Ant lion optimizer for CM8, and the satin bowerbird optimization approach9, have been proposed. To increase PSO performance, hybrid Nelder–Mead–fuzzy adaptive PSO10, hybridization of fuzzy with GSA and PSO11, fitness distance ratio, and fuzzy adaptive PSO12 have been proposed for GR. For most EA approaches, global best solutions are selected, whereas local best solutions are discarded during the exploitation phase, which may result in inaccurate results13.

Seyyedi et al.14 introduced a scheduling model for distribution systems integrated with microgrids, encompassing consumption loads, photovoltaic (PV) panels, and behind-the-meter energy storage systems. In light of the presence of uncertain parameters, such as the actual power generation of photovoltaic (PV) panels and the real and reactive power of loads, a robust optimization method has been developed to effectively model these uncertainties. Seyyedi et al.15 proposed the implementation of a robust, optimized spatiotemporal load management strategy for data centers to mitigate renewable curtailment. The literature16 examined a bi-level optimization problem aimed at determining the optimal location and size of flexible renewable virtual power plants (FRVPPs) within active distribution networks (ADNs). Debbarma et al.17 introduced an innovative Cheetah Optimizer to address the problems of CM. To simplify the problem into a single-objective optimization, the weighted sum approach was used instead of a multi-objective optimization. This approach was applied to resolve multiple instances of congestion and tested on an IEEE 30 bus system.

EA methods evaluate the fitness function repeatedly, which slows it down. The entire optimal power flow (OPF) program must be completed again for each requested transaction and load variation, resulting in a delayed response. Deep neural networks (DNNs) address these challenges by learning grid patterns from historical data in order to predict congestion risks and approximate optimal solutions. For example, predictive models utilize DNNs to forecast line flows and locational marginal prices (LMPs) by incorporating weather, demand, and generation data, thereby enabling proactive adjustments. Surrogate optimal power flow (OPF) models employ DNNs to replicate optimal power flow solutions within milliseconds, thereby circumventing iterative computations. Reinforcement learning (RL) agents develop adaptive redispatch strategies, balancing costs, and reliability under conditions of uncertainty. Hybrid systems leverage DNNs to prescreen feasible regions for traditional solvers, thereby accelerating convergence.

DNNs facilitate more rapid data-driven CM, particularly in electrical grids with significant renewable energy integration18,19. A multiple-input deep neural network (DNN) is an example of a deep learning-based application that can forecast solar power20. Day-ahead solar power forecasting using CNN-based deep learning21. Short-term PV power generation can be predicted using a DNN with LSTM and time-series approaches22. To forecast short-term wind speeds, CNN-LSTM was used23. Combining GWO with LSTM Deep Neural Networks (LSTM-DNN) offers a transformative approach to real-time CM in power grids. The LSTM-DNN excels in forecasting congestion risks by analyzing temporal patterns in load, renewable generation, and grid conditions, providing accurate short-term predictions of line flows and locational marginal prices (LMPs).

To train an LSTM-DNN using GWO generated CM data, the hybrid framework first simulates diverse scenarios (e.g., varying load patterns and line outages) and employs GWO to solve security-constrained optimization problems for each case. GWO computes cost-minimal generation rescheduling strategies under N−1 contingency constraints, ensuring feasible and reliable solutions. These optimal decisions, such as generator setpoints, load curtailment, or topology adjustments, are paired with their corresponding grid conditions (load, outages, and generation profiles) to form a labeled dataset. The LSTM was then trained on this dataset to learn the nonlinear relationship between real-time grid states (inputs) and optimal congestion management actions (outputs). Once trained, LSTM bypasses iterative GWO computations, enabling real-time prediction of rescheduling decisions for new, unseen scenarios. For example, during a sudden line outage, the LSTM leverages patterns from GWO-optimized historical data to rapidly prescribe cost-effective N-1 contingency redispatch actions. This approach combines GWO’s robust optimization with LSTM’s fast inference, addressing scalability and latency challenges, while ensuring reliability. Testing on IEEE bus systems showed 98% accuracy in replicating GWO decisions with 100x faster response times, making it ideal for dynamic grids.

Research gaps

Some of the research gaps identified from the literature survey are as follows.

  1. N-1 contingency ignorance: Most ML/RL methods lack embedded security constraints.

  2. Real-time scalability: Traditional methods are extremely slow for dynamic grids.

  3. Hybrid model limitations: Existing hybrids exclude critical-line outages and contingency analysis.

  4. Cost-reliability trade-offs: No framework minimizes congestion costs while ensuring N-1 compliance in real time.

This study addresses power system congestion through a hybrid framework that integrates cascaded LSTM-based deep neural networks. Multiple load scenarios, including line outage contingencies, were evaluated to identify the congestion-prone patterns. The Grey Wolf Optimizer (GWO) technique was employed to detect the adjusted power generation for the generation rescheduling CM. These data-driven insights were then used to train the LSTM networks, enabling a rapid real-time congestion resolution. By prioritizing computational speed, the hybrid model overcomes the latency limitations of traditional evolutionary algorithms, ensuring the timely and efficient management of dynamic grid constraints.

Contributions of the work

The proposed hybrid model comprises three cascaded LSTM DNNs. This cascaded approach ensures a systematic solution: LSTM1 identifies risks such as congested or non-congested load scenarios; LSTM2 assesses the severity, including line overloading; and LSTM3 recommends cost-effective corrective actions, such as congestion management costs. By training each LSTM module on both historical and perturbed data, the framework effectively captured the nonlinear relationships between load variability, line violations, and generation adjustments. This enables proactive congestion mitigation in real-time grids with a high renewable energy penetration.

Grey Wolf optimization (GWO)

In 2014, Mirjali30 introduced the GWO algorithm, a contemporary population-based metaheuristic optimization method. This approach simulates the hunting strategies of grey wolf pack s within multidimensional search spaces. Notably, grey wolves exhibit a distinct social hierarchy, where the group is led by alpha wolves, which can be either male or female. Within this structured system, wolves assume specialized roles such as scouts, caretakers, and hunters, contributing collaboratively to tasks like surveillance, exploration, and prey capture (Fig. 1).

Mathematical model of algorithm

In the GWO, the behavior of grey wolves, including tracking, encircling, and attacking prey, is modeled mathematically to replicate their hunting strategies and social hierarchy.

Social hierarchy

In the mathematical modeling of wolves’ social hierarchy, the authors posit that the alpha solution represents the optimal choice. Consequently, the second and third most effective results are found as beta and delta, respectively. Within the GWO algorithm, hunting behavior is driven by the delta, beta, and alpha wolves. The omega wolves follow the lead of these three wolves.

The GWO is extremely effective for CM due to its gradient-free optimization approach, allowing it to competently solve non-smooth and discontinuous objective functions. It effectively manages inequality constraints, such as transmission line limits and voltage constraints using feasibility-based techniques. Unlike other metaheuristic approaches like PSO and GA, GWO requires fewer tuning parameters primarily population size and iteration count while maintaining a strong balance between exploration and exploitation. This is accomplished through its unique social hierarchy structure using alpha, beta, delta, and omega wolves, which accelerates convergence. Additionally, GWO dynamically adjusts search positions to avoid local optima, outperforming traditional optimization methods in adaptability and solution quality.

Metaheuristic algorithms, such as the GWO, are employed despite concerns regarding computational time due to the complexities inherent in real-time congestion management. This process involves non-convex, non-linear alternating current (AC) power flow constraints, mixed-integer decisions (e.g., unit commitment), and stochastic N-1 contingency requirements, which traditional convex or linear solvers are unable to efficiently address. Although standalone metaheuristics may exhibit slower performance, the proposed hybrid GWO- LSTM framework effectively mitigates delays. The LSTM component predicts congestion hotspots and feasible solutions within milliseconds, thereby directing the GWO to concentrate on critical regions and reducing the dimensionality of the search space. This approach achieves a balance between near-optimality (1–3% gap) and real-time responsiveness (less than 30 s), which is crucial for power grids where exact solvers, such as Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP), fail to converge rapidly. Consequently, the GWO’s capability to navigate complex, non-differentiable problem landscapes, in conjunction with the LSTM’s acceleration, renders it a justified choice for reliability-aware and cost-effective congestion management.

Long short-term memory (LSTM) deep neural networks

An artificial neural network (ANN) characterized by multiple hidden layers situated between the input and output layers is termed a deep neural network (DNN). DNNs possess the capability to represent intricate nonlinear interactions, similar to shallow ANNs. LSTM is an extension of RNN that addresses RNN flaws by increasing the interactions within each module. Long-term reliance was prevented by the introduction of LSTM. Research methodology

To achieve congestion management (CM) goals, a cascaded LSTM framework was proposed to address different load conditions and N-1 contingencies. Initially, diverse loading scenarios are generated by perturbing the active and reactive power at each load bus and simulating bilateral/multilateral transactions, forming tP training patterns. Figure 4 illustrates the process of generating various test-loading scenarios. Figure 5 depicts the systematic stages of the LSTM DNN used to predict the CM.

The normalized active and reactive power values of all loads served as input features for a Deep Neural Network (DNN), ensuring uniform scaling to prevent data suppression. LSTM1operates as a binary classifier, predicting congestion occurrence (“0” for non-congested, “1” for congested) based on load patterns. If congestion is detected, LSTM2 estimates the severity by quantifying the line overload magnitudes for the critical transmission lines. Finally, LSTM3 predicts the optimal generator rescheduling values (active power adjustments) to alleviate congestion while minimizing operational costs, such as redispatch expenses or penalty fees.

This cascaded approach ensures a systematic solution: LSTM1 flags risks, LSTM2 diagnoses the severity, and LSTM3 prescribes cost-aware corrective actions. By training each LSTM module on historical and perturbed data, the framework captures the nonlinear relationships between load variability, line violations, and generation adjustments, enabling proactive congestion mitigation in real-time grids with high renewable penetration.

Evaluation and results

IEEE 30 bus system

The proposed method tested on the IEEE 30 Bus System consists of six generators, twenty-one loads, and forty-one branches. The incremental and decremental generator bids for CM are listed in Table A in the Online Appendix. Deep neural networks (DNN) consider the input variables as the real and reactive powers of all loads (PDi; i = 1, 2…, 21 and QDi; i = 1, 2,…, 21). A string of six-bit binary digits represents the out-of-service lines. As a result, the six bits, along with the real and reactive powers of the loads, are inputs for each deep neural network. 48 input variables (neurons) were necessary for LSTM1, LSTM 2, and LSTM 3. Six bits represent the binary equivalent of an out-of-service line, 21 variables reflect the real load power at all load buses, and 21 variables represent the reactive load power at all buses. The output of DNN1 is either ‘1’ (CLS) or ‘0’ (NCLS). Consequently, there is only one output neuron in the DNN1 module. The total number of output neurons for DNN2 is nine (i.e., the amount of overload in each of the nine lines), whereas the total number of output neurons for LSTM3 is six (Pgi; i = 1, 2,…,6).

The LSTM-DNN was trained independently using Adam’s learning techniques (optimizers). Based on the testing and training results, the following hidden layer configurations were chosen for LSTM1: two hidden layers with 30 -20 neurons. For LSTM 2, there were three hidden layers with 40-30-25 neurons, and for LSTM3, there were three hidden layers with 38-35-20 neurons in each layer. The total number of epochs was as 1000. A total of 2000 load patterns were randomly created with and without line outages. A total of 1780 load patterns caused line overloads, and 220 load patterns did not cause an overload. 80% of the total load patterns, or 1600, were chosen randomly for training, whereas the remaining 20% of load patterns, or 400, were used for testing purposes. Figure 6 depicts the fluctuations in the MSE on a log scale for epochs throughout the training process.

Congestion management analysis using LSTM models

A total of 2000 load patterns, with and without line outages, were analyzed for congestion management using Newton-Raphson power flow analysis. Among them, 1780 patterns showed transmission overloads, indicating congestion, whereas the remaining 220 were uncongested. For congested cases, the adjusted generation values to mitigate congestion were determined using the Grey Wolf Optimization (GWO) technique, minimizing the congestion cost. This dataset was used to train three LSTM models: LSTM1 for congestion status prediction, LSTM2 for power violation detection, and LSTM3 for adjusted power prediction. 80% of the data were used for training, and 20% for testing.

On testing with 400 patterns, LSTM1 correctly predicted 396 cases (congested and uncongested), achieving an accuracy of 99%. LSTM2 correctly identified 392 patterns, yielding a 98% accuracy in predicting power violations. The LSTM3 accurately predicted the adjusted power for 386 patterns. The study examines two standard higher bus test systems they are modified IEEE 57 bus system and IEEE 118 bus systems and the test cases are considered from the literature31,32. The modified IEEE 57-bus network comprising 50 load buses, 7 generator buses, and 80 transmission lines, and the larger IEEE 118-bus system with 54 generators, 64 load buses, and 186 branches.

In the training of the LSTM model, 2,500 load patterns were derived from modified IEEE bus systems, of which 1,906 were identified as overloaded. For LSTM1, 80% of the total load patterns were allocated for training, while the remaining 20% were reserved for testing. Similarly, among the 1,906 overloaded load patterns, 80% were used for training and 20% for testing in LSTM2 and LSTM3. Following the training phase, the model’s efficacy was evaluated using unseen test cases G and H on the modified IEEE 57 bus system. For the LSTM model, 80% of these load patterns were used for training and 20% for testing. LSTM1 utilized all load patterns, whereas LSTM2 and LSTM3 focused on the overloaded patterns. Advantage

  • The hybrid LSTM-GWO model facilitates rapid, sub-second predictions for congestion risks and optimal grid actions, thereby reducing the reliance on slow, iterative optimization processes.

  • It improves forecast accuracy by effectively capturing temporal patterns in renewable generation and load variability, while dynamically adapting to real-time events such as outages or demand spikes.

  • By updating decisions every 5–15 min, the model ensures real-time responsiveness and N-1 contingency compliance through training.

  • Additionally, the model decreases congestion costs through proactive rescheduling and demonstrates effective scalability to new scenarios, thereby minimizing retraining requirements.

Limitations

  • The hybrid LSTM-GWO model relies heavily on high-quality historical data, and its performance can degrade with incomplete or noisy inputs.

  • It faces challenges in generalizing to extreme or unmodeled scenarios, such as simultaneous N-2 outages.

  • Additionally, training LSTM on large datasets generated by GWO is computationally intensive, often requiring substantial GPU or cloud resources.

Conclusion

The proposed hybrid Deep Neural Network (DNN) Congestion Management (CM) method demonstrates efficacy in real-time emergencies, necessitating rapid solutions for system congestion control. Validation of the proposed method was conducted using an IEEE 30, modified IEEE 57 and IEEE 118 bus systems. The Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) responses were compared with the Grey Wolf Optimizer (GWO) evolutionary optimization CM. The results obtained were closely aligned with those derived from the GWO approach. The prediction values of three LSTMs exhibited an error margin below 2%, achieving 98% accuracy in untrained test cases. This method is well-suited for scenarios with and without line outages and facilitates fast computations in large systems. Based on the results obtained, the proposed method is deemed appropriate for managing congestion in power markets during emergencies.
