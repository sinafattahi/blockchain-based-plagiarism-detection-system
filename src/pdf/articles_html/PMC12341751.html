Abstract

Objectives:

Attrition from pediatric chronic pain clinics limits patients from receiving maximum benefit from evidence-based interventions, with likely persistence of chronic pain and﻿ functional and psychosocial impairments. We aimed to determine attrition prevalence from interdisciplinary pediatric outpatient chronic pain clinics and identify any associated patient and caregiver factors.

Methods:

A retrospective analysis was performed of longitudinal data from patients and caregivers attending 10 interdisciplinary pediatric outpatient chronic pain clinics in Australia or New Zealand contributing to the Paediatric electronic Persistent Pain Outcomes Collaboration (PaedePPOC) database. Data were retrieved for patient attrition, patient, and caregiver factors at treatment commencement and subsequent documentation of the episode end for treatment completers and noncompleters. Statistical associations with patient attrition were assessed using logistic regression analyses.

Results:

The prevalence of patient attrition was 19.3% in the PaedePPOC cohort (N=1051), occurring steadily over 11 months. Patients ending their treatment prematurely presented with longer pain duration (>12 mo) [χ2(1, n=879)=8.23, P=0.004] than treatment completers. In the regression model, only older patient age (P=0.010) and higher caregiver psychosocial burden (P=0.025) at treatment commencement were associated with attrition. While pain intensity, patient physical and psychosocial functioning scores, and caregiver partner status were not.

Discussion:

One in 5 patients commencing an interdisciplinary pediatric outpatient chronic pain clinic intervention did not complete it. Identifying patients at preintervention screening with longer pain duration, older in age, and from families with greater reported caregiver burden and providing tailored supports may minimize attrition, therefore optimizing efficiency of service provision.

Key Words: patient attrition, health care utilization, chronic pain, youth, pain clinics

Chronic pain, defined as persistent or recurring pain lasting 3 or more months,1 affects 1 in 5 youth.2 Pediatric chronic pain is associated with markedly impaired quality of life, and with physical and psychosocial impacts relative to healthy peers.3–7 Caregivers of youth experiencing chronic pain also report elevated emotional distress8,9 and socioeconomic burden.10–13 Unmanaged pediatric chronic pain may have substantial long-term consequences including persistent pain and socioeconomic disparities.14–18 Given these burdens, effective treatment is crucial. Interdisciplinary pain services (both inpatient and outpatient), characterized by coordinated teamwork between multiple health disciplines, have been effective in decreasing functional disability, pain severity, and depression.19–23 However, availability of this type of service is limited,24 and outpatient pediatric interdisciplinary pain services often have considerable wait times.25,26 Premature withdrawal from these valuable but limited services reflects inefficient resource utilization.3

Treatment attrition may be defined as a patient withdrawing from a service or discontinuing an intervention before the clinician and patient agree that the treatment is complete. The implications of attrition are substantial, limiting benefits of care, functional improvement, and potentially exacerbating health-related disparities.27,28 Several related terms appear in the literature. ‘Drop-out’ has been used,3,29 but may be less preferable due to potential negative connotations. ‘Treatment noncompletion’ has also been used, typically specifying a minimum number of appointments attended as criteria for completion versus noncompletion.30

A recent review of studies reporting attrition rates in pediatric outpatient chronic pain interventions found that attrition from interdisciplinary interventions ranged from 8% to 35%.31 There was considerable variability in the patient samples, intervention types, and how attrition was operationalized. Moreover, this literature is limited in identifying factors associated with patient attrition in this context. One relatively small study found that patients who completed a pediatric cognitive-behavioal treatment intervention for pain management (n=40) were comparable to noncompleters (n=44) on age, sex, race, ethnicity, insurance status, and comorbid anxiety levels.32 A second study found that completers of a manualized psychosocial intervention for adolescents with chronic pain (n=41) were comparable to noncompleters (n=25) on race, family composition, parental marital status, mother’s occupation, and family tensions/stressors.3 Those who dropped out had more comorbid unexplained painful and fatiguing conditions, as well as differences in coping style, school functioning, and sleep relative to treatment completers.3 Associations of attrition with patient pain intensity, disability, and pain duration have been explored in some adult studies,33 but not pediatric; caregiver factors such as catastrophizing, burden, and work capacity have not previously been investigated.

Thus, the current study had 3 primary aims. First, we sought to determine the prevalence and temporal patterns of attrition from a large, binational multicenter database of clinical interdisciplinary pediatric outpatient chronic pain clinics. Second, we assessed differences in reported sociodemographic, patient and caregiver characteristics (at treatment commencement) between the attrition group and treatment completers. Third, we aimed to determine the association of patient (eg, age, various pain variables, physical and psychosocial functioning) and caregiver factors (eg, partner status, caregiver burden, and work productivity) at treatment commencement with subsequent patient attrition versus completion status. The available data were predetermined by the standardized questionnaires used in the PaedePPOC database. There were no a priori hypotheses regarding impact of patient factors and caregiver factors on attrition from a chronic pediatric pain clinic.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

This was a retrospective analysis of longitudinal data entered in an Australian and New Zealand multicentre Paediatric electronic Persistent Pain Outcomes Collaboration (PaedePPOC) database.34,35 This database consists of predetermined standardized questionnaires completed by patients and caregivers attending 10 specialist chronic pediatric outpatient pain clinics across Australia and New Zealand.26 These clinics are interdisciplinary, with professionals from several disciplines working interdependently in the same setting, interacting both formally and informally. They comprise of a minimum of 4 disciplines, commonly pain physician or pediatrician, physical therapy, and psychology, some with child and adolescent psychiatry, occupational therapy, clinical nurse specialist, social work, music therapy, or a dietician. Ethics approval was granted by Sydney Children’s Hospitals Network Human Research Ethics Committee (2022/ETH02682). Patients/caregivers are made aware when completing the PaedePPOC questionnaires that their data will be deidentified and potentially used for future ethically approved research. Consequently, patient/caregiver consent was waived for this study.

Cohort

The inclusion criteria for this study were patients who (1) attended a chronic pain clinic contributing to PaedePPOC data collection for their first episode of care, (2) were referred to the clinic between 1 Jan 2014 and 31 Dec 2022, (3) were aged 18 years and younger, (4) the PaedePPOC records included a valid episode end date with a coded reason for treatment episode end (data entered by treating team clinicians either physician, nurse specialist or allied health professional), and (5) the patient completed treatment, discontinued by choice, or were lost to contact/not to follow up. An episode of care was defined as a continuous period of care for a patient in one pain management clinic.36

Patients were excluded from the study database if the: (1) patient’s pain duration was less than 3 months at the time of initial questionnaire completion, (2) data entry was for a second or subsequent clinical episode at a chronic pain clinic contributing to the PaedePPOC database to avoid duplicate entry, (3) pain was cancer related, (4) treatment modality was group intervention only, (5) patient was assessed at the pain clinic but treatment was provided remotely by an external provider, and (6) patient’s treatment episode duration exceeded 365 days—these patients were excluded from the attrition group, as this duration implied treatment was received and an incorrect end code entry was possible.

Data Extraction

Data extraction from the PaedePPOC database included patient and caregiver information collected before treatment commencement, treatment episode start and end dates (to calculate treatment episode duration), and reason for end of treatment episode coded by clinicians as one of the 7 PaedePPOC predetermined codes.36 Patients were categorized as treatment completers when the treatment episode end code was 1 of 2 codes of ‘treatment complete—self-management referral to primary care’ or ‘active treatment complete—ongoing review’. Patients were categorized in the attrition group when the treatment episode end was coded as 1 of 2 codes of ‘patient discontinued by choice’ or ‘lost to contact not to follow up’. End-of-treatment episode codes were entered by a clinician at the time of closing an episode of care.

Descriptive data were extracted from the questionnaire and consisted of youth/family demographics (age, sex, country of birth, socioeconomic status); caregiver characteristics of the caregiver completing the questionnaire (relationship to the patient, sex, partner status, employment status); patient clinical-status variables (presence of co-morbidities or disabilities, health service utilization, frequency of medication use); and pain-related variables reported by the patient and caregiver (duration, intensity, cause, frequency, sites of pain).

Data were collected using the following validated assessment tools:

Index of Relative Socioeconomic Disadvantage (IRSD)

The IRSD is an Australian socioeconomic index based upon postal code (zip code) providing information about socioeconomic disadvantage relative to other residential areas.37 The measure identifies socioeconomic deciles, with a lower decile denoting greater disadvantage.37

Pain Chart

The Pain Chart is a human body schematic used as a standardized assessment tool for location of and number of pain sites.38

Modified Brief Pain Inventory (BPI)

The Modified BPI assesses the youth’s pain intensity using 0 to 10 ratings.39 The current study used the patient’s response to ‘worst pain intensity over the past week’. The BPI has been validated for chronic noncancer﻿ pain, with satisfactory psychometric properties.40–42

Pediatric Quality of Life Inventory (PedsQL)

The PedsQL is a 23-item assessment tool for pediatric health-related quality of life.43 This measure has 4 subscales (physical, emotional, social, school) and 2 scales for physical and psychosocial functioning. The PedsQL uses a 5-point (for 8 to 18 y old) or 3-point (for 5 to 7 y old) Likert scale, and caregiver-proxy report for all ages.43 Higher scores reflect better functioning. This measure is a validated and reliable tool across community and pediatric patient samples.43,44

Bath Adolescent Pain-Parental Impact Questionnaire (BAP-PIQ)

The BAP-PIQ is a 62-item questionnaire assessing caregiver psychosocial burden associated with their youth’s chronic pain.45 The BAP-PIQ contains 8 subscales: depression, anxiety, child-related catastrophizing, self-blame and helplessness, partner relationship, leisure functioning, parental behavior, and parental strain. Higher scores reflect elevated levels of burden. This measure has sound validity and subscale internal consistency (Cronbach alphas [α] from 0.76 to 0.89).45

For the purposes of the current study, a BAP-PIQ total score was computed from the 8 subscales (total ranging 0 to 248). Series mean imputation was used for missing data in the partner relationship subscale if caregivers did not have a partner. The BAP-PIQ total score computed from the current cohort had satisfactory subscale consistency (α of 0.88).

Work Productivity and Activity Impairment Questionnaire (WPAI)

The WPAI measure evaluates the impact of a specific health condition on work-related functioning.46 The PaedePPOC questionnaires have adapted this to assess how caregivers’ work-related functioning is affected by their youth’s pain. The work impairment score was used in the current study, where caregivers rated the impact of their child’s pain on their work productivity over the past 7 days, converted to a percentage. The WPAI has widespread use in various other caregiver populations47,48 and has adequate validity.46

Statistical Analyses

All analyses were performed using IBM Statistical Package for the Social Sciences (SPSS Statistics v27.0.1.0). Descriptive analyses were conducted and prevalence of attrition from pediatric chronic pain clinics was computed. A Kaplan-Meier survival curve was plotted to investigate temporal patterns of attrition for those patients where the coded reason was episode incomplete—patient withdrew by choice; this did not include those patients coded as episode incomplete—lost to contact as the end date may be unreliable and obscure temporal patterns. Independent-samples t tests (for parametric data) and Mann-Whitney U tests (for nonparametric data) were utilized to compare differences between the attrition group and treatment completion group on sociodemographic, patient pain and functioning variables, and caregiver psychosocial and work-related variables. χ2 tests were used to compare the attrition and treatment completion groups on categorical variables. Level of significance was set at 0.05, with Bonferroni correction applied as appropriate. Logistic regression analyses assessed potential predictors of attrition. This method allows for the simultaneous consideration of all independent variables, accounting for their interrelationships. Unlike individual analyses of variables, logistic regression assesses each variable’s unique contribution to the outcome, which can result in different findings. Key patient variables were entered in step 1 of the logistic regression model. Age and duration of pain were included in light of their documented general associations with pediatric pain outcomes,49,50 even though specific associations with attrition have not been found. Worst pain intensity and﻿ physical functioning and psychosocial functioning were also entered in step 1 of the model, as these variables are considered of importance in the pain management field by clinicians and patients. Key caregiver variables were entered in step 2. These included relationship status, as this has been explored previously in a smaller study,3 and caregiver burden, which is associated with various measures of impaired functioning in children with chronic pain.51

RESULTS

Prevalence of Attrition

There were 1051 patients who met study inclusion criteria, with 203 (19.3%) in the attrition category. Post hoc power analyses found that this sample size afforded satisfactory statistical power (P<0.05) to detect medium effect sizes (power=0.80, α=0.05), if these exist.52,53

Temporal Patterns of Attrition

The Kaplan-Meier survival curve (Fig. 1) illustrates the duration for which attrition patients (defined as those who discontinued by choice, n=94) remained in their episode of care. The y axis represents the proportion of patients still receiving treatment at any given time, whereas the x axis tracks the time elapsed until treatment episodes were ended. Attrition was gradual over an 11-month period, with ~20% of discontinuations occurring early in the first month.

Comparison of the Attrition and Completion Groups

Comparative information for attrition and completion groups is presented in Table 3. The attrition group did not differ significantly from the completion group at the time of treatment commencement on sociodemographic, clinical-status, physical functioning, psychosocial functioning, and caregiver variables assessed as continuous variables (with Bonferroni correction applied). There was a significant association between attrition and longer pain duration (>12 mo) [χ2(1, n=879)=8.23, P=0.004], where a greater proportion of participants in the attrition group (76%) relative to the completion group (65%) had a pain duration more than 12 months. No other significant associations were found for other categorical variables including patient sex [χ2(1, n=1051)=2.33, P=0.127], socioeconomic status [χ2(9, n=947)=15.32, P=0.083], migration status [χ2(1, n=919)=1.169, P=0.280], caregiver employment status [χ2(1, n=813)=0.005, P=0.946], and frequency of medication use (never, rarely, sometimes, often, daily) [χ2 (4, n=639)=1.321, P=0.250].

Logistic Regression Analyses: Statistical Predictors of Attrition

Logistic regression analyses were performed to assess the effect of patient factors (step 1) and caregiver factors (step 2) on subsequent attrition status (Table 4). The model was statistically significant in both steps [step 1: χ 2 (5, n=698)=12.320, P=0.031; step 2: χ 2 (7, n=698)=19.291, P=0.007], with significantly greater variability explained by the added caregiver factors. In the complete regression model, older patient age (P=0.010), and increased caregiver psychosocial burden (P=0.025) emerged as the only 2 significant statistical predictors of attrition. Holding all other variables constant, the odds of attrition increased by 11.6% for each 1-year increase in age and increased by 8% for each 10-point increase (out of a maximum 248 points) in caregiver burden score.

DISCUSSION

The current study is among only a few31 to document attrition from pediatric chronic pain services, finding an attrition prevalence of 19.3% for interdisciplinary pediatric outpatient chronic pain clinics contributing to the PaedePPOC database. This figure falls within the previously reported attrition range of 8% to 35% for interdisciplinary pediatric outpatient chronic pain programs, based on a combined sample size from 6 studies of 1790 patients.31 The current study adds a further sample size of 1051 to the existing published body of knowledge, thereby providing greater confidence in our understanding of the prevalence of attrition from interdisciplinary pediatric outpatient chronic pain programs. This is of importance as those patients not completing treatment are potentially not receiving the full benefits of a pediatric chronic pain clinic’s intervention, thus resulting in the inefficient use of finite resources in the context of limited availability24 and considerable wait times25,26 for pediatric interdisciplinary pain services.

When examining the temporal patterns of attrition from pediatric chronic pain services, a steady pattern of treatment discontinuation was noted. Attrition in the first month was ~20%. Factors contributing to early attrition have not been explored previously in pediatric chronic pain. However, studies in pediatric weight-management interventions suggest several relevant factors including patients and families with low self-efficacy or motivation for change, insufficient buy-in to the offered treatment model, a belief that the program does not meet their expectations, or poor rapport or engagement with the treating team.54–56 Contributors to later attrition may differ and include patients finding the treatment requirements difficult, having competing priorities, or a sense of disappointment with early progress, as outlined in a recent model of patient attrition.31

To our knowledge, the current study is the first to investigate patient and caregiver factors associated with attrition in the pediatric chronic pain outpatient interdisciplinary clinic context. A greater proportion of participants in the attrition group relative to the completion group had a pain duration of more than 12 months (76% vs. 65%, respectively). This association has not previously been reported in pediatric studies. However, in a prospective cohort study of adults attending an interdisciplinary chronic pain intervention, a greater proportion of patients who dropped out of the intervention had a pain duration of more than 5 years, relative to those who completed treatment (57% vs. 34%, respectively).29 Longer pain duration may be associated with a decreased capacity to change or a sense of helplessness.57–61 Pain pathways may be more established and harder to modify, with central nervous system windup and neurophysiological alterations.62,63

Examination of patient factors revealed older patient age, as also found in pediatric weight-management programs,64,65 was significantly associated with attrition, whereas pain intensity and physical and psychosocial functioning were not. Adolescents may face more challenges in engaging with pediatric health care services, whereas pediatric services may be less equipped to meet the unique needs of adolescents.53,66–70 Comorbid mental health issues increase in prevalence through adolescence71,72 creating potential barriers to patient attendance and engagement, as with adult chronic pain73 and pyschosis74 interventions. In addition, adolescents’ desire for increased autonomy and independence75 may reduce caregivers’ ability to enforce continued health care appointment attendance. The only caregiver factor significantly associated with patient attrition was caregiver psychosocial burden. The highly burdened caregivers may lack mental, emotional, or physical capacity to facilitate their child’s attendance and engagement with the chronic pain service. These results are concerning given the widely documented prevalence of psychosocial burden among the caregivers of pediatric chronic pain patients.9,51

Limitations and Strengths

Several limitations should be acknowledged. The PaedePPOC database is not prescriptive about when data are entered, clinicians may be tardy in their documentation and may approximate rather than be accurate in entering the date that the treatment episode ended. This may lead to interclinic variation potentially obscuring temporal patterns of attrition. In addition, some patients who have not attended the clinic for an extended period may have effectively dropped out, but their drop-out status has not yet been recorded by the clinician. As a result, the attrition group may be slightly underrepresented in the data set. Nonetheless, any significant differences observed between groups are even more noteworthy, as they persist despite this potential under-representation. A further issue is that clinics do not provide details in the PaedePPOC database regarding the number of patients referred to a pediatric pain clinic who decline the appointment, fail to attend or do not start treatment (nonstarters), leading to an underestimated denominator. Notably, data are collected from patients and caregivers before their first assessment, without subsequent information regarding rapport, treatment experiences or intervention details. Moreover, caregiver data is limited to the caregiver completing the questionnaires. Thus, it is not possible to determine the impact other caregivers may have upon attrition.

The current study was limited by being retrospective and relying on the measures utilized in the PaedePPOC questionnaires and standardized clinician service provision and outcome coding. Specific reasons for attrition were not documented, such as motivation, distance, transportation and access issues including appointment bookings procedures, scheduling clashes and suitability, or if the family has moved from the area. The study’s strengths are that it draws from a moderately large sample, supplying additional much needed clinical data, providing a valuable contribution to the literature, and foundation for further work. The data set is multicenter, with broad geographic and sociodemographic diversity, thus achieving good generalizability. The mean socioeconomic (IRSD) index was found to be high, with no detailed information available regarding caregivers in the lower deciles, who may be more likely to be single parents, have limited access to assistance or childcare, or are balancing multiple jobs—factors that likely exacerbate their challenges in navigating the health care system to attend appointments. Prior published attrition research31 has primarily been conducted with self-selected and consequently more highly motivated participants, potentially limiting the generalizability.76,77 Patient and caregiver participants in the PaedePPOC data set were aware that their deidentified data may be later used for research purposes; however, their engagement with questionnaire completion and treatment was voluntary and primarily clinically focused.

Clinical Implications and Future Research Directions

Greater accuracy and standardization in reporting the timing and reasons for ending a treatment episode would improve our understanding and reliability of clinical attrition estimates. Also, more standardized use in the literature of terminology regarding premature treatment completion would facilitate more accurate comparison of treatment outcomes.

The current study highlights potential value in screening patients and families to identify those at greatest risk of subsequent attrition. Prompt identification of urgent or complex family psychosocial issues that may hinder the family’s ability to engage might help determine their capacity to commit to their child’s pain treatment requirements and whether treatment plan modifications or further education regarding treatment rationale are required.

Given the moderate rates of attrition from pediatric chronic pain clinics, more attention needs to be directed to patient engagement78,79 and retention80 strategies to avoid inefficient use of these finite resources. Such strategies are commonly used in clinical trials,81,82 albeit with ‘uncertain’ levels of evidence.83 Once developed, retention strategies should be routinely implemented in clinical contexts. To optimize efficiency of service provision and﻿ patient, health professional and organizational factors need to be methodically addressed.31 The current study’s results suggest potential value in directing particular attention to the engagement of older youth within the pediatric health care setting. This may require discussing scheduling difficulties and caregiver-involvement preferences, addressing expectations, tailoring motivational interviewing to adolescents,84 improving the adolescent patient’s understanding about the intervention,85–87 and considering the physical appearance of the waiting area and treatment rooms, which are sometimes designed for younger children.88–90 More research is needed to determine the specific issues that older youth may have in engaging with a chronic pain service.91 All these considerations could spare families from a treatment failure experience, which are known to impact upon future intervention success.92,93

Pain science education is widely regarded as being an important part of pediatric pain management94–96; however, its benefits in terms of child chronic pain and functional outcomes have, to date, been variable.97 Nevertheless, pain science education may enhance engagement with pain clinic programs. For example, familiarity and understanding of a proposed pediatric chronic pain treatment recommendation have been associated with greater patient follow-through.85 Optimizing education regarding the therapeutic model’s benefits before commencement and early-on in treatment may help patients and families to better engage or decide if they do not wish to commence with this management option.

Further research is needed to better understand the reasons why longer pain duration is associated with premature treatment termination. This would likely involve consideration of psychosocial factors including pain-related cognitions,98 motivational and self-regulatory factors,99 family dynamics,100 and neurophysiological and central nervous system changes.62,63 Longitudinal research is needed to explore these factors’ effects on subsequent attrition. Other areas of potential research interest include examination of critical turning-points when patients and families may consider “dropping out” versus factors that resulted in continuation with the intervention along with separate assessment of patient engagement, as attendance does not necessarily imply adherence to recommendations.85 An improved understanding of patient and family engagement in this context would shed light upon treatment-enabling or resilience factors (eg, flexibility, optimism, and﻿ problem-solving59) contributing to patients completing treatment, providing avenues for support strategies. Qualitative methodologies may provide rich and nuanced information about patient and family experiences, particularly regarding changes in engagement, before attrition. This would be enhanced by exploring patient/caregiver perceptions and experience of service-related factors (eg, intervention complexity65,101 and time commitment65,102), which have been associated with attrition from interdisciplinary pediatric weight-management interventions.

Finally, it has been questioned whether caregivers should be considered ‘co-therapists’ or ‘co-patients’ in the context of pediatric pain management.103 The current results suggest that involving caregivers as co-therapists might not be enough; targeted support may be needed to address caregiver burden, better enabling caregivers to facilitate their child’s ongoing engagement with the chronic pain program.

CONCLUSIONS

This study sheds light on the complex issue of attrition from pediatric interdisciplinary chronic pain clinics. Attrition was substantial, occurring for 1 in 5 patients who commenced treatment and associated with longer pain duration at presentation, older patient age and higher caregiver psychosocial burden. These findings suggest potential value of clinical screening for these risk factors. Moreover, the provision of additional holistic support to caregivers with higher psychosocial burden may be warranted.

Greater clarity regarding attrition is dependent on more standardized reporting practices across services and in the literature. Further research is needed into patient understanding of the models of care, factors influencing critical turning-points, the patient and caregiver’s level of engagement, and their proposed explanations for dropping out, including economic and access barriers. It is particularly important to identify any system issues that are not meeting their needs that can then be addressed. Such information could further help guide strategy development to reduce attrition from pediatric chronic pain services, thus optimizing quality of life and health-related outcomes for affected children.
