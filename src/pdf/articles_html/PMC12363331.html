Abstract

ABSTRACT

Injury is the leading cause of death and a cause of disability in children and negatively affects physical health, mental health, and quality of life in both the short- and long-term. The goal of a pediatric trauma system is to optimize the care for children within a state, regional, or national trauma system across the entire continuum of care regardless of where they live or where the traumatic event occurs. This continuum includes injury prevention, prehospital care, interfacility transport between hospitals providing different levels of care, acute and critical inpatient care, inpatient and outpatient rehabilitation, and reintegration into the community and primary care medical home. A systems-based approach, one that requires distinct elements of structure and function to perform together in an interrelated and cohesive manner to improve care quality, is essential. To improve outcomes after injury, a cohesive system must effectively provide optimal care for the “right child, at the right place, at the right time” across this continuum.

LEVEL OF EVIDENCE

Expert Opinion; Level V.

KEY WORDS: Pediatric trauma, systems-based care, care continuum, outcomes, pediatric injury

Injury has been the leading cause of death and a cause of disability in children in the United States for decades.1,2 Injury leads to more years of potential life lost than cancer, congenital anomalies, and perinatal health issues combined.1,3 Survivors of childhood injury frequently suffer mental and behavioral health sequelae and physical disabilities, sometimes with long-term consequences.4,5 Improving outcomes requires an approach that recognizes childhood injury as a significant public health problem. Using the continuum of care model for trauma system development and planning provides a framework to integrate care and optimize outcomes.6 This continuum of care includes injury prevention, prehospital care, emergency department (ED) care, interfacility transport, acute and critical inpatient care, inpatient and outpatient rehabilitation, and reintegration into the community and primary care medical home (Fig. 1).7 A systems-based approach requires distinct elements of structure and function to perform together in an interrelated and cohesive manner.8,9 A functional trauma system is represented by a sequential practice of evidence-based evaluation and management along the continuum of care and needs to be team-based, cohesive, and seamless to improve care quality in an efficient and effective manner.

Current pediatric trauma care lacks cohesion and has notable gaps in pediatric readiness across the nation, leading to preventable death and inferior outcomes.10 Recent data have highlighted disparities in care and outcomes after pediatric injury, lack of equitable access to resources, and the importance of the relationship between injury and poor mental health outcomes and quality of life.5,10–17 Systems-based approaches to pediatric trauma readiness, child abuse, head injury, and imaging practices have also been developed and/or updated.18–22

TRAUMA SYSTEMS

Children are injured in a variety of geographic locations, and involvement of emergency medical services (EMS) and both local and regional hospital systems is paramount to optimizing care. Pediatric trauma care refers to the care received in any setting by a child who has suffered a traumatic injury, and a trauma center is a hospital-based setting specifically designated or verified to provide trauma care.23,24 Trauma care is provided in a variety of settings including in the field, at nontrauma hospitals or centers, and at adult and/or pediatric trauma centers (PTCs). Additionally, in addition to the ED, minor trauma care may be provided at urgent care and private practice clinics.

Ideally, a comprehensive pediatric trauma system incorporates prehospital and hospital-based care capable of initial stabilization of children of any age and their transfer, when needed, to centers providing advanced pediatric trauma care. A cohesive system allows for prompt communication and coordination, with tiered care concordant with patient needs. A trauma system focuses not only on survival but also survivorship,25 following the patient through discharge to a rehabilitation or home environment. Integration of disaster response systems at the local, state, regional, and national levels is an essential component of any functional trauma system.26 Coordination of trauma system development generally includes physicians and nonphysician clinicians (e.g., nurses, nurse practitioners, or physician assistants) and their professional organizations; hospital, state, and regional emergency management teams; the Administration for Strategic Preparedness and Response Hospital Preparedness Program; state hospital associations; local and state departments of health; state and regional trauma advisory councils; state legislatures; and local community leaders, among others (Table 1).

Other subspecialty societies including but not limited to hospital medicine, urology, otolaryngology, vascular surgery, ophthalmology, plastic surgery, and oral and maxillofacial surgery, among others.

Independent public health organizations and agencies associated with families and child health are also important partners.

Effectiveness of state or regional pediatric trauma systems across the country has not traditionally been assessed. However, a state-based scoring system was created in 2022, called the Pediatric Trauma System Assessment Score (PTSAS), to measure each state's pediatric trauma capacity within the context of their trauma system (Fig. 2). Through a Delphi process, six domains were chosen (injury prevention and recognition, access to care, quality improvement and trauma registry, disaster, legislation, and funding), each of which included three to five seminal parameters reflecting a state's pediatric trauma capabilities. A PTSAS summary score ranging from 0 to 100 was computed based on the weighted averages of each domain (with a score of 100 being the highest capabilities). The PTSAS score has been associated with both out-of-hospital and in-hospital deaths, and every 1-point increase in PTSAS has been associated with a decrease in overall state-based mortality of 0.12 (95% confidence interval [CI], −0.22 to −0.02) deaths per 100,000 children (Fig. 3).

Because of regional geography and the limited number of PTCs, timely access to pediatric trauma care varies significantly across the nation.24,27,28 A 2017 US Government Accountability Office report found that only 57% of children in the United States lived within 30 miles of an American College of Surgeons (ACS)– and/or state-designated PTC, defined as centers able to treat children of all ages and all injuries regardless of severity.24 Considerable variation existed by state, with 15 states having less than a quarter of children living within 30 miles of a PTC.24 In 2023, Burdick et al.15 reported that the percentage of US children younger than 15 years who could not reach a level I, II, or III PTC within an hour of injury was 45.5% by ground and 25.9% by air transport. Non-PTCs within the trauma system, including adult trauma centers (ATCs), community hospitals, rural or critical access hospitals, and adult regional hospitals, among others, are relied on to safely provide stabilization and initial management of an injured child. This phenomenon is particularly true in rural and underserved areas of the country (Fig. 4). Hence, it is essential that any hospital, health care center, or urgent care site that may treat injured children has the necessary training and resources to provide pediatric-specific care (see section Pediatric Readiness). Collaborative efforts are important, as are novel approaches to improve care for children without immediate access to a trauma center (e.g., teleconsultation/telemedicine).30

INJURY PREVENTION

Injury prevention is the cornerstone of pediatric trauma care. The field of injury prevention uses scientific principles to identify areas for intervention and best practices. The Haddon matrix is a popular systematic approach to injury prevention, analyzing injuries by considering how the host (i.e., child) is susceptible to or injured by an agent (e.g., motor vehicle, firearm) in a specific environment (e.g., physical, social, economic) and in three different temporal phases: preinjury, during the injury event, and postinjury.30,31 Building on the Haddon matrix, an injury equity framework was recently designed to “provide clinicians, researchers, public health professionals, and policy-makers a theoretical basis for identifying and analyzing factors that influence who is injured, the severity of the injury, and the injury outcome using an equity lens.”32 The framework highlights the intersectionality between historical and current societal factors and community, family, and individual factors within the Haddon matrix structure. Overall, a multipronged approach to injury prevention is essential and may include legislation, advancing safety technology, improving the built environment (e.g., parks, roadways), addressing risk factors related to a child's environment (e.g., home, school), anticipatory guidance, and education of caregivers.30 Furthermore, interventions should be evidence based and equity focused.32

Mechanism- and community-specific injury prevention initiatives have a long history of success, including the traffic safety, safe storage of firearms, window falls, and car seat programs, to name a few.30,33–36 More than 20 years ago, Dr. Maureen Durkin and colleagues35 studied the incidence of severe traffic injuries before and after a community-based intervention involving the creation of safe and accessible play areas, traffic safety education, and supervised activities for school-aged children. During the intervention period, a 36% unadjusted reduction in the incidence of traffic injuries to school-aged children was observed (rate ratio [RR], 0.64; 95% CI, 0.58–0.72). Comparable reductions in traffic-related injuries were not identified in younger children (aged 0–4 years) or school-aged children who were not targeted by the program or for traffic fatalities involving school-aged residents in other communities in the city.35 Policy-based injury prevention efforts that were legislated to become state or federal statutes have proven some of the most effective, including seat belt and child passenger safety legislation.30,37

Often, a combination of approaches is essential. Dr. Barbara Barlow was instrumental in identifying, describing, and promoting regulatory changes related to window falls by children in New York City.33 After Dr. Barlow's work in Washington Heights led to a 1979 ordinance requiring landlords to install window guards, the number of injuries children sustained from falling out of windows decreased in the neighborhood by 96%.38 Effective public health messaging through the “Children Don't Fly” campaign helped solidify and disseminate her pivotal work.

More recently, Dr. Lois Lee and colleagues36 summarized the evidence regarding firearm injury prevention and harm reduction, describing safe firearm storage, anticipatory guidance, lethal means counseling, and legislation through an American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) policy statement. Policy statements endorsed by national organizations like the AAP have wide distribution and often guide advocacy efforts nationwide. The AAP has published a number of policy statements, clinical reports, technical reports, and other resources related to injury prevention in childhood. These include drowning prevention, pedestrian safety, child passenger safety, helmet use, and more.39–43 More resources can be found on the AAP website at https://www.aap.org/en/policy/ and for families at healthchildren.org. The AAP also designed The Injury Prevention Program, which helps pediatricians provide injury-related anticipatory guidance using a developmentally based schedule and supports pediatricians by providing a standardized resource about injury prevention for parents and caregivers. The Injury Prevention Program has recently been shown in a cluster randomized controlled trial to result in a reduction of parent-reported injuries in children.44 This highlights the important role of pediatric physicians and nonphysician clinicians (e.g., nurses, nurse practitioners, or physician assistants) in injury prevention and supports emphasize of age-appropriate safety topics during well-child visits and other health care encounters to educate families on injury prevention using available resources from the AAP and other organizations.45,46

The Consumer Product Safety Commission has been instrumental in industry-specific injury prevention initiatives, including but not limited to baby walker-related injuries, furniture safety, and more. Federal agencies like the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) have routinely contributed to injury prevention efforts. One example is sports-related injury prevention with resources, education, and guidance for health care staff, coaches, and parents regarding concussions and protective equipment.47 The CDC also provides research and resources related to child abuse and neglect.48 The Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA)'s National Fetal, Infant, and Child Death Review Program and local child death review programs have traditionally helped with outreach messaging. There are now child death review teams in all 50 states and most US territories, which generally incorporate multidisciplinary approaches to the community's death review process.49,50

Finally, nonprofit organizations have, and continue to, contribute to injury prevention. Safe Kids, Prevent Child Injury, Charlie's House, Prevent Child Abuse America, and the Injury Free Coalition for Kids have addressed injury risks related to drowning, television and furniture tip overs, child abuse, and all-terrain vehicle use, to name a few. Many of these organizations promote risk reduction behaviors such as bike helmet use and safe teen driving. Both Safe Kids and the Injury Free Coalition for Kids have been instrumental in research, education, and advocacy for childhood injury prevention nationwide, and Prevent Child Injury provides multimedia toolkits on injury prevention topics, including information for families and providers, reports and data, and social media materials for outreach.51–55

Despite incredible efforts in the injury prevention space, it is important to note that many causes of injury to children have not changed over the past decade.56 Aside from deaths due to motor vehicle collisions and pedestrian injuries, which have declined substantially, many injury-related causes of deaths have not changed for 20 years.2,41 This highlights the importance of continued focus on injury prevention initiatives at local, regional, and national levels.

PEDIATRIC READINESS

The 2006 Institute of Medicine report evaluating the US emergency care system described care for children as uneven and found that many of the country's EDs lacked the necessary equipment, drugs, and supplies to care for children adequately.57 The National Pediatric Readiness Project works to address these gaps with strategies to improve children's care including standardized guidelines and checklists. This includes the recommendation that there be a pediatric emergency care coordinator (PECC) position established in every ED in the nation. The PECC is tasked with providing education and training, ensuring maintenance of equipment and supplies, and completing quality improvement related to all types of pediatric trauma care and has been associated with improved ED pediatric readiness.58 The HRSA-funded Emergency Medical Services for Children (EMSC) Program has incorporated the National Pediatric Readiness Project performance measures for assessment of state-based programs (Table 2).59

Prehospital and ED pediatric readiness across the country is an essential component of pediatric trauma systems.60 New studies have validated the effectiveness of pediatric readiness in reducing in-hospital mortality after injury and critical illness.18,61,62 Emergency department pediatric readiness is also independently associated with long-term survival among injured children.62,63 This makes pediatric readiness of all EDs across the country an essential component of health care safety and quality.18,64–66 Because of its success, the program has also been extended to the prehospital care environment.67,68 The ACS Committee on Trauma is a partner in pediatric readiness and has outlined a blueprint along with recommendations for incorporating pediatric readiness into trauma system development.60

PREHOSPITAL AND INTERFACILITY PEDIATRIC TRAUMA CARE

Prehospital Care

Prehospital care may be provided by a variety of emergency health care clinicians, including emergency medical technicians, advanced emergency medical technicians, emergency medical responders, and paramedics.69 Police and/or fire departments, private or third-party ambulance services, and public agencies all play a role in parts of the country. Aside from specialty units associated with large children's hospitals, individual prehospital nonphysician clinicians infrequently care for critically ill or injured children, particularly those who volunteer or work in rural or underserved areas. Limited knowledge and experience with pediatric trauma patients are addressed by continuing education efforts through established courses supported by the AAP, American College of Emergency Physicians, American Heart Association, National Association of Emergency Medical Technicians, National Association of EMS Educators, and National Association of EMS Physicians, among others. Local and regional EMS-based pediatric-specific educational initiatives and practical experience at children's hospitals have been used to optimize competency and comfort with care of the injured child. Additional methods for skill maintenance include simulation and online and remote training.70–73 Simulation-based training for prehospital personnel in the management of pediatric emergencies is associated with improvement in some skills (i.e., choosing the correct airway equipment and use of Broselow tape), and data suggest that low-fidelity simulation training is more effective than high-fidelity simulation at improving pediatric resuscitation skills.72 However, limited exposure of any training (whether simulation-based, lecture, or online self-study) may not be sufficient to substantially improve skills,72 highlighting the need for iterative and varied approaches to capacity building and skill maintenance. The EMSC Program has introduced pediatric readiness to EMS systems, using collaborative approaches and promoting a PECC for each EMS service. The PECC is responsible for pediatric quality improvement to make sure that pediatric equipment is up to date and working and that all emergency health care clinicians know how to use the equipment and use periodic simulation to refresh skills.

Individual states have the responsibility of regulating their own EMS system. In some states, EMS agencies are required to submit practice guidelines and protocols to the state medical director for approval and other states require a standardized state protocol. Evidence-based and standardized pediatric-specific protocols for prehospital care are an essential component. In all states, licensure of EMS agencies requires an approved number of education hours and/or experience dependent on the level of service provided, although these hours may or may not include pediatric education.

Direct feedback from trauma centers can assist with continued process improvement for EMS teams and agencies. Feedback can be provided in real-time by including EMS teams in trauma quality improvement meetings, in case reviews, and/or in written or oral feedback through direct communication after a child's injury-related encounter is complete. Examples of follow-up guidelines and communications are included in the EMSC Inter-facility Toolkit.74

Although evidence guiding prehospital care is limited, there are increasing data regarding best practices for pediatric resuscitation in out-of-hospital traumatic cardiac arrest; fluid, drug, and product administration; cervical spine stabilization; and airway management.75–78 Field triage criteria are updated regularly by the ACS Committee on Trauma and the CDC.79–81 These criteria include anatomic and physiologic parameters that indicate whether transport to the highest level trauma center available within the geographic constraints of a regional trauma system is indicated. Additional parameters, including mechanism of injury and other considerations (including young age and concerns for child abuse), suggest transfer to a trauma center, although not necessarily the highest level.80 In general, trauma systems have a goal of undertriaging no more than 5% and overtriaging between 25% and 35%. Iterative review of field criteria is essential and guides updates to national field triage guidelines.82,83

Community Care and Interfacility Transport

More than 27% of injury- and poison-related ED visits in the United States in 2020 were for children younger than 18 years.84 Because of specific hospital characteristics, local triage patterns, and regional geography, many children are treated at community and/or adult-focused hospitals, including urgent care centers and EDs, with or without pediatric-specific protocols and programs. Although young and/or critically injured children may need transfer to a higher level of pediatric trauma care, many children are effectively and safely cared for at facilities that are not PTCs. For centers that evaluate and transfer injured children, there are national or multi-institutional guidelines that can guide pediatric care (e.g., Image Gently using “as low as reasonably achievable” principles).66,85,86 Nearly 25% of EDs do not have reduced-dose radiation protocols for computed tomography and radiographic imaging.87 Partnering with trauma centers that care for children for advice on appropriate workup before transfer is ideal. For centers that routinely care for and admit children, other guidelines are accessible through national organizations. For example, the Pediatric Trauma Society (PTS) Hub provides updated and vetted guidelines for the acute management of injuries by body system and is readily accessible to the public on the PTS website.88 The EMSC Inter-facility Toolkit provides guidance on specific components associated with interfacility transfer, including templates and examples of guidelines and agreements for public use, summaries of standards and regulations, and family considerations.74

Interhospital transfer guidelines, policies, and procedures facilitate patient transfer, often through multidisciplinary centralized “transfer centers.” Transfer centers allow hospitals that initially treat children to efficiently initiate transfer to higher levels of care with a single call. These calls can facilitate guidance on when and how a child should be transferred on the basis of their physiology and injuries, what diagnostic and therapeutic interventions are indicated prior to transfer, and what method of transport is most appropriate (e.g., ground vs. aeromedical, one-way vs. two-way with a specialized team, advanced life support vs. basic life support). Pediatric transport teams may be the best resource for severely injured children because of their specific expertise in pediatric resuscitation and trauma care.89–91 Generally, as soon as it is recognized that a child will likely need transfer to a higher level of care, further diagnostic tests beyond basic laboratory tests can be limited to those that will inform the need for intervention prior to transfer or those that dictate the need for transfer after consultation with PTC personnel. Only necessary imaging studies should be completed to minimize radiation and to avoid the need for repeat imaging after transfer.92,93

Transport decisions are a collaborative effort between referring and accepting physicians to determine mode (air vs. ground), the appropriate level of expertise of transport team members, and the necessary equipment to ensure a safe transport to optimize patient care, all while considering the cost and time for the mobilization of each transport type and the respective travel times.91 Although the patient's health is the utmost priority to guide interfacility transport decisions, recognizing the impact of transport on the child and family (e.g., separation of child and parent) is important.

Telemedicine and Teleradiology

The use of technology to support clinical care decision making has increased over the past decade. Telehealth services have the potential to enhance the reach of tertiary trauma care for children in rural and underserved areas where pediatric specialty care is not readily available.94 Services can include education, protocol sharing, “as low as reasonably achievable”–based guidelines, and administrative oversight.94 As a consequence of the COVID-19 pandemic, teleradiology has been progressively used around the nation. Data suggest that efficient cloud-based imaging may lead to decreased repeat imaging, lower costs, and fewer delays in care for pediatric trauma patients.95 With the growth of telehealth opportunities to improve care, appropriate and commensurate payment models must be developed to support telehealth services.

TRAUMA CENTERS

In 2006, the National Study on Costs and Outcomes of Trauma found that trauma center care was associated with decreased mortality, particularly for the most critically injured patients.96 In general, trauma centers are described by their ability to provide trauma-specific care, with level I being the highest, most comprehensive level of care (Table 2).24 Trauma centers can be self-described, state designated, and/or ACS verified. They can be classified as PTCs, ATCs, or combined adult/PTCs. The ACS verifies ATCs as levels I, II, and III and pediatric centers as level I or II, with level I as the highest level. Verification by the ACS is a process that is integrated into their quality programs and involves four components: adherence to standards, comprehensive infrastructure, rigorous data to guide quality improvement, and external peer review. State criteria for trauma level designation may differ from or be identical to ACS verification criteria and may include as many as four or five levels. American College of Surgeons verification criteria are reviewed and updated at regular intervals and published as “Resources for the Optimal Care of the Injured Patient,” which provides a comprehensive description of the necessary resources to provide optimal care for injured adults and children (Table 3).23 A 2022 standard for ACS verification at all levels of trauma centers addresses the pediatric readiness of the hospital ED: “In all trauma centers, the emergency department must evaluate its pediatric readiness and have a plan to address any deficiencies.”23 This standard aims to raise awareness of the importance of pediatric readiness, include the trauma surgeon as part of the team, and begin to optimize resources for pediatric care in all settings.

Injured children, including adolescents, may have improved outcomes at PTCs, although the data are mixed.24,97–101 For example, a 2019 analysis of Healthcare Cost and Utilization Project data for injured children 16 years and younger demonstrated improved mortality for children who were treated at a level I PTC compared with a level I trauma centers with no pediatric verification (adjusted odds, 0.6; 95% CI, 0.4–0.9).97 Similarly, a 2024 Trauma Quality Improvement Program (TQIP) analysis of injured children 15 years and younger with severe injury found treatment at a level I or level II PTC was associated with decreased mortality (adjusted odds, 0.72; 95% CI, 0.61–0.85) compared with centers without pediatric verification. In firearm-specific injury data from the 2010–2016 National Trauma Databank files in children 13 to 16 years of age, odds of mortality were 2.88 times higher at ATCs compared with PTCs (95% CI, 1.55–5.36; p < 0.001). However, using a 1:1 propensity score matching model, no difference in mortality was found by trauma center type.99 Perhaps most importantly, recent data suggest that risk-adjusted benchmarking in adults is not a reliable surrogate for pediatric outcomes.102 Hence, it is imperative that pediatric-specific metrics are measured and targeted for quality improvement.103

Pediatric trauma centers are generally capable of providing a broad range of pediatric services delivered by pediatric-focused staff, including physicians and nonphysician clinicians trained in pediatric emergency medicine, nurses with demonstrated competency in the care of injured children, pediatric anesthesia providers, and pediatric medical subspecialists and surgical specialists. Resources at PTCs required by the ACS include readily available/on-site interventional radiology, pediatric anesthesiology, pediatric surgery, pediatric orthopedic and neurosurgical care, and pediatric critical care, among others. A protocol for screening patients at high risk for psychological sequelae from their injury is also required, along with a process for referral to a mental health clinician trained in trauma-informed care.23 For the appropriately trained clinician, examples of evidence based therapies may include Trauma-Focused Cognitive Behavioral Therapy and Alternatives for Families: A Cognitive Behavioral Therapy. To help guide trauma centers, the ACS Committee on Trauma has published best practice guidelines to guide screening and intervention for mental health disorders in the acute trauma patient, including pediatric-specific resources.104 Pediatric trauma centers also often have certified child life specialists, and the 2023 standard for ACS verification requires all PTCs to have a child life program. In addition, PTCs often have child abuse physicians; pediatric registered dietitians and pharmacists; pediatric physiatrists; pediatric occupational, physical, and speech therapists; pediatric pain medicine specialists; and pediatric palliative care services. Many of these specialized services can be offered through consultation and, in some cases, telemedicine.

ED at the Trauma Center

Once a child arrives at the ED of the PTC, care continues by initiating or continuing resuscitation and identification of injuries, regardless of whether the child arrives from the field or is transferred from a referral center. Transferred patients meeting triage criteria are evaluated in the same manner as patients who have not been evaluated elsewhere. The ACS requires all trauma centers to have clearly defined tiered activation criteria to efficiently and expeditiously mobilize the appropriate staff and resources as a team.23 These criteria are used to ensure that the resources and personnel that are commensurate with the needs of the patient are available. The ACS defines patient criteria and trauma surgeons response times for the highest level of activation.23 Some critically injured patients may need to transition directly to the operating room or interventional radiology suite. Protocols for massive transfusion, management of noncompressible hemorrhage, replants, vascular injuries/critical limb ischemia, spine and neurological trauma, and others are critical to provide prompt limb and lifesaving care.105–108 Special consideration should be taken to formalize systems for optimal and efficient care for injuries that may require coordination with an adult center (i.e., vascular injury/compromise, replants, complex pelvic trauma). Additionally, guidelines for body region-specific imaging are critical to limit radiation and the need for repeat studies.85,86,109–112

Critical Care, Acute Care, and Operative Resources at the Trauma Center

A well-equipped and staffed pediatric intensive care unit (PICU) is essential for optimal care of the injured child,113 and the availability of PICU beds within a region may improve survival in pediatric trauma.114 Pediatric intensive care units offer a setting with necessary equipment and advanced technology to support physiologically unstable or intubated patients who need advanced monitoring. Pediatric intensive care units are staffed by physicians and nonphysician clinicians with pediatric critical care training or expertise. Pediatric intensive care unit resources are essential for the management of severe traumatic brain injury and advanced burn resuscitation.19,115 In the PICU, attention is paid not only to physiologic stability but also to minimizing secondary harm (e.g., preventable delirium, decubitus ulcers, hospital acquired infections). The ACS recommends that surgeons be the primary physicians and/or involved on all patients admitted with traumatic injuries; however, notable variability exists in the structure and care processes in place for critically injured children.116 Critically injured children likely benefit from collaborative care by both the trauma and PICU services. Likewise, comanagement with medical teams on acute care floors is often beneficial and/or essential depending on hospital-specific resources and processes (i.e., which teams are in house, relative expertise, etc.) and patient age and comorbid diagnoses (i.e., uncontrolled diabetes, prematurity, psychosis, complex care needs, etc.).

The acute care hospitalization at a PTC frequently incorporates a multidisciplinary team, including physical therapists, occupational therapists, speech therapists, and mental and behavioral health specialists (among others) concordant with each child's needs. Readily available access to the operating room at all times is critical, and the ACS requires an operating room to be staffed and available within 15 minutes of notification for levels I and II centers for cases that are considered to be emergencies.23 Urgent cases include the timely fixation of orthopedic fractures, and levels I and II trauma centers must have a dedicated operating room prioritized for fracture care in nonemergent orthopedic trauma.23

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS

Issues unique to management of children, irrespective of where they receive definitive care, include reducing diagnostic radiation exposure; addressing pain during invasive procedures; weight-based fluid, drug, and electrolyte management and blood transfusions; family-centered care; and child life specialty care, among others. Careful consideration of the extent of diagnostic radiation for trauma evaluation is important because of the risk of radiation-related malignancies over the lifetime of a child.21,22,117 Patterns of injury and pathology in children are different than those of adult patients, and much of the imaging protocols in adult trauma patients do not apply to pediatric trauma patient.22 Knowledge of incidental findings and normal variations of pediatric anatomy may mitigate false-positive diagnoses. The ability to provide a full range of safe but effective pain management strategies for children is important, including nonpharmacologic techniques.118,119 A child- and family-centered environment facilitates family presence during care that may range from wound debridement, suturing and dressings, fracture reduction, or postarrest resuscitations and optimizes communication and education between the treatment team and family.120 The presence of child life specialists before, during, and/or after invasive procedures has been associated with improved care-experiences.121 Family presence during trauma resuscitations requires a designated team member to be with the family at all times, when possible, to support the family and help them understand and process the events. Overall, trauma-informed care from the time of injury and throughout the care continuum, including rehabilitation, is critical.122

Child Abuse

A unique aspect of pediatric trauma care is the need for awareness and recognition of signs of child abuse and neglect. Various historical factors, examination findings, and diagnostic testing results are associated with child physical abuse.123–125 A combination of these findings is often included in institution-specific screening tools. It is important to note that multiple reports have identified disparities in child abuse reporting, with children from historically marginalized racial or ethnic groups disproportionately identified as potential victims of abuse and more likely to be investigated, to be subject to unnecessary tests, and to have a change of caregiver at discharge.126–133 Minimizing disparities in reporting (and unnecessary tests) requires a multipronged approach, including clinical pathways/guidelines and staff education on bias reduction.

Multidisciplinary teams that care for children with suspected child abuse often include social work and pediatricians with expertise in child abuse recognition and management, if available.133 The use of telemedicine or remote consultation may help expand the reach of trained child abuse pediatricians and their expertise. The ACS, EMSC Innovation and Improvement Center, and American Pediatric Surgical Association have all published resources and/or guidelines for assessment of child abuse. For children with suspected physical abuse, the ACS recommends consultation with a surgical trauma service, in addition to a child abuse physician and social worker.20,134,135 Diagnostic studies beyond what would typically be pursued for the injury of presentation may be required, including imaging to detect previous occult injuries and specific laboratory studies.22 Commonly used clinical decision rules (e.g., Pediatric Emergency Care and Research Network tools for traumatic brain injury and intra-abdominal injury) do not apply in child abuse.136–139 Although there is variability in state statutes, all health care workers in all states are mandated by law to report concern for child maltreatment.140

The AAP Council on Child Abuse and Neglect has resources, including clinical reports, policy statements, technical reports, and educational offerings for physicians, patients, and families141 pertaining to child abuse and neglect, including special populations and situations (e.g., children with disabilities, sex trafficking, abusive head trauma, child abuse fatalities, and long-term sequelae and management of abuse).141–148

Injury Trends

Historically, the leading cause of injury-related death in children, adolescents, and young adults 1 to 19 years of age was motor vehicle collisions. In 2019, firearm injury became the leading cause of death in this population.2 Both the ACS and the AAP have published resources for health care workers, patients, and families regarding firearm injury prevention and safe storage.46,149 The ACS, AAP, and American College of Emergency Physicians have all published position statements, policy statements, and/or technical reports on firearm safety and firearm-related injury prevention.34,36,150–152 There has also been an increase in suicide among adolescents and young adults in recent decades, highlighting the need for evidence-based suicide prevention strategies and suicide screening.153–156 The AAP Blueprint on Youth Suicide Prevention is a free online education resource to support pediatric health clinicians “identify equitable youth suicide prevention strategies” (https://www.aap.org/suicideprevention).155 Overall, since 2010, trauma-related ED visits have been increasing, although with fewer hospitalizations.157 The COVID-19 pandemic was associated with further shifts in injury epidemiology among children and adolescents.123,158–169

REHABILITATION AND REINTEGRATION INTO THE COMMUNITY

Injury during childhood has been associated with poor educational outcomes, functional status, and quality of life.5,170–174 The trauma community has more recently emphasized survivorship and recovery given the potential long-term physical and mental health effects of trauma on the child, family, and community.175 To this end, postinjury trauma-informed care, along with recovery and resiliency programs, are becoming more common.176–179 The goal of a trauma system is to return children to full, age-appropriate function with the ability to reach maximum adult potential. This process includes reintegration into the community, school or educational environment, and primary care medical home. The primary care pediatrician is an essential navigator in this postinjury phase of care, supporting children and adolescents with short- and long-term sequelae, in addition to assisting in the transition to adult trauma-informed care clinicians. Crisis intervention and ongoing support can be offered through hospital-based violence intervention programs to youth who are injured through interpersonal violence and are at risk of reinjury and psychosocial trauma.180–182 For injured children with extensive care needs after the acute injury, the availability of both inpatient and outpatient rehabilitation resources specifically for children is a scarce yet vital component of trauma care. Physical, occupational, cognitive, speech, and play therapies, along with psychological and social support, are essential elements of a comprehensive rehabilitation effort for the injured child and family. The ability to begin early rehabilitation during the acute hospitalization, particularly for children with traumatic brain injury and cognitive challenges or with fractures and mobility requirements, is inherent to recovery and reintegration.183–185 There are many areas of the country with no inpatient pediatric rehabilitation resources and limited outpatient resources that are barriers to optimal or expeditious recovery. Novel approaches to care, such as telehealth and mobile or wearable health technologies, may help increase access to pediatric-specific care to meet the needs of all children, despite where they live, grow, and play.

Mental Health

The impact of injury on a child goes well beyond the initial hospital encounter. Mental health sequelae, such as posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD) and depression, are common after pediatric injury. An estimated 20% of children report posttraumatic stress symptoms after injury, and previous injury is also associated with anxiety and depressive symptoms.16,104,186 In a managed Medicaid population (using the MarketScan database), an estimated 25% of children 0 to 17 years of age with nonfatal firearm injury were diagnosed with a new mental health condition.4 In another MarketScan study, documented mental health diagnoses increased by 63% in the 12 months after unintentional injury, from 95.9/1000 person-years preinjury to 156.7/1000 person-years postinjury (RR, 1.63; 95% CI, 1.39–1.92). The rate of psychotropic prescriptions also increased from 121.7/1000 person-years preinjury to 310.9/1000 person-years postinjury (RR, 2.55; 95% CI, 2.26–2.89).187 Similar trends have been identified in injury-specific pediatric populations, particularly among children and youth with firearm injuries.4,188 Estimates do vary, as yet another MarketScan study looking at and evaluating children 6 to 17 years of age admitted for traumatic injury found that only 3.3% developed a subsequent mental health diagnosis (i.e., PTSD, depression, anxiety, or adjustment disorder) and only 1.6% developed PTSD, presumably because of suboptimal recognition of these symptoms.17 Despite variability in estimates, there is widespread understanding that injury is intricately related to mental health and quality of life after injury and also affects families, parents, and caregivers.5,186,189 Resources including screening tools for children and adolescents can be found in the ACS “Best Practice Guidelines: Screening and Intervention for Mental Health Disorders and Substance Use and Misuse in the Acute Trauma Patient.”104

PERFORMANCE IMPROVEMENT, EDUCATION, AND OUTREACH

Effective clinical performance and quality improvement are integral components of trauma programs and indirectly for trauma systems as a whole. Performance improvement efforts often focus on patient safety and harm prevention, reporting of delays in diagnosis or care, near misses, trends in morbidity across service lines, mortality review to recognize opportunities for improvement, and/or communication issues. Review of trauma care provided to children is facilitated by comprehensive chart review. Collaborative reviews between transferring and receiving institutions and transport teams are important for children transferred for additional care. Each dedicated trauma center has an institutional trauma registry, which exports data to state and (often) national databases so that outcomes can be risk adjusted and benchmarked to allow for evaluation of care quality across institutions and inform opportunities for improvement. Quality review generally adopts the Donabedian framework of structure, process, and outcomes, with selection of performance improvement initiatives based on performance indicators that fall below a specific threshold based on benchmarking.190,191 Quality indicators should be remeasured after implementation, with reassessment of outcomes. Outcomes are compared through programs like the ACS Pediatric TQIP, the Cardiac Arrest Registry to Enhance Survival, and the National EMS Information System. Data systems, however, continue to be disjointed and lack the ability to link across data sets to fully understand the continuum of care and the connection between processes and outcomes. One consideration is to create a National Unique Identifier for each trauma patient that starts from the initial encounter (e.g., EMS, urgent care, or other) and is added to all centers (and their data set), at which the patient is treated. This identifier would follow a child from initial evaluation through acute treatment, rehabilitation, and postdischarge follow-up. This is an important step to obtain comprehensive understanding of the impact of a pediatric trauma system on long-term outcomes.

At all institutions that treat injured children, systematic child mortality and morbidity review processes help identify variations in standard of care, trends in outcomes, and systematic risk factors for which interventions can be developed, evaluated, and improved. The implementation of technologies such as video review of trauma activations aids in ongoing improvement of team dynamics, improvement of trauma and emergency resuscitation quality, and multidisciplinary team education.23,192

Capacity building, education, and training at all health care centers that treat injured children are important. The highest-level trauma centers often provide education and outreach to local and regional community centers and state agencies to improve care for children. National trauma and EMS organizations work jointly to include children's unique needs and interests in their programs to ensure that children receive timely and appropriate care no matter where they are injured. Continuing trauma education for physicians, nurses, and prehospital professionals can be accomplished by courses such as the Advanced Trauma Care for Nurses, Trauma Nursing Core Course, PTS/STN Pediatric Trauma Across the Care Continuum nursing course, PALS Plus: Trauma, NAEMT's PHTLS and EPC courses for prehospital providers, and the Advanced Trauma Life Support courses, all of which includes key components for children. The Emergency Nursing Pediatric Course has injury-specific modules as well.

PEDIATRIC DISASTER PREPAREDNESS AND SURGE

Disaster preparedness for adults and children in the United States has improved significantly since Hurricane Katrina in 2005. Hospital accreditation programs including The Joint Commission have strengthened their disaster preparedness requirements for institutions. There is considerable federal support through the Administration for Strategic Preparedness and Response Hospital Preparedness Program and the HRSA Regional Pediatric Pandemic Network (PPN) to improve pediatric disaster care nationally.193–196 As of 2022, there were 10 children's hospitals partnering with professional associations, nonprofit organizations, government agencies, and others through the PPN to improve the pediatric readiness of health care systems and ensure high-quality, equitable pediatric care in emergencies, disasters, and pandemics.

Children have unique needs for health care in mass casualty incidents involving trauma and burns.26 Along with physiologic considerations unique to the trauma management of children, additional issues of triage, identification, decontamination, tracking, and reunification are considered. Support services including child life, social work, child and adolescent psychologists, and bereavement counselors, among others, are important to recovery.197 Children and youth with special health care needs (CYSHCN) are a vulnerable population during disasters because of mobility challenges, language impairment, executive functioning and sensory difficulties, medical conditions with and without technology dependence, and other trauma exposure.196 Even with disaster planning, households with individuals with disabilities are less likely to be prepared, and very few studies have examined the optimal methods of disaster communication tailored for families of CYSHCN.198,199 The AAP has resources available for patients, families, and physicians related to CYSHCN to prepare before, help during, and recover after disasters.200,201

A process for recruiting pediatric health care professionals when a surge response is needed is a mainstay of institutional disaster plans. Although the needs of children in disasters can be anticipated, the capability of a trauma system to meet these needs requires frequent simulation practice and mock disaster drills, including multi-institutional, state, and regional planning.202 State, regional, and federal agencies manage the legal, operational, and information systems to provide effective pediatric mass casualty care through the following: (1) predisaster/mass casualty planning, management, and assessment with input from child health professionals; (2) close cooperation, agreements, public-private partnerships, and unique delivery systems; and (3) use of existing public health data to assess pediatric populations at risk and to model graded response plans on the basis of increasing patient volume and acuity.203 Regional Medical Operations Coordinating Centers can coordinate care across EMS agencies, hospitals, public health, and emergency management leadership.204 They can effectively load balance a trauma system ensuring that each child receives optimal care at the appropriate facility.205 Pediatric trauma systems may play a role in supporting adult centers during crisis, such as occurred during the COVID-19 pandemic, during which surgeons boarded in trauma and critical care provided intensive care services for adults.206

HEALTH EQUITY AND DISPARITIES IN CARE

Inequity and disparities in pediatric injury prevalence, trauma care, and outcomes are recognized as increasingly important considerations and key determinants of short- and long-term outcomes.13,32,207,208 Although the extensive use of protocol-guided management during trauma care might be presumed to mitigate treatment disparities, the evidence suggests otherwise.13 Disparities in injury incidence, treatment, and outcomes frequently result from racial, economic, and geographic drivers of health and may be attributable to individual or systemic bias or a combination of these.32,209–213

The burden of injury falls disproportionately on communities of color and those who are economically or geographically disadvantaged.160,208,214,215 Studies examining the impact of socioeconomic status on injury outcomes often use insurance coverage and median household income as proxies, as they are readily available data elements that may reflect financial and educational resources available to children and their families for primary, secondary, and tertiary injury prevention and trauma care.216 Recent literature explores composite scores of deprivation (or disadvantage or vulnerability), based on different geographic areas of residence (i.e., census tract, census block, zip code, or county), and have consistently identified an association between increased “deprivation” and incidence of injury and specific injury types.208 Additionally, disparities exist related to treatment experiences in the ED, such as pain control and sedation during fracture management, radiographic evaluations, child abuse screening, transfer to PTCs, and discharge to inpatient rehabilitation.127,217–219

Many studies have examined the association of race or ethnicity and/or insurance status with mortality. Black and Hispanic children who require operative intervention following major abdominopelvic trauma have a higher risk of postoperative mortality compared with White children.220 In other studies, lack of insurance and disparities in socioeconomic status were associated with mortality after pediatric injury.221–223 There are limited data on racial and ethnic disparities in pediatric trauma morbidities such as disability, quality of life, and mental health; however, studies suggest that Black children have worse functional outcomes after traumatic brain injury.224,225 These differences are not due to a biologic difference, but likely reflect complex underlying disparities.

Community violence exposure is also a prominent determinant of negative physical and mental health outcomes. Community violence exposure is linked to numerous negative psychological outcomes in children, such as PTSD, externalizing behavior, and internalizing symptoms as well as obesity, asthma, and health-risk behaviors. All pediatricians, pediatric subspecialists, and pediatric trauma care clinicians and partners can address community violence exposure and related health disparities by developing trauma-informed systems that routinely screen for community violence exposure, provide basic support for affected families, and link those in need to trauma-focused interventions through the mechanisms of trauma-informed care.226

Next steps at the ACS level involve including an equity-focused quality framework into data systems such as the TQIP and beginning to study patient-reported outcomes, which may provide more insight into disparities in care.227,228 The Center for Disease Control and Prevention's Division of Adolescent and School Health has defined action steps to address health disparities and include focused programmatic efforts, raising awareness, building partnerships, and documenting impact.229 Additionally, the US Department of Health and Human Services and the Office of Disease Prevention and Health Promotion's Healthy People 2030 focuses on “eliminating health disparities and creating equitable opportunities for people to live healthy lives.” More information on Health Equity in Healthy People 2030, including goals, objectives, and tools for action, can be found online (http://odphp.health.gov/).229 Altogether, disparities occur along the continuum of the pediatric trauma care and equity-based efforts to address these disparities must also span the continuum of care.

RESEARCH

State and federal financial support for research, advocacy, education, and trauma system development and maintenance provides integration of pediatric and adult care in all phases of planning and implementation of trauma systems. Regional, state, and national organizations advocate for the comprehensive support of pediatric trauma-related research. Current examples of organizations that fund or perform at least some pediatric trauma research include certain federally funded HRSA programs (e.g., the EMSC Program, EMSC Innovation and Improvement Center, Pediatric Emergency Care and Research Network, and PPN), the Pediatric Emergency Research Network, the Injury Free Coalition for Kids, Safe Kids, the Childress Institute for Pediatric Trauma, the PTS, the American Pediatric Surgical Association, the CDC, and the National Institutes for Health, among others. Research efforts to address disparities in pediatric injury are essential.230

The National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine called for a National Trauma Research Action Plan (NTRAP) in 2016, and the Department of Defense Combat Casualty Care Research Program funded its development in 2018. The NTRAP brings the “research and medical community of trauma systems across the United States together around a prioritized, comprehensive research agenda, develops the best assessment tools for long-term functional outcomes for injured patients, and identifies regulatory barriers to conducting trauma research and develops recommendations for best practices.”231 Since its inception, the NTRAP has used Delphi methodology to develop priorities of research regarding long-term outcomes, pediatrics, and injury prevention, among others.227,232,233

ADVOCACY

Advocating for the health and wellness of children and youth is integral to preventing death and disability from trauma and can lead to improved funding and resources to optimize both prevention initiatives and health care provided. The AAP is one of many organizations active in advocacy for child health and injury prevention at the national and state levels. Organizations including the ACS and the AAP offer advocacy-focused conferences for members, and they partner with many other organizations, including SafeKids, to promote issues such as injury prevention and firearm safety.46,51,234

CONCLUSION

Injury is the leading cause of death and a cause of disability in children in the United States. Using a systems-based approach along the continuum of care for trauma system development and planning along with integration of research, outreach, and iterative evaluation (Fig. 5) provides a framework to integrate care and optimize outcomes. The ideal pediatric trauma system is comprehensive and cohesive but seamlessly integrated with the adult trauma system of care to provide optimal and equitable care for the “right child, at the right place, and at the right time” across this continuum.
