Abstract

Eukaryotes have evolved defense systems to protect their genomes from invasive genetic elements. We report a fungal defense mechanism, tandem repeat–induced sexual silencing (TRISS), active during sexual stages. TRISS is a unique RNA interference (RNAi) pathway distinct from known mechanisms like quelling and meiotic silencing by unpaired DNA (MSUD). Triggered by tandem repeats, it involves RNAi components similar to quelling but uniquely includes the MSUD-specific Argonaute Sms2. TRISS operates independently of recombination. We identified small interfering RNA sassociated with TRISS (trasiRNAs) in tandem repeats. Sms2, guided by trasiRNAs, mediates translational repression of target mRNAs and is crucial for trasiRNA biogenesis. TRISS requires the DNA methyltransferase Rid but not Rid-mediated repeat-induced point mutation (RIP). Both Sms2 and Rid interact with replication protein A (RPA) to recruit the RPA-Qde1/RdRP complex, dependent on the helicase Qde3. This study reveals a potentially conserved strategy linking RIP and RNAi to silence tandem repeats during fungal sexual stages, offering insights into fungal genome defense.

TRISS, an RNAi-mediated defense mechanism, silences tandem repeats in conjunction with RIP during the sexual stages in fungi.

INTRODUCTION

All living organisms face threats from invading genetic elements, such as transgenes, transposons, and viruses. These elements can repeatedly insert themselves into the genome, disrupting normal genes, causing harmful recombination, and leading to uncontrolled gene activity, which ultimately threatens the stability of the genome (1, 2). To defend against these threats, most organisms have evolved advanced genome defense systems called homology-dependent gene silencing (HDGS). These systems detect and deactivate repeated sequences introduced by these parasitic genetic elements (3, 4).

One of the best-studied HDGS mechanisms is RNA interference (RNAi), which is conserved across eukaryotes and protects the genome from both internal elements like transposons and external invaders like viruses (5–8). The RNAi process begins with an enzyme called Dicer, which cuts long double-stranded (ds) RNA molecules into short fragments known as small interfering RNAs (siRNAs). These siRNAs are then loaded into Argonaute (Ago) proteins. One strand of the siRNA is removed, while the other strand guides Ago to identify and destroy matching RNA molecules in the cytoplasm. In addition to silencing RNA posttranscriptionally, RNAi can also act at the transcriptional level by modifying DNA in the nucleus (9–11). Some organisms also use RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRP) to create dsRNA from single-stranded (ss) RNA or to amplify siRNA signals, further strengthening the RNAi response (11–14).

During sexual reproduction, invading genetic elements can spread between the two genomes contributed by the parents. To prevent this, many organisms have evolved specific defense mechanisms that target and destroy these elements before they can be passed on to the next generation (15). For example, in plants, reproductive tissues produce specific siRNAs that guide DNA modifications, silencing transposons at the transcriptional level (16). In animals, a specialized RNAi pathway involving PIWI-interacting RNAs protects reproductive cells by silencing transposons, ensuring the integrity of the germline genome (17). In the yeast Cryptococcus neoformans, a defense mechanism called sex-induced silencing (SIS) uses RNAi to detect and silence repeated genetic sequences during the sexual cycle (18). In the filamentous fungus Neurospora crassa, three distinct genome defense mechanisms have been found: quelling, repeat-induced point mutation (RIP), and meiotic silencing by unpaired DNA (MSUD) (3, 4, 19, 20).

Both quelling and MSUD use RNAi to silence harmful genetic elements. Quelling occurs during normal growth, while MSUD is active during meiosis (21). The quelling process involves proteins such as RdRP (Qde-1), Dicer enzymes (Dcl-1 and Dcl-2), Ago (Qde-2), RecQ helicase (Qde-3), and a Qde-2–interacting protein (Qip). The MSUD process consists of overlapping components from quelling (Dcl-1 and Qip), as well as MSUD-specific components such as RdRP (Sad-1), Ago (Sms-2), and helicase (Sad-3). In quelling, siRNA production requires both DNA tandem repeats and a double-strand break (DSB) (22). The putative homologous recombination (HR) intermediates of repetitive sequences are recognized by Qde-3, leading to the recruitment of Qde-1 and ssDNA-binding protein replication protein A (RPA) (22, 23). Qde-1 exhibits RdRP and DNA-dependent RNA polymerase (DdRP) activities (24), producing aberrant ssRNAs (aRNAs) and then dsRNAs to activate the downstream RNAi pathway. MSUD, on the other hand, is triggered by unpaired DNA regions during meiosis. These unpaired regions produce siRNAs, which silence both matching and nonmatching copies of genes in the genome (25–27).

RIP, the first genome defense system found in eukaryotes, operates before meiosis during the sexual reproduction of filamentous ascomycetes (3, 28–30). RIP introduces C-to-T mutations into repeated DNA regions, effectively disabling them (31). RIP relies on the putative DNA methyltransferase Rid (32). In N. crassa, RIP-mutated sequences can also direct trimethylation of histone H3 lysine 9 (H3K9me3) by the histone methyltransferase Dim-5, which then leads to methylation of associated cytosines by the DNA methyltransferase Dim-2 (33). Recently, a Rid-independent pathway of RIP was found in N. crassa (34). This pathway tends to mutate the linker region between tandem repeats and requires Dim-2, Dim-5, and heterochromatin protein 1 (HP1). However, there is currently no known cross-talk between the RIP and RNAi mechanisms.

The filamentous fungus Fusarium graminearum is a major pathogen causing Fusarium head blight, a devastating disease on cereal crops worldwide. This fungus is self-fertile and produces abundant sexual fruiting bodies (perithecia) in laboratory conditions, making it an ideal model for studying genome defense mechanisms during sexual reproduction. Both RIP and MSUD have been observed in F. graminearum (35, 36). In addition, a regulatory mechanism called A-to-I mRNA editing, which occurs specifically during sexual stages, has been shown to play a critical role in the sexual development of this fungus (37–43).

In this study, we describe a genome defense mechanism in F. graminearum called tandem repeat–induced sexual silencing (TRISS). TRISS is a distinct RNAi pathway specifically activated by tandem repeat sequences. While it shares some components with the quelling pathway, TRISS uniquely involves the MSUD-specific Ago protein FgSms2. We identified TRISS-associated siRNAs (trasiRNAs) localized within tandem repeat regions. Our research demonstrates that FgSms2, guided by trasiRNAs, is essential for both suppressing the translation of target mRNAs and facilitating the production of trasiRNAs. Furthermore, we found that FgRid is indispensable for TRISS, functioning independently of the RIP pathway. We uncovered two key mechanisms that enhance the TRISS response, both of which operate independently of traditional transitive siRNA amplification: (i) FgSms2 interacts with FgRpa1 to recruit the FgRpa1-FgQde1 complex to ssDNA in a trasiRNA-dependent manner, and (ii) FgRid interacts with FgRpa1 to similarly recruit the FgRpa1-FgQde1 complex. These interactions rely on the helicase protein FgQde3, whose recruitment to tandem repeat regions is dependent on FgRid activity. This suggests that FgQde3 may recognize specific intermediates introduced by FgRid, triggering DNA unwinding and activating the TRISS pathway. Our findings provide evidence that TRISS may represent a conserved mechanism linking the RIP and RNAi pathways to silence harmful genetic elements during sexual reproduction. This study not only advances our understanding of fungal genome defense systems but also broadens our knowledge of RNAi mechanisms in eukaryotes.

RESULTS

Tandem repeats trigger sexual stage–specific gene silencing in F. graminearum

In a previous study (44), we performed complementation assays on the Fgama1 deletion mutant, which exhibits defects in ascospore morphology, by introducing the pFL2 plasmid containing the full-length FgAMA1 gene. Two distinct types of transformants were observed (Fig. 1A). One group produced asci with eight spindle-shaped ascospores, resembling the wild type and indicating successful complementation. The other group produced asci with eight oval ascospores, similar to the deletion mutant, suggesting unsuccessful complementation. Notably, all transformants contained the FgAMA1 gene (Fig. 1B). Subsequent quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) assays revealed that the complemented transformants had only one copy of FgAMA1, while the uncomplemented transformants had multiple copies (Fig. 1C).

Although gene silencing can be caused by RIP associated with multicopy transgene repeats, the severity and consistency of the defective phenotype in each ascus suggested that RIP was not the primary cause in this case. To investigate further, we isolated 15 ascospore progeny from the uncomplemented transformants and performed Sanger sequencing to detect RIP mutations in FgAMA1. Six progeny showed no amino acid changes caused by RIP mutations in FgAMA1 (Fig. 1D) despite the abnormal phenotypes observed in the isolated ascospores. These observations suggest that RIP is not the primary cause of gene silencing in the uncomplemented transformants. Similar phenomena were observed in complementation assays on the deletion mutants of CDC2A (45) and TUB1 (46) (fig. S1), indicating that the gene silencing phenomenon is not specific to FgAMA1 and may be a more general phenomenon.

To explore this silencing further, we transformed a linear fragment containing the full-length FgAMA1 gene fused with a hygromycin-resistance gene (hyg) into the wild-type strain PH-1. We expected two types of transformants: one with unlinked repeats of FgAMA1 due to random integration via nonhomologous end joining (NHEJ), and the other with tandem repeats of FgAMA1 at its native locus via HR (Fig. 1E). We obtained five transformants (R-FgAMA1) containing at least one additional copy of FgAMA1 (Fig. 1F), all of which displayed phenotypes similar to the deletion mutant (Fig. 1G). PCR assays confirmed that these transformants had tandemly repeated FgAMA1 at the native locus (Fig. 1H). Further analysis of eight ascospore progeny from a transformant with two tandemly repeated FgAMA1 copies revealed that five progeny showed no RIP mutations in at least one repeat (Fig. 1I), providing additional evidence that RIP was not the cause of silencing.

A similar experiment with CDC2A yielded four transformants (R-CDC2A) (Fig. 1F), three of which exhibited deletion mutant phenotypes and tandemly repeated CDC2A, while the fourth showed wild-type phenotypes without tandem repeats (Fig. 1, G and H). To confirm this observation, we introduced an ectopic copy of CDC2A at a specific locus (FG1G36140) in PH-1. The resulting transformants with unlinked repeats (UR-CDC2A) exhibited normal phenotypes (Fig. 1G). These results indicate that the silencing phenomenon is triggered by tandem repeats rather than unlinked repeats.

Since CDC2A and FgAMA1 deletion mutants exhibited defects only during sexual stages, these genes could only be used to determine whether silencing occurs during sexual development. To investigate whether silencing also occurs during vegetative stages, we examined the TUB1 gene, which affects both sexual reproduction and vegetative growth when deleted (46). Transformants with tandemly repeated TUB1 at its native locus (TR-TUB1) displayed silencing phenotypes during sexual reproduction but not during vegetative growth (Fig. 1, H and J). To confirm these findings, we conducted a similar experiment with PKS12, a gene involved in pigment biosynthesis (47). While the pks12 deletion mutant formed white colonies, the TR-PKS12 transformants displayed the normal red-pink phenotype of the wild type (Fig. 1, H and J). These results suggest that tandem repeats trigger gene silencing specifically during the sexual stage.

Since the silencing phenotypes are observed during self-fertilization, we can rule out MSUD as the cause of the silencing. Furthermore, transformants with tandemly repeated FgCLN2, which encodes a B-type cyclin essential for viability, exhibited normal vegetative growth but defects in crozier formation (Fig. 1, H and J). This indicates that silencing occurs premeiotically and can take place throughout sexual development, from the dikaryotic stage to ascospore differentiation. On the basis of these findings, we named this silencing phenomenon TRISS.

TRISS is independent of tandem-repeat formation mode and repeat orientation

To investigate whether the mode of tandem-repeat formation influences TRISS, we used two additional strategies to create tandem repeats of CDC2A. In the first strategy, HR occurred between the native gene and a linear homologous fragment, while in the second strategy, HR occurred in the linker region between the two repeats (Fig. 2A). In both cases, transformants with tandemly repeated CDC2A displayed deletion mutant phenotypes, indicating that TRISS is independent of the repeat formation mode.

We also constructed transformants with reverse tandem repeats of CDC2A. These transformants exhibited silencing of CDC2A (Fig. 2B). However, transformants ectopically expressing antisense transcripts of CDC2A from an extra copy inserted at the FG1G36140 locus displayed normal phenotypes (Fig. 2B). This suggests that antisense transcription alone cannot silence CDC2A, and TRISS is independent of the orientation of tandem repeats.

To rule out the possibility that gene silencing is caused by the expression of resistance marker genes in the linker between repeats, we constructed tandemly repeated FgAMA1 with a G418-resistance gene (neo) or a nongene sequence as the linker (Fig. 2C). In both cases, FgAMA1 was completely silenced, as observed in transformants with the hyg linker. Therefore, TRISS is unrelated to the linker sequence or whether it contains expressed genes.

TRISS efficiency depends on repeat length and distance between repeats

Our results suggest that two tandem repeats are sufficient to trigger TRISS during sexual reproduction. To determine whether silencing efficiency depends on repeat length, we created constructs with direct repeats of varying lengths [1080 to 3187 base pairs (bp)] of CDC2A separated by a neo linker (1433 bp) (Fig. 2D). Longer repeats (3187 and 2643 bp) induced complete silencing of CDC2A, resulting in very few ascospores. Shorter repeats (2117 and 1613 bp) exhibited reduced silencing efficiency, with deformed or normal ascospores observed. Repeats shorter than 1109 bp did not induce silencing. These results indicate that TRISS efficiency depends on repeat length, with longer repeats being more effective.

We also investigated the effect of linker length between tandem repeats. Increasing the linker length from 1433 to 7006 bp reduced silencing efficiency (Fig. 2D). This result aligns with the observation that unlinked repeats cannot induce TRISS. Thus, both repeat length and the distance between repeats are critical factors for TRISS efficiency, with longer repeats and shorter distances enhancing silencing.

TRISS can occur in outcrossing and functions in trans

Although F. graminearum is homothallic, it can undergo outcrossing. To test whether TRISS occurs during outcrossing, we crossed a TR-FgAMA1 transformant (male) with a self-sterile but female-fertile mat1–1-1 deletion mutant labeled with H1–green fluorescent protein (GFP) (44). For comparison, we also subjected the Fgama1 deletion mutant to a similar outcross to evaluate MSUD efficiency.

As expected, the outcross involving the Fgama1 deletion mutant resulted in asci containing ascospores with a 1:1 segregation of eight ascospores with and without GFP signals in each ascus (Fig. 2E). However, some ascospores exhibited abnormal morphologies, such as oval shapes or intermediate forms between oval and spindle-shaped, indicating that MSUD could only partially silence FgAMA1. In contrast, during the outcrossing of the TR-FgAMA1 transformant, all eight ascospores in each ascus were oval-shaped (Fig. 2E), resembling the results observed during self-fertilization. This suggests that TRISS, in combination with MSUD, achieves complete silencing of all three copies of FgAMA1 during outcrossing. Similar results were obtained when testing the TR-CDC2A transformant, indicating that TRISS occurs in both selfing and outcrossing scenarios.

The outcrossing results also demonstrate that TRISS can function in trans. To further explore the trans silencing of the native CDC2A gene, we introduced a tandem repeat construct of CDC2A at an ectopic locus (FG1G36140) in PH-1 (Fig. 2B). The resulting transformants exhibited defects in ascospore formation, providing evidence that the native CDC2A gene was silenced in trans by the tandem repeat construct.

TRISS is mediated by an RNAi mechanism distinct from quelling and MSUD

The F. graminearum genome encodes two Ago proteins (FgQde2 and FgSms2) and two Dicer proteins (FgDcl1 and FgDcl2) (Fig. 3A) (48, 49). To determine whether TRISS depends on the RNAi machinery, we created deletion mutants for each of these four genes and transformed a linear fragment containing the full-length FgAMA1 gene fused with the neo marker into these mutants to generate tandemly repeated FgAMA1. We found that deletion of FgQDE2, FgDCL1, or FgDCL2 had no effect on FgAMA1 silencing. However, in the Fgsms2 mutants containing tandemly repeated FgAMA1, oval ascospores were no longer produced (Fig. 3B), indicating that the Ago protein FgSms2, but not FgQde2, is essential for TRISS.

Since Dcl-1 and Dcl-2 are functionally redundant in quelling (50, 51), we generated double mutants (Fgdcl1 Fgdcl2) and observed that FgAMA1 silencing was completely abolished (Fig. 3B). This indicates that FgDcl1 and FgDcl2 are functionally redundant in TRISS.

To identify additional RNAi components involved in TRISS, we created deletion mutants for other core RNAi machinery components in F. graminearum, including Qde2-interacting protein (FgQip), five RdRPs (FgQde1, FgSad1, and FgRdrp3–5), and two helicases (FgQde3 and FgSad3) (Fig. 3A) (48). Silencing of FgAMA1 was abolished in the Fgqde1, Fgqde3, and Fgqip mutants (Fig. 3B), suggesting that FgQde1, FgQde3, and FgQip are also required for TRISS. However, deletion of FgSAD1, FgSAD3, and FgRDRP3–5 had no effect on FgAMA1 silencing. In addition, we generated Fgrdrp3 Fgrdrp4 Fgrdrp5 triple mutants and confirmed that these genes are not involved in TRISS.

In conclusion, TRISS is mediated by an RNAi mechanism that uses a distinct combination of components from the RNAi machinery involved in quelling and MSUD pathways. These components include FgQde1, FgQde3, FgSms2, FgDcl1, FgDcl2, and FgQip (Fig. 3A).

siRNA biogenesis in tandem repeats is promoted by native transcription

To investigate whether small RNAs are generated during TRISS, we isolated small RNAs from 7-day postfertilization (dpf) perithecia of the TR-FgAMA1 and TR-CDC2A transformants, which contain tandem repeats of full-length FgAMA1 and CDC2A at their native loci, respectively. We then performed small RNA sequencing (sRNA-seq).

Abundant small RNAs, ranging from 20 to 27 nucleotides (nt) in length, were detected in the tandem repeat regions of FgAMA1 and CDC2A, with 24 nt being the predominant size (Fig. 4, A and B). These small RNAs exhibited a strong preference for uracil (U) at the 5′ end, but no obvious biases at the 3′ end (Fig. 4A and fig. S2). The small RNAs were derived from both sense and antisense strands of the tandem repeats (Fig. 4B), indicating that their biogenesis requires the formation of dsRNA. We refer to these small RNAs as trasiRNAs.

As a control, only a few small RNAs were detected in the FgAMA1 and CDC2A loci in the wild-type strain PH-1, and these lacked the 5′-U preference (fig. S3). Most of the small RNAs detected in PH-1 were sense RNAs likely resulting from mRNA degradation.

To determine whether trasiRNA production is associated with the transcription of repeated genes by native promoters, we generated transformants containing tandem repeats of an intergenic region (IGR; chr3: 5806769–5810188 bp) in situ. The IGR repeat length (3420 bp) was comparable to the lengths of the FgAMA1 (3602 bp) and CDC2A (3187 bp) repeats. Abundant trasiRNAs were detected in the IGR repeat region by sRNA-seq (Fig. 4, A and B), suggesting that native transcription of target genes is not strictly necessary for trasiRNA production. However, the amount of trasiRNAs generated from the IGR repeat region was only 50.9 and 27.5% of that produced in the repeat regions of FgAMA1 and CDC2A, respectively. This indicates that native transcription of repeated genes can enhance trasiRNA production.

Consistent with our earlier observation that TRISS efficiency depends on repeat length, the amount of trasiRNAs produced from the 2643-bp tandem repeat of the 3′-region of CDC2A was nearly sevenfold lower than that from the 3187-bp tandem repeat of the full-length CDC2A (Fig. 4, A and B). The amount of trasiRNAs from the CDC2A repeat (3187 bp) was higher than that from the FgAMA1 repeat (3602 bp) despite its shorter length. This discrepancy may be due to differences in transcription levels during sexual development: CDC2A transcription is higher in earlier stages but lower in later stages compared to FgAMA1 (fig. S4). These findings suggest that higher transcription of tandem repeats in early sexual stages is critical for trasiRNA accumulation in later stages.

FgQde1 and FgSms2 are essential for the biogenesis of trasiRNAs

In the quelling pathway, Qde-1 generates aRNA and dsRNA molecules from tandem repetitive transgenes, which are subsequently processed into siRNAs by Dicer proteins (Dcl-1 and Dcl-2) (22). To investigate whether FgQde1 plays a similar role in trasiRNA biogenesis during TRISS, we conducted sRNA-seq analysis of perithecia from the Fgqde1 mutant containing the FgAMA1 tandem repeat.

As expected, trasiRNAs were completely absent in the repeated FgAMA1 region in the Fgqde1 mutant (Fig. 4A). This result demonstrates that FgQde1 is essential for the biogenesis of trasiRNAs in TRISS, likely through its role in generating aRNA or dsRNA from tandem repeats. Unexpectedly, our study also revealed that trasiRNAs were absent in the Fgsms2 mutant in the repeated FgAMA1 region (Fig. 4A). This finding suggests that FgSms2, an Ago protein, plays a critical role in trasiRNA biogenesis or stability.

Previous studies have shown that mutations in the first or second Asp (D) residue of the DDH motif in Ago proteins abolish their slicer activity, without affecting their ability to bind siRNAs (52, 53). To investigate the role of FgSms2 in trasiRNA production and stability, we generated slicing-defective FgSMS2D696A mutants. In these mutants, silencing of FgAMA1 was abolished in transformants containing tandemly repeated FgAMA1 (Fig. 3B). Furthermore, sRNA-seq analysis revealed that trasiRNAs were absent in the repeated FgAMA1 region in the FgSMS2D696A mutant (Fig. 4A). These results demonstrate that the slicer activity of FgSms2 is essential for trasiRNA production in TRISS. The absence of trasiRNAs in the Fgsms2 and FgSMS2D696A mutants is not merely involved in binding and stabilizing trasiRNAs but is directly required for their biogenesis.

No transitive biogenesis of secondary trasiRNAs in TRISS

In yeast, plants, and nematodes, siRNA-programmed Ago proteins can recruit RdRPs to synthesize dsRNA using the targeted RNA as a template. This dsRNA is then cleaved by Dicers into secondary siRNAs, a process known as transitivity (11–13). The critical role of FgSms2 in trasiRNA production prompted us to explore whether transitivity occurs in TRISS.

To investigate this, we generated a TR-PKS12 transformant containing tandemly repeated 3′-regions of PKS12. If transitivity were present, secondary trasiRNAs would be produced corresponding to the upstream (5′) nonrepeated regions of native PKS12 transcripts, as dsRNA would extend from the siRNA primer in the 3′-repeated regions to the 5′end of the RNA template. However, sRNA-seq analysis detected abundant trasiRNAs corresponding only to the repeated regions of PKS12. No trasiRNAs were observed in the upstream nonrepeated regions of PKS12 in the TR-PKS12 transformants (Fig. 4C and fig. S5).

To further investigate whether secondary siRNAs could be generated downstream of a primary siRNA trigger, we created a TR-C2AGFP transformant containing a tandem repeat of CDC2A with the C terminus of the upstream copy fused to a GFP tag. sRNA-seq analysis did not detect trasiRNAs corresponding to the GFP region downstream of the repeated regions (Fig. 4C). These results indicate that trasiRNA-programmed FgSms2 does not trigger transitive siRNA amplification in TRISS.

TRISS is primarily caused by translational repression by trasiRNAs

To confirm the suppression of gene expression by trasiRNAs during TRISS, we performed quantitative reverse transcription PCR (qRT-PCR) to analyze the expression levels of FgAMA1 in transformants containing an FgAMA1 tandem repeat, both with (TR-FgAMA1 Fgqde1) and without (TR-FgAMA1) deletion of FgQDE1. As expected, in the TR-FgAMA1 Fgqde1 transformant, where TRISS is disrupted, the transcript abundance of FgAMA1 was approximately twofold higher than that in the wild type, consistent with the presence of two copies of FgAMA1 (Fig. 5A). Unexpectedly, in the TR-FgAMA1 transformant, which exhibited complete silencing phenotypes, the transcript abundance of FgAMA1 was comparable to that in the wild type. Similar results were observed for CDC2A and TUB1 in transformants containing tandem repeats of these genes (Fig. 5A).

In a TR-CDC2A transformant (#7) that exhibited complete silencing phenotypes, the CDC2A transcript abundance was even higher than in the wild type. This suggests that transcript degradation is not the primary mechanism of TRISS. To further investigate, we used the TR-C2AGFP transformant, which contains tandem repeats of CDC2A fused with GFP, and measured expression levels using repeat-specific primers. As a control, we generated a C2AGFP transformant containing a single CDC2A copy fused with GFP. We also created a TR-C2AGFP Fgqde1 strain by deleting FgQDE1 in the TR-C2AGFP transformant (Fig. 5B).

qRT-PCR analysis revealed that during vegetative growth, the transcript abundance of both the upstream and downstream CDC2A repeats in the TR-C2AGFP transformant was comparable to that in the C2AGFP transformant and wild type (Fig. 5B), consistent with the absence of TRISS in hyphae. During sexual reproduction, the transcript abundance of both the upstream and downstream CDC2A repeats was reduced by approximately half in the TR-C2AGFP transformant. However, the total amount of CDC2A transcripts expressed by the TR-C2AGFP transformant remains comparable to that in the C2AGFP transformant and wild type (Fig. 5B). These results suggest that transcript cleavage occurs but is not the primary cause of TRISS-associated silencing phenotypes.

Western blot analysis provided further evidence supporting translational repression as the primary mechanism of TRISS. While the Cdc2A-GFP protein was readily detected in perithecia of the C2AGFP transformant, it was barely detectable in perithecia of the TR-C2AGFP transformant (Fig. 5C). However, the Cdc2A-GFP protein was detected in perithecia of the TR-C2AGFP Fgqde1 strain and in the hyphae of the TR-C2AGFP transformant. Similar results were observed for Tub1 protein expression in the TR-TUB1 transformant containing tandem repeats of TUB1 (Fig. 5C).

Polysome profiling analysis revealed only a modest decrease in the amount of polysome-associated CDC2A mRNAs in the TR-C2AGFP transformant compared to the wild type (Fig. 5D). This suggests that the base pairing of trasiRNAs with target mRNAs may cause ribosome stalling, leading to impaired translational elongation. Together, these findings indicate that TRISS is primarily driven by the translational repression of target mRNAs by trasiRNAs rather than transcript degradation.

TRISS is not coupled to DSB repair pathways

DSB-induced HR is critical for siRNA and quelling-induced siRNA (qiRNA) biogenesis in N. crassa, as it facilitates aRNA production at tandemly inserted transgenes and ribosomal DNA loci (22). To investigate whether TRISS depends on DSB repair pathways, we examined the role of key components in these pathways (Fig. 6A).

Canonical meiotic recombination requires Spo11 to produce DSB (54). We generated Fgspo11 deletion mutants containing tandem repeats of FgAMA1 and observed that the mutants produced oval ascospores (Fig. 6B), indicating that TRISS still occurs in the absence of Spo11. This suggests that TRISS does not depend on canonical meiotic recombination.

We next examined the roles of Rad51 and Rad54, which are essential components of the eukaryotic HR pathway (55). Deletion of FgRAD54 in transformants containing tandem repeats of FgAMA1 did not affect TRISS, as oval ascospores were still produced (Fig. 6B). Since FgRAD51 deletion mutants did not form ascospores, we used a “silencing the silencer” approach (27) by generating transformants with tandem repeats of FgRAD51. If FgRAD51 was required for TRISS, it would not be efficiently self-silenced by tandem repeats. However, FgRAD51 was completely silenced in the TR-FgRAD51 transformants (Fig. 6B), suggesting that neither FgRAD51 nor FgRAD54 is required for TRISS.

We also examined TRISS occurrence in deletion mutants of other genes involved in DSB repair and checkpoint pathways, including two responsible for sensing DSBs (FgATM and FgATR) (56) and two for NHEJ (FgKU70 and FgKU80) (55). As with HR mutants, TRISS was still observed in these DSB checkpoint and NHEJ mutants (Fig. 6B).

Together, these results demonstrate that TRISS does not rely on DSB repair pathways. Recombination between repeats can lead to the loss of repeats. To determine whether trasiRNAs play a role in repeat stabilization, we analyzed ascospore progeny from transformants containing tandem repeats of FgAMA1 with (TR-FgAMA1 Fgqde1) or without (TR-FgAMA1) FgQDE1 deletion. PCR analysis revealed that the FgAMA1 tandem repeat was stable in all progeny, regardless of FgQDE1 deletion (Fig. 6C). This indicates that tandem repeats are stable during sexual reproduction in F. graminearum and independent of trasiRNAs.

TRISS does not depend on H3K9me3 and DNA cytosine methylation

DNA cytosine methylation and H3K9 methylation are critical for siRNA production in many organisms (12, 57). To investigate their role in TRISS, we analyzed mutants lacking key components of these pathways.

Deletion of the DNA methyltransferase gene FgDIM2 and the HP1 gene FgHP1 in TR-FgAMA1 transformants did not affect TRISS. Oval ascospores were still produced in both TR-FgAMA1 Fgdim2 and TR-FgAMA1 Fghp1 mutants (Fig. 6D), indicating that TRISS is independent of DNA methylation mediated by FgDim2 and FgHP1.

To examine the role of H3K9 methylation, we deleted FgDIM5, which mediates H3K9me3 required for constitutive heterochromatin formation (58), in TR-FgAMA1 transformants. The mutants still produced oval ascospores (Fig. 6D), indicating that H3K9me3 is not required for TRISS activation. These findings show that TRISS does not depend on DNA cytosine methylation or H3K9me3-mediated heterochromatin formation.

TRISS is promoted by FgRid

Since tandem repeats induce both TRISS and RIP, we investigated whether there is cross-talk between these two processes. To investigate potential cross-talk between these processes, we analyzed the role of FgRID, the ortholog of the N. crassa rid gene, which is crucial for RIP in F. graminearum (35).

In the TR-FgAMA1 Fgqde1 strain, oval ascospores were observed in 20% of asci in 10-dpf perithecia (Fig. 7A). Deletion of FgRID in this strain abolished oval ascospore production (Fig. 7A), confirming that the observed oval ascospores were caused by RIP. These results indicate that RIP operates independently of FgQde1 and TRISS.

Unexpectedly, deletion of FgRID in the TR-FgAMA1 strain resulted in a substantial reduction in TRISS. Oval ascospores were rarely observed in 7-dpf perithecia of the TR-FgAMA1 Fgrid strain (Fig. 7A). sRNA-seq analysis revealed a limited number of trasiRNAs in the repeated FgAMA1 region in 7-dpf perithecia of the TR-FgAMA1 Fgrid strain (Fig. 7B), highlighting the critical role of FgRID in trasiRNA production.

The proportion of asci with oval ascospores increased in 10-dpf perithecia of the TR-FgAMA1 Fgrid strain (Fig. 7A). Sequencing of PCR products from 15 oval ascospore progeny of the TR-FgAMA1 Fgrid strain did not detect any RIP mutants in the repeated FgAMA1 regions (fig. S6), confirming that the mutant phenotype is attributed to TRISS rather than RIP. Since later-formed ascospores undergo more mitotic divisions during the dikaryotic stage (59), this suggests that the dikaryotic stage is critical for TRISS.

TRISS requires the catalytic activity of FgRid but is independent of RIP

The putative DNA methyltransferase domain of Rid orthologs contains a conserved glutamate (E) residue in Motif VI, essential for catalytic activity (Fig. 7C) (3). To investigate the role of FgRid’s catalytic activity in TRISS, we generated a catalytically inactive mutant (FgRIDE408A) and transformed it with tandem repeats of FgAMA1. Similar to the TR-FgAMA1 Fgrid strain, the TR-FgAMA1 FgRIDE408A strain exhibited a low occurrence of oval ascospores in 7-dpf perithecia (Fig. 7D), indicating that the catalytic activity of FgRid is essential for TRISS.

We also identified a functional divergence site between Rid and Dim2 orthologs, where Rid orthologs have a serine (S) residue while Dim2 orthologs have a glycine (G) residue (Fig. 7C). In TR-FgAMA1 FgRIDS366G strains, TRISS remained intact, as oval ascospores were readily observed (Fig. 7D). However, RIP was abolished in the TR-FgAMA1 Fgqde1 FgRIDS366G strain as no oval ascospores were observed in its 10-dpf perithecia (Fig. 7D), indicating that the S366G mutation disrupts RIP but not TRISS. These findings demonstrate that TRISS relies on the catalytic activity of FgRid but operates independently of RIP.

FgRid promotes the recruitment of FgQde1 and FgQde3 to tandem repeat regions

In N. crassa, Qde-1 preferentially binds ssDNA via its DdRP activity and ssRNA via its RdRP activity, but it is inactivated when dsDNA is used as a template (24, 60). To investigate the association of FgRid and FgQde1 with tandem repeats, we used the DamID method (61), which uses the DNA adenine methyltransferase (Dam) from Escherichia coli to probe DNA-protein interactions. We expressed an FgRid-Dam fusion protein in situ or Dam alone at the FG1G36140 locus in the TR-FgAMA1 strain and assessed adenine-methylated FgAMA1 using digestion with methylation-sensitive restriction enzymes followed by qPCR (DamID-qPCR) (62).

Analysis of genomic DNA from 7-dpf perithecia treated with MboI, which specifically digests unmethylated GATC sites but not methylated ones, revealed that FgRid-Dam was enriched nearly fivefold at the tandem repeat of FgAMA1 compared to Dam alone (Fig. 8A). This enrichment was not observed when the genomic DNA was treated with Sau3AI, which digests both methylated and unmethylated GATC sites, serving as a negative control. In addition, FgRid-Dam was not enriched at the IGR in the tested strains (fig. S7), providing further validation as a negative control. Using the same approach, we observed that FgQde1-Dam was also enriched at the tandem repeat region of FgAMA1 (Fig. 8B). These findings indicate that both FgRid and FgQde1 are associated with tandem repeats. Notably, deletion of FgRID abolished the association of FgQde1 with the FgAMA1 tandem repeat as no enrichment of FgQde1-Dam was detected in the TR-FgAMA1 FgQDE1-Dam Fgrid strain (Fig. 8B). These results suggest that FgQde1 is specifically recruited to tandem repeat regions, and this recruitment is facilitated by FgRid.

Qde-3 is thought to unwind duplex DNA and DNA/RNA hybrids using its adenosine triphosphate–dependent 3′–5′ DNA helicase activity, which generates ssDNA and ssRNA templates for Qde-1 (24, 63). FgQde3-Dam was also enriched at the tandem repeat region of FgAMA1, and this association depended on the catalytic activity of FgRid but not on its RIP function. FgQde3-Dam enrichment was lost in the Fgrid and FgRIDE408A mutants but was still observed in the FgRIDS366G mutants (Fig. 8, C and D). Therefore, FgRid is also critical for recruiting FgQde3 to tandem repeat regions.

FgRid interacts with RPA and requires FgQde3 for this interaction

To identify proteins associated with FgRid, we generated transformants expressing a C-terminal, 3×FLAG–tagged FgRid (FgRid-3×FLAG) under the control of the RP30 promoter. FgRid-3×FLAG and its interacting proteins were copurified from total protein extracts isolated from 7-dpf perithecia using anti-FLAG affinity beads, followed by mass spectrometry analysis. Among the copurified proteins, we identified FgRpa1, a homolog of the RPA subunit 1. To confirm the interaction between FgRid and FgRpa1, we performed coimmunoprecipitation (Co-IP) experiments using strains expressing FgRid-3×FLAG and C-terminal GFP–tagged FgRpa1 (FgRpa1-GFP) under the RP30 promoter. Since FgRID contains a premature stop codon (PSC) editing site and only expresses the full-length functional protein during sexual development after RNA editing (39), the edited FgRIDTGG allele was used in the FgRid-3×FLAG construct. Western blots confirmed that FgRpa1 interacts with FgRid in vivo (Fig. 8E).

In humans, RPA interacts with BLM and WRN, helicase homologs of Qde-3, to enhance their DNA unwinding activity (64, 65). In Neurospora, RPA-1 is known to interact with both Qde-1 and Qde-3, with the interaction between Qde-1 and RPA-1 requiring Qde-3 (24). To confirm these interactions in F. graminearum, we performed Co-IP experiments using strains expressing C-terminal 3×FLAG–tagged FgRpa1 (FgRpa1-3×FLAG) along with N-terminal GFP–tagged FgQde1 (GFP-FgQde1) or FgQde3 (GFP-FgQde3). We confirmed that FgRpa1 interacts with FgQde1 (Fig. 8F). However, GFP-FgQde3 could not be detected by Western blot, making Co-IP assays for FgQde3 challenging. Despite this limitation, we demonstrated that the interaction between FgQde1 and FgRpa1 requires FgQde3 (Fig. 8F). Deletion of FgQDE3 disrupted the association of FgQde1 with the FgAMA1 tandem repeat (Fig. 8G), highlighting the critical role of FgQde3 in recruiting FgQde1 to ssDNA within tandem repeats. It is likely that FgQde3 and FgRpa1 generate the ssDNA template for FgQde1 in TRISS, similar to the quelling process in Neurospora. In addition, the interaction between FgRid and FgRpa1 also requires FgQde3 (Fig. 8E), suggesting that FgQde3 mediates their interaction either through direct protein-protein interaction or by providing an interaction condition.

FgSms2 also interacts with FgRpa1 and requires FgQde3 for this interaction

To investigate the role of FgSms2 in trasiRNA production, we performed mass spectrometry analysis on FgSms2–3×FLAG transformants expressing a C-terminal 3×FLAG–tagged FgSms2 in 7-dpf perithecia. FgRpa1 was identified as a copurified protein. Using strains expressing FgRpa1-GFP and N-terminal hemagglutinin (HA)–tagged FgSms2 (HA-FgSms2) under the RP30 promoter, we confirmed the in vivo interaction between FgSms2 and FgRpa1 through Co-IP experiments (Fig. 8E). Furthermore, this interaction was shown to depend on FgQde3 (Fig. 8E). Since FgRid is only expressed during sexual stages due to PSC editing, the observed interaction between FgSms2 and FgRpa1 in hyphae suggests that this interaction is independent of FgRid. In addition, native FgSMS2 expression is minimal in hyphae (fig. S8), and its overexpression did not enhance the interaction between FgRid and FgRpa1 (Fig. 8H), indicating that FgSms2 is not required for this interaction.

In summary, our findings reveal two distinct mechanisms that enhance the TRISS response, independent of traditional transitive siRNA amplification (Figs. 8I and 9). The first mechanism involves FgRid, which facilitates the recruitment of the FgRpa1-FgQde1 complex to ssDNA in tandem repeats by interacting with FgRpa1. The second mechanism involves the interaction between FgSms2 and FgRpa1, which promotes the recruitment of the FgRpa1-FgQde1 complex to ssDNA. This process is guided by trasiRNA-programmed FgSms2 to target ssRNA molecules produced in tandem repeat regions.

DISCUSSION

Genome defense systems are critical for restricting the proliferation of selfish genetic elements and ensuring genomic stability, particularly during sexual reproduction when genomes from two distinct parental sources combine. In filamentous ascomycetes, two well-known genome defense mechanisms, RIP and MSUD, are active during the sexual cycle. RIP functions by introducing C-to-T mutations on both DNA strands of repetitive sequences in a paired manner, effectively inactivating both linked and unlinked repetitive elements within a haploid parental genome (3, 19, 28, 31). Meanwhile, MSUD acts during meiosis by identifying unpaired DNA segments between homologous chromosomes and silencing all homologous copies posttranscriptionally through RNAi (3, 21, 27). The genome of F. graminearum is characterized by a low abundance of repetitive sequences, a feature partially attributed to the activity of RIP (30, 66, 67). However, the efficiency of RIP in F. graminearum is substantially lower than that observed in N. crassa (32, 66). Moreover, given that F. graminearum is predominantly self-fertile and rarely outcrosses, the natural activation of MSUD is predicted to be infrequent.

In this study, we identified and characterized TRISS, an RNAi-dependent genome defense system in F. graminearum. TRISS is specifically triggered by tandem repeats and primarily functions to repress the translation of repeated genes during sexual reproduction. This mechanism operates from the dikaryotic stage through ascosporogenesis, overlapping with the stages during which both RIP and MSUD are active. Unlike MSUD, which is predominantly active during outcrossing, TRISS functions during both selfing and outcrossing. Our results indicate that TRISS operates independently of the mode of tandem-repeat formation, suggesting that it could potentially target suitable tandem repeats derived from both endogenous transposons and exogenous nucleic invaders. These findings position TRISS as an additional genome defense mechanism that may contribute to the remarkably low repeat content observed in the F. graminearum genome.

The TRISS mechanism in F. graminearum shares notable similarities with the quelling pathway observed during vegetative growth in N. crassa. Both systems are triggered by tandem transgene repeats and use similar RNAi components, including Qde1, Qde3, Dcl1, Dcl2, and Qip (21, 22). However, there are key distinctions. While Qde-2 is essential for quelling, FgSms2, an Ago protein specific to MSUD, is necessary for TRISS. Despite the conservation of these components, quelling has not been observed in F. graminearum. This is evidenced by a TR-PKS12 transformant containing eight tandem repeats of PKS12, which displayed a wild-type phenotype (Figs. 1J and 4C), indicating minimal or absent quelling activity during vegetative growth.

Another major difference between TRISS and quelling lies in their dependence on HR. While quelling requires DSB-triggered HR of repetitive sequences (22, 23), TRISS does not. In addition, MSUD and recombination-independent quelling (RIQ), a recently reported quelling-associated process in N. crassa, do not rely on DSB-triggered HR but depend on Sad-6, a conserved SWItch/Sucrose Non-Fermentable (SWI/SNF) chromatin remodeler (68–70). However, our findings indicate that FgSad6 is not required for TRISS (fig. S1). Furthermore, while Qde-2 is not involved in siRNA production during quelling and RIQ (22, 53, 68), FgSms2 is indispensable for siRNA production in TRISS. The discovery of TRISS unveils an RNAi pathway in filamentous ascomycetes that is distinct from established RNAi pathways.

SIS is another RNAi mechanism triggered by tandem transgene arrays, identified in both opposite-sex and unisexual reproduction in the basidiomycete yeast C. neoformans (18, 71). Unlike TRISS, which is restricted to sexual stages, SIS can propagate in progeny during vegetative growth for many generations once initiated. Notably, TRISS appears to be a more efficient genome defense mechanism than SIS and quelling. A single tandem repeat is sufficient to completely silence the targeted gene in TRISS, whereas this is insufficient to trigger quelling in N. crassa (22) or SIS in C. neoformans (18, 71).

While miRNAs typically repress gene expression through translational repression, siRNAs are generally known to induce mRNA cleavage (72). Recent studies in plants and animals have shown that siRNAs can also mediate translational suppression (73–77). However, genome defense mechanisms involving translational repression are rarely documented. In this study, we demonstrate that trasiRNAs in F. graminearum predominantly mediate translational repression, providing a clear example of translational repression being used as a mechanism for genomic defense. Contrary to the traditional inference that imperfect base pairing between small RNAs and target RNAs favors translational repression (72), our results show that trasiRNAs, despite being perfectly complementary to their target RNAs, effectively mediate translational repression but are less efficient at inducing target cleavage. Our findings suggest that the base pairing between trasiRNAs and their target RNAs may cause ribosome stalling, although the precise mechanism remains to be elucidated. It is possible that other factors associated with the FgSms2-trasiRNA complex contribute to facilitating this translational repression.

In various organisms such as fission yeast (14), Mucor (78), nematodes (13), and plants (76), Ago-mediated slicing not only cleaves target RNAs but also initiates transitivity by recruiting RdRP to synthesize dsRNAs, which are then processed into secondary siRNAs, amplifying the RNAi response. However, our study found no evidence of transitive secondary siRNA production in the FgSms2-dependent trasiRNA pathway. This deviation from the classical model is likely attributable to Qde-1, which has both DdRP and RdRP activities (24), enabling it to independently generate aRNAs and dsRNAs without requiring transitivity. Our study demonstrates that the slicer activity of FgSms2 is crucial for trasiRNA production. Ago’s slicer activity is known to play a role in removing the passenger strand from siRNA duplexes (53, 79). Our findings show that FgSms2 interacts with FgRpa1, and FgRpa1 interacts with FgQde1. This suggests that siRNAs may guide FgSms2 to complementary target mRNA or aRNA scaffolds, facilitating the recruitment of FgRpa1 and FgQde1 to ssDNA in tandemly repeated regions. This recruitment initiates aRNA and dsRNA production, amplifying the silencing signal without relying on transitive RNAi.

Previous studies have identified self-reinforcing positive feedback loops linking siRNAs to DNA cytosine and H3K9 methylation in organisms like Schizosaccharomyces pombe, N. crassa, Arabidopsis, and mammals (12, 57). However, these epigenetic marks are barely detectable in F. graminearum (35), and our results confirm that TRISS functions independently of DNA cytosine and H3K9 methylation. Given that RPA is a conserved eukaryotic ssDNA binding protein (80) and that the essential RNAi components in TRISS are conserved, at least in filamentous ascomycetes (fig. S9), the positive feedback loop involving the interaction between Sms2 and RPA might represent a conserved mechanism in these organisms.

Rid has long been recognized as a key mediator of RIP (32), but its connection to RNAi mechanisms was previously unknown. Our study revealed that FgRid plays a crucial role in TRISS, likely by recruiting the FgQde1-FgRpa1 complex to ssDNA within tandem repeats. In N. crassa, siRNAs derived from endogenous transposon relics that have undergone RIP have been detected in the vegetative stages (81). Similar to our findings for TRISS, the presence of these siRNAs depends on functional Qde-1, Qde-3, and two dicers, but not Qde-2 (81). Although siRNA production during sexual stages and the role of Sms2 were not directly assessed, an increase in siRNA production was observed in the qde-2 mutant (81), possibly due to the induction of SMS-2 expression following the deletion of QDE-2, as SMS-2 is predominantly expressed during sexual stages (82). Therefore, a TRISS-like mechanism may also operate in N. crassa, although its efficiency may be less critical than in F. graminearum. Unlike in F. graminearum, the RID gene in N. crassa does not contain PSC-editing sites in its coding regions and can express the full-length functional protein outside sexual stages (39), which might account for the lower amount of siRNAs detected in vegetative stages. Given the conservation of both TRISS and RIP components among filamentous ascomycetes (fig. S9), it is plausible that the coupling of RIP and RNAi mechanisms to inactivate tandem repeats at both genetic and posttranscriptional levels during sexual stages is a common strategy in these organisms. This dual mechanism could provide a robust means of maintaining genomic stability by effectively silencing potentially harmful genetic elements.

RIP was found nearly four decades ago (29), yet the precise mechanism underlying the C-to-T mutations remains unresolved. Recent studies have shown that RPA can protect ssDNA from uracil DNA glycosylases (UNG), enabling C-to-T mutagenesis by allowing replicative DNA polymerases in yeast to faithfully copy uracil (U) bases formed by C-to-U Apolipoprotein B mRNA-Editing Catalytic Polypeptide-like (APOBEC) deaminases (83). Our discovery of the interaction between FgRid and FgRpa1 provides insights into the Rid-mediated RIP mechanism. This interaction suggests that RPA may play a protective role by shielding T or U bases generated by Rid in ssDNA from the actions of thymine-DNA glycosylase and UNG, thereby facilitating the mutagenic process. The recruitment of FgQde3 to tandem repeat regions relies on the catalytic activity of FgRid but is independent of RIP-induced mutations. This observation suggests that FgQde3 may recognize specific intermediates generated by Rid, thereby initiating DNA unwinding in these regions.

Besides their role in RIP, Rid orthologs have been reported to be essential for sexual development in many filamentous ascomycetes (84–87). Similarly, Sms2 orthologs are critical for sexual development in various filamentous ascomycetes (49, 82, 88, 89). However, the mechanisms by which these proteins regulate sexual development remain unclear. The catalytic activity of PaRid in Podospora anserina is essential for sexual development (87), consistent with our finding that FgRid catalytic activity is crucial for TRISS. Our observation that FgRid functions in TRISS suggests that Rid orthologs may regulate sexual development through RNAi mechanisms. Furthermore, the discovery of RPA interacting with both Sms2 and Rid offers valuable insights into their roles in fungal sexual development. Given that RPA is a multifunctional protein involved in DNA replication, repair, and other DNA-related activities (80), these interactions suggest that Sms2 and Rid may have broader roles in coordinating crucial DNA processes during sexual development, potentially linking RNAi pathways with DNA maintenance and modification.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Strains and culture conditions

The wild-type strain PH-1 of F. graminearum (66), along with its derived mutants and transformants (data S1), were routinely cultured on potato dextrose agar (PDA) plates at 25°C. For transformant selection, we added hygromycin B (H005, MDbio, China) and geneticin (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO) to Top agar medium (0.3% yeast extract, 0.3% casamino acids, 20% sucrose, and 1.5% agar) to final concentrations of 300 and 200 μg/ml, respectively. We assayed growth rate and colony morphology on PDA plates after 3 days. We harvested conidia from 3-day-old liquid carboxymethylcellulose medium cultures inoculated with mycelial plugs at 25°C using a rotary shaker set at 175 rpm. We obtained vegetative hyphae for DNA and RNA isolation and protoplast preparation from 12- to 24-hour liquid yeast extract peptone dextrose cultures. For selfing in sexual reproduction, we pressed 5-day-old aerial hyphae grown on carrot agar plates down with 500 μl of sterile 0.1% Tween-20 and subsequently cultured them under black light at 25°C. For outcrossing, we fertilized the female strains grown on carrot agar plates with 1 ml of conidial suspension (1 × 106 conidia/ml) derived from the male strains. We observed ascogenous hyphae, asci, and ascospores with an Olympus BX-51 microscope in squash mounts of perithecia 7 dpf.

Generation of gene deletion, complementation, and tandem repeat transformants

We used polyethylene glycol–mediated protoplast transformation to generate various transformants of F. graminearum. For gene deletion, we used the split-marker approach (90, 91). We amplified fragments of approximately 1.0 kb upstream and downstream of the target genes and connected them to the N- and C-terminal regions of either a hygromycin-resistance marker or a recyclable marker module (92) that confers resistance to hygromycin and sensitivity to the nucleoside analog 5-fluoro-2′-deoxyuridine (floxuridine) through overlapping PCR. After transforming PH-1 protoplasts, we screened hygromycin-resistant transformants and confirmed them via PCR assays. For gene complementation, we amplified the full-length genes, along with their native promoter, and cloned them into a pFL2 vector. We confirmed the complementation construct by DNA sequencing and then transformed it into the protoplasts of the corresponding deletion mutants. We screened transformants containing the complementation constructs with geneticin and confirmed them using PCR assays.

To perform site-directed mutagenesis of FgSMS2 and FgRID, we first generated gene deletion mutants using the recyclable marker module. We then created allelic fragments with the desired mutations through overlapping PCR. We subsequently transformed the resulting fragments into protoplasts of the corresponding deletion mutants. We screened transformants for resistance to floxuridine (25 μg/ml; HY-B0097, MCE, USA) and confirmed the presence of the desired mutations by DNA sequencing. In addition, we used qPCR to ensure that no unintended integration of allelic fragments occurred in the transformants.

To generate transformants containing tandem transgenic repeats, we connected the target gene fragment (either full-length or partial) to a hygromycin (hyg)– or G418 (neo)–resistance marker through overlapping PCR. We then transformed the resulting linear fragment into protoplasts of PH-1 or specific gene deletion mutants. We screened transformants with hygromycin or G418 resistance and assayed tandem repeats by PCR with specific primer pairs. We used the split-marker approach to generate direct or reverse tandem repeats of CDC2A at either the native locus or the FG1G36140 locus in PH-1. We screened transformants for resistance to hygromycin or geneticin and confirmed them using PCR assays. To generate transformants ectopically expressing antisense transcripts of CDC2A at the FG1G36140 locus, we amplified fragments of approximately 1.0 kb upstream and downstream of the FG1G36140 gene. We then connected these fragments to the reverse complementary fragment of the CDC2A coding region under the control of the PR27 promoter through overlapping PCR and transformed them into protoplasts of the FG1G36140 deletion mutant, where the FG1G36140 gene had been replaced by the recyclable marker module. We screened transformants for resistance to floxuridine. To generate transformants containing tandemly repeated FgAMA1 with a nongene linker, we first transformed a linear fragment containing the FgAMA1 gene connected with the recyclable marker module into PH-1 protoplasts. This allowed us to obtain transformants containing tandemly repeated FgAMA1 with the recyclable marker module as the linker. We then amplified fragments corresponding to the 3′-end of the upstream repeat and the 5′-end of the downstream repeat and connected these fragments to a nontranscriptional region from the pKNT plasmid. We used the resulting linear fragment to replace the recyclable marker module in the previous transformants. Data S1 list all the strains generated in this study, while data S2 provide a comprehensive list of the primers used.

Isolation of ascospore progeny and characterization of RIP mutations

To isolate ascospore progeny from the Fgama1 uncomplemented strain and the TR-FgAMA1 and TR-FgAMA1 Fgrid transformants, we collected ascospore cirrhi from 10-dpf perithecia under a dissecting microscope. We resuspended the cirrhi in 1 ml of sterile distilled water in a 1.5-ml centrifuge tube. We then spread the ascospore suspensions on 2% water agar and isolated individual oval ascospores, which were subsequently transferred onto PDA plates. To identify RIP mutations in the ascospore progeny, we extracted genomic DNA from each ascospore progeny using cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) solution. We amplified the FgAMA1 gene from the isolated DNA and sequenced it using Sanger sequencing. For the Fgama1 uncomplemented strain, we could not distinguish which repeats of FgAMA1 were amplified. For the TR-FgAMA1 and TR-FgAMA1 Fgrid transformants, we used specific primer pairs (data S2) to distinguish the upstream and downstream repeats of FgAMA1. We visualized the Sanger sequencing traces using SnapGene Viewer 4.3 (www.snapgene.com/snapgene-viewer/) and manually checked the quality of the sequencing. We aligned the assembled high-quality sequences for each repeat to the wild-type sequence of the FgAMA1 gene. We used these aligned sequences to detect RIP mutants in each ascospore progeny and analyzed their consequences on protein sequences using the Biopython package (https://biopython.org/).

Analysis of repeat copy number and gene expression

We extracted genomic DNA from 24-hour hyphae using a CTAB solution. We isolated total RNA from 24-hour hyphae or 7-dpf perithecia using the TRIzol reagent kit (Invitrogen, USA). Following this, we synthesized cDNA using the First cDNA Synthesis Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. We then amplified the resulting cDNA products and genomic DNA by real-time PCR using the primers listed in data S2. We calculated repeat copy numbers and relative expression levels using the 2−ΔΔCT method, with the GzUBH gene serving as the internal control.

Western blot analysis

We ground samples from 24-hour vegetative hyphae or 7-dpf perithecia into a fine powder in liquid nitrogen and homogenized them in protein extraction buffer. We then centrifuged the samples for 15 min at 12,000 rpm at 4°C, heating the supernatants for 5 min in boiling water. Next, we separated the supernatants on 10% SDS–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) gels and transferred the proteins to nitrocellulose membranes. We performed Western blot assays to analyze the samples using specific antibodies, including anti-GFP (Abcam, ab290), anti-Tub1 (93), and anti–glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH) (Sangon Biotech, D1100160200). We used detection with the anti-GAPDH antibody as the loading control.

sRNA-seq analysis

For sRNA-seq analysis, we isolated total RNA from 7-dpf perithecia using the TRIzol Reagent kit (Invitrogen, USA) and quantified it with the Nanodrop 2000c spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). We assessed RNA quality and integrity using the Experion RNA Standard Sense kit (Bio-Rad, USA), ensuring that all RNA samples had an RNA quality index ≥8. We prepared the sRNA library using the TruSeq Small RNA Sample Prep kit (Illumina, USA) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Specifically, we ligated 1 μg of total RNA from each sample to specific adapters, reverse transcribed it, and amplified it to obtain a population of double-stranded cDNAs. During PCR amplification, we used barcode-tagged primer pairs to uniquely tag each sample. We then size-selected the resulting cDNAs on a 6% PAGE gel, excised the gel band corresponding to the target fragments, ethanol-precipitated them, and eluted them in ultrapure water. We evaluated the libraries for quality using the Agilent 2100 BioAnalyzer (Agilent, USA). Last, we carried out sequencing reactions using the MiSeq instrument (Illumina, USA) in 50-bp single-end read mode at Novogene Bioinformatics Institute (Tianjin, China), obtaining at least 10 million reads for each library. The information regarding the sRNA-seq data generated in this study has been listed in table S1.

We obtained the reference genome and annotation files of F. graminearum PH-1 from FgBase (http://fgbase.wheatscab.com/) (94). We aligned sRNA-seq reads to the tandem repeats and the PH-1 genome using HISAT2 v2.2.1 (95). We analyzed detailed information on aligned reads using bioawk (https://github.com/lh3/bioawk) and samtools (http://htslib.org/). The length distribution and base preference were visualized using the matplotlib (https://matplotlib.org/) and seaborn (https://seaborn.pydata.org/) packages. Using bioawk, we filtered reads of 20 to 27 nt with a 5′ preferred U. We obtained BAM files of aligned reads using samtools and converted them to BigWig coverage files with deeptools2 (https://deeptools.readthedocs.io/). We parsed the BigWig coverage files using the pyBigWig Python package (https://github.com/deeptools/pyBigWig) and plotted the sRNA coverage using the matplotlib package.

DamID-qPCR

We isolated genomic DNA from 24-hour hyphae and 7-dpf perithecia using a CTAB solution. For each sample, we digested 1 μg of genomic DNA from the strain expressing a Dam or Dam fusion with 1 μl of MboI (Thermo Fisher Scientific, ER0811) for 16 hours in a total reaction volume of 20 μl. We then analyzed the digested samples by qPCR. To normalize the DNA content, we amplified a DNA fragment without GATC sites. We also amplified DNA fragments containing GATC sites to determine whether they were cut by MboI. We calculated the enrichment of the Dam fusion protein on target DNA using the 2−ΔΔCT method by comparing the cycle threshold (Ct) values of each sample with that of the control (Dam only protein). As a negative control, we treated samples with Sau3AI as described above.

Polysome profiling analysis

We froze samples from 7-dpf perithecia in liquid nitrogen immediately after collection. We ground the tissue samples into a fine powder in liquid nitrogen and added an equal volume of the tissue powder from each sample to an equal volume of lysis buffer, which contained 100 mM KCl, 20 mM Hepes-KOH (pH 7.5), 10 mM magnesium acetate (pH 7), 15 mM 2-mercaptoethanol (BME), cycloheximide (100 μg/ml), and a protease inhibitor cocktail including phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (1:100), leupeptin (1:1000), pepstatin (1:1000), and dithiothreitol (1:1000). We centrifuged the lysates at 15,000 rpm for 10 min. We measured the absorbance at 260 nm of the supernatant using a NanoDrop Microvolume Spectrophotometer and obtained 500 μl of lysate with the same optical density (20 OD260nm) for each sample. From each sample, we used 50 μl of lysate as input, while the remaining lysate was loaded onto a 10 to 50% sucrose gradient buffer containing 20 mM Hepes (pH 7.6), 0.1 M KCl, 5 mM MgCl2, cycloheximide (10 μg/ml), 1× protease inhibitor cocktail, and SUPERase•In (10 U/ml).

We centrifuged the sucrose gradients at 35,000 rpm for 2 hours at 4°C using a SW41Ti rotor in a Beckman Coulter Optima L-80 ultracentrifuge. After ultracentrifugation, polysomal RNAs distributed along the sucrose gradient according to their different sedimentation coefficients and were separated using a gradient fractionator system (BioComp Instruments). We collected fractions containing monosomes to the heaviest polysomes and subjected them to RNA isolation using TRIzol followed by qPCR. We also isolated RNA from input lysates using TRIzol followed by qPCR. We normalized the polysome distributions of CDC2A mRNA levels against their own input mRNA levels and calculated CDC2A expression in each polysomal fraction as a percentage of its expression in total RNA.

Coimmunoprecipitation

To assess the interaction of FgRid and FgSms2 with FgRpa1 using a Co-IP assay, we first created the TR-FgAMA1 Fgrid and TR-FgAMA1 Fgsms2 strains. This was accomplished by deleting the FgRID and FgSMS2 genes in the TR-FgAMA1 strain, respectively, using a recyclable marker module. We PCR amplified approximately 1 kb of the upstream and downstream regions of FgRID and FgSMS2 and linked them to the FgRID-3×FLAG and HA-FgSMS2 fusion constructs, respectively, under the control of the RP30 promoter through overlapping PCR. We then transformed these PCR products into the protoplasts of the TR-FgAMA1 Fgrid and TR-FgAMA1 Fgsms2 strains. We screened transformants of FgRID-3×FLAG TR-FgAMA1 and HA-FgSMS2 TR-FgAMA1 using floxuridine (25 μg/ml; HY-B0097, MCE, USA). Using a similar method, we sequentially generated the HA-FgSMS2 FgRID-3×FLAG TR-FgAMA1 and HA-FgSMS2 FgRID-3×FLAG TR-FgAMA1 Fgqde3 strains in the FgRID-3×FLAG TR-FgAMA1 background. In each strain, we created the FgRRA1-GFP construct by inserting the GFP sequence before the stop codon of the FgRPA1 gene using a hygromycin resistance marker.

To investigate the interaction between FgQde1 and FgRpa1 in the Co-IP assay, we constructed the FgRPA1–3×FLAG fusion construct under the control of its native promoter and the GFP-FgQDE1 fusion construct under the control of the RP27 promoter by inserting their respective sequences into the pKNTF and pDL2 vectors. We then cotransformed these constructs into both the wild type and the Fgqde3 mutant strains, selecting transformants with hygromycin and G418 resistance.

For each strain, we extracted total proteins from 24-hour hyphae. We incubated the supernatant containing the target protein with anti-GFP affinity beads (Smart-lifesciences, SA070001) and subsequently eluted the proteins. We performed Western blots on both the total proteins and the proteins eluted from the anti-GFP affinity beads, using anti-FLAG (F31651, Sigma-Aldrich), anti-GFP (Abcam, ab290), anti-HA (AE008, Abclonal), and anti-GAPDH antibodies for detection.
