Abstract

In order to focus light at a desired depth, wavefront shaping of the incident light is required. Light focusing is used in applications such as medical imaging, sensing, power dividers, etc. For this purpose, various phase plates can be exploited, but we propose a periodic phase plate. The reason for considering periodic structures is to profit from wavefront shaping of several periods to focus light at larger depths, which can be a challenging task without a periodic phase plate. Using this method, the focus quality is such that its Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM) reaches in the order of the diffraction limit, while maximizing power concentration. We also assume that the phase plate elements are of micron-scale, so that it does not require nanofabrication. The modelling and analysis method used in this paper accurately takes the coupling among adjacent phase plate elements into account, i.e., it does not rely on local phase approximation. Since the introduced phase plate has a periodic structure, the focusing is repeated periodically, which finds several applications. Using our computational technique, one is able to rigorously analyze and design phase plates as wide as . In a sample design, we show that of the illuminated power reaches a depth of or 1m at a wavelength of . Approximately of the incident power is focused within only of the unit cell width. Moreover, despite large depth of focusing, its FWHM is in the order of the diffraction limit. In this paper, in addition to achieving light focusing at larger depths, we also demonstrate that the proposed methodology can be utilized for focusing with a FWHM close to the diffraction limit down to , double focusing, and focusing with a high Numerical Aperture (NA) of 0.97.

Introduction

Metasurfaces are arrays of subwavelength scatterers that are thin, lightweight, multifunctional, and capable of integration into optical systems1–5. These structures, often referred to also as metalenses, metamasks, or phase plates, have attracted significant attention for their applications in optical wavefront manipulation leading to light focusing at a given depth6–8. Furthermore, they also serve as phase correction elements in Spatial Light Modulators (SLM)9,10. They are widely used in other applications such as ultrathin lenses11, biological imaging12–14, hyperthermia15, sensing16, power dividers17, optical tweezers18–20, photonic jet21, and optical communication22 to name a few. This article focuses on periodic phase plates the periodicity of which enables light focusing at significantly large depths through the contribution of several unit cells. Without such contribution, achieving deep focusing would be considerably challenging. To analyze these structures, we employ the rigorous and full-wave Transmission-Line Formulation (TLF) method which is specifically designed for periodic structures. Our goal is to design a periodic phase plate capable of focusing light at depths as large as 1 m (equivalent to at a wavelength of ) through leveraging the contribution of several unit cells. The aim of this design is to maintain a FWHM in the order of the diffraction limit while maximizing power concentration.

To take fabrication simplicity into account, we concentrate on the design of those periodic phase plates which have a binary structure. We name them as Binary Periodic Phase Plate (BPPP). Unlike most methods that use nanometer-scale scatterers in their structure, our design employs micron-scale scatterers, eliminating the need for complex nanofabrication23. We are also aware that the usually small spacing of the phase plate scatterers creates significant coupling effects. Existing analysis methods often approximate structure modelling through ignoring interactions among adjacent elements. For example, the unit cell approach11,24, is a method where one assumes that the electromagnetic field is highly confined to the interior of the scatterers due to their high refractive index. This reduces coupling among nearby scatterers, allowing the phase at each position of the structure to depend only on the scatterer existing at that position. Other methods, such as Locally Periodic Approximation (LPA)25 and Overlapping-Domain Approximation (ODA)26, model the problem using local phase approximation. Modelling large-scale structures while exactly considering the mutual interaction of their elements can be difficult, which is why there are limited exact methods that take the coupling of adjacent elements into account. The Coupled-Mode Theory (CMT)27 is one such exact modelling methods. In the present work, to address this issue, we use the TLF, a technique that accurately models dielectric periodic structures. The TLF has been successfully applied to various problems28–30.

The advantage of our proposed focusing solution is, on the one hand, the accuracy of the computational technique, and on the other hand, the relative ease of fabrication due to using micrometer-scale features. However, due to the limited degrees of freedom in the assumed binary structure, we face certain limitations in achieving demanding design goals. Moreover, our BPPP provides periodically repeated focused beams which has applications such as simultaneous multiple-imaging and power division. Materials and methods

Problem modelling

In Fig. 1, the BPPP under study is seen. Its unit cell has a width of with a periodicity along the x-axis. It is symmetric with respect to the z-axis and invariant along the y-axis. The unit cell has a simple structure consisting of a layer of teeth with a height of placed on a substrate with a height of . The teeth pattern is specified as either full or empty (binary). TLF modeling supports analysis of arbitrary periodic structures (e.g., triangular37, square and circular38), but binary designs uniquely combine fabrication simplicity with mechanical robustness, making them ideal for low-cost applications. Obviously, an incident plane wave will be diffracted by this structure into several diffraction orders. In this paper, we show that through adjusting the amplitude and phase of these diffraction orders, we can shape the wavefront passing through the BPPP and focus the incident light at a given depth. To facilitate manufacturing, the teeth dimensions are assumed to be on the micron-scale. As shown in Fig. 1, a TE-polarized plane wave, represented as with a time variation of , is incident on the structure. The goal is to focus the incident plane wave to equally spaced spots with a FWHM in the order of the diffraction limit at a depth of d. The BPPP design is carried out by means of a rigorous full-wave computational technique without relying on local phase approximation. We will show that increasing the contribution of several unit cells results in stronger light focusing along with high transmittance, and appropriate focusing efficiency. The main goal of the present research work is to conduct a feasibility study whether a micron-scale structure can perform similar to nano-scale structures to focus electromagnetic waves almost arbitrarily. To this end, 2D structures are chosen as the starting point. Note that the computational technique used in our study, i.e., TLF, is capable to analyze 3D problems where periodicity is in two directions. See, for instance,38. We would like to add that using 3D phase plates with 2D periodicity for focusing purposes is the subject of our current research.

Designing BPPP using a combination of TLF-analysis and PSO-algorithm

As shown in Fig. 3, the BPPP design process is performed in three stages and involves combining of full-wave and rigorous TLF-analysis and the PSO-algorithm. In the first stage, the structure modelling and analysis is performed using TLF, as explained in the previous section. The modeling process starts with the input parameters listed in Block 1 of Fig. 3. It concludes by computing output parameters, including the reflected and transmitted diffraction orders for each layer of the structure. Using these diffraction orders, all characteristics related to electromagnetic (EM) fields can be extracted. In the second stage, the PSO-algorithm is used. The PSO configuration is set to population size: 150, convergence criteria: 0.001 and no limit on the number of iterations. A small population size reduces search accuracy, while a large population size increases computation time. Our experiments in the present problem show that by selecting a population size of 150, we strike a balance among various effects, achieving optimal results. The number of teeth is scaled with the structure period while maintaining a minimum feature size greater than to satisfy standard fabrication constraints. Due to the symmetric nature of the BPPP, only half of the structure is designed. For instance, periods of and correspond to 30 and 240 teeth per half-period, respectively. The optimization process begins with an initial guess of binary variables (representing tooth states) and iteratively refines them to converge toward the optimal pattern. Additionally, both the substrate () and tooth heights () are treated as optimization variables. In terms of convergence behavior, convergence is achieved in approximately 10 minutes for a period of and 27 hours for a period of . As a resonant structure, the optimized BPPP exhibits strong sensitivity to the tooth filling pattern while demonstrating robustness against variations in both substrate and tooth heights. Our sensitivity analysis confirms that a manufacturing error (a realistic tolerance) results in merely less than variation in peak field intensity, demonstrating robustness of the design to fabrication inaccuracies. During the iterative optimization process, a high-speed solution method like TLF is essential. One reason for the time-efficient solution of this method is its use of analytically calculated Fourier series coefficients required for the BPPP structure; hence, there is no need to calculate them numerically.

In the optimization process, a Gaussian mask with a beam waist of is placed at a desired depth behind the BPPP. The power of the Gaussian mask is set equal to the incident power. Then, the difference between the numerically calculated field distribution and the desired mask is computed. If the optimal response is not obtained, the search process is repeated to achieve the minimum error. After determining the optimal BPPP, in the third stage, the diffraction orders are determined. Using them, the field distribution, transmittance, and focusing efficiency can be evaluated.

As far as BPPP material is concerned, we have chosen due to its low loss at the assumed working wavelength. On the other hand, the BPPP geometrical form is assumed to be binary whose feature size and AR are maintained in the range of typical fabrication processes as explained in what follows. Reference41 achieved transparent metalens by directly etching a substrate to fabricate nanopillars. However, studies have shown that nanopillars with an AR exceeding 6.6 tend to collapse, indicating a key limitation in directly etching substrates to achieve high-AR geometries. Alternative materials with higher refractive indices (e.g., , SiN, GaN, and ZnS) can reduce the required AR to ease fabrication. However, they introduce higher Fresnel reflection losses at air or substrate interfaces. References42 presents a method for fabricating visible-range metalenses composed of nanopillars, achieved through thermal oxidation of Si nanopillars. During this process, 210nm-wide Si pillars expand to 300nm after oxidation, achieving robust structures with AR up to 8.7 and minimal collapse. Considering the aforementioned facts, we have forced realistic and practical constraints to our optimizer by maintaining the feature size larger than and the AR smaller than 5. Hence, our optimized BPPP can be realized with the help of typical fabrication techniques.

In inverse-designed metasurfaces optimized to reflect or transmit a specific diffraction order, the target diffraction order typically exhibits near-unity diffraction efficiency, while all other orders are suppressed (close to zero). Figure 8 illustrates the diffraction efficiency for our Design 1, quantifying the percentage of incident power transferred to each diffraction order. Unlike conventional designs that maximize diffraction efficiency for a single diffraction order, our approach leverages a large number of diffraction orders to achieve fine focusing with a strong intensity peak. Figure 8 demonstrates that diffraction orders up to in our design have significant magnitude instead of being suppressed. The cumulative efficiency across all diffraction orders, representing the total transmitted power, reaches . This multi-diffraction order strategy enables subwavelength wavefront shaping, which is of prime importance for applications such as high-resolution imaging or optical trapping.

Let us consider a similar principle in the time domain, and this is the principle behind generation of laser pulses of short duration. In those lasers, one typically employs mode-locking of a large number of optical modes inside the laser cavity to achieve short pulses. The coherent participation of these modes enables generation of intense pulses with ultrashort temporal width and precise periodicity. In our research, we apply a similar principle, but instead of operating in the time domain, we work in the spatial domain. By leveraging a large number of diffraction orders in the spatial frequency domain, we achieve high-intensity focal spots with fine spatial resolution and precise periodicity. Furthermore, by controlling the amplitude and phase of these diffraction orders, we demonstrate light focusing with a given characteristic.

In TLF, to implement this kind of excitation for the proposed periodic phase plate, the input Gaussian beam is repeated periodically as shown in Fig. 16b. The period of this repetition is a large multiple of the BPPP period to avoid any interference between two adjacent Gaussian beams. The input Gaussian beam has a beam waist radius of . As mentioned above, the beam waist radius is greater than .

As explained in the previous sections, BPPP cells are designed for a normally incident plane wave. To demonstrate the contribution of BPPP cells in increasing the intensity of the focused light, we excite the BPPP with a Gaussian beam with different beam radii. This is visualized in Fig. 17 where we show that as the beam waist increases and more BPPP cells are involved, the peak intensity of the focused light increases and gradually approaches the state achieved under plane-wave illumination. If the incident wave is a plane wave, the focused beams are formed periodically with identical amplitudes. Eventually, it approaches the focused intensity achieved under plane wave excitation. In this study, the period of the BPPP unit cell is , and the desired depth of focus is 1 m. The distance between adjacent Gaussian beams is set to 4 m, which is large enough to ensure that they are almost mutually independent. As the beam waist expands and more BPPP cells contribute, the peak intensity of the focused light becomes stronger.

Conclusion

In this paper, we introduced a simple structure called BPPP, which could be used to shape the incident wavefront and to focus it at an almost arbitrary depth without the need for nanofabrication. The main idea of this paper was to utilize the contribution of several unit cells to focus the electromagnetic field at depths as large as . In most existing metalens optimization methods the coupling between adjacent elements is often ignored, and the structure is typically modeled using the local phase approximation. However, in this work, we account for all the couplings between adjacent elements without relying on the local phase approximation, resulting in significantly higher accuracy. Consequently, this leads to numerical results that align more closely with experimental expectations. Furthermore, to fully exploit the benefits of our accurate inter-element coupling model, we intentionally increased the period to enhance the number of diffraction orders. As a result, we showed that by using a large number of diffraction orders and also with the help of adjacent cells, we can achieve strong focused spots at large depths, which would have been challenging otherwise. Additionally, our precise modeling of coupling effects allowed for extremely fine field focusing, achieving a FWHM near the diffraction limit at a significant distance from the metalens. Furthermore, the high number of diffraction orders and their accurate computational treatment facilitated new functionalities such as double focusing. The proposed method is promising for several applications in realization of complex passive optical components.
