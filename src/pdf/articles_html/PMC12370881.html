Abstract

Debris flow hazard mapping (DFHM) played an important role in reducing the threat of debris flows. Conventional DFHM usually requires numerical simulations to obtain debris flow intensity, which is usually quite time-consuming. This paper is to introduce a combined application framework of numerical simulation and machine learning to improve the efficiency of DFHM. The FLO-2D model was employed to simulate debris flows with different recurrence intervals of 20 years, 50 years and 100 years, respectively. Corresponding maximum accumulation depth and flow velocity were collected as dependent variables. Rainfall with corresponding recurrence intervals, altitude, slope, plane curvature, profile curvature, topographic humidity index, normalized difference vegetation index and Manning’s coefficients were collected as independent variables. Then the 20-year, 50-year and 100-year datasets were prepared by combining the independent and dependent variables. The 20-year and 50-year datasets were used for training a machine learning model called gradient boosted decision tree (GBDT). The 100-year dataset was used for validation. The results showed that the predicted results are quite close to the simulated results, which verified the validity and rationality of the proposed method. In addition, the training and prediction process of machine learning models is more than 10 times faster than the running process of numerical model. This study proposed the potential use of machine learning models as alternatives to hydraulic simulations, which could provide a more efficient solution for large-scale DFHM.

Keywords: Debris flow, Hazard assessment, Numerical analysis, Machine learning

Subject terms: Natural hazards, Information technology

Introduction

Debris flows, which are usually considered to be highly destructive and unpredictable, have been a major threat to the lives and property of people living in mountainous areas1,2. Considering the impact of extreme rainfall in recent years, debris flow control deserves more attention. Generally speaking, the development of debris flow control measures requires adequate knowledge of debris flow events3.

Debris flow hazard mapping (DFHM) proved to be an effective tool for visualizing the hazard scope and the intensity of debris flows4. Generally speaking, there are three approaches to create a debris flow hazard map and these approaches could be categorized as experience-based, experimentally-based and simulation-based5. The experience-based models aim to establish a relationship between environmental factors and debris flows and thus to predict their hazard, which requires a great deal of detailed data and expert experience6. The experimentally-based methods usually imply high cost and experimental requirements, which limited the development of the corresponding methods7. It can be seen that both methods are difficult to replicate on a large scale. With the development of hardware and software, the application of simulation-based approaches has became the mainstream for DFHM. By determining appropriate input parameters, numerical models capable of reproducing debris flow movements could be built8,9.

Recently, some software has also been developed for DFHM, which promoted the further diffusion of numerical simulation methods. Hunger et al. developed a software named DAN3D, which has been widely used in debris flow simulation10. Peng et al. simulated a debris flow event that occurred in Selvetta based on FLO-2D11. Franco-Ramos et al. (2020) utilized RAMMS to simulate a debris flow event to provide references for local mitigation strategies12. Horton et al. (2019) used Massflow to develop a debris flow hazard map for land use planning13. Overall, the development of the software has dramatically reduced the difficulty of applying numerical simulation. However, it is still a quite time-consuming process, especially for areas requiring high-precision simulation, the software runtime is sometimes intolerable14.

Machine learning have been extensively applied in geotechnical engineering, each with distinct methodologies and strengths. Nguyen et al. (2024) estimated the risk of landslides based on the spatial probability of landslides obtained from a one-dimensional convolutional neural network15. Song et al. (2025) established a model for predicting debris flow entrainment rate based on generative AI16. In addition, machine learning has also been widely used to predict the probability of geological hazards in recent years due to its advantages of high efficiency and low parameter dependency17. Based on suitable training datasets and machine learning models, high-quality debris flow susceptibility maps (DFSM) could be obtained18. Inspired by the core ideas of DFSM, we firstly sampled the study area with a spatial precision of 5 m to form a sample set. Subsequently, we extracted the factors related to debris flow generation and movement as independent variables, and regarded the maximum flow velocity and accumulation depth obtained by simulation as dependent variables. The training dataset could be obtained by combining the independent variables with the dependent variables. Then the training dataset was used to train a model called gradient boosting decision tree (GBDT). We believe that with proper training, GBDT could play an important role in DFHM.

To summarize, numerical simulations currently play a dominant role in DFHM studies, but the computational costs are sometimes intolerable. This study was designed to introduce machine learning to improve the efficiency of DFHM. The FLO-2D model was employed to simulate debris flows with recurrence intervals of 20 years, 50 years and 100 years, respectively. Corresponding maximum debris flow accumulation depth and flow velocity were collected as dependent variables. Multiple factors related to debris flow movement including rainfall with different recurrence intervals. were collected as independent variables. The independent and dependent variables comprise the 20-year dataset, 50-year dataset, and 100-year dataset. The GBDT algorithm was used for modeling. The 20-year and 50-year datasets were used for training, and the 100-year dataset was used for validation. This study explored the potential of machine learning as an alternative to numerical simulation in DFHM, which could provide a more efficient option for large-scale DFHM.

Materials

Study area

Based on the field survey results and historical records, the Nanjiao watershed in Fangshan District, Beijing, was selected as the study area. Located in the warm temperate semi-humid continental monsoon climate zone, the rainfall in Nanjiao watershed is concentrated from June to September every year. Strong incision effect results in a high density of gullies in this region, which could provide favorable topographic conditions for debris flow formation. In addition, the severe weathering of the region’s lithology and the widespread distribution of collapses and landslides could provide a rich source of material for the formation of debris flows. Finally, as shown in Fig. 1, there are 1 main gulch and 6 branch gulches in Nanjiao watershed.

  1. Independent variables

In general, conditioning factors associated with the debris flow movement process can be treated as independent variables to build a regression model23. In the present study, a total of 8 conditioning factors were collected.

Rainfall is the hydrodynamic source of debris flows in the watershed. The greater the intensity of rainfall, the greater the scope and intensity of the debris flow hazard24. In this study, the maximum rainfall in 1 h with recurrence interval of 20 years (72.1 mm), 50 years (87.9 mm) and 100 years (101.2 mm) were applied for different datasets.

The remaining conditioning factors, in addition to rainfall, were shown in Fig. 5. The topographic factors have played an important role in debris flow studies. Altitude is a important conditioning factor since debris flows are terrain-driven in many cases25. Slope is closely related to the debris flow circulation and accumulation processes26. Plane curvature determines the convergence and dispersion of debris flows, and profile curvature influences the acceleration and deceleration of debris flows27. TWI quantified topographic control of basic hydrologic processes28. All the topographic factors were obtained based on the collected 5-m precision DEM.

In addition, NDVI and Manning’s coefficients are two conditioning factors used to characterize the surface conditions, which have an important impact on the movement process of debris flows.

    Dependent variables

In this study, the maximum accumulation depth and flow velocity of debris flows under different rainfall conditions were used as the dependent variables.

The distribution of maximum debris flow velocity corresponding to different rainfall conditions was shown in Fig. 7. The statistical results of the grid cells at each maximum flow velocity level were shown in Table 8. It can be seen that for all rainfall conditions, the maximum debris flow velocities are mostly under 3 m/s. The high flow velocity mostly occurs within the gulches, which is due to the fact that there is no obvious blockage in the gulches, and there are some areas with low Manning’s coefficient. On the contrary, since both sides of the gulches are diffuse extension zones, the maximum flow velocities were significantly reduced. On the other hand, the proportion of higher flow velocity units increased significantly with the increasing rainfall intensity. In addition, there are also obvious differences in maximum flow velocities between different gulches, which are determined by a combination of the watershed area, the peak flood flow, the source reserve, and the longitudinal slope drop of the gulches.

Validation of numerical simulation results

Generally speaking, the simulation results need to be verified based on more reliable field data. However, for debris flows, the collection of filed data is usually quite challenging. After an extensive literature review, we collected the accumulation depth of the main gulch under a heavy rainfall36. Historical records showed that the rainfall reached 67 mm in one hour, which is very close to the rainfall with 20-year recurrence interval. Therefore, the accumulation depth obtained by monitoring can be used to verify the rationality of the simulation. The monitoring location was shown in Fig. 9. The results showed that the accumulation depth obtained based on numerical simulation is 0.84 m, while the data obtained from monitoring is about 0.74 m, with an error of 13.5%. Compared with previous studies, it could meet the requirements of simulation accuracy.

Debris flow hazard maps

Based on the judgment criteria in Table 7, the debris flow hazard maps under different rainfall conditions were obtained as shown in Fig. 10. The hazard maps were statistically analyzed and the results were shown in Table 10. Under the rainfall condition with a recurrence interval of 20 years, the proportions of very low, low, moderate and high hazard in the map are 66.7%, 28.4%, 0.7% and 4.2% respectively. Under the rainfall condition with a recurrence interval of 50 years, the proportions of very low, low, moderate and high hazard are 59.8%, 31.8%, 1.6% and 6.8%, respectively. Under the rainfall condition with a recurrence interval of 100 years, the proportions of very low, low, moderate and high were 55.2%, 33.4%, 2.3% and 9.1%, respectively. As the intensity of rainfall increases, more areas are categorized into higher hazard classes. In addition, in terms of the spatial distribution of the high hazard class, most of the high hazard areas are located in the main gulch, which is due to the fact that the peak debris flow discharge of the main gulch is larger than that of the rest of branch gulches. On the other hand, debris flows in the branch gulches would eventually converge with the main ditch, which further enhanced the hazard in the main gulch.

The comparison between the simulated and predicted results were shown in Fig. 12. Table 13 demonstrated the debris flow hazard class proportion based on predicted and simulated results. Discussion

DFHM has evolved significantly over the years, with diverse methodologies developed to address the complexity and dynamic characteristics39. Traditional experience-based methods are typically simple and highly interpretable, but it would also make the results too subjective and difficult to generalize to areas where historical data are scarce40. The experiment-based approach is a reproduction of debris flow generation and movement, which is convincing in most cases. However, building a physical model of debris flow is usually challenging41. For example, the terrain and surface conditions in nature are complex and variable, which is difficult to realize in the laboratory. As a result, the experimental conditions are usually simplified to some extent in practice, which would lead to the accuracy of the results being compromised as well. Recently, numerical simulation has emerged as a important tool for investigating the intensity characteristics of debris flows. By leveraging advanced computational frameworks, numerical simulations enable the precise quantification of flow velocity and accumulation depth. However, high computational costs remain a significant challenge, particularly for high-resolution models that require substantial processing resources. In addition, the reliability of simulation outcomes heavily depends on the availability and quality of input parameters. which are often subject to uncertainties due to sparse field measurements42,43.

Overall, the current methods used for DFHM have obvious advantages and disadvantages. Numerical simulation methods have been widely used in DFHM but are also limited by the high computational cost and scarce input parameters. Therefore, improving computational efficiency and reducing parameter dependence are important considerations in exploring advanced DFHM methods.

Compared to numerical simulations, machine learning have demonstrated the potential of developing models that minimize dependency on extensive input parameter tuning while maintaining low computational overhead44. Accordingly, we proposed to introduce machine learning to perform DFHM. For machine learning models, classification and regression are the two main tasks. In previous debris flow studies, classification models have been widely used in debris flow susceptibility prediction. By inputting conditioning factors closely related to the formation of debris flow into a classification model, the probability of debris flows can be predicted45. For DFHM, the key lies in the acquisition of motion intensity characteristics. Therefore, we mainly focused on the application of regression models. The datasets corresponding to rainfall with 20-year recurrence interval and 50-year recurrence interval were used for training. The dataset corresponding to rainfall with 100-year recurrence interval was used for validation. The results showed that the predicted results are quite close to the simulated results and have a higher efficiency, which verified the validity and rationality of the proposed method in this study. Therefore, we believe machine learning has the potential to play a more important role in DFHM.

Limitations and future works

In this study, machine learning was introduced to assist numerical simulations for DFHM to improve the efficiency. The following are some of the limitations that need to be addressed in future studies: (1) FLO-2D is applied in this paper to obtain the debris flow velocity and accumulation depth, and more advanced numerical simulation methods can be employed to further improve the accuracy and efficiency. (2) The accumulation depth of a debris flow at one monitoring point during a rainfall was used to validate the reliability of simulation results, more field data could be prepared to increase the persuasiveness in future studies. (3) A single machine learning model was used in this study and more models can be explored to improve reliability. (4) The performance of machine learning models for unseen situations is highly dependent on their training datasets. If the datasets corresponding to a new situation exhibits significant differences from the training datasets, the model performance is difficult to guarantee. In addition, compared to physics-based models, the interpretability was also limited.

In future studies, we believe that by improving algorithms and models and collecting more training data, machine learning will have broader application prospects in DFHM. In addition, this study provided an important framework of machine learning as an efficient alternative to time-consuming numerical simulations, which could offer an efficient solution for similar time-consuming numerical simulation application scenarios.

Conclusion

DFHM is an effective tool for significantly reducing the threat of debris flows. This study proposed the introduction of machine learning to improve the efficiency of DFHM. The following conclusions could be drawn:

  1. Various methods for DFHM have been developed in recent years, each with its advantages and disadvantages. Numerical simulation methods can be useful in most cases, but limitations such as high computational costs and parameter scarcity still exist.

  2. The introduction of machine learning proved to be effective in compensating for the shortcomings of numerical simulation and achieving high-quality DFHM.

  3. With the introduction of more training data, models with higher performance can be built and machine learning is expected to play a dominant role in DFHM.
