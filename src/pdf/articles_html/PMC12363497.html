ABSTRACT

To investigate factors influencing vaccine recommendations by healthcare workers (HCWs) in Zhejiang Province, China. A cross-sectional survey was conducted among 569 HCWs to assess their beliefs and attitudes toward vaccines, vaccine recommendations and barriers to recommending vaccinations. Logistic regression was utilized to analyze influencing factors. Most participants (89.4%) believed that vaccines can effectively protect children’s health. Higher professional titles (aOR = 1.24, 95% CI: 1.03–1.50) and increased vaccine confidence (aOR = 1.10, 95% CI: 1.05–1.16) were positively associated with vaccine recommendation behaviors, while increased vaccine risk perception significantly reduced recommendations (aOR = 0.84, 95% CI: 0.78–0.90). Moreover, vaccine hesitancy among HCWs was primarily driven by two main factors: safety concerns regarding immunization (60.8%) and cognitive biases related to vaccine necessity and efficacy (25.8%). This study elucidates professional title advancement and vaccine confidence as critical determinants of vaccine recommendation behaviors among HCWs in Zhejiang Province, China. To improve vaccine acceptance and achieve higher immunization coverage, targeted vaccine education and confidence-building programs should be integrated into HCWs’ professional training.

KEYWORDS: Vaccination, healthcare workers, recommendation behavior, vaccine hesitancy, vaccine confidence

Introduction

Vaccination is a critical strategy for infectious disease prevention and has significantly contributed to global public health achievements. Since WHO’s initiation of the Expanded Programme on Immunization (EPI) in 1974, vaccines have prevented approximately 154 million deaths, which indicates that vaccination has become one of the most cost-effective public health interventions.1 Despite these successes, approximately 20% of children globally still lack essential immunizations, leading to millions of preventable deaths and severe disabilities annually.2 The regional resurgence of traditional infectious diseases such as measles, pertussis (whooping cough), and poliomyelitis, as well as the periodic outbreaks of influenza and coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID‐19), highlights the fragility of herd immunity and the imperative for sustained, equitable vaccination coverage.3–6

Vaccine hesitancy refers to the delay in acceptance or refusal of vaccination despite the availability of vaccination services. This phenomenon is complex and context specific, varying with time, place and type of vaccines, and has gradually become a global issue.7,8 The Strategic Advisory Group of Experts (SAGE) has constructed a three-dimensional analytical framework – confidence, complacency, and convenience, to study the phenomenon of “vaccine hesitancy.”7,9

Currently, the vaccination coverage has been maintained at an optimal level for the general population through its National Immunization Program (NIP) in China. However, exist literature revealed that vaccination deferral is common in children with special health condition in our country (e.g., Zhejiang, Jiangsu, and Chongqing).10–12 It was found that over half of Chinese parents have doubts about the efficacy and safety of the vaccines included in the NIP, especially regarding routine vaccinations such as polio and diphtheria, tetanus, and pertussis vaccines.13 During the COVID-19 pandemic, 36.2% of the public doubted vaccine efficacy, and 77.6% expressed concerns over safety risks.14

HCWs are the primary and most trusted sources of guidance and influence in parents’ decisions about vaccination. However, existing empirical studies indicate that the prevalence of COVID-19 vaccination hesitancy worldwide in HCWs ranged from 4.3 to 72% and the reported influenza vaccination coverage among surveyed HCWs for 2018/2019 season in China was only 11.6%.15,16 Vaccine hesitancy is not uncommon among HCWs themselves. A Finnish study on 2962 HCWs showed that HCWs who perceive vaccines as offering lower benefits and safety demonstrate significantly greater hesitancy, delay, or refusal in vaccinating their own children. Furthermore, they exhibit diminished recommendation behavior when encountering vaccine-hesitant patients.17 Similarly, a 2021 Italian study highlighted that the introduction of COVID-19 vaccines has significantly exacerbated the risk of vaccine hesitancy among HCWs.18 HCWs frequently encounter children who are either in the acute/chronic phase of illness or in the recovery phase in the course of their daily duties. These vulnerable children are often at a heightened risk of contracting vaccine-preventable diseases. As a result, the advice provided by specialists plays a crucial role in shaping vaccination decisions for the parents of children with special health conditions. Health professionals’ misunderstanding of vaccines may amplify the public’s concerns about vaccination. However, currently there is very limited research regarding the vaccination recommendation behaviors and its correlated factors among HCWs.

Therefore, we conducted this study to reveal the following issues through multidimensional empirical analysis: 1) HCWs’ awareness and attitudes toward different types of vaccines; 2) Characteristics of HCWs’ vaccine recommendation behaviors and reasons for refusing to recommend vaccination; 3) the correlation between HCWs’’ demographic characteristics educational background, occupational attributes, and their recommendation behaviors; and 4) the predictive capacity of a modified Vaccine Hesitancy Scale (VHS) regarding clinical recommendations; 5) the correlation between vaccine attitudes (such as vaccine confidence and risk perception) and vaccine recommendation behaviors. The study will provide scientific basis for formulating training programs for HCWs regarding vaccine administration and improve the implementation mechanisms of immunization policies.

Materials and methods

Study population and ethics

This cross-sectional study targeted active HCWs from public hospitals across Zhejiang Province. Inclusion criteria comprised HCWs ≥18 y with continuous clinical practice for ≥12 months. The Ethics Committee of Children’s Hospital, Zhejiang University School of Medicine approved the study protocol (Approval No.: [2025-IRB-0005-P-01]). All participants provided electronic informed consent. Data were collected anonymously to reduce social desirability bias.

Instrument development

The questionnaire was developed based on WHO’s “3C” (confidence, complacency, convenience) framework and the modified VHS.9 Five sections included (See Supplementary Material for details):

• Demographic information: age, gender, education level (Bachelor’s/Master’s/PhD or above), department (pediatrics/non-pediatrics), current professional title (trainee physician/resident physician/attending physician/associate chief physician or above), type of hospital (general hospital/women’s and children’s hospital/other), whether they have obtained a physician qualification certificate and whether they have received training in child health specialty knowledge.

• Vaccine Confidence and Risk Perception (modified VHS, Cronbach’s α = 0.81): The main question on vaccine confidence is “Do you believe vaccines can protect children from serious diseases? (Believe/Do not believe).” The modified scoring of the VHS is adapted from the SAGE standard 10-item scale, changing “my child” to “children” to fit the professional perspective. A 5-point Likert scale is employed for scoring, where 1 means “strongly disagree” and 5 means “strongly agree.” Items L1 through L7 reflect vaccine confidence and are scored positively, while items L8 through L10 reflect vaccine risk perception and are scored negatively. The total score ranges from 10 to 50.

• Attitudes toward various vaccines: The study includes a three-level classification of vaccine attitudes: acceptance, hesitancy, and refusal, along with 13 multiple-choice attributes, such as concerns about side effects, barriers to accessing information, and past negative medical experiences.

• Assessing Vaccine recommendation behaviors: Assessing cognitive decision-making through clinical scenario questions such as “Do you refrain from recommending vaccination because you believe the vaccine is ineffective?” (Recommend/Do not recommend; Assessing practice records through questioning, such as, “Have there been any instances of vaccine refusal or postponement in your past practice?” Yes, please describe the reasons/No). HCWs who do not recommend vaccination form the non-recommendation group, while those who do comprise the recommendation group.

• The impact of negative vaccine information on Vaccine recommendation behaviors: Participants were asked whether they had encountered negative information about vaccines (Yes/No). If so, they identified the source from five multiple-choice options: experiences of friends or patients, news media, personal experiences, the internet, or other sources. Behavior change: After encountering negative information, did you continue to recommend vaccines? (Yes/No)

The questionnaire underwent pre-testing (n = 20) to ensure clarity and reliability.

Data collection protocol

The survey, conducted from January 1 to February 28, 2025, utilized “Questionnaire Star,” China’s leading online questionnaire platform, employing the combination of convenient sampling technique and snowball sampling method via professional WeChat groups.19,20 The Children’s Hospital of Zhejiang University School of Medicine serves as the Zhejiang Provincial Quality Management Center for Child Healthcare. In this role, we coordinate specialized training and clinical guidance for pediatric practitioners across all 90 county-level divisions of the province. Capitalizing on this existing network, we maintained dedicated WeChat (a free messaging and calling app) groups with partner hospitals. Concurrently, internal departmental WeChat groups facilitated communication within our hospital. We disseminated the online survey via these established digital channels, requesting participants to further share the questionnaire link within their clinical teams.

Quality assurance

Data quality controls included logical validation, IP deduplication, minimum response times (>5 min), and exclusion of incomplete/contradictory questionnaires.

Statistical analysis

Data analysis employed SPSS 26.0. Descriptive statistics presented demographic characteristics. The sample size for this study was determined using the standard formula: n=Z2×p×1−p/ e2. Categorical variables are shown as frequencies (percentages), while continuous variables are shown as medians (interquartile ranges). For the sections of reasons for vaccine hesitancy and sources of negative information, responses were collected using a multiple-choice format. We then calculated the ratio of selections for each option to the total number of selections across all options.

ROC analysis evaluated the predictive validity of the modified VHS. The Mann-Whitney U test and chi-square test were used to compare differences between the vaccine recommendation group and the group that did not receive a recommendation. Logistic regression identified factors influencing vaccine recommendation behaviors. The odds ratios (OR) and 95% confidence intervals (CI) for univariate and multivariate models were calculated, with statistical significance set at p < .05.

The majority of HCWs (89.4%) strongly recognize the efficacy of vaccines for children’s health, though safety concerns (29.3%) and complacency (22.7%) persisted. Attitudes toward specific vaccines and the impact of negative vaccine information

The analysis of the attitudes toward 15 types of vaccines (see Figure 1) indicates that health-care professionals generally have a high acceptance rate for all vaccines. The acceptance rate for basic immunization vaccines (e.g., HBV, MMR, DTaP) is over 98%. The pentavalent combination vaccine (DTaP-IPV-Hib) containing novel adjuvants demonstrates a hesitancy rate of 7.0% (40/569) and refusal rate of 1.4% (8/569) in the study population. However, the influenza vaccine (FluV) shows a hesitancy rate of 12.1% (69/569) and a refusal rate of 4.6% (26/569). The COVID-19 vaccine (COVID-19 Vax) has the highest refusal rate at 7.2% (41/569) and a hesitancy rate of 24.1% (137/569), both over 50 times higher than those for vaccines included in the immunization program. The varicella vaccine (VZV) also has a high acceptance rate of 97.7%, distinguishing it from other nonscheduled immunization vaccines.

Based on the analysis of the responses from 569 health-care professionals (total responses count n = 876), we found that vaccine hesitancy and refusal is caused by multiple factors (see Figure 2). The leading cause of vaccine hesitancy is HCWs’ skepticism about vaccine safety (60.8%). This includes worries about side effects (17.5%), personal adverse reactions (13.5%), and doubts stemming from negative information (29.8%). The second factor is cognitive bias about vaccines (25.8%), primarily the belief that vaccines are unnecessary (18.7%) and skepticism about their efficacy (6.0%). Notably, exposure to negative vaccine information significantly influences health-care professionals’ decision-making—43.3% of refusal behaviors are linked to this exposure, which includes media reports (18.2%), negative personal vaccination experiences (13.5%), and information spread through social networks (11.6%).

The survey reveals that 81.2% of health-care professionals (462 out of 569) have come across negative information about vaccines. According to the multiple response analysis (see Figure 3), the sources of negative vaccine information include online platforms (38.6%), indirect negative experiences from friends or patients (29.7%), and news media (25.2%). Only 6.2% of the negative information comes from direct vaccination experiences. Notably, 25.3% (117 out of 462) of individuals who were exposed to negative information would stop recommending vaccines. This indicates that indirect risk information, such as negative information from social media, is more likely to lead them vaccine recommendation avoidance than their personal experiences.

Vaccine recommendation behaviors

Approximately 25.1% of HCWs refrained from recommending vaccines due to doubts about their efficacy, while another 33.9% recommended deferring vaccinations for reasons related to disease treatment. An analysis of the reasons for deferring vaccination indicates several key factors. The most common reason was acute illnesses in children (56.2%) and a history of Kawasaki disease or blood product use (11.7%). Other reasons included immune deficiencies, the use of hormones or immunosuppressant (8.0%), as well as seizures, prematurity, congenital heart disease (11.7%), severe allergic reactions, and a history of adverse events following immunization (AEFI).

Predictive validity of modified VHS

The modified VHS did not significantly predict recommendation behaviors (AUC = 0.51, p =.74). Similarly, the scale showed non-significant predictive performance in the pediatric subgroup (AUC = 0.523, p =.624).

Factors influencing recommendations

A multidimensional analysis of the valid questionnaires by 569 HCWs reveals that vaccine recommendation behavior is significantly influenced by professional and cognitive factors (see Table 3). It is found that the recommendation group (74.9%, n = 426) significantly outperformed the non-recommendation group in terms of the proportion of pediatric specialization (79.7% vs 20.3%, χ2 = 10.10, p = .001), the proportion of senior professional titles (83.7% vs 16.3%, χ2 = 13.51, p = .004), and the dimension of vaccine awareness (vaccine confidence and vaccine risk perception). In addition, compared with the non-recommendation group (32 points, p = .000), the recommendation group (34 points) had a higher median score for vaccine confidence. Conversely, the median score for vaccine risk perception was lower in the recommendation group (9 points) than in the non-recommendation group (11 points, p = .000). The effect size for the vaccine confidence dimension was measured by a Cliff’s delta of −0.23 (95% CI: −0.34 to 0.12), while the effect size for the vaccine risk perception dimension was 0.34 (95% CI: 0.25 to 0.43), both indicating moderate effects. No significant differences were found between the two groups regarding sociodemographic variables: gender (female recommendation group 64.6% vs non-recommendation group 58.7%, χ2 = 1.55, p = .21), highest education level (χ2 = 2.83, p = .24), hospital attributes (χ2 = 5.37, p = .07), possession of a physician qualification certificate (χ2 = 1.85, p = .17), or training in child health specialty knowledge (χ2 = 0.002, p = .96) (p > .05).

Logistic regression analysis showed that higher professional titles and vaccine confidence positively correlated with recommendations, whereas increased risk perception negatively impacted behaviors (see Table 4). The multivariable model indicates that: (1) each level of professional title advancement increases the probability of recommendation by 24% (aOR = 1.24, 95%CI:1.03–1.50, p = .024); (2) each point increase in the vaccine confidence score raises the recommendation probability by 10.4% (aOR = 1.10, 95%CI:1.05–1.16, p = .000); and (3) each point increase in the risk perception score decreases the recommendation probability by 16.3% (aOR = 0.84, 95%CI:0.78–0.90, p = .000). Pediatric specialty training also showed no significant independent effect (p > .05). The multivariate model demonstrated adequate fit in the Hosmer–Lemeshow goodness-of-fit test (χ2 = 5.6, df = 8, p = .69).

Discussion

This study presents a comprehensive analysis of vaccine recommendation behaviors and their determinants among HCWs in Zhejiang Province, China. The results of the study show HCWs generally have a high level of confidence in vaccination. However, they tend to be skeptical about novel vaccines and non-NIP vaccines. This hesitancy substantively influences their vaccine recommendation practices in clinical practices.

The majority of participants (89.4%) expressed confidence in vaccines’ efficacy for protecting pediatric health, with 62.6% strongly trusting government-endorsed immunizations，consistent with previous research.21 However, one-third of them demonstrated significant apprehension regarding potential AEFI with novel vaccines. The results suggest that, despite their extensive professional expertise, HCWs still harbor considerable concerns regarding the safety and clinical efficacy of novel biologics, including recently developed COVID-19 vaccines, perhaps largely attributable to perceived insufficiency of comprehensive clinical trial data and long-term safety surveillance.22 Simultaneously, the prolonged global COVID-19 vaccination campaign has contributed to vaccine fatigue, a phenomenon previously documented with influenza vaccinations and linked to suboptimal uptake. Notably, this fatigue persists even among HCWs.23,24 This might also have an impact on their vaccination recommendations.

Our comparative analysis of acceptance across vaccine types revealed significantly lower receptivity toward combination vaccines containing novel adjuvants (e.g., DTaP-IPV-Hib) and non-NIP vaccines25 (e.g., HPV, rotavirus, influenza, pneumococcal vaccine) compared to those integrated within established immunization schedules. This differential acceptance of combination vaccines stems partly from concerns regarding the complexity of new-adjuvant-containing formulations, coupled with vaccine cost considerations.26 In addition, HCWs preferentially recommend NIP vaccines due to their government-guaranteed safety profiles, cost-free access, and preventative efficacy against high-risk diseases. Conversely, non-NIP vaccines encounter barriers including: 1) immunization cost discussions with parents, 2) perception as non-essential for ‘mild’ infections (rotavirus/influenza), 3) complex immunization schedules causing fatigue, and 4) insufficiently communicated safety data – exacerbated by systemic promotion gaps.27 Interestingly, varicella vaccine, despite its non-NIP status in Zhejiang Province, demonstrated relatively high acceptance – potentially attributable to its high disease incidence and established inclusion in immunization programs in metropolitan regions such as Beijing and Shanghai, as well as internationally in countries including the USA, Australia, and Japan. Public advocacy for its integration into the NIP has gained considerable momentum, contributing to widespread recognition among HCWs of vaccination as the optimal prophylactic strategy against varicella infection.28–30

Further analysis into the determinants of vaccine hesitancy revealed that nearly two-thirds (60.8%) of hesitancy cases stemmed from safety concerns, with one-quarter (25.8%) attributable to doubts about the necessity and effectiveness of vaccination. These findings corroborate previous research, suggesting that hesitancy primarily stems from inadequate recognition of vaccine-preventable disease severity, insufficient vaccine literacy, and concerns about transparency throughout vaccine development and regulatory approval processes.31 Additionally, the pervasive anti-vaccine misinformation across digital platforms significantly influences HCWs’ risk perceptions. Our study found that over 80% of participants encountered negative vaccine-related information through various channels, predominantly online platforms, news media, and indirect experiential accounts. Previous epidemiological investigations indicate that scientific information source substantially shapes both public and professional perceptions, particularly among early-career HCWs.32 Furthermore, insufficient information dissemination and social media exposure exacerbate vaccine hesitancy, consequently affecting recommendation behaviors.33,34 Safety apprehensions and cognitive biases were identified as principal determinants underlying vaccine hesitancy among HCWs. Thus, addressing safety concerns and cognitive biases through evidence-based communication via digital platforms represents a critical strategy for enhancing vaccine advocacy among HCWs.

Our study also found that when deciding whether to recommend postponing vaccination, the predominant factor influencing HCWs’ decision is the concerns on the impact of vaccine safety on underlying medical conditions. Given their routine clinical encounters with patients, HCWs typically adopt a conservative approach toward immunization recommendations, particularly junior physicians with limited clinical experience. Previous investigations have documented inadequate knowledge among pediatricians and primary care physicians regarding contraindications and precautionary considerations for vaccination in children with comorbidities, resulting in suboptimal recommendation rates and reduced immunization coverage among these vulnerable populations.35–37

Multivariate analysis of factors influencing vaccine recommendation behaviors revealed that HCWs with higher professional titles exhibited greater confidence in vaccines. Senior professionals demonstrated fewer irrational concerns about adverse events, thereby showing a greater propensity to recommend vaccination and engaging more confidently in vaccine-related communication with parents. These senior HCWs typically had more extensive involvement in vaccine clinical trials, establishing a positive feedback loop between vaccine knowledge and clinical practice, which is consistent with findings from parallel studies.32,38–40 Evidently, higher vaccine confidence increased the likelihood of vaccine recommendation, while heightened risk perception negatively influenced recommendation behaviors. Therefore, enhancing HCWs’ scientific literacy, strengthening their vaccine confidence, and mitigating risk perception are of vital importance. Also, we have compared key socio-demographic variables (such as gender, education level, and hospital attributes) between the two groups and found no statistical differences. This further emphasizes that the differences in vaccine recommendation among HCWs are significantly related to intrinsic factors rather than demographic confounding factors.

Within contemporary Chinese medical education frameworks, vaccine-related curricula are predominantly situated within pediatric preventive health-care modules, featuring dedicated immunology chapters emphasizing NIP vaccination schedules, contraindications, and standard protocols. However, advanced content addressing the comparative advantages of combination vaccines, immunological waning patterns, and specialized vaccination strategies for children with comorbidities remains limited to approximately 1–2 instructional hours, with notable deficiencies in educational depth. Continuing medical education programs similarly lack systematic training in practical vaccine management competencies, including AEFI differential diagnosis and emerging vaccine technologies (e.g., rotavirus, pneumococcal conjugate, COVID-19 vaccines). Consequently, many HCWs excessively rely on vaccine package inserts rather than evidence-based guidelines when confronting complex immunization scenarios. Our study found no significant correlation between pediatric preventive healthcare training experience and vaccine recommendation behaviors, highlighting substantial knowledge translation gaps within current medical education systems.

We propose coordinated, multi-dimensional strategies to enhance vaccine-related competencies among health-care professionals:

  1. Enhance vaccinology training during standardized residency programs;

  2. Require mandatory vaccinology Continuing Medical Education (CME) with credit certification;

  3. Integrate Clinical Prevention Services into Vaccination Practices

  4. Operate government-managed digital platforms for real-time vaccine updates.

Limitation

First, using a convenient sampling technique combined snowball sampling method limits the generalizability of the study findings and might create selection bias. To mitigate the limitation, we implemented extensive dissemination of survey links across available networks and maximally increased the sample size. Furthermore, the voluntary nature of participation introduces potential non-response bias, as respondents may systematically differ from non-respondents in key characteristics. So, rewards for participants may be useful. Additionally, we took a brief questionnaire in this survey, thus the number of questions was limited, and the questionnaire design may inadequately capture the full spectrum of professional diversity among HCWs, potentially introducing information bias. Furthermore, as Larson et al. have elucidated, vaccine hesitancy represents a dynamic phenomenon influenced by continuously evolving determinants.41 The cross-sectional design employed herein may inadequately capture the temporal complexity of vaccine attitudes and recommendation behaviors. Future investigations should employ larger, more heterogeneous samples and longitudinal methodologies to validate these findings and explore deeper psychosocial factors influencing vaccine hesitancy and recommendation practices. Moreover, effective scientific communication strategies warrant further investigation to enhance public vaccine acceptance.

Conclusion

This study elucidates patterns of vaccine hesitancy and vaccine recommendation behaviors among HCWs in Zhejiang Province, China, highlighting professional title advancement and vaccine confidence as critical determinants of recommendation behaviors. In the future, to improve vaccine acceptance and achieve higher immunization coverage, targeted vaccine education and confidence-building programs should be integrated into HCWs’ professional training. These improvements will ultimately enhance the public acceptance of novel and traditional vaccines, thereby strengthening the immune barrier for the general population.
