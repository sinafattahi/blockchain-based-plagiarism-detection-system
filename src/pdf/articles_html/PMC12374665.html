Abstract

Introduction

Acute respiratory tract infections (ARIs) are a major cause of antibiotic use among under-five (U5) children, often leading to inappropriate prescriptions that contribute to antimicrobial resistance, adverse drug reactions and higher healthcare costs.

Objectives

To assess the pattern and rate of antibiotic prescriptions and evaluate their appropriateness based on WHO and Paediatric Association of Nigeria guidelines for ARI treatment among U5 children at Usmanu Danfodiyo University Teaching Hospital, Sokoto

Methods

A retrospective review of medical records (case folders and prescription sheets) was conducted for U5 children diagnosed with ARI over a 10-month period at the paediatric outpatient and inpatient units, Department of Paediatrics, UDUTH. Data extracted included patient sociodemographic characteristics, ARI diagnosis based on Integrated Management of Childhood Illnesses/WHO criteria and antibiotic/medication details (class, name, route, dose, frequency, duration and regimen). Analysis was performed using SPSS V.20.0; p<0.05 was considered statistically significant.

Results

A total of 2140 drugs and 1545 antibiotics were prescribed, averaging 2.2 and 1.6 per patient, respectively. Upper respiratory tract infections accounted for 66.2% of antibiotic indications. The antibiotic prescription rate was 100.0 %, with 71.0% administered parenterally. Most prescriptions used generic names (96.5%) and were listed in the National Essential Medicines List (100%). Cephalosporins were most frequently prescribed (46.4%), followed by penicillins; fluoroquinolones were least prescribed (1.2%). Mono-antibiotic therapy was used in 53.2% of cases. Notably, 81.5% of prescriptions came from second-line therapies. Appropriate prescribing was observed for duration (93.6%), dose (66.0%), frequency (69.7%) and indication/choice (66.5%).

Conclusion

Despite encouraging adherence to appropriate antibiotic dosage regimen, the high rate of antibiotic prescription, parenteral antibiotic administration and alternative antibiotic use highlights ongoing concerns with risk of resistance, adverse effects and treatment failure. Strengthening antibiotic stewardship and adherence to first-line guidelines is crucial to optimise ARI treatment in resource-limited settings.

Keywords: Child Health

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

  • Antibiotics are frequently overprescribed for acute respiratory tract infections (ARIs), particularly in low- and middle-income countries, despite most ARIs being viral and self-limiting. However, there is limited data from Northwestern Nigeria on the prescribing pattern, appropriateness of antibiotic prescribing for paediatric ARIs using WHO and national guidelines.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

  • This study reveals significant gaps in antibiotic prescribing for under-five with ARIs, including high antibiotic prescribing rate (100%), deviations in recommended dosing regimen, antibiotic choice and over-reliance on broad-spectrum antibiotics. Additionally, frequent use of second-line agents, non-guideline recommended antibiotics and add-ons like azithromycin and cotrimoxazole in cases where they were not indicated was common.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY

  • These findings underscore the urgent need for targeted interventions such as prescriber education, improved dissemination of WHO ARI treatment guidelines and implementation of antimicrobial stewardship programmes. This study provides baseline data to guide policy reforms and future quality improvement efforts in paediatric antibiotic use.

Introduction

About 3.5% of the global disease burden is caused by acute respiratory tract infections (ARIs).1 Under-five (U5) children experience approximately 6–8 episodes of ARI per year, accounting for about 30–50% of hospital visits, 20–40% of outpatient consultations and 30–40% of hospital admissions. ARIs contribute to approximately 70% of morbidity among U5 children in developing countries.2 This makes ARI one of the major causes of hospital admission and referrals among children, and it is responsible for at least 6% of the world’s disability and death—resulting in substantial morbidity and mortality.3 Consequently, ARI remains one of the most common indications for antibiotic therapy.

Unfortunately, despite the availability of standard, recommended guidelines for antibiotic therapy in ARI, nearly half of all antibiotic prescriptions in emergency departments—where most ARI cases are managed—are inappropriate.4 This is concerning, as inappropriate prescribing contributes to the surge in antimicrobial resistance (AMR), increased morbidity and mortality, higher treatment costs, prolonged hospital stays and poor quality of care delivery.5 6

This situation contradicts the WHO principles on the rational use of medicines, which emphasise that patients should receive medications appropriate to their clinical needs, in doses tailored to individual requirements, for an adequate duration and at the lowest possible cost to them and the community.7 8 To address these concerns, the WHO recommends the adoption of standard treatment guidelines for antibiotic use. In Nigeria, the WHO and the Paediatric Association of Nigeria (PAN) have issued a joint guideline for the management of ARI in both inpatient and outpatient settings.9 10

Adopting these guidelines can help standardise treatment, minimise medication errors, improve patient safety and healthcare delivery and promote behaviour change in antibiotic prescribing. Additionally, it may help reduce the economic impact of AMR, alleviate the psychological and financial burden of caring for a sick child and ensure that clinical decisions are driven by evidence-based practice.11 12

Despite the public health significance and global burden of ARI, there is a paucity of research on the pattern of antibiotic prescriptions, antibiotic prescription rate (APR) and the appropriateness of antibiotic use for ARI in U5 children, particularly in this region. Although a previous study by Aminu et al at the same centre explored antibiotic prescribing patterns among paediatric outpatients, it did not assess the appropriateness of antibiotic use against standard treatment guidelines.13 In contrast, the present study comprehensively evaluates both the rate and appropriateness of antibiotic prescriptions based on key parameters—indication, choice, dose, frequency and duration in line with the WHO/PAN guidelines for ARI management. Additionally, this study incorporates data from both the paediatric outpatient and emergency paediatric units (EPUs), providing a broader and more representative analysis of prescribing practices. This distinction addresses an important knowledge gap and contributes more robust evidence to inform antibiotic stewardship efforts and policy recommendations in similar low-resource settings.

Aims and objectives

This study aimed to:

  1. Determine the pattern and rate of antibiotic prescriptions.

  2. Evaluate their appropriateness based on the WHO and PAN guidelines for ARI treatment among U5 children receiving care at the paediatric outpatient and EPUs of the Usmanu Danfodiyo University Teaching Hospital (UDUTH), Sokoto.

Method and materials

Study design and site

This was a retrospective descriptive study with data collected over a 10-month period (1 September 2021–1 July 2022). The study was conducted in the Department of Paediatrics, UDUTH, Sokoto State, Northwestern Nigeria. UDUTH is a tertiary referral centre providing care for patients from Sokoto and neighbouring states including Kebbi, Zamfara, Katsina and Niger. The paediatrics department runs regular outpatient clinics (Monday to Friday) and offers 24-hour inpatient and emergency paediatric care for children aged 0–15 years.

Inclusion criteria

All children aged 2 months to 5 years, admitted through the EPU or seen in the paediatric outpatient department (POPD) with a clinical diagnosis of ARI and who received treatment were included (prescribed at least one antibiotic) in the study.

ARI diagnoses were made based on the Integrated Management of Childhood Illnesses (IMCI)/WHO guidelines, which are routinely used in clinical decision-making in these facilities.

IMCI/WHO classification criteria used for ARI diagnosis

  • Upper respiratory tract infection (URTI):10 Cough, runny nose, low-grade fever, without fast breathing or chest in-drawing.

  • Lower respiratory tract infection (LRTI):

    • Pneumonia: Cough or difficulty breathing with fast breathing (≥50 breaths/min for 2–12 months; ≥40 breaths/min for 1–5 years).

    • Severe pneumonia: Cough or difficulty breathing with one or more general danger signs: inability to drink/breastfeed, vomiting everything, convulsions, lethargy/unconsciousness, chest in-drawing or stridor in a calm child.

Exclusion criteria

Prescriptions were excluded if they met any of the following

  • Age <2 months or >5 years.

  • Missing or incomplete clinical information (eg, age, diagnosis or treatment data).

  • ARI cases not treated with antibiotics (ie, prescriptions without antibiotics).

Patient and public involvement

No patient involved. This was a retrospective study using case folders and treatment sheets. As such, patients and the public were not involved in the design, recruitment or conduct of the study, nor in the dissemination of its results.

Data collection and sources

Records of eligible children seen and/or admitted between 1 September 2021 and 1 July 2022 were retrieved from EPU and POPD admission registers, which contained patient identifiers, sociodemographic characteristics, diagnosis and admission/discharge details. Additional clinical and prescription information was extracted manually from patient’s case notes and prescription sheets, including type and number of drugs prescribed (antibiotic and other medication), antibiotic name, dose, route, frequency and duration, use of generic versus brand names, classification of antibiotics according to WHO/PAN guideline, adherence to the National List of Essential Medicines (NLEM). Only prescriptions meeting the inclusion criteria and containing at least one antibiotic were analysed.

Role of authors

The authors were directly involved in manual retrieval, review and data extraction from patient case notes and prescription sheets. A standard pro forma was used to ensure consistency during data collection.

Operational definitions

  1. Recommended dose/frequency/duration: Antibiotic therapy aligns with WHO/PAN guideline for ARI treatment. In this study, dosage regimen refers to the complete therapeutic plan for an antibiotic prescription, encompassing three core components:

    • Dose: the amount of drug administered at each administration.

    • Frequency: how often the drug is administered per day.

    • Duration: the total number of days the antibiotic was prescribed. Each component was evaluated individually and as part of the overall dosage regimen to assess adherence to guideline recommendations as:

      • Adequate recommendation: All parameters (dose, frequency and duration) appropriate.

      • Under-recommended: Appropriate antibiotic but dose regimen below recommendation.

      • Over-recommended: Appropriate antibiotic but dose regimen above recommendation.

  2. Not recommended antibiotic: Antibiotics not endorsed by the WHO/PAN guideline for ARI management.

  3. Add-on antibiotics: Antibiotics like azithromycin or cotrimoxazole, typically reserved for comorbidities (eg, HIV, sickle cell disease), prescribed for ARI without those conditions

  4. Appropriate antibiotic prescribing: Prescriptions made for the correct indication, using the correct drug and dose regimen for the diagnosis of ARI according to national and WHO guidelines.9 10

  5. Inappropriate antibiotics prescribing includes unnecessary prescribing of antibiotics; and when an antibiotic is needed but the wrong antibiotic is prescribed (inappropriate selection/choice) or the wrong dose is given or the antibiotic is prescribed for the wrong length of time.9 For this study, this included antibiotics prescribed for the treatment of ARI which were categorised to be under, over and not recommended and add-on antibiotics based on the WHO/PAN guideline.9 10

Data analysis

Data were entered and analysed using SPSS V.23. Data were analysed using both descriptive and inferential statistical methods. Descriptive statistics were used to analyse sociodemographic characteristics variables such as age (summarised using median and IQR) and sex (expressed as frequencies and percentages). Types/patterns of ARI diagnosed and classes of antibiotics prescribed were also presented as frequencies and percentages and presented in tables. Differences in age group distribution and sex in children with ARI were tested for statistical significance using χ². A p value<0.05 was considered statistically significant.

Results

During the study period, 964 U5 children were diagnosed with ARI. Of these, 749 (77.7%) were aged <36 months, an age range of 2–59 months and median age of 22 months, IQR of 14–33 months. Males accounted for 51.3% (495) and females 48.7% (469), giving a male-to-female ratio of 1.05:1. There was a statistically significant association between age/sex and ARI occurrence (χ² = 28.055, p<0.001) (table 2).

Discussion

The majority of U5 children in this study fell within the age group of <36 months, with a mean age of 24.4±13.2 months. This finding aligns with previous studies.14,16 However, a lower mean age of 18.3±13 months was reported in Ghana.17 This discrepancy may be attributed to the inclusion of infants as young as 15 days in that study, compared with 2 months in the current study. Males constituted the majority of study participants, consistent with other studies, possibly due to a greater predisposition of males to infections.16 18 19

URTIs accounted for about two-thirds of antibiotic prescriptions for ARI. Most ARI consultations occurred in the EPU, similar to studies in Nepal and Pakistan.20 21 This is expected, as most ARIs begin as mild URTIs that, if untreated or poorly treated, may progress to LRTIs.22 Conversely, some studies reported higher antibiotic prescriptions for LRTIs.14 23 Those studies included bronchial asthma under LRTIs, which was excluded in our study, resulting in fewer LRTI cases.

All prescriptions in this study contained antibiotics, with an APR of 100%. This is significantly higher than the WHO recommended range of 20–26.8% and 13.4–26.1%, respectively.24 Locally, similar patterns have been reported: Adisa et al observed an APR of 98.9% among U5 children in Ibadan, despite only 42.2% of cases showing clinical justification for antibiotics.25 Onubogu et al found that 83% of cough-related visits in Port Harcourt resulted in antibiotic prescriptions, with guideline compliance below 50%.26 These data suggest a persistent culture of empiric and potentially inappropriate antibiotic use across Nigerian paediatric outpatient settings.

This high prevalence aligns with findings from other low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), where APRs range from 70% to 85%.23 27 28 The overuse of antibiotics in this context poses multiple risks: increased AMR, higher treatment costs, blood-borne infections from injectable routes and potential vascular injuries among U5 children in resource-limited settings.24 29 Moreover, antibiotic overuse can damage gut flora, predisposing children to more severe infections.27 Beyond procedural risks, specific classes of antibiotics prescribed carry substantial toxicological burdens. Aminoglycosides, which were commonly used in combination regimens in our setting, are well-documented to cause nephrotoxicity in paediatric populations.30 Young children are particularly vulnerable due to immature renal clearance mechanisms and dosing variability in resource-limited settings. Additionally, frequent use of penicillins and cephalosporins raises the risk of hypersensitivity reactions.31 Such high prescribing rates may reflect weak regulatory systems, poor enforcement of antimicrobial stewardship (AMS) and lack of political will in LMICs.29 32 These challenges have also been documented locally. Although not ARI-specific, Isezuo et al reported widespread empirical antibiotic use and poor guideline adherence among hospitalised children at the same tertiary facility in Sokoto, underscoring systemic issues in paediatric antibiotic prescribing.33

Parenteral antibiotics were prescribed in 71.0% of cases, with a marked contrast between the EPU (94.1%) and the POPD (5.2%). Similar trends were observed by Briggs et al in Rivers State, where 77.4% of hospitalised children received antibiotics, and injectable use was common.34 In northern Nigeria, Aliyu et al also reported high injectable antibiotic use (67.7%) among paediatric inpatients.35 These findings suggest a widespread reliance on parenteral routes in tertiary facilities, possibly driven by clinical urgency, severity of cases seen in tertiary and referral facilities, limited diagnostics or entrenched prescribing habits. However, this is concerning, given the associated risks. On the contrary, a lower prevalence of 40% was reported in Bangladesh, likely due to different case definitions and the inclusion criteria for ARI.36 This study’s hospital-based nature further explains the higher rate, as patients typically presented with more complicated cases requiring urgent intervention. The average number of drugs (2.2) and antibiotics (1.6) per prescription was close to the WHO-recommended ranges, suggesting a reduced risk of polypharmacy and its associated problems, such as drug interactions and adverse effects. However, previous studies reported higher averages (>3).17 23 37 38

Most antibiotics (96.5%) were prescribed by generic name, aligning with WHO targets and findings from other studies.24 33 39 Generic prescribing is the cornerstone of rational medicine use and essential for ensuring access and affordability in LMICs.40 However, Okoye et al found a much lower generic prescribing rate of 51.6% in Bingham Teaching Hospital, highlighting variability in prescribing culture even within the country.41 All antibiotics were from the NLEM, aligning with rational prescribing principles, improving access, ensuring quality-assured medications and reducing costs for both health systems and patients.42 This finding is consistent with prior studies.17 23 39 In India, a lower adherence (77.4%) was observed, likely due to the absence of a national list at the time.38

The most commonly prescribed antibiotic classes were cephalosporins and penicillins, followed by aminoglycosides and nitroimidazoles. Macrolides and sulphonamides were also used, while fluoroquinolones were rarely prescribed, aligning with global patterns.1723 28 38 43,45 The most frequently used antibiotics were ceftriaxone and amoxicillin, consistent with WHO and PAN guidelines. Fluoroquinolones, not recommended for paediatric ARIs, were accordingly underused. However, they were more frequently prescribed in private clinics in India lacking formal guidelines.46 Combination therapy was common, with nearly half of prescriptions containing two antibiotics and one-third involving monotherapy. Common combinations included cephalosporins with nitroimidazoles or aminoglycosides, while penicillin-aminoglycoside and fluoroquinolone-metronidazole combinations were least common. This pattern was also in previous studies.33 47 Alternative therapies were most frequently prescribed, followed by non-recommended options, with less than one-fifth being first-line treatments. This is not unrelatable, as cephalosporins—an alternative line of treatment for ARIs according to WHO/PAN guidelines—were the most commonly prescribed antibiotics in this study.9

Encouragingly, the majority of antibiotics were prescribed for an appropriate duration, aligning with WHO and PAN recommendations that advocate for short-course treatment (5–7 days) for uncomplicated ARIs. This finding suggests an increasing awareness of global antibiotic stewardship principles among prescribers. However, deviations were noted with azithromycin, where 5-day regimens were occasionally used instead of the recommended 3-day course. Prolonged treatment beyond evidence-based guidelines contributes unnecessarily to antimicrobial exposure and increases selective pressure for resistance.48 49 Similar patterns have been reported in other LMIC settings, including India, where overuse of macrolides in ARIs remains common despite their limited indication in such contexts.45 50

Despite the high compliance with treatment duration, only two-thirds of the prescriptions followed the recommended frequency of administration. Errors in frequency—either underprescription or overprescription—were particularly evident with intravenous cefuroxime and azithromycin. Such deviations may reduce therapeutic effectiveness or increase toxicity risks, especially in paediatric populations where pharmacokinetics vary significantly by age and weight. Azithromycin, for example, is associated with gastrointestinal toxicity (nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea) and rare cases of pyloric stenosis and may prolong QT intervals.48 Incorrect dosing intervals may reflect knowledge gaps or inconsistencies in guideline dissemination, as highlighted in recent AMS implementation studies in India.51

A further area of concern was dosage appropriateness, with only two-thirds of prescriptions conforming to recommended weight-based doses. Underdosing was especially common with oral amoxicillin and intravenous cefuroxime, while over-dosing occurred with agents such as oral cefuroxime. Inadequate dosing is associated with increased risk of treatment failure, prolonged illness and development of resistance.27 29 32 These findings echo those of previous studies in South Asia and Africa, where underdosing in paediatrics remains a widespread but under-recognised driver of AMR.23 28 33 47

Notably, one-third of the antibiotics prescribed were either non-recommended or add-on agents, contrary to guideline-based recommendations for uncomplicated ARIs. This inappropriate antibiotic selection undermines rational prescribing efforts and aligns with prior evidence showing high rates of empiric broad-spectrum antibiotic use in paediatric practice.33 38

Similar patterns have been documented across LMICs; for example, Ardillon et al reported widespread inappropriate antibiotic prescribing among outpatient children in Cambodia, Madagascar and Senegal, underscoring the systemic nature of such practices in resource-limited settings.50 The use of non-’Access’ category antibiotics, such as those in the WHO ‘Watch’ or ‘Reserve’ groups, may further contribute to resistance, as emphasised by the Access, Watch, and Reserve (AWaRe) classification framework.12 52

These findings are reinforced by similar data from Nigeria. Aminu et al reported high antibiotic use and poor adherence to standard treatment guidelines for ARI among children in tertiary hospitals in Sokoto, Nigeria, emphasising the urgent need for AMS implementation and guideline-based prescribing.13 Comparable trends were noted in New Delhi, India, where broad-spectrum antibiotics were frequently prescribed for uncomplicated RTIs in primary care.45 Recent AMS programmes have shown improved compliance to WHO AWaRe classification, reduced overprescription and better stewardship outcomes.12 52 Pakistan’s multicentre surveys echoed these findings, with irrational antibiotic prescribing contributing significantly to national AMR burden.44 53

Limitation of the study

Incomplete or missing records/investigations have contributed to not being able to analyse some important data leading to the exclusion of these folders from the study.

Additionally, patient compliance with prescribed antibiotics could not be assessed, as data were obtained solely from medical records. Consequently, this study focused exclusively on prescribing patterns and guideline-based appropriateness, without evaluating treatment outcomes or adherence.

Future research

Future studies should incorporate pharmacoeconomic analyses to assess the cost-effectiveness of antibiotic prescribing practices and their financial burden on both the healthcare system and patients, particularly in resource-limited settings.

Conclusion

While the study revealed a high APR and reliance on alternative therapies, it also demonstrated encouraging levels of appropriate prescribing in terms of duration, dosing, frequency and indication/choice. These findings indicate a moderate level of rational prescribing. However, the persistent overuse of antibiotics—particularly broad-spectrum and second-line agents—raises concerns about increased risks of antibiotic-related adverse effects, AMR and treatment failure. Strengthening antibiotic stewardship programmes and promoting adherence to first-line guidelines are urgently needed to optimise ARI treatment and protect child health in resource-limited settings.
