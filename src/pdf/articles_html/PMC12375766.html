Abstract

Fiber Bragg gratings represent a pivotal advancement in the field of photonics and optical fiber technology. The numerical modeling of fiber Bragg gratings is essential for understanding their optical behavior and optimizing their performance for specific applications. In this paper, numerical solutions for the revered optical fiber Bragg gratings that are considered with a cubic-quintic-septic form of nonlinear medium are constructed first time by using an iterative technique named as residual power series technique (RPST) via conformable derivative. The competency of the technique is examined by several numerical examples. By considering the suitable values of parameters, the power series solutions are illustrated by sketching 2D, 3D, and contour profiles. The results obtained by employing the RPST are compared with exact solutions to reveal that the method is easy to implement, straightforward and convenient to handle a wide range of fractional order systems in fiber Bragg gratings. The obtained solutions can provide help to visualize how light propagates or deforms due to dispersion or nonlinearity.

Keywords: Fiber Bragg gratings, Cubic–quintic–septic form, Residual power series technique, Conformable derivative

Subject terms: Mathematics and computing, Physics

Introduction

Optical fiber is now a primary component of global and local telecommunication systems. A few basic experiments in signal transmission in the 1960s have grown into a multi- billion dollar industry, establishing fiber-based communication networks around the world1. Over the past three decades the advancements in optical fiber have undoubtedly improved and reshaped fiber optic technology so that optical fibers plus related components have become synonymous with telecommunication. In addition to applications in telecommunications, optical fibers are also utilized in the rapidly growing field of fiber sensors2. Optical fiber would not have been possible without fiber Bragg gratings (FBGs)3. FBGs are now commercially available and have found key applications in routing, filtering, control, and amplification of optical signals in the next generation of high-capacity Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM) telecommunication networks4. Rehman and Ahmad5 investigated a distinct set of optical solitons solutions to FBGs with dispersive reflectivity possessing the Kerr law of nonlinear refractive index.

FBGs have a wide range of uses in optical communication systems when chromatic dispersion (CD) is limited. CD is a phenomenon that occurs when various wavelengths of light move at different speeds through fiber. By providing wavelength-specific filters that compensate for changes in propagation velocity, Bragg gratings can be used to lessen the effects of CD. By minimizing signal distortion and retaining the integrity of the sent data, this strategy can help to improve the quality and dependability of optical communication systems, particularly over long distances. Furthermore, Bragg gratings have a number of other vital applications and physical relevance when CD is minimal. Among these applications are the following: The compensation of CD-induced signal distortion is a significant use of Bragg gratings in the setting of CD running down. Bragg gratings can be constructed to cause a precise phase change in reflected light, which can be used to compensate for the phase shift caused by CD. By doing so, the optical signal’s distortion can be decreased or eliminated, enhancing the quality and reliability of data transmission. This capability is especially relevant in high-capacity optical communication systems, because CD effects can be a substantial limiting factor in system performance. Bragg gratings are the versatile and necessary component6.

The analysis of differential equations and dynamical systems is fundamental in modeling and understanding complex physical as well as engineering phenomena. A variety of analytical and numerical methods are employed to obtain solutions to these systems, depending on their linearity, order and complexity. The implementation of unique techniques to create precise solutions of nonlinear PDEs appearing in optical fibers, thermodynamics, wave propagation, and nonlinear optics is one of the most rapidly developing areas of nonlinear dynamics in recent years. In order to obtain the solutions of nonlinear PDEs, the various approaches such as the extended (G’/G)-expansion technique7, extended direct algebraic method8–10, modified mapping method11, sardar subequation approach12,13, the improved generalized Riccati equation mapping technique14, -model expansion method15, the Hirota bilinear symbolic computational method16, the generalized exponential rational function method16, modified exponential technique17, modified extended tanh-expansion method18 and generalized Kudryashov method19 have been employed in literature.

Several topics have been discussed so far in this area, including, Koh et al.20 studied optical solitons by He variational Principle in a Non-Kerr Law media. Biswas et al.21 studied resonant 1-soliton solution in anti-cubic nonlinear medium with perturbations. Yan et al.22 discussed dromion-like structures and periodic wave solutions for variable-coefficients complex cubic-quintic Ginzburg-Landau equation influenced by higher-order effects and nonlinear gain.

The incorporation of conformable calculus into the study of optical solitons enhances the understanding of their dynamics by providing a more accurate and versatile framework for modeling. This approach offers a deeper insight into soliton behavior, stability, and propagation, which is essential for advancing the design and optimization of photonic devices in optical communication systems29,30.

The residual power series technique is a useful mathematical tool for constructing the numerical solutions of various differential systems. RPST is effective and easy to use for solving linear and nonlinear initial value problems without linearization, purturbation or discritization. This technique constructs an analytical approximate solution in form of series that are infinitely many times differentiable. Also, it doesn’t take a lot of time or computer memory to arrive at the results. Its nature is global in terms of the approximative analytical answers, and it can be applied to research a range of physical, mathematical, and engineering issues. In addition to applying approximate analytical solutions and their derivatives, it also gives the option of selecting any point inside the integration period. However, the primary technique for computing coefficients in the RPST has two main drawbacks. First, it can be cumbersome due to the repeated evaluation of residual errors at each iteration. Second, it involves numerous calculations of fractional derivatives during the solution process. Nevertheless, these drawbacks are relatively minor and do not outweigh the overall effectiveness and reliability of the method. Consequently, RPST remains a valuable and dependable approach for solving nonlinear equations involving conformable derivatives.

This proposed technique is used to solve time-fractional Whitham–Broer–Kaup equations31, KdV equation32, Fractional Bagley–Torvik problems33, fractional Klein–Gordon problem34, time fractional gas dynamics equations35 and other physical phenomena. Alquran36 discussed in depth the residual power series (RPS) for the time-fractional foam drainage equation. Al-Khaled’s et al.37 purpose of his research is to present a RPST for approximating a Drinfeld-Sokolov-Wilson system. Kumar et al.38 used a robust approximate iterative mathematical approach based on residual power series (RPS) algorithm in order to get the numerical solutions of the nonlinear time fractional Black-Scholes (BS) equations based on European options. Abu Arqub et al.39 obtained approximate analytical solutions to a complex nonlinear fractional models that are fractional Kundu-Eckhaus and massive Thirring models. Based on the generalization of conformable power series, this method proposes a systematic process for creating a set of periodic wave series solutions and provides the unknown coefficients in a clear pattern. Al-Smadi et al.40 taken into consideration an innovative and successful analytical strategy to solve classes of nonlinear coupled fractional Schrödinger equations and nonlinear time-fractional resonant Schrödinger equations under conformable fractional derivatives. The methodology for the solution entails minimizing the residual error functions to produce an infinite conformable series solution with a trustworthy wave pattern. El Ajou et al.41 used RPST for nonlinear fractional KdV-Burgers equation via conformable fractional derivative. Senol et al.42 introduced the RPST for finding approximate solutions of the time-fractional Burgers type equations using the conformable fractional derivative definition. El-Ajou43 investigated the conformable fractional derivative based time-fractional nonlinear dispersive (TFND) partial differential equations using RPST.

Extensive research has been devoted to the analytical treatment of coupled nonlinear Schrodinger equations incorporating cubic-quintic-septic nonlinearities and dispersive reflectivity, governed by a polynomial law for the nonlinear refractive index. Despite these efforts, there has been little to no attention given to the numerical investigation of such models, leaving a significant gap in the literature regarding their computational analysis and practical behavior under general conditions.

The primary motivation of this work is to obtain, for the first time, numerical solutions of the coupled nonlinear Schrodinger equation with cubic-quintic-septic dispersive reflectivity, incorporating a polynomial law for the nonlinear refractive index. In this article, the RPST is employed, for the first time, to construct numerical solutions to the proposed problem. RPST offers a series-based approach to solving dynamical systems without the need for an analytical solution. By utilizing a truncated power series, this method effectively approximates the desired solutions. The graphical results illustrate the impact of the temporal conformable derivative on the behavior of the solutions, highlighting the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The paper is organized as follows: The brief introductions to conformable fractional derivative and RPST are presented in “Some fundamental concepts from conformable calculus” and “Fundamental concepts for the RPST”, respectively. “RPST to FBGs for cubic-quintic-septic dispersive reflectivity with time-fractional derivative” gives a description of the basic processes of the suggested method. “Application of RPST” represents the power series solutions and graphical findings for various applications. Results and discussion

In the optical fiber industry, RPS helps model and predict the behavior of light and signals in fibers under real-world conditions. The power series represents how the optical field evolves along the fiber. Each term refines the approximation, giving more accurate insight into the behavior of the light wave. Also, it will help to predict how ultrashort pulses evolve under nonlinear and dispersive effects. In short, these solutions can be used to enhance fiber design, optimize performance, and reduce cost in the optical fiber industry. RPSM solutions have direct physical meanings: they describe light intensity, phase, pulse shape, and wave behavior in optical fibers. These solutions help the industry by enabling: accurate modeling of nonlinear and dispersive effects, faster design and optimization of fibers and better performance of communication systems.

Conclusion

For the first time, the RPST was successfully applied within the framework of conformable derivatives to obtain numerical solutions for a coupled nonlinear Schrodinger equation with cubic-quintic-septic dispersive reflectivity, incorporating a polynomial law for the nonlinear refractive index. The use of the conformable derivative simplified the fractional modeling process while preserving essential physical characteristics. RPST via conformable derivative does not require transformations, perturbations, linearizations, or discretizations. One of the key strengths of RPST lies in its ability to ensure the convergence of the series solution by minimizing the residual error. The applicability of the method was validated by solving three distinct problems, demonstrating its robustness. In each case, RPST produced rapidly convergent, series-based approximations with high accuracy. Accurate solutions were obtained and illustrated using 2D, 3D, and contour plots, all of which demonstrated consistent behavior across varying values of the fractional order. The results confirm that RPST is a powerful and effective method for solving high-order nonlinear equations, providing reliable solutions even in the presence of strong nonlinear effects. Realistic models along with variable-order systems may be the focus within future extended approaches.
