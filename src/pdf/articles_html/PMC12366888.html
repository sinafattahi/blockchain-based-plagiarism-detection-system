Abstract

Background:

The incidence of total knee arthroplasty (TKA), an effective orthopaedic procedure for end-stage osteoarthritis, is increasing substantially. The shift toward performing this procedure in the outpatient setting necessitates precise patient selection criteria to ensure optimal postoperative outcomes. This study examines the predictive value of preoperative patient-reported outcome measures (PROMs), including the Brief Resilience Scale (BRS), in determining candidacy for outpatient TKA.

Methods:

A retrospective analysis of 2,167 patients undergoing elective primary, unilateral TKA from May 2020 to June 2023 was conducted. Preoperative PROMs, including Knee Injury and Osteoarthritis Outcome Score for Joint Replacement (KOOS, JR), Patient-Reported Outcomes Measurement Information System (PROMIS)-10, Risk Assessment and Prediction Tool (RAPT), and BRS, were collected. Patients were stratified into three groups by BRS score: low (BRS score < 3), average (BRS score ≥ 3 and < 4.3), and high (BRS score ≥ 4.3) resilience. Length of stay (LOS), discharge disposition, and complications were assessed postoperatively. Logistic regression and receiver operative characteristic (ROC) curve analyses were used to assess the predictive validity of PROMs for discharge to a skilled nursing facility (SNF) in conjunction with extended LOS.

Results:

Patients with low resilience (BRS score < 3) were found to have a significantly higher likelihood of discharge to a SNF and extended LOS compared with the other two groups (11.5% vs. 1.6%, P < 0.001). Logistic regression demonstrated that BRS, RAPT, and PROMIS-10 scores were also notable predictors, with a combined model predicting an extended LOS and discharge to SNF with 82.4% sensitivity and 79.9% specificity.

Conclusions:

Preoperative PROMs such as BRS can serve as a valuable tool in predicting the failure of outpatient TKA. Incorporating preoperative PROMs into presurgical decision making can enhance patient selection for ambulatory procedures and improve surgical outcomes. Additional prospective studies are warranted to validate these findings within other elective surgical populations and to determine how to best modify resilience.

Total knee arthroplasty (TKA) is a highly effective treatment of knee osteoarthritis that has failed conservative management.1 As the population ages, the incidence of TKA has also increased in kind, with a 143% rise expected by 2050.2 The average hospital stay after TKA has decreased substantially in recent years, especially after 2018 when the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services removed TKA from their inpatient-only list,3 with most of the TKAs performed either in a hospital and billed as an “extended recovery” outpatient overnight stay, or at an Ambulatory Surgery Center (ASC) as an outpatient same-day surgery.

For many patients, outpatient or same-day discharge TKA is a safe and effective option. Outpatient TKA has increased from 1.8% in 2015 to 57.2% in 2020, although many patients with an outpatient designation have the surgery performed in a hospital setting and remain overnight.3 True same-day knee arthroplasty in free-standing ASCs is also increasing markedly. ASCs often cater to a younger, healthier population.4 Some surgeons, especially those most accustomed to observing their patients overnight, are wary of the limited resources that ASCs provide and the consequences of failed same-day discharge. Preliminary analyses do not show worse outcomes regarding readmission or complications in patients treated in the ASC setting, but concerns about patient selection remain because not all patients are suited for TKA at an ASC.2,5-7

Preoperative patient-reported outcome measures (PROMs) have been used to determine a baseline for patient function and to predict the likelihood of patient success after TKA.8,9 PROMs have also been used to evaluate other aspects of a patient's health, including their mental health.10 These PROMs have historically been used to look at whether a patient will have a good outcome 1 year after healing from the index surgery10 and are currently being used by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services as the sole metric of success after surgery.11,12 While good baseline physical health is an important metric for determining candidacy for outpatient TKA, it is becoming increasingly evident that mental health also plays a role in patient selection.13

Resilience, defined as the ability to bounce back from stress or adversity, has been a popular focus of clinical research in recent years. There are numerous orthopaedic studies across all subspecialties that assess resilience in relation to surgical outcomes. However, these studies often assess resilience at a single postoperative time point. Few studies have looked at preoperative resilience as a factor in outcomes. The Brief Resilience Scale (BRS) is a validated instrument developed to assess resilience and may help provide new insights into the relationship between resilience and postoperative outcomes. Greater resilience is associated with lower anxiety responses, suggesting that resilient patients may experience better postoperative outcomes, including lower analgesic requirements.14-16 Higher scores on the BRS are correlated with better mental health and improved knee function after TKA.17 A small 2019 prospective study used the BRS in TKA to show that preoperative resilience is a notable predictor of physical health and overall quality of life at 12 months postoperatively.18 While this study looked at long-term patient-reported outcomes, short-term outcomes such as discharge to a skilled nursing facility (SNF) or extended length of stay (LOS) have not been assessed with a focus on resilience.

The purpose of this study was to determine whether the BRS along with other preoperative PROMs could be used to determine whether a patient was at risk of failing outpatient TKA. SNF discharge or extended LOS (greater than 3 days) was used as a surrogate for failure of outpatient TKA. We hypothesize that patients with low preoperative BRS scores are at higher risk of failing the ASC setting, whereas patients with a high BRS score are more likely to succeed in the ASC setting.

Methods

Institutional review board (IRB) approval was granted before beginning this retrospective study (HHC-2023-0227). The data set included all patients aged 18 to 89 years undergoing elective, primary, unilateral TKA between May 15, 2020, and June 30, 2023. This yielded a total of 3,370 patients. Patients with incomplete preoperative patient-reported outcomes or patients taking preoperative opioid medications were excluded, as pain and morphine milligram equivalents (MMEs) were examined. This yielded a final study group of 2,167 patients who had completed all of their preoperative patient-reported outcome measures, including the Knee Injury and Osteoarthritis Outcome Score for Joint Replacement (KOOS, JR), the PROMIS-10 Global Health Survey (PROMIS-10), the Risk Assessment and Prediction Tool (RAPT), and the Brief Resiliency Scale (BRS). All patient-reported outcomes were completed in the 30 days before their index procedure. Surveys were collected using Force Therapeutics (New York, NY), an online engagement platform.

All surgeries were performed by fellowship trained arthroplasty surgeons. Each surgery was performed using a medial parapatellar approach, without resection of the posterior cruciate ligament. Multiple vendor implants and implant designs, including cruciate-retaining, posterior-stabilized, and medial-congruent, were included without restriction, with equal distribution. Both noncemented and cemented fixation techniques were included, although 90.1% of implants were cemented. All patients were enrolled in a dedicated preoperative class and received postoperative physical therapy support multiple times while inpatient. Extensive counseling was provided to all patients to ensure that the patient could return home, including confirming a care partner in place. Patients were not approved for surgery unless they met these criteria.

Patients were divided into three groups, per Smith et al19: those with a BRS score < 3 were classified as having low resilience, those with a BRS score ≥ 3 and < 4.3 were classified as having average resilience, and those with a BRS score ≥ 4.3 were considered to have high resilience. For the PROMIS-10, Physical Function and Mental Health subscores were calculated and compared with those of the population as a whole. A change of 10 points, or one standard deviation, was considered significant, with scores less than 40 in either domain considered to be representative of poor health.13

The patient's electronic medical record was used to extract demographic data, medical and social history, surgical and inpatient stay parameters, and 30-day complications such as emergency department visits. Single outcome measures, such as discharge disposition, psychiatric history (confirmed diagnosis of anxiety or depression), or a 30-day complication, were compared using Chi-squared tests of proportion. Continuous variables, such as surgical time or ambulation distance, were compared using one-way analyses of variance or Kruskal-Wallis tests. The statistical analysis conducted depended on the underlying distribution of the variable. For values that were reported multiple times over the LOS, such as pain and MMEs, an average was taken. Physical therapy parameters, such as ambulation distance, and Timed Up and Go (TUG) tests were assessed using the best score that the patient was able to achieve.

Preliminary data analysis showed no difference in BRS scores based on implant type or fixation; therefore, the entire data set was used. Univariate logistic regression models were used to determine whether the BRS was predictive of discharge to a skilled nursing facility or a LOS greater than two standard deviations from the mean. Patients who had an extended LOS and who were discharged to a skilled nursing facility were assigned a categorical variable of 1, whereas those who did not meet criteria were assigned a value of 0. A nonparametric receiver operative characteristic (ROC) curve analysis was conducted, and variables with an area under the curve of greater than 0.7 were considered to be predictive in the model. Sensitivity and specificity were calculated, as were cut points for all variables of interest. Analyses were completed using STATA (Version 17, StatCorp) or Microsoft Excel 2016 (Microsoft). If P < 0.05, the result was considered to be statistically significant.

Results

Table 1 lists the demographic characteristics of the 2,167 patients included in the study. The low resilience group (BRS score < 3) was the smallest, consisting of 156 patients. Patients with low BRS scores were more likely to be female (P < 0.001) and were much more likely to have a psychiatric history (P < 0.001), with 28% of patients having a known diagnosis of anxiety or depression, compared with 10% of patients in the high resilience group (BRS score ≥ 4.3). The preoperative RAPT score was lower in patients with a BRS score < 3 (P < 0.001). Patients in the low resilience group were also much more likely to be discharged to a SNF, with 11.5% of patients going to a SNF instead of home. Among patients in the high resilience group, fewer than 1.6% were discharged to a SNF (P < 0.001) instead of home.

Univariate modeling of preoperative factors showed that RAPT score, age, PROMIS-10 Physical Function score, and BRS score were all highly predictive of a SNF discharge, with an area under the curve greater than 0.70. The BRS and RAPT scores were predictive of an extended LOS. Combining these two models, a multivariate model was used to obtain cutoffs for a combination of extended LOS and SNF discharge (Table 5), which would make a patient more likely to benefit from an inpatient stay. Supplementary Figure 1 (http://links.lww.com/JG9/A429) shows ROC curves for these variables, as well as the PROMIS-10 Mental Health and Knee Injury and Osteoarthritis Outcome Score for Joint Replacement scores for the combined model. This multivariate model had a sensitivity of 82.4% and a specificity of 79.9% in predicting an extended LOS plus a SNF discharge. Every one-point decrease in the BRS score was associated with 2.78-fold increased odds of SNF discharge in conjunction with an extended LOS (P < 0.001).

Discussion

Because 36% of the cost after total joint arthroplasty can be attributed to SNF discharge, and because patients discharged to SNFs are at greater risk of postoperative complications, minimizing discharge to a facility is advantageous for both the surgeon and the patient.20,21 As more patients undergo their TKAs in ASCs, the ability to use preoperative PROMs to predict which patients might be poor candidates for outpatient surgeries should be critically evaluated. These patients, who might require extended LOS and subsequent discharge to a SNF, may not have standard comorbidities but may lack the resilience needed to bounce back from a painful procedure like a TKA. As healthcare costs continue to increase, there is a strategic push to optimize care and minimize costs where applicable.

This study demonstrates that patients with low resilience, as evidenced by a preoperative BRS score less than three, are much more likely to be discharged to a SNF after TKA and require an extended LOS. While patients with low resiliency were not more likely to have a complication, their initial physical function and mental health were much less robust than those with a higher BRS score. These patients were more likely to have a provider-diagnosed psychiatric history and have a lower RAPT score than patients with a BRS score greater than 4.3. While psychiatric history alone is not predictive of SNF discharge, it is associated with extended LOS.22We did not see an increase in postoperative emergency department utilization or readmission associated with low resiliency or psychiatric history, as had been reported previously,23 although the absolute numbers do trend toward significance.

In addition, while patients in the low BRS group had significantly lower PROMIS-10 Mental Health T-scores, the PROMIS-10 Mental Health T-score did not contribute to the extended LOS/SNF discharge model in a statistically significant way. Conversely, the PROMIS-10 Physical Function T-score was a statistically significant part of the model, with patients scoring below 40 more likely to require extended LOS/SNF discharge; this correlates with the results of Hays et al,24 indicating that scores in this range are two standard deviations below the mean.

The BRS, RAPT, and PROMIS-10 Physical Function consist of a total of 22 questions, which, when collected preoperatively, factoring in age, can give a predictive value over 80% of needing extended LOS and subsequent SNF discharge. The BRS alone was modeled, and this showed that the BRS was highly sensitive, but not as specific as the three preoperative PROMs combined (sensitivity: 83.7%, specificity: 57.1%). The cutoff for the BRS was 3.83 in both models. Our model also showed a RAPT score less than 6 as being predictive of requiring extended LOS and SNF discharge, which is supported in the literature.25,26

If a patient has a BRS score less than 3.8, especially combined with a low RAPT score (less than 6) or PROMIS-10 Physical Function T-score (less than 40), we recommend avoiding the ambulatory surgery setting. The involvement of a care partner or care partners preoperatively might also help support this patient so that they can have a better outcome after TKA27 because shared decision making is crucial for patient success. In addition, referral to psychotherapy before their TKA might be beneficial to certain patients to address their expectations and potential catastrophizing behavior. If lifestyle modification programs are available, referral to these programs is also recommended. Preoperative physical therapy may also be of benefit as our physical therapists use an ambulation distance of 50 feet as a benchmark for home discharge. We found that while BRS scores were the same for patients who walked 50 feet compared with those who did not while inpatient, 12% of those who did not walk 50 feet were discharged to a SNF, compared with 2% of those who could walk that distance.

This study does have limitations. This was a retrospective review, and a number of patients were excluded because of incomplete preoperative PROMs, which does increase the risk of selection bias. The PROMs evaluated were statistically significantly different, although one might argue that a difference of six points is not clinically relevant. In addition, only preoperative PROMs were assessed during model development, and postsurgical outcomes, such as physical therapy parameters, were not incorporated. The addition of these parameters might improve the model, but we chose to focus on only preoperative factors in this study. The BRS cutoffs were based on a previously established threshold, which may not be accurate in the total joint population. Although the study population was large, all patients came from a single institution and were relatively homogeneous regarding race, ethnicity, and insurance status. Therefore, the results of this study might not be generalizable for a more diverse population.

The results of this study suggest that it would be beneficial to measure the BRS scores in all patients scheduled to undergo a TKA, especially those who are opting for a same-day procedure. Patients with a score below the cutoff should be further evaluated to determine whether they would still be a candidate for surgery in the ambulatory setting. The BRS, in combination with the RAPT, PROMIS-10 Physical Function, and age, is sensitive and specific in determining patients who might require an extended LOS and SNF discharge. These can be incorporated in a standard presurgical evaluation for all patients undergoing TKA, especially for patients wanting to undergo TKA in an ASC.
