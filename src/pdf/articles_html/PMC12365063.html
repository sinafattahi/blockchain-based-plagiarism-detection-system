Abstract

This study presents a comprehensive theoretical analysis of PD-L1-In-1 (C21H23N5O2) using the B3LYP functional with the 6-311G(d) basis set, focusing on its structural, electronic, and spectroscopic properties. Fourier Transform Infrared (FT-IR), Raman, and UV–Vis spectra were simulated, and vibrational modes were assigned via potential energy distribution (PED) analysis using the VEDA 4 program. Natural Bond Orbital (NBO) analysis revealed hyperconjugative interactions (E2) and provided insights into donor–acceptor electron densities. The energy band gap was obtained from HOMO–LUMO calculations and further analyzed through the density of states (DOS) spectrum. Electron Localization Function (ELF) and Localized Orbital Locator (LOL) analyses, performed using Multiwfn, highlighted regions of electron localization and orbital overlap. Reduced Density Gradient (RDG) analysis uncovered non-covalent interactions. Ground-state 1H and 13C NMR chemical shifts were predicted using the Gauge-Independent Atomic Orbital (GIAO) method. Fukui function analysis identified reactive sites and evaluated the chemical reactivity of the molecule. Molecular docking studies using AutoDock Vina explored interactions between PD-L1-In-1 and the PD-L1 checkpoint protein, shedding light on its potential biological activity. Notably, the simulations indicated strong ligand–protein interactions, positioning PD-L1-In-1 as a promising candidate for cancer immunotherapy targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway.

Introduction

The interaction between Programmed Death-1 (PD-1) and its ligand PD-L1 is a key mechanism by which cancer cells evade immune detection, allowing tumors to escape the host’s immune response1. This PD-1/PD-L1 pathway impairs T-lymphocyte proliferation, cytokine secretion, and cytotoxic function, ultimately leading to the exhaustion and apoptosis of tumor-specific T cells2. In recent years, checkpoint inhibitors targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway have achieved remarkable success, driving significant advancements in immunotherapy3–5. These inhibitors have demonstrated efficacy across various tumor types, treatment routes, drug combinations, and therapeutic regimens, positioning them as promising agents in cancer treatment6,7.

Our study centers on PD-L1-In-1 (C21H23N5O2), a biphenyl-based small-molecule inhibitor targeting PD-L1. Initially identified by Bristol Myers Squibb (BMS), biphenyl-derived PD-L1 inhibitors have shown efficacy in disrupting PD-1/PD-L1 interactions through a unique mechanism involving protein dimer formation8–13. Small-molecule inhibitors like PD-L1-In-1 offer an attractive alternative to antibody-based immunotherapies, potentially mitigating some of their associated drawbacks, such as immune-related adverse events, high production costs, limited stability, poor tumor penetration, inability for oral administration and immunogenicity concerns14,15. Moreover, unlike other small-molecule inhibitors that struggle to target the relatively flat and highly hydrophobic PD-1/PD-L1 surface, the BMS-developed PD-L1 inhibitors overcome these challenges, offering enhanced binding affinity and stability11,16.

Despite the promising potential of PD-L1-In-1, it has not yet been the subject of a thorough theoretical investigation. This study aims to address this gap by employing Density Functional Theory (DFT) to predict the structural, molecular, electronic, and vibrational characteristics of PD-L1-In-1. We utilized the B3LYP hybrid exchange-correlation functional, known for its efficiency and reliability in examining conjugated systems and species containing lone pairs17–19. Additionally, we analyzed the molecule in different charge states and in the solvent DMSO to better understand its chemical reactivity and stability in various environments. Moreover, we explored interactions between PD-L1-In-1 and the PD-L1 checkpoint protein, providing insights into its potential biological activity. Our theoretical findings offer valuable insights that may guide future experimental research.

Methodology

All spectroscopic and quantum chemical calculations for the title compound were performed using the DFT/B3LYP/6-311G(d) level of theory with Gaussian 09W20. In this study, Density Functional Theory (DFT) was employed to predict the optimized molecular structure, harmonic vibrational frequencies, UV–Vis spectra, 1H NMR and 13C NMR spectra, frontier molecular orbitals (FMO), molecular electrostatic potential (MEP), natural bond orbital (NBO) analysis, Mulliken atomic charges, Fukui functions, and thermodynamic properties of the title molecule. The 6-311G(d) basis set was selected for its balance between computational cost and accuracy21,22. Multiwfn23 was used for the analysis of localized orbital locator (LOL), electron localization function (ELF), and reduced density gradient (RDG) of the title molecule. The density of states (DOS) spectra were generated using GaussSum24. Potential energy distribution (PED) assignments were made using VEDA 425. Additionally, molecular docking studies were carried out with AutoDock Vina26. Data processing, statistical analyses, and visualization were conducted using Python and Microsoft Excel. All calculations and software executions were performed on an MSI GL65 9SEK personal laptop.

NH vibration

N–H stretching vibrations are typically observed in the region between 3100 and 3500 cm−131. In our study, the N–H stretching modes (Mode Numbers 147 and 146) were found at harmonic frequencies of 3530.44 cm−1 and 3487.75 cm−1 in the gaseous neutral state. In DMSO solution, these modes appeared at 3532.90 cm−1 and 3488.40 cm−1 for the neutral state. For the gaseous anionic state, the N–H stretching vibrations were identified at 3489.69 cm−1 and 3453.76 cm−1; in DMSO solution, they were observed at 3490.70 cm−1 and 3450.70 cm−1. In the gaseous cationic state, the harmonic frequencies were noted at 3517.63 cm−1 and 3466.14 cm−1; in DMSO, they were detected at 3520.92 cm−1 and 3467.37 cm−1. These assignments have a 100% PED value. Despite variations in the physical state of the molecule, the observed spectra are remarkably consistent, as corroborated by the literature30. Slight differences were noted when comparing spectra across different charged states.

CH vibration

Typically, CH stretching vibrations in aromatic compounds are observed as multiple weak and strong bands in the range of 3100–3000 cm−132. In our study, all CH stretching vibrations (Mode Numbers 145–125) were detected within this expected range. For the title molecule in the neutral state, the CH stretching bands were observed from 3097.09 to 2923.84 cm−1 in the gas phase, and from 3096.14 to 2925.16 cm−1 in DMSO solution. In the anionic state, the CH stretching bands were found in the range 3084.82–2904.51 cm−1 in the gas phase and 3086.72–2912.25 cm−1 in DMSO solution. Similarly, in the cationic state, the CH stretching bands were identified in the range 3124.25–2929.32 cm−1 in the gas phase and 3126.00–2933.29 cm−1 in DMSO. The best PED values for the neutral, cationic, and anionic states, in both the gas phase and DMSO, ranged between 85 and 100%. Notably, the peaks corresponding to CH stretching vibrations in the Raman spectra were more prominent compared to those in the IR spectra.

C=O vibration

The region between 1870 and 1540 cm−1 is characteristic of the C=O stretching mode. The C=O stretching vibrations are influenced by the physical state, electronic effects, mass effects of neighboring substituents, conjugations, and intramolecular and intermolecular hydrogen bonding33–36. In PD-L1-In-1, the C=O stretching mode (mode 124) is observed at 1716.37 cm−1 in the gas phase and 1705.08 cm−1 in DMSO for the neutral state. For the anionic state, it appears at 1701.62 cm−1 in the gas phase and 1687.13 cm−1 in DMSO. In the cationic state, the C=O stretching mode is detected at 1645.78 cm−1 in the gas phase and 1704.30 cm−1 in DMSO. These assignments exhibit PED values in the range of 75–80%. The peaks corresponding to C=O stretching vibrations are more intense in the IR spectra compared to the Raman spectra. Additionally, C=O stretching vibrations with small PED contributions (< 25%) were observed at various harmonic frequencies across all charged states in both the gas phase and DMSO solvent.

CC vibration

C–C ring stretching vibrations in aromatic groups typically appear in the regions of 1280–1380 cm−1, 1430–1465 cm−1, 1470–1540 cm−1, 1575–1590 cm−1, and 1590–1625 cm−1 with variable intensity32. For the title molecule, C–C ring vibrations in the neutral, cationic, and anionic states, both in the gas phase and in DMSO solvent, were observed within these expected ranges, with PED contributions exceeding 30%.

CN vibration

In our study, the C=N stretching vibrations were assigned to the region of 1450–1580 cm−1 for all neutral, cationic, and anionic states, both in the gas phase and in DMSO solvent. This range generally aligns with the assignment by Silverstein36, which is 1500–1600 cm−1. In these regions, C=N stretching vibrations were often accompanied by HCN bending vibrations. Moderate to strong intensities of C=N stretching vibrations with PED values around 60% were observed. Additionally, C–N stretching vibrations were detected in the region of 1100–1390 cm−1, frequently in conjunction with HCN bending vibrations, HCH bending vibrations, and other modes. The overlap of several bands in the region 1270–1440 cm−1 made the identification and classification of different vibrations challenging.

NMR analysis

Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy provides comprehensive insights into molecular structure, chemical environment, dynamics, and concentration by analyzing the magnetic properties of atomic nuclei37–40. NMR calculations were performed on the PD-L1-IN-1 molecule in its neutral gas phase using the SCF GIAO method at the B3LYP/6-311G(d) level of theory. The theoretical 1H and 13C NMR chemical shifts for PD-L1-In-1, as detailed in Fig. 5, reveal a broad distribution of signals. The 1H chemical shifts range from 23.78 to 30.64 ppm, while the 13C shifts span from 7.08 to 167.53 ppm. This wide range of 13C shifts suggests diverse carbon environments within the molecule. Particularly, the highest 13C shift of 167.53 ppm is indicative of a carbonyl group, and the shifts between 115 and 139 ppm are characteristic of aromatic or unsaturated carbons. The 1H spectrum shows multiple instances of equivalent protons, with notable degeneracies observed at 24.79 ppm (degeneracy 4) and 24.99 ppm (degeneracy 3).

NBO analysis

The Natural Bond Orbital (NBO) procedure provides an efficient method for identifying the bond and lone pairs that compose an optimized Lewis structure of a molecule41. The set of orthonormal NBOs offers a compact and stable representation of the electron density in a molecule42 and serves as a useful basis for investigating charge-transfer or hyperconjugative interactions43. Delocalization of electron density between occupied (bond or lone pair) NBOs and formally unoccupied (antibond or Rydberg) NBOs corresponds to stabilizing interactions that often influence the features of the potential energy surface41.

The interaction between filled orbitals of one subsystem and vacant orbitals of another represents a deviation from the Lewis structure and can be used to measure intermolecular delocalization, also known as hyperconjugation. A larger E(2) value indicates a more intense interaction between electron donors and acceptors, reflecting a greater donating tendency and an increased degree of conjugation within the system. The second-order perturbation theory analysis of the Fock Matrix in the NBO basis for PD-L1-In-1, summarized in Table 3, demonstrates significant electron delocalization within the triazine ring system. Strong donor-acceptor interactions between the N–C bonds of the ring were identified, exhibiting energies ranging from 37.04 to 43.8 kcal/mol. Notably, the interaction from BD N7–C25 to BD* N6–C27 showed the highest stabilization energy of 43.8 kcal/mol, indicating substantial electron delocalization and confirming the highly conjugated nature of the triazine rings, characterized by a nearly symmetrical distribution of interaction energies around the ring. Additionally, significant lone pair interactions involving the carbonyl group were observed, with the lone pair on oxygen (O2) exhibiting strong interactions with adjacent bonds, specifically BD* N4–C26 (24.43 kcal/mol) and BD* C26–C28 (19.91 kcal/mol), which underscores the electron-withdrawing nature of the carbonyl group. Furthermore, notable lone pair interactions were found for nitrogen atoms N3 and N4; LP N3 to BD* N7–C25 displayed a high stabilization energy of 64.8 kcal/mol, while LP N4 to BD* O2–C26 showed 59.63 kcal/mol, suggesting that nitrogen lone pairs play a crucial role in the overall electronic structure of the molecule. The NBO analysis also revealed multiple interactions between carbon-carbon bonds with energies around 20 kcal/mol, characteristic of aromatic systems (e.g., BD C8–C9 to BD* C10–C14 at 21.03 kcal/mol and BD C11–C18 to BD* C22–C23 at 21.19 kcal/mol). Electron Localization Function (ELF) and Localized Orbital Locator (LOL)

Real space function analysis is one of the most important functions of Multiwfn especially ELF and LOL for locating high localization regions. ELF explains the electron pair density whereas LOL explains maximum localized orbitals overlapping due to gradient of orbitals46.ELF is within the range of [0,1]. A large ELF value means that the electrons are greatly localized, indicating that there is a covalent bond, a lone pair or inner shells of the atom involved. The value range of LOL is identical to ELF, namely [0,1]. Small (large) LOL value usually appears in boundary (inner) region of localized orbitals because the gradient of orbital wave function is large (small) in this area. From ELF plot in Fig. 6, high ELF regions are seen around hydrogen atoms, indicating the presence of higly localized bonding and non-bonding electrons. The blue regions around few carbon atoms show the delocalized electron cloud. In LOL plot shown in Fig. 7, covalent regions between hydrogen and nitrogen atoms are highlighted by red areas, signifying high LOL values. The electron-depleted regions between valence shell and inner shell are depicted as blue circles around the carbon and oxygen nuclei.

Reduced Density Gradient (RDG)

Weak interactions have a significant influence on the conformation of macromolecules and the binding modes of proteins and ligands. RDG and sign(λ2)ρ are a pair of very important functions employed in Multiwfn for revealing weak interaction regions. The strength of weak interactions has a positive correlation with electron density ρ in the corresponding region. Van der Waals interaction regions typically have very small ρ, while the regions corresponding to strong steric effects or evident attractive weak interactions (e.g., hydrogen bonds, halogen bonds) generally have relatively large ρ47,48. A graphical representation of ρ(r) versus sign(λ2)ρ, where sign(λ2)ρ shown in Fig. 8 is derived from the second eigenvalue of the electron density Hessian matrix, provides convenient information regarding the strength and nature of these interactions. Strong attractive interactions, arising from hydrogen bonds, are indicated by the blue regions at negative sign(λ2)ρ values (< −0.02 a.u.). The extensive green areas (− 0.01 < sign(λ2)ρ < 0.01 a.u.) suggest prevalent weak van der Waals interactions, which likely contribute to the overall structural stability. Notably, significant red regions at positive sign(λ2)ρ values (> 0.02 a.u.) indicate the presence of repulsive steric interactions.

Molecular electrostatic potential

The Molecular Electrostatic Potential (MEP) is a valuable descriptor for identifying sites of potential electronic attacks, nucleophilic reactions, and hydrogen-bonding interactions49–51. On the MEP map, the highest positive and negative regions are typically represented by blue and red colors, respectively, indicating favored sites for nucelophilic and electrophilic attacks.

To predict the reactive sites for electrophilic and nucleophilic attacks in the title molecule, the MEP was calculated using the B3LYP/6-311G(d) level of theory. In the neutral state, the PD-L1-In-1 molecule shows a minimum electrostatic potential primarily localized around the oxygen atoms, suggesting these regions are more reactive towards electrophilic attacks. Conversely, the maximum electrostatic potential is concentrated around hydrogen, nitrogen, and carbon atoms, highlighting these areas as highly reactive for nucleophilic attacks. In the anionic state, most atoms become favorable for electrophilic attacks, while in the cationic state, they are more inclined to nucleophilic attacks, as illustrated in Fig. 9.

The contour lines shown in Fig. 10, which vary in color from yellow to red, represent regions of equal electron density. Red lines indicate higher electron density, while yellow lines denote lower values. In the neutral state, contour lines are densely packed around oxygen (O1, O2) and nitrogen atoms (N1–N7), indicating high electron density due to their electronegativity. In the anionic state, the contour lines become denser and more concentrated, reflecting an increased electron density as the molecule gains an electron, leading to a redistribution of electron density. Conversely, in the cationic state, the contour lines are less dense and more widely spaced, particularly around oxygen (O1), indicating a decrease in electron density due to the loss of an electron. The nitrogen atoms (N1–N7) also show less dense lines, reflecting an overall reduction in electron density.

Charge distribution

The charge distributions over the atoms in the molecule suggest the formation of donor and acceptor pairs, indicative of charge transfer within the structure. Mulliken atomic charge calculations are pivotal in quantum chemistry, as charge distribution significantly impacts the electronic structure, dipole moment, polarizability, and various molecular properties52,53.

In this study, Mulliken atomic charges were computed using the DFT/B3LYP/6-311G(d) basis set. The computed Mulliken charge values are illustrated in Fig. 11. Frontier molecular orbital

The Highest Occupied Molecular Orbital (HOMO) and the Lowest Unoccupied Molecular Orbital (LUMO) are critical frontier molecular orbitals (FMOs) that play a pivotal role in determining the chemical stability of a molecule54. The HOMO primarily acts as an electron donor, while the LUMO functions as an electron acceptor55. The energy gap between the HOMO and LUMO characterizes the molecular chemical stability56.

In this study, we evaluated the frontier molecular orbitals of the PD-L1-In-1 molecule in both the gaseous phase and DMSO solvent using the 6-311G(d) basis set, as illustrated in Figs. 12 and 13. The plots indicate positive regions in green and negative regions in red. The energy gap between the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) reflects the molecule’s kinetic stability and chemical reactivity57. Furthermore, the effectiveness of PD-L1 inhibitors is influenced by the energies and spatial distributions of their HOMOs and LUMOs58.

For the neutral state, the energy gap remains relatively constant, with values of 0.20173 eV in the gas phase and 0.20296 eV in DMSO, indicating minimal solvent influence. In the anionic state, the alpha energy gap increases from 0.02259 to 0.05017 eV, and the beta energy gap rises from 0.02448 to 0.18409 eV upon transitioning from the gas phase to DMSO. This suggests enhanced stability and reduced reactivity in the solvent. Conversely, in the cationic state, the alpha energy gap slightly narrows from 0.16893 to 0.15324 eV, indicating a slight increase in reactivity, while the beta energy gap increases from 0.01017 to 0.03348 eV, reflecting greater stability in DMSO. Density of states

Since nearby orbitals in the boundary region may have quasi-degenerate energy levels, relying solely on the HOMO and LUMO to describe the frontier orbitals might be unrealistic59. Therefore, the Density of States (DOS) spectrum was generated using the Gauss Sum 3.0 program with a full width at half maximum (FWHM) of 0.3 eV. The DOS spectra for the PD-L1-In-1 molecule in different phases and states are presented in Figs. 14,15, and 16 spanning the energy range from − 15 to 15 eV.

The Density of States (DOS) plot aids in identifying whether interactions between orbitals are bonding or anti-bonding. High-intensity peaks in the DOS spectrum at specific energy levels indicate multiple available states for occupation, while zero intensity signifies the absence of available states. Negative intensity, on the other hand, is indicative of anti-bonding interactions45. The energy values obtained from the HOMO-LUMO analysis are consistent with the energy gaps depicted in the DOS spectrum, corresponding to the gaps between occupied and virtual orbitals.

Conclusions

This study presents the first comprehensive theoretical analysis of the PD-L1-In-1 molecule in various charged states, examined both in the gas phase and in DMSO solvent. Using a range of computational methods, we performed virtual structural analysis, simulated spectroscopic studies (IR, Raman, NMR, UV–Vis), and applied theoretical techniques such as PES scan, MEP mapping, Mulliken charge analysis, FMO, DOS, ELF, LOL, RDG, and Fukui function analysis. This multi-faceted computational approach provided a detailed characterization of the molecule’s properties, offering valuable insights for drug design applications.

Key computational findings from our analysis include subtle structural variations in PD-L1-In-1 driven by solvent effects, the identification of its key vibrational modes and electronic transitions, and a deeper understanding of its charge distribution and reactivity patterns. Additionally, thermodynamic parameters were calculated, and their temperature-dependent behavior was analyzed, highlighting the molecule’s stability and adaptability across temperatures relevant to biological systems. Notably, our simulations revealed strong ligand-protein interactions between PD-L1-In-1 and PD-L1, suggesting its potential as a promising therapeutic candidate for cancer immunotherapy targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway. Furthermore, our findings indicate that PD-L1-In-1 may serve as a valuable candidate for the development of orally bioavailable immune checkpoint blockade therapies in cancer treatment.

Our findings establish a robust theoretical foundation for understanding PD-L1-In-1 and lay the groundwork for future experimental investigations to validate its efficacy. These results underscore the importance of integrating computational approaches into cancer immunotherapy to facilitate the rational design of next-generation immunotherapeutic agents, thereby advancing precision oncology. While this study offers comprehensive theoretical insights into PD-L1-In-1, the lack of experimental validation remains a limitation. Future research should focus on experimental studies to corroborate our findings and further explore the therapeutic potential of this molecule.
