Abstract

Variations in host species significantly impact bacterial growth traits and antibiotic resistance, making it essential to consider host origin when evaluating the zoonotic potential of pathogens. This study focuses on multiple Brucella species, which share highly similar genetic material, to explore the relationship between host origin and zoonotic potential by integrating pan-genome-wide association studies (pan-GWAS) with machine learning (ML). Our results present an open pangenome of Brucella spp. derived from the whole-genome sequencing (WGS) data of 991 strains and identify 268 genes potentially associated with the zoonotic potential of Brucella. Integrating these genes into an ML model based on the support vector machine (SVM) algorithm allows us to predict the zoonotic potential of various Brucella strains with high accuracy. Our findings reveal that zoonotic potential varies by host origin: Brucella melitensis strains isolated from humans exhibit higher zoonotic potential than those isolated from cattle, goats, and sheep, while Brucella suis biovar 2 strains isolated from domestic pigs display higher zoonotic potential than those isolated from wild boars. Our study proposes a method for predicting and quantifying the zoonotic potential of closely related bacterial pathogens from different host origins, providing valuable insights for risk assessment and public health strategy.

Subject terms: Bacteria, Genome informatics

This study proposed a method for identifying the host origins for zoonotic pathogens by integrating pan-genome-wide association studies with machine learning algorithms. The application of the proposed method on the Brucella species revealed diverse zoonotic host origins and potentials.

Introduction

Bacteria exhibit an exceptionally broad distribution across natural environments, occupying nearly all ecosystems, including soil, aquatic systems, and the internal environments of plants and animals1. This extensive distribution underscores the diverse survival strategies of bacteria, enabling them to persist within a variety of hosts and to form intricate interactions with hosts2â€“4. Numerous pathogenic bacteria display distinct host specificity, thriving and reproducing solely within defined specific hosts, while some bacteria demonstrate the ability to infect a wide range of host species5,6. Furthermore, the phenomenon of host jump among bacteria has drawn increasing attention, as it enables bacteria to cross species boundaries, potentially leading to the emergence of novel infectious diseases7. The transition of pathogenic bacteria from one host to another, particularly when shifting from animal hosts to humans, poses a substantial threat to public health. Consequently, the surveillance and investigation of bacterial host adaptability and their potential for interspecies transmission are critical for the prevention of infectious disease outbreaks in future.

The COVID-19 pandemic has underscored the significant challenges that novel pathogens pose to global public health, highlighting that zoonotic pathogen spillovers into human populations can act as precursors to new pandemics. In this context, accurately predicting the zoonotic potential of pathogens has become critically important. The current approach to predicting the zoonotic potential of closely related bacterial pathogens primarily relies on phylogenetic analysis8. However, the phylogenetic analysis often lacks the resolution required for high-precision zoonotic risk assessments9. This limitation restricts our ability to reliably forecast the zoonotic potential of closely related bacterial pathogens, consequently hampering the design of effective intervention strategies. Thus, there is an urgent need to develop more refined and accurate assessment tools capable of rapidly identifying and mitigating the threats posed by closely related bacterial pathogens to human health.

Brucellosis is an infectious disease caused by members of the genus Brucella that can be transmitted from livestock and wildlife to humans. The genus Brucella consists of highly homogenous species of bacteria with minimal genetic variation, which comprises six classical Brucella species and six atypical Brucella species10,11. For a considerable period of time, only six classical species were recognized within the genus Brucella, including the main causative pathogens for human brucellosis: Brucella melitensis, B. abortus, and B. suis12,13. Except for the three Brucella species as above that are highly harmful to both humans and their natural hosts, B. canis and B. neotomae have been reported to cause human brucellosis, which are also members of the classical Brucella species14,15. It is noteworthy that B. neotomae was previously classified as a non-zoonotic species among classical Brucella species, but recent evidence suggests that B. neotomae may have zoonotic potential12,16. In recent years, several new atypical species of Brucella have been described, including B. ceti, B. pinnipedialis, B. microti, B. inopinata, B. vulpis, and B. papionis. Nevertheless, the zoonotic potential of these atypical Brucella species remains largely unknown. Therefore, it is necessary to evaluate the zoonotic potential of various Brucella species within the genus Brucella to provide valuable data for the control and prevention of brucellosis.

The number of publicly available whole-genome sequencing (WGS) data for the genus Brucella is continually increasing due to advancements in next-generation sequencing technology17, providing a prerequisite for assessing the zoonotic potential of Brucella based on WGS data. However, it is challenging to extract meaningful information from the large complex WGS data of Brucella using conventional statistical methods18. Machine learning (ML) has the ability to classify, cluster, and recognize patterns for large amounts of data19. It can be categorized into supervised, unsupervised, and semi-supervised learning depending on the need for human intervention during the training process20. Supervised ML uses labeled datasets to train algorithms such as Random Forest (RF), Decision Trees (DTC), Support Vector Machine (SVM), K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), and Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) to predict output based on labeled input data21â€“23. There have been numerous studies using ML as a tool to investigate the correlation between pathogenic genetic information and virulence or host preference24â€“27. This showcases the immense application potential of ML.

In this study, we performed pangenome-wide association studies (pan-GWAS) on 991 Brucella genomes to identify specific genes associated with zoonotic phenotypes. These genes were then employed as features in ML models. Subsequently, we conducted various ML models using different algorithms, and compared their performances in evaluating the zoonotic potential of Brucella strains in the test dataset. The SVM algorithm was selected for developing the prediction model due to its optimal performance among these ML algorithms. The strong generalization ability of the SVM prediction models facilitated its broad applicability across various strains within the genus Brucella. Furthermore, the SVM models provided a more refined evaluation of the zoonotic potential of Brucella strains from diverse host origins. This capability enables high-resolution predictions of zoonotic potential for strains within the same Brucella species. Model explanations have identified important genes for the evaluation of the SVM prediction models, and these genes may contribute to the zoonotic potential of strains from Brucella species.

Results

Genomic analysis indicates open pangenome of Brucella

To examine the genomic features of Brucella, we used 11 different Brucella species of publicly available genomes to characterize and define its pangenome. Core genes were defined as a set of genes shared with all 991 strains, accessory genes presented in at least two strain genomes or up to n-1 strain genomes (n: 991, total number of genomes), and the genes presenting only in one strain genome were defined as unique genes. The pangenome was constructed with the dataset consisting of 582 core genes, 4,121 accessory genes, and 2,462 unique genes from 991 Brucella spp. strains. Functional annotation of genes in the pangenome was conducted utilizing the Clusters of Orthologous Genes database (COGs), with the results revealing a distinctive distribution of functional categories across the three different pangenome subsets (Fig.Â 1A). Unique genes in the pangenome exhibited a higher ratio of genes annotated to the L category (Replication, recombination, and repair) than core and accessory genes (13.3% vs. 2.3% and 5.6%), with the majority of unique genes that belonged to the L category originating from B. inopinata (73.6%) (Supplementary Fig.Â 1). Furthermore, we found that the partial L category genes of B. inopinata were related to DNA modification, such as DNA adenine methylation, ISP type restriction/modification enzyme and DNA (cytosine-5-)-methyltransferase, indicating that B. inopinata may possess substantial epigenetic plasticity to aid in niche shift.

Pangenome can be classified into two different types based on openness: open pangenome and closed pangenome. A highly open pangenome indicates that species inhabiting diverse environments possess the ability to exchange genetic material through various mechanisms, while species occupying the strained ecological niches find it challenging to acquire exogenous genes, resulting in a closed pangenome28,29. For Brucella, we calculated that the relationship between pangenome size (P) and the number of genomes (N) was Pâ€‰=â€‰1260.58*991^0.25, Î³ equals 0.25, and the pangenome size increased with the addition of new genomes (Fig.Â 1B), indicating that Brucella had an open pangenome and the pangenome repertoire of these 11 Brucella species was unable to be completely characterized using only the 991 genomes. Subsequently, we counted the number of accessory and unique genes within each strain of Brucella, and the result revealed that the majority of Brucella strains had a low number of unique genes, ranging from 0 to 100. Only five strains, including both two B. inopinata strains, had more than 100 unique genes. The number of accessory genes in most Brucella strains was around 2300. It is worth noting that both B. neotomae strains possessed a relatively low number of accessory genes, and the B. neotomae NCTC10070 strain had 1891 accessory genes, which is significantly lower than in other Brucella strains (Fig.Â 1C). This lower count is probably ascribed to the comparatively small size of the genome of this particular strain, which has a size of 2.9 Mbp.

Quantifying the zoonotic potential of Brucella spp. via phylogenetic analysis presents substantial complexities

For analyzing the genetic structure among the 991 Brucella strains, we used the core gene alignment result to construct an ML tree of all Brucella strains. In particular, we focused on whether phylogenetic analysis could separate classical zoonotic Brucella species from B. ovis, the only non-zoonotic species identified, and predict the zoonotic potential of other Brucella species accordingly. While Brucella species exhibited a close genetic relationship, they display notable variations in virulence, host preferences, and zoonotic capacity17. B. melitensis, B. abortus, and B. suis biovar 1 and 3 are the primary causative agents of brucellosis in both domesticated animals and humans30,31. Nevertheless, B. ovis infects animals exclusively and is the only known non-zoonotic Brucella species32â€“35. As shown in Fig.Â 2, B. abortus and B. melitensis each formed a highly differentiated clade, while the other clade consisted of a mixture of various Brucella species. The two atypical Brucella species, B. inopinata and B. vulpis, were distant from classical Brucella strains, and the phylogenetic relationship between the two species was also found to be quite distant. Zoonotic Brucella strains were clearly divided into three distinct groups, while the non-zoonotic species B. ovis were located in a separate clade. However, with the exception of these three zoonotic groups and the isolated clade of non-zoonotic species, it was difficult to determine the zoonotic potential of other species such as B. ceti and B. neotomae, based solely on phylogenetic analysis (Fig.Â 2).

Unsupervised ML algorithms are incapable of distinguishing between zoonotic and non-zoonotic Brucella strains

To acquire suitable input data for ML, we classified B. ovis, B. suis biovar 5, B. abortus 104â€‰M strain, and B. suis S2 strains as non-zoonotic Brucella strains. Conversely, the zoonotic Brucella dataset encompassed all strains of B. melitensis, B. abortus, and part of the B. suis strains (B. suis biovar 1 and B. suis biovar 3), while excluding the aforementioned non-zoonotic strains. In total, 883 Brucella strains were manually annotated with zoonotic labels, among which 861 were classified as zoonotic pathogens and 22 as non-zoonotic pathogens. 268 genes with statistically significant associations to zoonotic or non-zoonotic phenotypes (P.adjust<0.05, Benjamini-Hochberg method) were identified as features through pan-GWAS analysis (Supplementary DataÂ 1).

We initially employed the PCA algorithm to analyze data from all 991 strains, and PCA plots revealed the presence of non-zoonotic strains that were intermingled with zoonotic strains (Fig.Â 3A). To ascertain the optimal parameter for the K-Means model, the Elbow method and silhouette coefficient were performed to determine the optimal value for the number of clusters in the unsupervised ML algorithm K-Means, and the results revealed that the optimal number of clusters was three (Supplementary Fig.Â 2). The K-Means algorithm classified all strains into three clusters, although most non-zoonotic strains were split into cluster 3, while clusters 1 and 2 contained a mixture of both zoonotic and non-zoonotic strains (Fig.Â 3B). The performance of the DBSCAN clustering algorithm was inadequate in distinguishing between zoonotic strains and non-zoonotic strains (Supplementary Fig.Â 3). These findings implied that unsupervised ML algorithms encountered challenges in discerning the distinction between zoonotic and non-zoonotic strains with precision.

The supervised ML models based on SVM algorithm can accurately distinguish between zoonotic and non-zoonotic strains

Due to the limited performance of unsupervised ML algorithms in distinguishing between zoonotic and non-zoonotic strains, five different supervised ML algorithms (RF, DTC, SVM, KNN, and MLP) were used to develop models for predicting the zoonotic potential of Brucella strains. Briefly, we divided 883 Brucella strains manually annotated with zoonotic labels into training and test datasets using random stratified sampling to ensure that both datasets maintained a proportional representation of zoonotic and non-zoonotic strains. The training dataset was then utilized to train each ML model and assessed the effectiveness of the models by stratified 3-fold cross validation. This entire process was repeated 100 times, and the results were averaged to obtain a more factual quality score for the models. The results showed that the models constructed with supervised ML algorithms achieved exceptional performance, with accuracy, recall, F1, and AUPRC mean scores all over 0.90, indicating that the supervised ML models were able to distinguish between zoonotic strains and non-zoonotic strains. Further analysis showed that the SVM ML model obtained the highest mean scores for all metrics except for the AUC and AUPRC score. Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC) score is a reliable metric for evaluating the quality of ML model in an imbalanced dataset. Among the five different ML models, the SVM model obtained the highest mean MCC score (0.78), and its MCC score distribution was more concentrated than that of the other ML models. These results suggested that SVM was the most appropriate algorithm for supervised ML model construction for Brucella zoonotic and non-zoonotic strain classification (Fig.Â 4A-F).

The SVM prediction models accurately quantify zoonotic capacity of Brucella strains in the test dataset

As the most suitable ML algorithm, we retrained the models using the hyperparameters of SVM models with the top 10% MCC score previously. To predict the zoonotic potential of Brucella strain, the output results of these retrained SVM models were averaged to obtain decision value for quantifying the zoonotic potential of the strain.

The SVM prediction models were designed to generate decision values representing the zoonotic potential of Brucella strains on a continuous scale, with zero established as the threshold. This predictive framework allowed for the classification of zoonotic potential, where positive values indicated zoonotic potential and negative values denoted non-zoonotic potential. Moreover, the magnitude of the decision value reflected the strength of the corresponding classification, providing a quantitative measure of zoonotic potential. As shown in Fig.Â 5A, most of the Brucella strains from the zoonotic subset of the test dataset obtained decision values above +0.3, while certain B. suis strains demonstrated substantially lower decision values than others within the zoonotic subset. One strain of B. suis bv. 1 from the zoonotic test subset was erroneously assigned a decision value of -0.37 (below the threshold of zero), resulting in its misclassification as a non-zoonotic pathogen. (Fig.Â 5A).

In addition, the SVM prediction models accurately predicted the zoonotic potential of the non-zoonotic subset in the test dataset. The decision values for all non-zoonotic Brucella strains were below the threshold of zero. Notably, the SVM predictive models accurately predicted that the B. ovis had minimal zoonotic potential. B. ovis are generally recognized as non-zoonotic bacteria, exhibited decision values lower than -0.70, which further supported their classification as non-zoonotic bacteria. Two strains of B. suis bv. 5 were assigned decision values of -0.26 and -0.25, respectively, which were higher than those of B. ovis strains, indicating that their non-zoonotic potential was comparatively weaker than that of B. ovis strains (Fig.Â 5B).

The SVM prediction models exhibit strong generalization ability

Lipopolysaccharide (LPS) serves as a crucial virulence factor in Brucella, playing a pivotal role in the intracellular survival of Brucella within host cells. Brucella spp. are classified into smooth or rough phenotypes depending on whether O-polysaccharide (O-PS) is present on the LPS, and the rough phenotype Brucella strains frequently exhibit significantly attenuated virulence compared to the smooth phenotype Brucella strains, such as B. melitensis and B. abortus36. The natural rough phenotype of Brucella includes B. ovis and B. canis37. Although B. canis is currently the only species within Brucella spp. sharing the same LPS as B. ovis, there have been several reports of human infected by B. canis in recent years, indicating its zoonotic potential15,38,39.

While the SVM prediction models demonstrated excellent predictive performance in the test dataset, it is essential to further evaluate their generalization ability. B. canis, which is the only zoonotic species sharing the absence of O-PS with the non-zoonotic B. ovis species in Brucella spp., provided appropriate data for assessing the generalization ability of SVM prediction models. Therefore, we utilized the SVM prediction models to predict the zoonotic potential of 26 strains of B. canis. The mean decision value for B. canis was +0.49. Among the strains of B. canis, the highest positive decision value was +0.70. Notably, B. canis was not included in the training dataset of the SVM prediction models. The prediction results suggest that B. canis exhibits an intermediate zoonotic potential, which is in correspondence with the current understanding of this pathogen. The result indicated that the SVM prediction models demonstrated a strong generalization ability and can be utilized to estimate the zoonotic potential of other species strains within the genus Brucella (Fig.Â 6).

Multiple various Brucella species display a wide range of zoonotic potential

The high similarity (~97%) at the genomic level within the genus Brucella allows for the utilization of our pan-GWAS-based SVM prediction models among different species of Brucella strains and various host-derived isolates of the same species. The marine mammal-derived Brucellae, including B. ceti and B. pinnipedialis, have been demonstrated to have potential zoonotic capacity40. Here, nine strains of B. ceti were evaluated for zoonotic potential using the SVM prediction models with an average decision value of +0.36. The highest decision value of +0.57 was obtained from a B. ceti strain isolated from a common bottlenose dolphin (Tursiops truncatus), while the lowest decision value of +0.10 was obtained from a B. ceti strain isolated from common dolphin (Delphinus delphis). Similarly, for B. pinnipedialis, the strains of this species exhibited an average decision value of +0.32. The highest decision value of +0.53 was observed in a B. pinnipedialis strain isolated from hooded seal (Cystophora cristata), while the lowest decision value of +0.11 was observed in a B. pinnipedialis strain isolated from harbor seal (Phoca vitulina). These findings highlight the necessity of surveilling zoonotic potential of marine mammal Brucellae. (Fig.Â 6).

B. neotomae, first isolated from desert woodrats (Neotoma lepida) in the United States in 1957, was previously considered a non-zoonotic bacterium. However, two cases of B. neotomae infected human were reported in 201714, indicating that humans may be part of its host range. The SVM prediction models predicted an average decision value of +0.17 for five B. neotomae strains. Among these strains, the highest decision value was at +0.26, and the lowest decision value was at +0.03 (Fig.Â 6).

B. microti is the only known species capable of surviving in soil within the Brucella spp.41. In this study, two strains of B. microti were predicted to have zoonotic potential, one strain was isolated from a vole (Microtus arvalis), while the other was found in a pool frog (Pelophylax lessonae). These strains had an average decision value of +0.37. Notably, the strain of B. microti derived from the vole exhibited a higher decision value of +0.54 compared to the strain isolated from the pool frog, which obtained a decision value of +0.21 (Fig.Â 6).

Apart from B. ceti, B. pinnipedialis, and B. microti, the atypical Brucella species include B. vulpis and B. inopinata10. Two strains of B. vulpis were assessed for their zoonotic potential by the SVM prediction models. The two highly similar strains of B. vulpis were isolated from red foxes (Vulpes vulpes) in 200842. As a result of their similarity, the decision values for both strains were identical, with a value of +0.15. This lack of variation in decision value made it impossible to construct a kernel density plot for B. vulpis.

A strain of B. inopinata BO1 was isolated from the breast implant wound fluid of a 71-year-old female patient in 200843, and its zoonotic potential was predicted to be +0.076 based on the SVM prediction models. Despite the B. inopinata 141012304 strain being isolated from a bluespotted ribbontail ray (Taeniura lymma), its predicted zoonotic potential was slightly higher at +0.080 compared to B. inopinata BO1 (â€‰+â€‰0.076) isolated from the human patient (Fig.Â 6).

B. suis is divided into 5 various biovars, with B. suis bv 1, 3, and 5 being used to construct the SVM prediction models. The zoonotic potential of B. suis bv. 2 was subsequently predicted using the SVM prediction models, with an average decision value of +0.26. Additionally, B. suis bv. 4 strains obtained an average decision value of +0.42. Two distinct strains of B. suis bv. 4 strains isolated from a reindeer (Rangifer tarandus) and a wild boar were assessed for their zoonotic potential, yielding decision values of +0.29 and +0.54, respectively (Fig.Â 6).

The zoonotic potential of Brucella strain is affected by both species and isolated host

Statistical analysis further corroborated that the SVM prediction models provided species-specific and host-specific zoonotic potential for Brucella strains. The results revealed that the zoonotic potential of B. abortus and B. melitensis is significantly higher than that of most other Brucella species. Notably, the SVM prediction models provided species-specific decision values for B. melitensis strains in the test dataset based solely on the gene features of the training samples, even in the absence of the input species information for all the Brucella strains, which is the most virulent Brucella species for humans32. Conversely, the zoonotic potential of B. ovis was significantly lower compared to B. abortus, B. melitensis, B. suis, B. canis, B. neotomae, and marine mammal Brucella spp. (Fig.Â 7A, B).

Additionally, B. melitensis strains isolated from Homo sapiens exhibited significantly higher zoonotic potential than those isolated from Bos taurus, Capra hircus, and Ovis aries (Fig.Â 7C). Among the B. suis bv. 2 strains, the highest decision value was +0.65, isolated from a swine (Sus scrofa domesticus), while the lowest decision value was -0.04, isolated from a wild boar (Sus scrofa) (Fig.Â 6). The considerable difference between the highest decision value and the lowest decision value indicated a need to compare the zoonotic potential of B. suis bv. 2 strains derived from different hosts. We compared the decision values of Brucella strains isolated from different hosts and found that those isolated from swine exhibited significantly higher zoonotic potential than those isolated from wild boars, despite both belonging to the Sus scrofa species (Fig.Â 7D).

Model explanations reveal the genes potentially used to estimate the zoonotic potential of Brucella strains

Model explanations were conducted using the SHAP method on a set of 268 genes, which served as the features for constructing the SVM prediction models. The SHAP method offers two distinct perspectives for explaining the impact of features on ML model outputs: a global explanation and a local explanation. As shown in Fig.Â 8A, all 268 features were ranked according to the mean absolute SHAP values in the global model explanation, identifying the ten most influential features on the predicted results (Fig.Â 8A). Furthermore, the SHAP dependence plot showed how variations in feature values (gene present or gene absent) affected the predictions for Brucella strains. The feature group_2874 was the most significant contributor to the output of SVM prediction models, resulting in predictions towards zoonotic direction when absent and towards non-zoonotic direction when present, with a greater distance of the shift than that observed in the absence of group_2874. It has been preliminarily identified as an uncharacterized protein, and currently, there is no definitive functional characterization available. This indicated that group_2874 was more likely to be associated with a diminished zoonotic potential of Brucella strains (Fig.Â 8B).

The predicted results for each Brucella strain in the test dataset were explained in Fig.Â 8C. The upper x-axis represented each sample, while the y-axis represented the contributions of features to the predicted results. As the blue part of the sample increased, the likelihood of the Brucella strain being pathogenic to humans was decreased (Fig.Â 8C). In addition, the local explanation of two Brucella strains, including the typical zoonotic Brucella strain (B. melitensis 16â€‰M strain, GCA_000740415.1) and non-zoonotic Brucella strain (B. ovis ATCC 25840 strain, GCA_000016845.1), provided a personalized analysis of the predicted results. For instance, the absence of group_2974 resulted in an increase in the predicted zoonotic potential of the B. melitensis 16â€‰M strain. In contrast, the presence of group_2974 in the B. ovis ATCC 25840 strain decreased its predicted zoonotic potential. The local explanation clearly demonstrated the roles that various features play in the zoonotic potential prediction process for individual Brucella strains (Fig.Â 8D, E).

Discussion

The construction of the pangenome of Brucella spp. provided a finer resolution insight into the genetic diversity of this zoonotic intracellular pathogen. The pangenome of Brucella spp. consisted of 582 core genes, 4121 accessory genes, and 2462 unique genes. The proportion of genes annotated to the L category among the unique genes was higher than that of core and accessory genes (Fig.Â 1A). This was likely due to individual Brucella strains facing distinct selective pressures, necessitating the retention of genes that are beneficial for their genetic information transfer, thereby ensuring their survival under such conditions. This result reflected the diversity of ecological niches inhabited by Brucella strains. Furthermore, our study demonstrated that these 11 Brucella species possessed an open pangenome (Fig.Â 1B), which was consistent with the ongoing expansion of the genus Brucella, indicating once again that pathogens belonging to the Brucella spp. inhabited the varied ecological niches44. The coexistence of specialist Brucella strains (such as B. melitensis and B. abortus) infecting livestock or wildlife and generalist Brucella strains (such as B. microti) living extensively in the environment underpinned the movement of diverse Brucella strains among the environment, animals, and humans.

Given the largely unknown zoonotic potential of novel species of Brucella and the emergence of human brucellosis caused by Brucella strains previously thought to lack zoonotic capability, such as B. neotomae considered a non-zoonotic species over the past years45, the understanding of the zoonotic potential of B. neotomae has been changed by the isolation of B. neotomae from the cerebrospinal fluid of two patients diagnosed with human brucellosis in Costa Rica46. This finding necessitates a reconsideration of its host range and implications for public health. It was necessary to develop a prediction model to further characterize the zoonotic potential of Brucella strains. To establish the most effective prediction model, we conducted a comparative analysis of three distinct methods, encompassing phylogenetic analysis, unsupervised ML model, and supervised ML model. Our findings revealed that both phylogenetic analysis and unsupervised ML model were ineffective in distinguishing between artificially categorized zoonotic and non-zoonotic Brucella strains. Conversely, supervised ML models successfully discriminated between zoonotic and non-zoonotic Brucella strains in the test dataset. The SVM-based model exhibited superior discriminative performance on the validation dataset compared to other supervised ML models (Fig.Â 4). Consequently, we developed SVM prediction models to obtain quantitative indicator decision values, which were then utilized for assessing the zoonotic potential of Brucella strains.

The generalization ability of ML model is a crucial metric for evaluating its performance47. This ability determines whether the developed model can maintain suitable performance for dealing with unseen data. In this study, we assessed the generalization ability of the SVM prediction models using B. canis strains. The SVM prediction models yielded an average decision value of +0.49 for B. canis strains (Fig.Â 6), ranking closely behind zoonotic Brucella species B. melitensis (+1.11) and B. abortus (+0.91) in the test dataset (Fig.Â 5A). This precise prediction of the zoonotic potential of unseen data from B. canis strains indicated the strong generalization ability of the SVM prediction models. Furthermore, the SVM prediction models demonstrated a surprising efficacy in evaluating the zoonotic potential characteristics of Brucella species at both interspecific (among different species) and intraspecific (among strains of the same species) levels. Among the various Brucella species, B. melitensis is recognized as the most pathogenic, thereby establishing itself as a critical focus for zoonotic potential assessments32,48. Notably, the SVM prediction models exhibited the capability to accurately differentiate B. melitensis from other zoonotic Brucella species, even in the absence of any species-related input data (Fig.Â 7A, B).

Holzapfel et al. addressed the virulence of Brucella species isolated from different hosts, revealing that the B. melitensis strains isolated from various hosts did not exhibit any host-specific characteristics. This research focused on B. melitensis strains identified as clones, isolating these pathogens from multiple hosts during the same outbreak over a span of one year. The relatively short isolation period of merely one year may present challenges for B. melitensis in achieving effective host adaptation. This limitation may have influenced the observed relationship between host factors and the characteristics of Brucella strains49. Brucella have been isolated from several bodily fluids such as urine, semen, breast milk, and blood of infected patients50â€“52. The transmission routes of Brucella, such as maternal transmission, sexual contact, and blood transfusion, have been described53â€“55. Our findings further suggest that B. melitensis strains isolated from humans exhibit a higher predictive zoonotic potential. Consequently, this highlights the critical need for further investigations into the impact of diverse hosts on the virulence and zoonotic potential of strains within the same Brucella species. In addition to accurately assessing the zoonotic potential of different Brucella species, the SVM prediction models have also effectively distinguished B. melitensis isolates from human and differentiated between B. suis bv. 2 strains isolated from swine and those isolated from wild boars, despite the lack of host information of isolates (Fig.Â 7C, D). These results provide valuable insights into the impact of the host on the characteristics of Brucella strains.

The circulation at the wildlife-livestock interface is a prominent characteristic for B. melitensis, B. abortus, and B. suis56. As previously described, the distribution of B. suis bv. 2 is progressively expanding from wild boars and hares to domestic pigs; this transition is particularly concerning because domestic pigs have a significantly closer association with human communities compared to wild boars, turning this pathogen, which is rarely infective to humans, into a significant public health concern57â€“59. Our SVM prediction models indicated that the zoonotic potential associated with B. suis bv. 2 from domestic pigs is greater than that from wild boars. Therefore, it is essential to implement continuous monitoring of B. suis bv. 2 within animal husbandry to find the potential risk of host jumps targeting humans in the future. These findings indicate that the predictions made by the SVM prediction models are informative.

The zoonotic potential of Brucella strains varies among different species. Human brucellosis is commonly caused by three Brucella species transmitted from animals to humans: B. melitensis, B. abortus, and B. suis bv. 1 and 3. However, the zoonotic potential of novel members of the genus Brucella, such as B. ceti, B. pinnipedialis, B. inopinata, B. microti, B. vulpis, and B. suis bv. 2 and 4 remains largely unknown, despite the fact that some strains of certain Brucella species have been isolated from patients with brucellosis. The predicted zoonotic potential indicated the necessity for ongoing monitoring among these Brucella species. Due to the limited number of available genomes for these Brucella species, the prediction results generated by the SVM prediction models may not fully characterize the true zoonotic potential of these species. In the future, the performance of the SVM prediction models could be enhanced by increasing the WGS data for Brucella.

As an effective ML model explanation tool, the SHAP method identified 10 genes that are most important to evaluate the zoonotic potential of the Brucella strain in SVM prediction models. The gene group_1020 encodes a protein belonging to the pentapeptide MXKDX repeat protein family, and this gene was annotated as â€œReduced virulenceâ€ (http://www.phi-base.org), which suggests that the gene may enhance the virulence of pathogen. The gene group_1603 was predicted encodes ATPase component belong to ABC-type transport system and was also annotated as â€œReduced virulenceâ€, ABC transporter plays an important role in bacterial pathogenesis and virulence60. In addition to the top 10 genes, the gene group_4450 encodes urea transporter, which has been observed to be up-regulated during the acid stress response for B. melitensis 16â€‰M61. Given that the consumption of contaminated food is a common transmission route for Brucella, this observation suggests that group_4450 may play an important role in aiding Brucella strains in resisting the acidic environments within the host stomach, thereby facilitating the establishment of infection.

The dataset used for training the SVM prediction models is imbalanced, with the number of zoonotic Brucella strains exceeding that of non-zoonotic Brucella strains. This discrepancy may result in an overestimation of the predicted zoonotic potential for Brucella strains62. To mitigate the sample imbalance, SVMSMOTE was performed to alleviate the skewed class distribution of the training dataset. Furthermore, the MCC score was adopted to assess the performance of ML models, thereby improving the reliability of the results. Although there remains a risk of overestimating zoonotic potential for SVM prediction models, it is more important from a public health perspective to avoid misclassifying zoonotic Brucella strains as non-zoonotic; such errors may result in undetected brucellosis outbreaks and pose a serious threat to public health.

Currently, the prediction of zoonotic potential based on ML utilizing genomic data is typically conducted within the same species for bacteria63,64. Our study achieves cross-species zoonotic potential predictions using ML on multiple highly conserved Brucella species, demonstrating the applicability of ML to predict zoonotic potential in bacterial pathogens with greater genetic variability. However, an anticipated challenge is that as genetic variability increases, it becomes increasingly difficult to capture sufficient information from the genomes. Relying solely on the presence or absence of genes may become inadequate for predicting zoonotic potential in bacterial pathogens with greater genetic variability. It is therefore necessary to provide ML with more information at the nucleotide-level, which can capture additional genetic variations such as Single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) and Insertions and Deletions (Indels). SNPs have been shown to grant bacteria the ability to infect new host species or acquire antibiotic resistance65,66, highlighting the risk that gene-level analyses may miss important trait-associated information. Thus, integrating both gene-level and nucleotide-level features in future studies may improve the accuracy of zoonotic potential prediction for bacterial pathogens with high genetic variability.

In conclusion, we successfully combined pan-GWAS with ML to predict the zoonotic potential of closely related bacteria within the genus Brucella. Among the 11 Brucella species, most strains were predicted to be potential zoonotic pathogens. The SVM prediction models delivered species-specific and host-specific predictions of zoonotic potential for these closely related bacteria isolated from various hosts. This technical framework offers a viable analytical method for predicting the zoonotic potential of the closely related bacterial pathogens at the host resolution.

Methods

Preparation of the binarized dataset for ML

A total of 1042 assembled genomes from 11 different Brucella species were downloaded from NCBI Assembly database (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/assembly). The quality of these genomes was checked by QUAST, and a total of 991 Brucella genomes were considered to be of acceptable quality67. The visualization of species and host source information pertaining to these 991 Brucella strains was generated using the CNSknowall platform (https://cnsknowall.com), a comprehensive web service for data analysis and visualization (Supplementary TableÂ 1). The annotation of 991 quality-passed genomes was performed using Prokka, a bioinformatics tool for annotating prokaryotic genome (https://github.com/tseemann/prokka)68. The pan-genome analysis was performed by Roary (https://sanger-pathogens.github.io/Roary)69. The COG annotation was conducted using the online web service eggNOG-mapper v2 (http://eggnog-mapper.embl.de)70. A Python script was used to calculate Heapâ€™s law to characterize the openness of the Brucella pangenome (Supplementary DataÂ 2). To classify Brucella strains according to their zoonotic potential, B. abortus, B. melitensis, and part of B. suis (biovar 1 and biovar 3) strains were assigned to the zoonotic group71. B. ovis and B. suis biovar 5 strains were assigned to the non-zoonotic group17,72. Additionally, the vaccine strains B. abortus 104â€‰M and B. suis S2 were also assigned to the non-zoonotic group73,74. Subsequent to annotating the zoonotic potential information for the Brucella strains, the pan-GWAS tool Scoary was used to identify statistically significant associated genes with either zoonotic or non-zoonotic groups (P.adjust<0.05, Benjamini-Hochberg method) and these genes were used to build the input binarized dataset (Supplementary DataÂ 1)75.

Phylogenetic analysis and unsupervised ML for Brucella spp. strains

The maximum-likelihood tree based on the core genes was constructed using raxmlGUIv2.0 (https://antonellilab.github.io/raxmlGUI)76, the model was set to GTRâ€‰+â€‰G and the reliability of the internal branch was evaluated using 200 bootstrap replicates. The resulting phylogenetic tree was modified using tvBOT (https://www.chiplot.online/tvbot.html)77. The PCA on the input binarized dataset was performed using the Scikit-learn Python package (https://scikit-learn.org/stable/index.html). The K-Means clustering and DBSCAN clustering models were built on the PCA transformed input binarized dataset using the Scikit-learn and SciPy Python package (https://scipy.org). All figures were plotted using the Matplotlib Python packages (https://matplotlib.org).

Supervised ML for Brucella spp. strains

The binarized dataset was used to develop a supervised ML model aimed at generating predictions for the zoonotic potential of Brucella strains. Five different supervised ML algorithms (RF, DTC, SVM, KNN, and MLP) were used to build the prediction models. Due to the limited number of non-zoonotic Brucella strain samples, a stratified sampling approach was employed to allocate training and test datasets in a 50-50% split for the input binarized dataset. To tackle the class imbalance problem, the imbalanced-learn Python package was utilized to perform SVM Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SVMSMOTE) for oversampling on the training dataset, ensuring an adequate representation of non-zoonotic Brucella strain samples for training the ML model. Hyperparameter optimization for the ML model was carried out using a grid search with 3-fold cross-validation, with the evaluation score set as the MCC score. The ML model was trained on the training dataset using the optimal hyperparameters identified from the grid search: H1(class_weightâ€‰=â€‰â€˜balancedâ€™, Câ€‰=â€‰0.005, degreeÂ =Â 0, kernelâ€‰=â€‰â€˜rbfâ€™), H2(class_weightâ€‰=â€‰â€˜balancedâ€™, Câ€‰=â€‰0.005, degreeÂ =Â 10, kernelâ€‰=â€‰â€˜polyâ€™), and H3(class_weightâ€‰=â€‰â€˜balancedâ€™, Câ€‰=â€‰0.005, degreeÂ =Â 1, kernelâ€‰=â€‰â€˜polyâ€™). Its performance was then evaluated on the test dataset. To mitigate potential random sampling errors, the sample split, model establishment, and validation process were repeated 100 times for different algorithms. The performance of the ML models established with different algorithms was evaluated based on various metrics, including Accuracy score, Recall score, F1 score, AUC score, AUPRC score, and MCC score, calculated as the average of all 100 repetitions. The sample split and ML model establishment were executed using the Scikit-learn Python package.

The establishment of SVM prediction models and quantification of the zoonotic potential of Brucella strains

After identifying SVM as the algorithm for establishing the zoonotic potential prediction model, the hyperparameters of the models with MCC scores in the top 10% were extracted from the results of the grid search. Three distinct SVM prediction models were subsequently developed using these optimal hyperparameters: H1, H2, and H3. For each Brucella strain sample, the decision value is calculated as the average of the raw decision_function outputs from these three SVM models. The SciPy Python package was used to conduct Gaussian kernel density estimation of predicted probabilities for the strains in the test dataset and various species of the genus Brucella. The kernel density plot was generated using the Matplotlib Python package.

SVM prediction models explanation and annotation of gene pathogenicity

The oversampled training dataset was clustered into 100 weighted samples via K-Means clustering and employed as background samples for the SHAP method. Each SVM prediction model used the kernel SHAP method to explain decision values, followed by averaging SHAP values across all models to derive the final output. Feature importance was assessed by ranking the averaged SHAP values, and both global and local explanations were constructed using the shap Python package (https://shap.readthedocs.io/en/latest). The pathogenicity of genes was annotated using PHIB-BLAST on the Pathogen-Host Interactions database (PHI-base, http://www.phi-base.org).

Statistics and Reproducibility

Statistical significance was determined by independent samples t-test for the decision values of B. suis biovar 2 strains isolated from swine or wild boar, which conformed to normal distribution. Since the decision values of the eight Brucella species did not all conform to normal distribution and some Brucella species have small sample sizes, permutation test was performed to determine interspecies statistical significance, with p values were corrected by the Benjamini-Hochberg method. The decision values for B. melitensis strains isolated from different hosts did not conform to normal distribution, and the Kruskal-Wallis test was used to determine statistical significance with p values were corrected by the Bonferroni method. All p values described in the study are two-sided. The independent samples t-test, Kruskal-Wallis test, and Bonferroni method were performed using BONC DSS Statistics 25 software. The permutation test and Benjamini-Hochberg method were performed using SciPy Python package.
