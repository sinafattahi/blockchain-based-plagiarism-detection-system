Abstract

Fusarium graminearum colonizes the maize ear, causing Gibberella ear rot (GER) and producing harmful mycotoxins, including deoxynivalenol (DON) and zearalenone (ZEA). The disease can be managed in part by breeding and planting resistant maize cultivars. Resistance to GER is a quantitative and complex trait. Evaluation of diverse germplasm to identify regions and candidate genes associated with resistance may be useful for crop improvement efforts. Screening for GER is time‐consuming and costly. Thus, identifying other traits that may serve as a proxy for GER resistance may accelerate resistance breeding efforts. We hypothesized that grain phenylpropanoid content and kernel composition are genetically and mechanistically related to GER resistance. We screened a diverse set of maize inbred lines for disease severity, DON, ZEA, ferulic acid, p‐coumaric acid, pericarp thickness, and several kernel composition traits. Using a genome‐wide association study, we identified markers associated with each phenotype and genomic regions that harbor alleles for both disease and metabolite‐related phenotypes. Pathways significantly associated with GER‐related traits were related to detoxification, cell wall integrity, and lignin biosynthesis. End‐season ferulic acid and p‐coumaric acid concentrations are not strong proxies for GER resistance, but secondary metabolites are important components of the maize–Fusarium graminearum pathosystem. Furthermore, lignin‐deficient brown midrib mutants exhibited increased susceptibility to GER, underscoring the importance of lignin composition in limiting fungal colonization. The study highlights the multifaceted nature of GER resistance, involving both biochemical and structural defenses. These findings provide valuable targets for breeding programs aiming to enhance GER resistance and reduce mycotoxin contamination in maize.

Core Ideas

  • Resistance mechanisms for Gibberella ear rot (GER) likely involve detoxification and cell wall fortification.

  • Lignin concentration and composition are important for defense against Fusarium graminearum colonization in maize.

  • End‐season ferulic and p‐coumaric acid are not reliable proxies for GER resistance.

Plain Language Summary

Gibberella ear rot (GER) is a global threat to human health as it contaminates maize with harmful toxins. To protect the food supply against mycotoxin contamination, resistant maize cultivars are needed. Identifying candidate genes and potential mechanisms of GER resistance can assist the breeding process. We screened an inbred maize panel for disease severity, toxin accumulation, and several kernel characteristics. We identified candidate genes and pathways involved with resistance. We examined metabolites in the phenylpropanoid pathway, and there appears to be a role for these metabolites in resistance. Lignin‐deficient mutants were more susceptible to GER, stressing that maize grown for silage should be monitored for mycotoxin accumulation. The candidate genes and pathways identified in this study can be targets of future studies related to GER resistance.

1. INTRODUCTION

The health of people and animals worldwide is threatened by grain rots and mycotoxin‐contaminated grain. Maize is an important global staple, but 19.5%–41.4% of yields are lost due to plant pathogens and pests (Savary et al., 2019). Fusarium graminearum, a major pathogen of maize, is the fourth most threatening plant‐pathogenic fungus in the world (Dean et al., 2012). On maize, F. graminearum causes Gibberella ear rot (GER), Gibberella stalk rot, Fusarium root rot, and seedling rots and blights (Munkvold & White, 2016). Fusarium graminearum produces multiple mycotoxins, which are a threat to human and animal health. Yield losses due to GER have been reported (Mueller et al., 2020, 2016). However, post‐harvest deductions and losses due to mycotoxins, the most important risk associated with GER, are challenging to estimate. Deoxynivalenol (DON) and zearalenone (ZEA) are common mycotoxins produced by F. graminearum. DON is commonly referred to as vomitoxin, as it causes vomiting, food refusal, gastroenteritis, and impaired reproduction (Desjardins, 2006; Pestka, 2010; Ueno, 1985; Vesonder et al., 1973). ZEA has estrogenic effects, which can cause hormone imbalances, reproductive toxicity, and potentially cancer (Desjardins, 2006; Kuiper‐Goodman et al., 1987; Rogowska et al., 2019). In North America, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has employed advisory levels for DON contamination in grain products depending on the intended end product (Center for Veterinary Medicine, 2010). There are no guidelines for ZEA contamination in the United States. The European Union has detailed guidelines for DON and ZEA in commodities destined for human and animal consumption (European Commission, 2006, 2023, 2024). Despite these regulations, mycotoxin exposure in North America and Europe is still prevalent (Bulder et al., 2011; Mahato et al., 2021; Munkvold et al., 2019; Patriarca & Fernández Pinto, 2017). Furthermore, outside of North America and Europe, many countries lack the infrastructure to effectively mitigate mycotoxin risk in foodstuffs, leaving many communities at risk to mycotoxin exposure (Echodu et al., 2019; Foerster et al., 2024; Mishra et al., 2020).

The F. graminearum disease cycle is well established (Sutton, 1982). The fungus either overwinters on infested residue or lives endophytically on other grass species (Leplat et al., 2012; Lofgren et al., 2018). Ascospores and macroconidia are produced as temperatures warm and are typically disseminated via wind and rain to a susceptible host (Munkvold, 2003b). Fusarium graminearum typically infects via the silk channel (Oldenburg et al., 2017; Thompson & Raizada, 2018), but can also directly infect kernels if the husk has been wounded, such as from insect feeding (Mesterházy et al., 2012; Munkvold, 2003b). Infection rates are the highest when silks have just emerged until they begin to brown (Reid, Bolton et al., 1992). After infection, the ear is colonized by a bright pink to white mold from the first inoculation point outward. During the penetration, infection, and colonization phases, mycotoxins are produced and accumulate in the cob and different parts of the kernel (Oldenburg & Ellner, 2015; Schaafsma et al., 2004).

Integrated management strategies and the deployment of resistant cultivars are critical to effectively control GER. Host resistance to GER is quantitatively inherited, and maize genotypes with complete resistance are not available. Disease severity is considered a moderately reliable predictor of DON accumulation (Hart & Gendloff, 1984; Reid, Stewart et al., 1996). Therefore, breeding programs aim to reduce disease severity as a proxy for lowering DON accumulation (Mesterházy et al., 2012). However, DON levels well above FDA thresholds can occur in seemingly healthy grain (Dalla Lana et al., 2021; Lofgren et al., 2018). It seems that maize defends against mycotoxin accumulation and fungal colonization differently, and further exploration of the underlying mechanisms of GER resistance is necessary.

For GER resistance, several quantitative trait loci (QTLs) have been identified for disease severity, DON, and ZEA concentration (Lipps et al., 2024). However, only three studies have mapped QTLs for DON accumulation (Han et al., 2018; M. Martin et al., 2011; M. Martin et al., 2012), and a single study reported QTL for ZEA accumulation (M. Martin et al., 2011). There is a need to identify loci that reduce toxin accumulation, as, ultimately, toxin concentration is the trait of most interest. Continued screening for GER resistance enhances breeding efforts by identifying highly resistant donors and facilitating the development of resistant cultivars. A challenge associated with screening for ear rot resistance is the need for inoculated trials with sufficient disease pressure for evaluation and genetic mapping (Butoto et al., 2021). Even with appropriate inoculation techniques, GER is environmentally variable, and disease ratings are subject to rater variability. Identifying quantifiable, environmentally stable deterrents of disease may be useful in both understanding resistance mechanisms and streamlining breeding progress.

Secondary metabolites, like products of the phenylpropanoid pathway, generally function as antimicrobial phytoalexins (Muñoz‐Hoyos & Stam, 2023) or as signaling molecules for the production of other downstream compounds such as lignin (Upadhyay et al., 2024). The phenylpropanoid pathway has been well characterized, starting with phenylalanine through the production of lignin (Biala & Jasinski, 2018; Chateigner‐Boutin & Saulnier, 2022; Deng & Lu, 2017). Hydroxycinnamic acids (HCAs), flavonoids, and lignin are products of the phenylpropanoid pathway. Both in planta HCAs and flavonoids may potentially be involved in resistance to GER, but their role is less clear for DON and ZEA accumulation (Lipps et al., 2024). HCAs like ferulic acid (FerA) and p‐coumaric acid (PCoA) are environmentally stable and highly heritable traits (Butts‐Wilmsmeyer et al., 2017, 2020). Studies in maize have shown that phenolic compounds are produced during the early stages of GER development (Reid, Mather et al., 1992; Sekhon et al., 2006). Silk FerA levels sharply increase after inoculation and subsequently decrease over time (Reid, Mather et al., 1992). The rate of silk FerA decrease is much slower in resistant genotypes compared to susceptible genotypes (Cao et al., 2011; Reid, Mather et al., 1992). At the kernel level, HCAs and flavonoids are localized in the pericarp and aleurone layers (Chatham et al., 2019; Sen et al., 1994). This is particularly interesting as 55% of quantified DON can be found in the pericarp in maize kernels (Schaafsma et al., 2004). Pericarp thickness is highly heritable (Helm & Zuber, 1972; Ho et al., 1975), and genotypes with thicker pericarps have higher concentrations of secondary metabolites (Chatham et al., 2019). Anecdotally, the GER‐resistant inbred line, K127, was reported to have thick pericarps compared to more susceptible lines (Chungu et al., 1997). However, the relationship between HCAs, pericarp thickness, and GER resistance has not been thoroughly examined in a genetically diverse population.

Our overarching hypothesis is that grain phenylpropanoid content and kernel composition are genetically and mechanistically related to GER resistance. To test this hypothesis, we evaluated a diverse panel of maize lines with three objectives: (1) examine the effect of inoculation with GER on grain secondary metabolites, (2) quantify the relationship between GER resistance, secondary metabolites, and kernel composition, and (3) examine similarities and differences in the genetic architecture underlying GER and secondary metabolites. We employed both a genome‐wide association study (GWAS) to identify significant markers and the Pathway Association Study Tool (PAST) to identify influential metabolic pathways underlying our phenotypes. Here, we apply multiple statistically robust methods to examine the biochemical and structural potential of the maize kernel in GER resistance.

Core Ideas

  • Resistance mechanisms for Gibberella ear rot (GER) likely involve detoxification and cell wall fortification.

  • Lignin concentration and composition are important for defense against Fusarium graminearum colonization in maize.

  • End‐season ferulic and p‐coumaric acid are not reliable proxies for GER resistance.

2. MATERIALS AND METHODS

2.1. Germplasm

We developed and screened a GER diversity panel (n = 318) for GER in 2021 and 2022 (Supporting Information S1). Our panel included a subset of lines from the USDA Plant Introductory extension in Ames, IA (Romay et al., 2013), as well as lines previously evaluated for GER resistance (Hart & Gendloff, 1984). We selected lines that are well adapted to the growing conditions in east‐central Illinois and that flower no later than 80 days after planting. All lines are publicly available, and seed can be requested through the US National Plant Germplasm System.

2.2. Experimental design

The GER diversity panel was screened in 2021 and 2022 (Figure S1). Field experiments were organized in an augmented alpha‐lattice incomplete block design using the agricolae package (de Mendiburu & Muhammad, 2021). Each incomplete block was augmented with PHZ51 as a check line. Each environment was further blocked by flowering time: early, intermediate, and late. Within each flowering time, block plots of NC344, Oh7B, A554, and MS153 were added to meet alpha lattice parameters. NC344 and Oh7B were selected as they have previously been reported to be resistant to and susceptible to multiple fungal diseases, respectively (Lopez‐Zuniga et al., 2019). A554 and MS153 were included as susceptible and moderately susceptible check lines, respectively, based on results from preliminary experiments.

In 2021, the augmented alpha‐lattice design was set up as a split‐plot and repeated twice at each field in the Crop Sciences Research and Education Center in Urbana, IL (CFAR2021) and in Savoy, IL (Maxwell2021). At CFAR2021 and Maxwell2021, one replication was inoculated, and the other was uninoculated. Due to field size constraints, we could not have the split‐plot design replicated twice in the same field. Inoculation treatments were spatially separated within each environment to facilitate easy and efficient inoculation. Plots at CFAR2021 received supplemental overhead irrigation. In 2022 the GER diversity panel was evaluated once in each of two fields at the Crop Sciences Research and Education Center in Urbana, IL (CFAR2022 and ANSC2022). In 2022, all plots were inoculated. Supplementary drip irrigation was provided for the CFAR2022 plots.

2.3. Inoculations

Plants were inoculated with a prepared spore suspension (Lipps et al., 2025). We used a single F. graminearum isolate, 16Fg001, collected from wheat that is aggressive and an excellent producer of DON (Fall et al., 2019; Krone et al., 2024). To maximize conidia production, the isolate was cultured on potato dextrose at room temperature with a 12‐h light/12‐h dark photoperiod, then transferred to potato dextrose broth and cultured at room temperature in complete darkness (Lipps et al., 2025). Three days before inoculation, 4 mL of liquid culture was transferred to half‐strength potato dextrose agar plates and cultured for 48 h under constant black light at 25°C. To inoculate, a spore suspension was prepared and stored at 4°C. Sterile water was poured on 48‐h‐old plates and gently scraped with a glass rod to dislodge conidia. The concentrated suspension was diluted to 1 × 105 spores/mL using a hemacytometer. Plants were inoculated by injecting 1 mL of the prepared suspension into the midpoint of a 14‐ to 15‐day‐old ear with a perforated needle. Care was taken to not puncture the cob. A maximum of 10 ears per plot were inoculated.

2.4. Disease severity and flowering time

During each growing season, all plots were evaluated for days to anthesis (DTA) and days to silking (DTS). DTA is defined as the number of days after planting when 50% of the plot has visible anthers, while DTS is the number of days after planting when 50% of the plot has visible silks. Inoculation occurred 14–15 days after silking. At the end of the growing season, plots were harvested when the black layer was visible. Ears were bulked on a per plot basis. All ears were dried for 7–10 days in a dryer to remove excess moisture. Ears were visually evaluated for disease severity, or the percentage of visibly colonized kernels (SEV) on a 0%–100% scale, with 0 representing no disease and 100 representing the ear completely covered in mold (Lipps et al., 2025). The mean disease rating per plot was calculated. After disease evaluation, ears were shelled, and the grain was bulked on a per‐plot basis (Figure S1). Grain samples were finely ground to a homogenous consistency using a grain mill.

2.5. Mycotoxin and hydroxycinnamic acids

DON and ZEA were extracted using a modified version of the protocol presented by Sulyok et al. (2006). First, 0.5 g of milled grain was mixed with 2 mL of a 79:20:1 mixture of acetonitrile, water, and glacial acetic acid. Samples were incubated for 90 min at 27°C then centrifuged for 2 min at 3000 rpm (3300 g). The supernatant was collected and stored at −20°C until HPLC (high‐performance liquid chromatography) analysis. The frozen supernatant was thawed to room temperature and then centrifuged at 3000 rpm (3300 g) for 20 min. Finally, 900 µL of supernatant was collected for HPLC analysis.

Insoluble‐bound PCoA and FerA were extracted following the protocol described by Butts‐Wilmsmeyer et al. (2017). We collected PCoA and FerA profiles for both inoculated (PCoAinoc and FerAinoc) and uninoculated (PCoAun and FerAun) experiments. Samples were defatted using hexane, and 70% acetone was used to remove water‐soluble compounds. Samples were subjected to overnight starch digestion followed by alkaline hydrolysis using 2N NaOH for 2 h. The pH was adjusted using 7N HCl. We extracted insoluble‐bound HCAs via liquid–liquid phase extraction with ethyl acetate. The ethyl acetate fraction was separated and concentrated to dryness using a Labconco CentriVap system with a cold trap. Samples were reconstituted with 1 mL of ethyl acetate, vortexed for 30 s, and centrifuged at 2000 rpm (2200 g) for 30 min. For HPLC analysis 500 µL of the reconstituted, centrifuged extract was collected.

Both mycotoxin and HCA extracts were analyzed on a Shimadzu iSeries 2020 HPLC with Gemini C18 column, 100Å, 150 × 4.5 mm inner diameter, and 5 µm particle size. DON and ZEA were quantified from the same mycotoxin extract, while FerA and PCoA were quantified from the same HCA extract. Carrier solvents were H2O with 0.1% formic acid (Solvent A) and acetonitrile with 0.1% formic acid (Solvent B). We used a previously published gradient programming (Sulyok et al., 2006). The flow rate was 0.8 mL/min at 30°C. All chemical reagents used in this study are HPLC grade and available from Fisher Scientific. Purified water was obtained using a Milli‐Q water purification system (MilliporeSigma). Mycotoxin standards are available from MilliporeSigma. HCA standards are available from Fisher Scientific. All extractions used 0.5 g of milled grain in a 15 mL centrifuge tube. One centrifuge tube was prepared for each of the mycotoxin and HCA extraction procedures.

2.6. Kernel composition

Kernel composition was assessed on open‐pollinated grain collected from the uninoculated plots in 2021 (Figure S1). Concentrations of starch, protein, and oil were estimated from bulked, shelled grain using near‐infrared reflectance spectra acquired from a Perten DA 7200 Analyzer (Perkin Elmer), with two subsample readings per sample. The Perten DA 7200 Analyzer was previously calibrated using the following protocols. Grain composition was predicted from a custom calibration developed from partial least squares modeling of wet chemistry analysis of diverse maize samples as described (Perten Instruments Application, www.perkinelmer.com). Kernel starch content (starch) was determined using acid hydrolysis (Vidal et al., 2009), protein/total nitrogen (protein) via combustion analysis with an elemental analyzer (CE Elantech/ThermoFisher), and oil concentration (oil) by Soxhlet extraction, Method 920.39 (AOAC, 2003). Calibration sample values ranged from 5.2% to 35.8% protein, 1.6% to 20.0% oil, and 31.0% to 76.1% starch. Standard errors of prediction were 0.21, 0.58, 0.38, and 0.88 for kernel moisture, protein, oil, and starch concentrations, respectively. Kernel content values are presented as percentages.

Lines were assayed for pericarp thickness (Figure S1). We indirectly measured pericarp thickness by removing and weighing kernel pericarps (Diaz, 2003; Serna‐Saldivar et al., 1991). Briefly, five kernels per genotype from the same plot were evaluated, with each genotype tested four times using seeds from the same packet. We selected uniform seeds from each packet for all replications. Mo17 was included as a check every 30 samples to control for any variation due to extraction, drying, or weighing. Whole kernels were dried in an oven at 100°C for 72 h and weighed. Dried kernels were nixtamalized in 0.02 M NaOH for 15 min and cooled in deionized H2O. Pericarps were separated from the kernels with a scalpel and tweezers. The dissected kernels were dried at 100°C for 48 h. Pericarps were weighed separately from the remaining portion of the kernels. Pericarp percent weight (PCpct) is the percentage of the total dry kernel weight attributed to the pericarp. Here, a higher PCpct represents a thicker pericarp.

2.7. Genotypic data

The maize inbred lines were previously genotyped (Romay et al., 2013) using genotyping by sequencing (Elshire et al., 2011; Glaubitz et al., 2014). Missing data were imputed previously using the haplotype‐based imputation method described by Romay et al. (2013). The genotypic dataset was recently uplifted to the AGPv4 coordinates and consists of 943,000 SNPs for 17,280 taxa and is available at: panzea.org/genotypes. We filtered the marker dataset to include markers with no more than 20% missing data and a minor allele frequency greater than 5%. Our final marker dataset for all 318 lines consists of 203,465 markers spanning the entire genome.

2.8. Analysis of the effect of inoculation on hydroxycinnamic acid concentrations

The 2021 split‐plot experiment was first analyzed separately to examine the effect of inoculation on insoluble‐bound HCA concentration. A mixed model was fit using the PROC MIXED feature in SAS 9.4 (SAS Institute Inc., 2024).

Yijk=μ+Ei+Tj+ε1ij+Gk+TGjk+ε2ijk,

where Yijk is the FerA or PCoA concentration from the ith treatment, jth treatment, and kth genotype (i = 1, 2; j = 1, 2; k = 1, 2, …, 318); μ is the grand population mean; Ej is the random effect of the ith environment; Tj is the fixed effect of the jth treatment; εij is the random error term associated with the whole plot unit; Gk is the fixed effect of the kth genotype; TGjk is the fixed interaction between treatment and the genotype; and ε 2 ijk is the random error term associated with the subplot unit.

2.9. Estimation of least squared means

We estimated least squared means (LS Means) for traits from inoculated and uninoculated treatments separately. Mixed models were fit for the following phenotypes: SEV, DON, ZEA, PCoAinoc, PCoAun, FerAinoc, FerAun, PCpct, starch, protein, oil, DTA, DTS, and respective LS Means were estimated. LS Means were estimated using the PROC MIXED feature in SAS software version 9.4 (SAS Institute Inc., 2024). For all phenotypes, genotype was fit as a fixed effect. Mixed models were optimized to only include factors that were significant based on analysis of variance (ANOVA) and to minimize both Akaike information criterion (Akaike, 1973) and Bayesian information criterion (Schwarz, 1978) values. Incomplete block and checkline were not included in any of the models as they were not significant based on ANOVA. The following model was fit to estimate LS Means for SEV, PCoAinoc, and FerAinoc:

Yij=μ+Gi+Ej+GEij+εij.

The factors are defined as follows: Yijk is the value for either SEV, PCoAinoc, and FerAinoc value from genotype i in environment j; Gi is the fixed effect of genotype i; Ej is the random effect of environment j; GEij is the random effect of the interaction between genotype i and environment j, and εij is the error term. LS Means were calculated from estimates of Gi from the fitted models for SEV, PCoAinoc, and FerAinoc. LS Means for SEV, PCoAinoc, and FerAinoc were fit only using data from the inoculated treatments in 2021 and 2022. For SEV, LS Means were weighted by the number of ears evaluated per plot. To estimate LS means for DON, ZEA, protein, starch, oil, FerAun, PCoAun, DTA, and DTS, the same model was fit except GEij was not included as it was not significant based on ANOVA. For each kernel quality trait and untreated metabolic trait, the fixed effects for each genotype were extracted and used for further analysis. We log transformed both DON and ZEA. We transformed oil by a value of 0.8 based on a boxcox transformation. The best lambda was identified using the "boxcox" feature from the Hmisc package v.5.1‐0 (Harrell, 2023). As with the previous model, LS Means were calculated from the estimates of Gi from the fitted model. For PCpct, LS Means were estimated using the following model:

Yij=μ+Gi+Rj+εij,

where Yij is the value for PCpct for genotype i in replication j, Gi is the fixed effect for genotype i, Rj is the random effect for replication j, and εij is the error term. The fixed effects for genotype were extracted and used for further analysis. For all previously mentioned phenotypes, LS Means of Oh7B were not estimated for any trait as there was little to no seed set in every environment. Spearman rank correlation coefficients between all LS Means were estimated using the rcorr() function in the Hmisc package v5.1‐3 (Harrell, 2023).

2.10. Association mapping and identification of metabolic pathways associated with GER and related traits

We conducted association mapping with SEV, DON, and ZEA and any traits that were strongly correlated with SEV, DON, or ZEA (Figure S1). First, we employed the FarmCPU (fixed and random model circulating probability unification) method (Liu et al., 2016) in GAPIT (genome association and prediction integrated tool) version 3 (Lipka et al., 2012; Wang & Zhang, 2021) to identify markers associated with SEV, DON, ZEA, PCoAinoc, PCoAun, FerAinoc, FerAun, and PCpct. For all traits, we included no principal components for population structure for association mapping, as determined using the ‘Model.selection’ feature. Significant markers were determined by using Benjamini–Hochberg adjusted p‐values (Benjamini & Hochberg, 1995), and a threshold of α = 0.1 was used.

We then employed the PAST to identify metabolic pathways associated with SEV, DON, ZEA, PCoAinoc, PCoAun, FerAinoc, FerAun, and PCpct (Figure S1). PAST analyzes association mapping results and annotated metabolic pathways to identify metabolic pathways associated with a phenotype of interest (Thrash et al., 2020). PAST requires TASSEL results as input, so association analysis was also conducted using TASSEL. First, using TASSEL v.2.81 (Bradbury et al., 2007), we generated a centered IBS (identical‐by‐state) kinship matrix and ran association mapping using the mixed linear model (MLM) approach with an optimum compression level. We also generated a linkage disequilibrium file using TASSEL v2.81 (Bradbury et al., 2007). We did not include a principal component matrix for the MLM in TASSEL, as the GAPIT software did not include principal components. We conducted the PAST analysis with R v4.4.1 (R Core Team, 2024) using the PAST package v1.22.0 (Thrash et al., 2020). A gene assignment window of 1000 base pairs was used, an R 2 cutoff of 0.8 was applied, and pathways with at least five genes were considered. Significance was determined based on 1000 permutations and a threshold of α = 0.05 was used to identify significant pathways. For all association analyses, we used the same filtered marker dataset and phenotypic file (Supporting Information S1).

2.11. Screening lignin‐deficient mutants for Gibberella ear rot

We screened brown midrib single mutants with mutations in bm1, bm2, and bm4, and double mutants of bm1 and bm2 (bm1bm2), and bm3 and bm4 (bm3bm4) in an A619 background (Barrière & Argillier, 1993). Lignin‐deficient genotypes were screened in 2022 and 2023 at the Crop Sciences Research and Education Center in Urbana, IL. In both years, experiments were organized in a randomized complete block design with four complete blocks per year. All lines were inoculated and evaluated for GER severity as previously described. We first identified and removed two outliers within our data that had studentized residuals more than two standard deviations from the mean and had significantly elevated Cook's distance values. We then fit the filtered data to a linear model and ran an ANOVA to ensure that genotype was significant. SEV was transformed by a value of 0.63 based on a boxcox transformation. The best lambda was identified using ‘boxcox’ feature from the Hmisc package v.5.1‐0 (Harrell, 2023). We then used a Dunnett's test to identify brown midrib mutants that were statistically different from the A619 control (α = 0.1). The Dunnett's test was performed using the ‘DunnettTest’ feature from the package DescTools v0.99.57 (Signorell, 2024).

3. RESULTS

3.1. Gibberella ear rot and toxin concentration are environmentally variable traits

In the 2021 experiment, SEV, DON, and ZEA values were higher in inoculated trials than in untreated trials (Table 1). In the uninoculated treatments, the highest observed SEV was 6.5%. The FDA guideline for DON in grain products intended for human consumption is 1 part per million (ppm) (Center for Veterinary Medicine, 2010). Two uninoculated plots had DON levels above 1 ppm, with a maximum concentration of 1.48 ppm. Advisory levels are not set for ZEA in the United States. The European Commission (2023) has set a threshold of 0.35 ppm (350 µg/kg) for ZEA in “unprocessed maize grains before first‐stage processing.” Three uninoculated plots were above this threshold and the maximum uninoculated ZEA concentration was 8.2 ppm. There was no overlap between uninoculated plots with the highest DON and ZEA levels nor with the highest severity scores. All plots with DON and ZEA levels above regulatory thresholds had less than 1% average disease severity. In the inoculated treatments, we observed between 0% and 100% disease severity, DON levels as high as 2977.96 ppm, and ZEA levels as high as 80.53 ppm (Figure 1).

3.2. The dynamics of hydroxycinnamic acids

To test whether inoculation had an effect on HCAs, we conducted a split‐plot trial in 2021 that included inoculated and uninoculated plots of the GER diversity panel and evaluated the plots for HCA concentrations. We observed significant genotypic variation in PCoA and FerA concentration (Figure 2; Table S2). Inoculation had a significant effect on end‐season FerA but not end‐season PCoA (Table S2). Significant treatment × genotype interactions were observed for both FerA and PCoA (Table S2), indicating that end‐season HCA varied in response to inoculation and responses varied by genotype. Some genotypes had a stronger response to inoculation compared to others (Figures S2 and S3). Specifically, NC344 and PHZ51 had elevated mean PCoA concentrations after inoculation, while mean A554 and MS153 PCoA concentrations were reduced after inoculation. This trend was also similar for mean FerA concentrations across the same set of lines (Figure S2). Inoculation has a significant effect on end‐season FerA and PCoA concentrations, but the extent of the effect is genotype specific.

A similar distribution of FerA and PCoA concentrations was observed across all inoculated environments (Figure 1). Variance component estimates for Environment and Genotype × Environment explained minimal variation for PCoAinoc and FerAinoc (Table S3). For PCoAun and FerAun environment also explained minimal variation (Table S3).

A central hypothesis of this study is that HCAs underlie GER resistance in maize. Two pathways were associated with increasing either FerAinoc or PCoAinoc levels: PWY‐5041:S‐adenosyl‐l‐methionine salvage II and PWY‐6457:cinnamoyl‐CoA biosynthesis, respectively. S‐Adenosyl‐l‐methionine production is a key substrate in the phenylpropanoid pathway and is necessary for FerA and downstream lignin production (Sauter et al., 2013; Tang et al., 2014; Figure S6). PWY‐5041 was also associated with decreasing SEV levels, suggesting that lignin may be associated with GER resistance. Cinnamoyl‐CoA is reduced by cinnamoyl‐CoA reductase to produce cinnamaldehydes, which are immediate precursors to the various lignin subunits (Davin et al., 2008; Figure S6). Additionally, PWY‐6457 was associated with increasing DON levels. Fusarium graminearum isolates that produce DON are more efficient in intracellular colonization (Armer et al., 2024). Interestingly, PWY‐6040:chlorogenic acid biosynthesis II significantly decreased PCoAinoc and ZEA levels. PWY‐5313:superpathway of anthocyanin biosynthesis was associated with increasing SEV and ZEA. Anthocyanin is a separate branch of the phenylpropanoid pathway that can be produced in place of lignin (Figure S6). Finally, the pathway R‐ZMA‐1119314.1:cellulose biosynthesis was significantly associated with increased PCoAinoc and DON. The plant cell wall is comprised of cellulose and lignin, which form orthogonally arranged layers that are typically cross‐linked with polysaccharides (Alberts et al., 2002). PCoA is associated with cell wall fortification (Ishii, 1997). Ultimately, the association of these phenylpropanoid‐related pathways with GER‐related traits suggests that lignin may be important in GER resistance.

3.6. Lignin‐deficient mutants are more susceptible to Gibberella ear rot

There were significant differences in GER susceptibility for the brown midrib mutants (Table S6). The double mutants of bm1 and bm2 (bm1bm2) (p = 0.10) and the single mutant of bm4 (p = 0.07) were significantly more susceptible to GER when compared to A619 (Figure 5; Table S6). A previous study that screened brown midrib mutants bm1, bm2, bm3, and bm4 in a W64A background found that single mutants bm1 and bm3 were significantly more susceptible to GER compared to the W64A control (Kolkman et al., 2023). We did not have enough seed for the bm3 mutant to examine the effect of the bm3 single mutant in our experiment. Thus, there are varying degrees of GER susceptibility among different brown midrib mutants, with bm1bm2 double mutant and bm4 single mutant showing increased susceptibility compared to A619.

4. DISCUSSION

The maize–F. graminearum system is beautifully complex. There is an established foundation regarding the genetic architecture of resistance (Akohoue & Miedaner, 2022; Lipps et al., 2024). Breeding programs continue to work toward decreasing disease severity and mitigating mycotoxin accumulation (Mesterházy et al., 2012; Miedaner et al., 2020; Munkvold, 2003a). Epidemiological studies have elucidated contributing environmental covariates that are drivers for mycotoxin production (Dalla Lana et al., 2021). Still, little is known regarding the underlying mechanisms of GER resistance. There is a critical need to identify and exploit GER resistance mechanisms to develop resistant germplasm. We comprehensively screened a diverse panel of inbred maize lines for GER in relation to HCA content and kernel composition. We observed correlations between secondary metabolites and GER, identified markers for secondary metabolites in the same regions as markers for GER, and identified metabolic pathways shared between secondary metabolites and GER. We identified candidate genes related to detoxification, and cell wall integrity, among others. These results provide a platform for future research to continue to examine distinct mechanisms to mitigate fungal colonization and mycotoxin detoxification.

4.1. Variability for disease severity and mycotoxin accumulation

In the maize–F. graminearum system environmental covariates, tissue type, biochemical composition, host genetics, and pathogen genetics all interact to produce visual disease symptoms and mycotoxin (Agrios, 2005; Leveau, 2024). Fusarium graminearum employs separate genetic machinery to synthesize DON and ZEA (Desjardins, 2006). The relationship between SEV and mycotoxin levels is nuanced. In many genetic backgrounds, disease severity is not predictive of mycotoxin concentration, and DON concentration varies across the growing season (Miller & Young, 1983; Reid & Sinha, 1998). We observed strong, significant correlations for SEV among environments, while correlations for DON and ZEA between environments were nonexistent. Furthermore, we observed weak but significant correlations between DON, ZEA, and SEV. This is consistent with the GWAS results, where there are no overlapping markers for these traits. Thus, there are likely alternative mechanisms underlying mycotoxin accumulation that are separate from disease severity.

We report very high values for DON but not for ZEA. DON concentrations from 0 to 1922 ppm have been reported in inoculated studies (Han et al., 2018; Hart & Gendloff, 1984; M. Martin et al., 2011; Reid, Mather et al., 1996; Reid, Stewart, et al., 1996). ZEA levels up to 74 ppm have been reported in other inoculated studies (Hart & Gendloff, 1984; M. Martin et al., 2011). Our Fusarium graminearum isolate was isolated from wheat and was the most aggressive and prolific DON producer on wheat from a population of 56 isolates collected in Illinois (Fall et al., 2019; Krone et al., 2024). Previously, DON has been evaluated in small samples of inbred lines (Hart & Gendloff, 1984; Reid, Mather et al., 1996), in elite European Flint and Dent populations (Han et al., 2018), or in a biparental population developed from elite European germplasm (M. Martin et al., 2011). Similarly, ZEA has previously been evaluated in a biparental population developed from elite European germplasm (M. Martin et al., 2011). It is possible that we have lines that exhibit extreme susceptibility to DON accumulation. Additionally, the environment and management of the field sites likely contributed to high mycotoxin concentrations.

4.2. Putative host resistance mechanisms

Resistance to mycotoxin accumulation is the most important GER resistance phenotype. Only three mapping studies have identified QTL for DON (Han et al., 2018; M. Martin et al., 2011; Martin et al., 2012) and one mapping study for ZEA (M. Martin et al., 2011). For SEV, the candidate gene Zm00001d029716 on chromosome 1, encoding a putative heavy metal transport/detoxification superfamily protein identified by the GWAS, implicates detoxification as a potential mechanism of resistance. DON detoxification mechanisms have been characterized in wheat and Arabidopsis (W. J. He et al., 2024; McLaughlin et al., 2021). In wheat infected with Fusarium crown rot, TraesCS5D01G141600.1, a gene encoding a heavy metal transport/detoxification superfamily protein, was significantly upregulated in two wheat cultivars after inoculation with F. pseudograminearum and Fusarium culmorum (Jin et al., 2020). In barley, two defense responses to manage trichothecene accumulation have been proposed: detoxification and neutralization of the mycotoxin via ubiquitination and transportation, and ubiquitination that leads to eventual programmed cell death (Boddu et al., 2007). Within this proposed model, the maize candidate gene Zm00001d029716 could function in the transportation and detoxification of DON and ZEA. For ZEA, Arabidopsis thaliana employs other transporters such as ABC (ATP synthase‐binding cassette) transporters and glucosyltransferases are effective host detoxification strategy (Adam et al., 2015). Another detoxification candidate gene is Zm00001d051863, which encodes a cytochrome P450 on chromosome 4. Cytochrome P450s are implicated in the production of various phytoalexins such as camalexin, isoflavonoids, and DIMBOA (Dixon, 2001; Dixon et al., 2002). In plants, cytochrome P450s function in detoxification of herbicides (Goldberg‐Cavalleri et al., 2025; Powles & Yu, 2010) and activated oxygen species (Werck‐Reichhart & Feyereisen, 2000). In insects, cytochrome P450s are also functional in detoxifying many mycotoxins (Berenbaum et al., 2020; De Zutter et al., 2016). Detoxification solutions within maize seem a promising avenue to further explore to manage mycotoxin accumulation.

We also identified candidate genes associated with pathogen defense for PCoAinoc and FerAinoc (Table S5). For PCoAinoc, two defense‐related candidate genes are Zm00001d028999, which encodes a NAC (N‐acetyl cysteine) transcription factor N‐acetylmuramic acid2 on chromosome 1, and Zm00001d018620, which encodes a Peroxidase 1 on chromosome 7. NAC transcription factors are associated with stress response and hormone signaling in response to invasion from many fungal pathogens (Yuan et al., 2019). Peroxidases serve many potential functions in plant defense including cell wall reinforcement and as signaling molecules for phytohormones and phytoalexins (Almagro et al., 2009). We also identified the candidate gene Zm00001d029940 on chromosome 1, which encodes an ethylene‐response transcription factor 5 for FerAinoc. Ethylene is a plant hormone associated with biotic and abiotic stress response and often interacts with other defense‐related hormones (Broekaert et al., 2006). It seems that when infested with F. graminearum, HCA levels are, in part, controlled by defense‐related mechanisms.

4.3. Potential strategies to maintain cell integrity

Fusarium graminearum secretes many putative cell wall degrading enzymes (Brown et al., 2012). Thus, resistance strategies that prevent cell death by fortifying the cell wall or maintaining cell integrity are essential. The mobilization of antifungal compounds and compounds that strengthen the cell wall is one plausible defense strategy against necrotrophic pathogens like F. graminearum. We identified candidate genes involved with cytoskeleton rearrangement and cell wall reinforcement (Table 5; Table S5). A candidate gene for SEV, Zm00001d006549, encodes a putative formin‐like protein 11 on chromosome 2, and a candidate gene for FerAinoc, Zm00001d044819, encodes a putative myosin‐11 protein encoding gene on chromosome 9. Myosin and actin are interconnected. Myosin traffics vesicles by moving across actin filaments within the cell (Duan & Tominaga, 2018). Formin proteins aid in cytoskeleton reorganization to assist in targeted defense response at the plant cell wall (Deeks, 2021; Sassmann et al., 2018). We also identified candidate genes associated with cell wall composition for ZEA and PCoAun. For ZEA on chromosome 1, Zm00001d026066 encodes a xyloglucan galactosyltransferase and is functionally important in xyloglucan biosynthesis (Jensen et al., 2012), which is a key component of the cell wall in pericarp cells (Santiago‐Ramos et al., 2018). For PCoAun, the candidate gene Zm00001d015186 on chromosome 5 encodes a glycosyltransferase. Glycosyltransferases function to build complex polysaccharides that are structural components of the plant cell wall (Amos & Mohnen, 2019). We also identified Zm00001d012477 on chromosome 8, annotated as a putative laccase family protein (Table S5). Laccases are associated with downstream lignin production and the formation of plant cell walls (Bai et al., 2023). The plant cell wall is a complex, layered matrix of polysaccharides, HCA‐amides, lignin, and pectin (Alberts et al., 2002; Ishii, 1997; Santiago‐Ramos et al., 2018).

4.4. Kernel composition and disease resistance

The first tissue F. graminearum encounters during kernel colonization is the pericarp (Lipps et al., 2024). Thicker pericarps have the potential to contain larger quantities of secondary metabolites (Chatham et al., 2019). In the GER diversity panel, PCpct was positively correlated with inoculated and uninoculated FerA and PCoA. Furthermore, PCpct and SEV were negatively correlated. While we did not observe significant relationships between PCpct and mycotoxin accumulation, the pericarp is where the most DON is found in infested maize grain (Schaafsma et al., 2004). Thus, a thicker, more complex pericarp with higher HCA concentration may limit fungal spread, but it may increase DON and ZEA accumulation.

The role of kernel composition in GER resistance is not well understood. For Aspergillus ear rot, caused by Aspergillus flavus, kernel fiber and ash content were strongly negatively correlated with aflatoxin, and carbohydrate levels were positively correlated with aflatoxin (Mideros et al., 2012). We observed similar, albeit weaker, trends with SEV and ZEA in the GER resistance panel. In wheat, cellulose levels decreased in grain due to infection (Boyacioǧlu & Hettiarachchy, 1995). In infected barley, β‐glucan, a soluble fiber in endosperm cell walls, significantly decreased after infection with F. graminearum (C. Martin et al., 2018). Furthermore, the most resistant barley cultivars had the highest β‐glucan levels (C. Martin et al., 2018). The grain structure, the physical arrangement of components within the grain, rather than simply composition, the chemical makeup of grain, is important for GER resistance. The more efficiently F. graminearum can break down cell walls, and access starches, the more aggressive the pathogen seems to be.

4.5. Hydroxycinnamic acids, lignin, and resistance to Gibberella ear rot

HCAs and other secondary metabolites are multifunctional components of plant response to biotic stress. These metabolites can act as phytoalexins and signaling molecules; however, the underlying mechanisms by which they regulate plant defense are not fully understood (Erb & Kliebenstein, 2020; Muñoz‐Hoyos & Stam, 2023; Upadhyay et al., 2024; Zeiss et al., 2021). Adding to the complexity, the multiple functions of these compounds are highly interconnected. In horticultural crops, both FerA and PCoA function as weak antimicrobials effective against fungal, bacterial, and viral pathogens (J. He et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2023; Islam et al., 2018, 2019; Shu et al., 2021). In the case of Fusarium sp., amending media with either FerA or PCoA significantly reduces fungal colonization (Assabgui et al., 1993; Fajardo et al., 2025; Martínez‐Fraca et al., 2022; Ponts et al., 2011; Yan et al., 2023). This antimicrobial function may partially explain the elevated PCoA observed in our inoculated treatments; however, the interactions are quite complex and genotype specific. HCAs may also serve as signaling molecules or damage‐associated molecular pattern, triggering defense signaling upon their release or activation during pathogen invasion (Erb & Kliebenstein, 2020; Upadhyay et al., 2024; Zeiss et al., 2021). In tomato, the accumulation of HCA amides, derivatives of HCAs, has been associated with the upregulation of WRKY transcription factors, hypersensitive response genes, and increased salicylic acid concentrations (Campos et al., 2014; Etalo et al., 2013). HCA production is dynamic over the course of the disease cycle, complicating the interpretation of their role in pathogen defense (J. He et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2023; Islam et al., 2018, 2019; Shu et al., 2021). In the maize–F. graminearum system, HCA levels fluctuate, but resistance seems to depend on the host's ability to produce HCAs at rates that are faster than the pathogen can metabolize them (Cao et al., 2011; Fajardo et al., 2025; Reid, Mather et al., 1992). Overall, HCAs are likely functioning at multiple levels to provide resistance to diseases such as GER. Key open questions in the maize–F. graminearum system include what specific defense responses are elicited by HCAs in the ear and how F. graminearum perceives, detoxifies, or otherwise interacts with these compounds.

A final product of the phenylpropanoid pathway is lignin (Figure S6). In maize, Ustilago maydis manipulates the phenylpropanoid pathway to produce anthocyanin over lignin (Tanaka et al., 2014, 2018). Many proteins secreted by F. graminearum have predicted functions associated with cellulose degradation, phenolic polymer lignin, laccases, peroxidases, and FerA esterases (Brown et al., 2012). In maize, the brown midrib genes affect distinct aspects of the final steps in lignin biosynthesis (Figure S6). The Bm1 gene encodes a cinnamyl alcohol dehydrogenase (Barrière et al., 1994; Halpin et al., 1998), one of the final enzymes in lignin synthesis. Both Bm2, a methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase, and Bm4, a folypolyglutamate synthase, are within the S‐adenosyl‐methionine pathway (Li et al., 2015; Tang et al., 2014). S‐Adenosyl methionine functions as a methyl donor for both caffeoyl CoA 3‐O‐methyltransferase and caffeic acid O‐methyltransferase (Figure S6), encoded by Bm3 (Barrière et al., 1994; Vignols et al., 1995). The lignin‐deficient, brown midrib maize mutants are more susceptible to multiple foliar diseases, anthracnose stalk rot, and GER (Kolkman et al., 2023). We also observed increased susceptibility to GER in brown midrib maize mutants (Figure 5). The bm1 and bm3 mutants in a W64A background are more susceptible to GER (Kolkman et al., 2023). In this study, bm1bm2 and bm4 mutants in an A619 are significantly more susceptible to GER. These discrepancies could be partially attributed to differences in the genetic background between A619 and W64A or differences between F. graminearum isolates. Brown midrib mutants have reduced overall lignin content and altered ratios of the lignin subunits: guaiacyl (G), syringyl (S), and p‐hydroxyphenol (P). (Spangler, 2008). Maize bm2 and bm4 mutants have a higher S/G lignin ratio despite decreased total lignin content (Vermerris et al., 2010). A higher S/G lignin ratio in maize cobs increases resistance to A. flavus (Spangler, 2008). It is surprising to see that the bm1bm2 and bm4 mutants were more susceptible to GER, as they likely also have higher S/G lignin ratios. Fusarium graminearum and A. flavus are distinct pathogens. Meta‐analyses have reported some overlap between F. graminearum and other ear rot pathogens (Akohoue & Miedaner, 2022; Mideros et al., 2014). Fusarium graminearum clearly possesses the molecular machinery to degrade and break down lignin. It is unclear how F. graminearum interacts with lignin during colonization. However, it does seem that lignin is a critical component of GER resistance. Maize grown for silage has altered lignin concentrations and ratios compared to maize grown for grain. The increased susceptibility of bm mutants to GER implies that silage corn is at risk for mycotoxin contamination and should be monitored for mycotoxin accumulation (Reed et al., 2022).

5. CONCLUSIONS

GER is a complex, quantitative disease, and it is unlikely that a single gene or metabolic pathway will completely control the disease. In our comprehensive screening of the GER diversity panel, we identified traits, markers, candidate genes, and metabolic pathways that are associated with resistance to GER. We identified regions on chromosomes 1, 3, and 8 with markers significantly associated with SEV, ZEA, HCA concentration, and PCpct. HCAs, like FerA and PCoA, are multifunctional but their roles as antimicrobial compounds, signaling molecules, and cell wall fortifiers appear critical in GER resistance. Furthermore, lignin concentration and composition appear to be meaningful in limiting fungal colonization. Of note, we identified candidate genes and pathways associated with HCA production, lignin biosynthesis, and cell wall fortification. Brown midrib mutants, particularly bm1bm2 and bm4, had higher fungal colonization compared to wild type, suggesting that lignin, specifically the ratio of lignin subunits, may be important in limiting fungal spread. Further studies examining the temporal dynamic of the maize–Fusarium graminearum system will provide insight into the complexities of the host–pathogen interaction.
