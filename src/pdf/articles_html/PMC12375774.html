Abstract

In this paper, the influence of surface energy (SE) on the linear and nonlinear frequencies of anodic aluminum micro beams with [100] and [111] crystalline orientations resting on an elastic substrate are analyzed based on the Timoshenko beam (TB) and Euler–Bernoulli (EB) models, spanning Nano- to micro-scale dimensions. Given the high ratio of surface-to-volume the studied micro beams, the proposed model incorporates SE effects. To extract the micro beam frequencies, the governing the Galerkin method with trigonometric and polynomial shape functions corresponding to clamped–clamped, clamped-simply supported, and simply supported boundary conditions are used. By assuming a harmonic temporal response, the natural frequencies are derived. The study examines the influence of crystalline orientation ([100] and [111]), beam length, elastic substrate coefficient, moment of inertia, and shear deformation (via the TB) under various boundary conditions on the nonlinear and linear frequencies of the micro Timoshenko beam models and Euler–Bernoulli. A comparative analysis reveals that the EB yields higher estimates for both linear and nonlinear frequencies compared to the TB, which accounts for rotational inertia and shear deformation effects. Furthermore, the results demonstrate that crystalline orientation significantly impacts the linear and nonlinear frequencies. Specifically, the anodic aluminum microbe am with [111] crystalline orientation exhibits higher linear and nonlinear frequencies due to its greater stiffness compared to the [100] orientation.

Keywords: Surface elasticity, Residual stress, Micro beam vibrations, Crystalline orientation, Euler–Bernoulli beam, Timoshenko beam, Galerkin method, Elastic substrate

Subject terms: Engineering, Materials science, Mathematics and computing, Physics

Introduction

In recent years, micro- and nanostructures have garnered significant attention in scientific and engineering communities due to their exceptional properties, including high sensitivity, superior reliability, miniaturized dimensions, and enhanced mechanical durability. These advanced structures have enabled groundbreaking applications across multiple disciplines, such as: High-precision instrumentation (atomic force microscopy probes1,2), Bio sensing and chemical detection (lab-on-a-chip diagnostic systems3–6), MEMS/NEMS devices (resonators, energy harvesters, and micro actuators4,7–9) and Biomedical engineering (e.g., targeted drug delivery systems and implantable sensors). A critical characteristic of these miniaturized structures is their exceptionally high ratio of surface-to-volume, which becomes increasingly dominant at reduced scales. As extensively demonstrated in prior research10,11, surface-related phenomena—including surface elasticity, residual stresses, and atomic-scale adhesion effects—play a non-negligible role in determining the mechanical, electrical, and thermal responses of micro/nanoscale devices. This surface dominance leads to size-dependent behaviors that frequently deviate from classical continuum mechanics predictions. Current Research Challenges are modeling complexities, fabrication-induced stresses and anisotropy considerations. Recent studies have employed modified beam theories (e.g., nonlocal elasticity or strain gradient approaches) to better capture these size-dependent phenomena, particularly for dynamic applications where resonant frequency control is crucial. The development of comprehensive models that incorporate surface energy effects, geometric nonlinearities, and substrate interactions remains an active area of research in Micro-Nano electro-mechanical systems (MEMS/NEMS). These effects result in fundamentally distinct physical and mechanical properties at material surfaces compared to their bulk counterparts12–14. Consequently, classical elasticity theory fails to accurately predict surface-dominated behaviors at small scales due to its inherent inability to incorporate size-dependent parameters15. This limitation has motivated extensive research efforts to develop more sophisticated models capturing surface stress and strain effects on physical behavior. Lagowski et al.16 pioneered the analysis of resonant frequencies in GaAs micro beams using classical beam theory, where residual surface stress was modeled as a compressive axial force. This approach oversimplified surface stress distribution by neglecting its tonsorial nature. Gurtin and coworkers17 enhanced Lagowski’s model by introducing a distributed tensile force across the beam surface to more accurately represent residual surface stress. Their work revealed that while surface elasticity significantly influences natural frequencies (contrary to16), residual surface stress plays a negligible role in dynamic response highlighting the need for model specificity. Surface elasticity (stiffness) and residual stress (pre-strain) exhibit competing influences on vibrational characteristics, requiring separate treatment in constitutive models. At nanoscale, surface energy contributions may exceed bulk energy, necessitating non-classical continuum approaches. Recent AFM-based measurements confirm that surface elasticity dominates frequency shifts in Si micro beams, supporting Gurtin-type models over compressive-stress assumptions. The Laplace-Young equation was specifically developed to model surface stresses in fluids. Gurtin et al.18 utilized this equation to develop a continuum model of surface elasticity in solids. Through the Laplace-Young equation, Wang and Feng19 investigated the natural frequency of an EBMB. He and Lilley20 studied the effects of surface elasticity and residual surface stress on the vibrations of nanowires with various boundary conditions, and their results were validated by experimental studies21. Chen et al.22 also proposed a generalized Laplace-Young equation for curved surfaces in nanostructures. The TB has been widely used to study the dynamic behavior of micro- and Nano-sized beams23,24. Abbasionejad et al.25 examined the linear free vibrations of a TBMB in the presence of surface elasticity and residual surface stress. In recent years, the nonlinear vibrations of micro beams under size-dependent effects have attracted significant attention. Mohammadi and Abbasi26 investigated the nonlinear vibrations of an atomic force microscope micro beam using perturbation theory. They derived expressions for the nonlinear frequency parameter and nonlinear factor of damping, observing their dependence on size-dependent parameters in the results. Toğan27 examined the nonlinear vibrations of EB on an elastic foundation, incorporating third-order nonlinearities. He analyzed the of nonlocal influence, the stiffness of elastic medium, and boundary conditions on the frequency response. Shafiei et al.28 studied the size-dependent nonlinear vibrational behavior of FG porous micro beams. A comprehensive investigation into the nonlinear free vibrations of piezoelectric Nano actuators was conducted by Kaghazian et al.29 employing nonlocal elasticity theory. Their study systematically examined the influence of key parameters including applied direct voltage, scale factor, and geometric dimensions on the natural frequency characteristics of piezoelectric micro beams. The findings contribute to a deeper understanding of the dynamic behavior of microscale piezoelectric systems under varying operational and structural conditions30–35. Farsad et al.30 introduces a coupled XFEM-level set approach to model nanoscale surface and interface effects, validating it through analytical, atomistic, and multiscale comparisons for nanoplates and nanobeams. He and Lilley31 investigate how surface stress affects the resonance frequencies of bending nanowires by integrating the generalized Young–Laplace equation into beam theory, deriving analytical solutions for different boundary conditions and proposing an empirical relationship between the overall Young’s modulus and a surface effect factor. He et al.32 systematically investigate frame-dependent constitutive equations for surfaces, defining surface elastic properties and proposing a hyperelastic incompressible surface model, with robust finite element implementations validated against theoretical results. In the present study, based on the model proposed in Ref.25, the characteristics of free vibrations of a TBMB and EBMB resting on an elastic foundation are investigated. Furthermore, the effects of surface elasticity and residual surface stress as size-dependent functions on the resonant frequencies of micro beams are discussed. The linear and nonlinear frequencies of Micro beam based on the both beam theories including TBMB and EBMB are computed and compared.

Results and discussion

First, to validate the obtained results, a comparison between the normalized natural frequencies (obtained by dividing the linear frequencies of TBMB and TBMB by ) and the reference results25 is presented in Figs. 2 and 3. For this comparison, the beam support conditions are considered as simply supported, consistent with reference25. Figures 2 and 3 illustrate the variations of the normalized linear frequency versus beam length scaling from Nano to micro dimensions for two crystalline orientations: [111] and [100]. The analyzed beam has the following mechanical properties: Young’s modulus , density , Poisson’s ratio , and shear correction parameter . Aluminum, due to its orientation-dependent properties, may exhibit either negative or positive surface elasticity depending on different surface residual stresses25,32. This study examines two crystalline orientations:

  1. [100] with surface elasticity N/m and residual stress

  2. [111] with surface elasticity and residual stress 25,32.

A strong correlation is observed between the present results and the reference data in the normalized frequency variation curves25. Furthermore, it can be inferred that as beam length increases, the surface elasticity effect diminishes significantly. For sufficiently long beams, this effect becomes negligible in the beam modeling.

Figure 4 demonstrates the effect of employing different shape functions on the natural frequency of an EB with [111] crystalline orientation and elastic coefficient . The frequency curves presented in Fig. 4 were derived using both trigonometric and polynomial shape functions for beams with simply-supported (SS), clamped-simply supported (CS)and clamped–clamped (CC) boundary conditions, according to Eqs. (18) and (23). As shown one can be concluded that the polynomial shape functions yield lower estimates of the linear frequency compared to trigonometric shape functions for all boundary conditions examined. This frequency reduction effect is consistent across the SS, CC, and CS boundary configurations. The observed behavior suggests that the choice of shape function significantly influences the predicted dynamic characteristics of Nano-micro-beams.

Figure 5 presents the variation of the beam’s linear frequency with respect to length changes using four different beam theories including TBMB with surface elasticity, EBMB with surface elasticity, Conventional TB and EB. The analysis considers simply-supported boundary conditions with [111] crystalline orientation and elastic coefficient . The results indicate that the EB predicts higher frequencies than the TB at shorter lengths and both theories converge for sufficiently long beams as rotational inertia and shear deformation effects diminish. In addition, the [111] crystalline orientation increases system stiffness, yielding higher natural frequencies compared to models neglecting surface elasticity. Figure 6 examines surface elasticity effects by comparing [111] crystalline orientation, [100] orientation and without surface elasticity effect. The results indicate that the crystalline orientation significantly impacts short-beam frequencies. In addition, the [111] orientation produces the highest frequency predictions and the [100] orientation’s negative surface elasticity reduces system stiffness. Figure 7 evaluates boundary condition effects. Clamped supports (CC) yield the highest frequencies due to constraint stiffness and frequency magnitudes follow: CC > CS > SS for all beam models.

A comprehensive analysis of the numerical results presented in Figs. 5, 6, 7 reveals several key findings regarding the dynamic behavior of nanobeams with surface elasticity effects. The most significant observation indicates that incorporation of surface elasticity in the theoretical model produces a marked enhancement in the frequency response characteristics of nanobeams across all considered boundary conditions. This enhancement manifests as a systematic upward shift in the natural frequency spectrum, with the magnitude of this shift being strongly dependent on the beam’s characteristic dimensions. The comparative study between models with and without surface elasticity effects demonstrates particularly pronounced differences for nanobeams with shorter lengths (L < 25 nm in the current study), where surface-to-volume ratios are substantially higher. In these cases, the frequency response may increase by as much as 10–20%, depending on the specific boundary conditions and mode number under consideration. However, this effect exhibits clear length-scale dependence, gradually diminishing for longer beam specimens (L > 25 nm) until becoming practically negligible at macroscopic scales. Furthermore, the investigation of boundary condition effects yields consistent trends across all length scales. The frequency spectrum follows a hierarchical pattern corresponding to the degree of constraint, with fully clamped (CC) configurations producing the highest natural frequencies, followed by clamped-simply supported (CS), and finally simply supported (SS) arrangements displaying the lowest frequency values. This behavior aligns with classical beam theory predictions, though the absolute frequency values are notably modified by surface effects, particularly in the nanoscale regime. The observed phenomena can be physically interpreted through two complementary mechanisms: (1) the surface elasticity introduces additional stiffness contributions that become increasingly significant at smaller scales, and (2) the boundary conditions govern the relative dominance of bending versus stretching deformation modes, with constrained configurations naturally exhibiting higher stiffness characteristics. These findings have important implications for the design of nanoelectromechanical systems (NEMS) where both dimensional scaling and support conditions can be engineered to achieve desired dynamic performance.

To analyze the effectiveness of two different beam models in calculating the fundamental frequencies for modes 1 to 4, Fig. 8 has been generated. Considering identical mechanical and geometric properties for both models under investigation, it can be observed that as the mode number increases in shorter lengths, the discrepancy in predicting natural frequencies becomes more pronounced. Consequently, for higher modes, the TB should be employed to obtain accurate results. Additionally, as the beam length increases, this discrepancy diminishes, such that the results converge for relatively long beams. Therefore, for short beams, the TB must be used to derive precise solutions.

Figure 9 illustrates the curve of variations in the non-dimensional nonlinear frequency of the beam as a function of its dimensionless amplitude, using the TBMB, EBMB, TB, and EB models for the [111] crystal orientation with an elastic coefficient of k = 0.05 GN/m2. For better representation, the nonlinear frequency is presented in non-dimensional format by dividing it by . Given the dependence of the nonlinear frequency on the amplitude of motion, Fig. 5 demonstrates that, for all presented models, the nonlinear frequency increases with rising amplitude. Additionally, similar to the linear case, accounting for surface elasticity results in a higher estimation of the nonlinear frequency compared to when it is neglected. Figure 10 displays the effect of surface elasticity on the dimensionless nonlinear frequency of the TB for the [111] and [100] crystal orientations, as well as the case without surface elasticity. From Fig. 10, it can be inferred that the nonlinear frequency for all amplitude values is predicted to be higher for the [111] crystal orientation compared to both the case without surface elasticity and the [100] crystal orientation.

The standard boundary conditions influence on the dimensionless nonlinear frequency for the [111] crystal orientation with an elastic foundation coefficient of k = 0.05 GN/m2 is illustrated in Fig. 11. Based on the curves presented in Fig. 11, it can be concluded that the beam with CC boundary conditions exhibits the highest stiffness compared to CS and SS boundary conditions. Consequently, similar to its linear behavior, the nonlinear behavior also results in a higher natural frequency for the clamped beam.

Conclusion

This paper investigates the linear and nonlinear fundamental natural frequencies of a rectangular cross-section beam based on both the EB and TB models, incorporating the effects of surface energy. The linear natural frequency is derived using the eigenvalue method, while the nonlinear frequency is obtained by first extracting algebraic equations (with nonlinear frequency and motion amplitude as unknowns) via the harmonic balance method and then solving them using the Newton–Raphson method. The effects of [111] and [100] crystal orientations for aluminum (anodic alumina), different boundary conditions, two distinct beam models, and an elastic foundation coefficient on both linear and nonlinear natural frequencies are analyzed. The key findings are summarized as follows:

  1. Surface energy significantly influence the linear and nonlinear frequencies of Nano-micro beams with a high ratio of surface-to-volume.

  2. The [111] crystal orientation in aluminum beams exhibits higher stiffness than the [100] orientation, leading to greater predicted linear and nonlinear natural frequencies.

  3. As beam length increases, the effect of surface elasticity diminishes, becoming negligible for sufficiently long beams.

  4. The elastic foundation coefficient positively affects both linear and nonlinear natural frequencies, increasing system stiffness and frequency as it rises.

  5. Polynomial shape functions yield lower frequency estimates compared to trigonometric shape functions in determining the linear and nonlinear fundamental natural frequencies of Nano-micro beams.

  6. Based on consideration of rotary inertia and shear deformation, the TB results are more accurate than EB results to predict the linear and nonlinear fundamental natural frequencies for short beams.

  7. The comparative analysis reveals that the TB model provides significantly more accurate predictions than the ones from the EB model for higher vibration modes in short beams.
