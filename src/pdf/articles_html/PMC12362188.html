Abstract

BACKGROUND

Infantile hemangiomas (IHs) are the most common benign vascular tumors of infancy, although intracranial IHs remain exceedingly rare. Diagnosis is typically postnatal, with some cases requiring medical or surgical intervention. Here, the authors report the first case of a congenital cavernous sinus IH diagnosed via prenatal imaging and managed conservatively with successful spontaneous regression.

OBSERVATIONS

A 28-year-old female underwent routine fetal ultrasound at 32 weeks’ gestation, revealing a 1.8-cm perithalamic lesion. Fetal MRI at 33 weeks confirmed an extra-axial mass in the right middle fossa with a dural attachment. Postnatal MRI on day 1 of life demonstrated imaging features consistent with an IH. Given the neonate’s asymptomatic status, conservative management was pursued. Serial MRI at 10 weeks and 6 months showed progressive lesion regression, with near-complete resolution. The infant remained asymptomatic with normal neurodevelopmental progress.

LESSONS

This case highlights the potential for spontaneous regression of congenital intracranial IHs diagnosed prenatally. Despite the risk of bleeding, conservative management with close clinical and radiological follow-up may be a viable option in select asymptomatic cases, particularly in the neonatal period when surgical risks are significant.

Infantile hemangiomas (IHs) are the most common benign vascular tumors of childhood, predominantly affecting the superficial tissues of the head and neck region. Intracranial IHs are rare, with an isolated incidence of approximately 0.1%.1 Association with hemangioma syndromes has been drawn, with the neuroaxial involvement rate as high as 48%.2 Intracranial IHs typically present as extra-axial enhancing masses, often adjacent to venous sinuses, with imaging features that mimic other extra-axial tumors, such as meningiomas. On MRI, they demonstrate T1 isointensity, T2 hyperintensity relative to brain parenchyma, diffuse enhancement, and elevated perfusion on arterial spin-labeling sequences.2

Intracranial IHs are most frequently found in the cerebellopontine angle, but other locations such as the tentorium and supratentorial anterior and middle fossae with cavernous sinus involvement have been reported.1 While most hemangiomas follow a proliferative phase during the 1st year of life, followed by spontaneous involution by 5 years of age,3 management strategies range significantly across reports, from observation to pharmacological or surgical intervention, depending on clinical and radiological findings as well as the risk versus benefit assessment of each health professional team. Reported medical strategies include propranolol, prednisolone, thalidomide, and interferon;1 from a neurosurgical perspective, both decompressive surgery and resection have been attempted. Few cases of prenatally diagnosed intracranial IH have been reported, which makes their natural history unclear, but we assume that they evolve similarly to those diagnosed postnatally. If that is true, some could think that prenatally diagnosed intracranial IHs, being in the very beginning of a potential proliferative phase, warrant early intervention. To the best of our knowledge, no reported newborn case with a known prenatal diagnosis has been managed conservatively.1

We present the first reported case of a congenital cavernous sinus IH diagnosed via prenatal MRI and managed conservatively with a rapid spontaneous regression of the mass over the first 6 months of life, thus emphasizing the potential for noninterventional management in select cases.

Illustrative Case

A 28-year-old female with a stable medical history of Crohn’s disease and a nonspecific collagenosis was monitored with serial fetal ultrasound from the 24th week of gestation because of the risk of preterm labor (early contractions). Ultrasounds showed no abnormalities until the 32nd week of gestation, when a new 1.8-cm hyperechoic spherical perithalamic lesion was seen. Subsequent fetal MRI performed at the 33rd gestational week confirmed the presence of an extra-axial mass located in the right middle fossa, with a dural attachment (Fig. 1). Of note, the suspected diagnosis at that time was meningioma or infant glioma; the possibility of IH was not evoked as the images did not seem typical for it.

The patient underwent vaginal delivery at 39 weeks without complications, with APGAR scores of 9 and 10. MRI performed on day 1 of life confirmed the lesion’s location centered on the cavernous sinus and Meckel’s cave. The lesion, measuring 30 × 20 × 15 mm, was hypointense on T1, hyperintense on T2, and showed homogeneous and intense enhancement, with no restriction on diffusion and no susceptibility artifact; all these characteristics were suggestive of a benign vascular tumor. The diagnosis of IH was favored (Fig. 2). Meningioma was considered in the differential diagnosis, although it was considered very unlikely considering the age of the patient. Genetic analysis, including a panel for mutations associated with intracranial tumors, revealed no mutations, including in the NF2 gene, thus ruling out the possibility of a neurofibromatosis type 2–associated meningioma.4 Of note, on clinical examination, the child had 2 minor hemangiomas (on the neck and on the trunk).

Since the child was asymptomatic and neurologically intact and considering the nonnegligible risks of a brain surgery in the neonatal period, a conservative approach was adopted, with a watchful clinical, laboratory, and radiological follow-up because of the risk of spontaneous bleeding reported in the literature.5

A follow-up MRI at 10 weeks of age showed a significant reduction in the lesion size, which was then measuring 20 × 8 × 15 mm. Subsequent MRI at 6 months of age revealed near-complete resolution of the mass (Fig. 3). Her serial complete blood counts remained normal.

From a clinical perspective, the child remains asymptomatic with a normal neurological and developmental status. The two known hemangiomas on the neck and on the trunk have slightly increased in size at the 6-month follow-up.

Discussion

Observations

Congenital IHs are rare. To the best of our knowledge, only two reports described antenatal detection of these lesions via fetal ultrasound. Frei-Jones et al.6 reported a newborn with a prenatal ultrasound suggestive of an intracranial hemorrhage, who was diagnosed at birth with an extra-axial lesion in the middle and posterior fossae, causing right cranial nerve VII palsy. A biopsy confirmed IH, with an initial progression on imaging at 1 month of age; the child was started on thalidomide, with a significant reduction of the mass observed at 20 months of life. Cavalheiro et al.7 described a fetal ultrasound detecting a lesion in the posterior fossa, confirmed at birth on MRI as a suspected IH causing hydrocephalus, requiring ventriculoperitoneal shunt placement. This patient was treated with propranolol (3 mg/kg twice a day), with a slight tumor reduction at the 2-month mark, a 50% regression at 6 months, and complete resolution by 1 year of life. Of note, all these patients were symptomatic, and this prompted the initiation of treatment. Importantly, while tumor regression was observed following the start of treatment, a definitive causal relationship could not be clearly established in these reports, considering the likelihood of spontaneous regression, which is characteristic of these lesions.8

The most reported intracranial IH location is the cerebellopontine angle, although cases have been described in the middle cranial fossa, cavernous sinus, and Meckel’s cave, as was the case for our patient. Unlike previous cases, no biopsy was performed in our newborn due to the absence of symptoms, the bleeding risk related to a biopsy, and postnatal imaging features consistent with intracranial IH. Furthermore, the negative NF2 genetic testing made even more unlikely the possibility of a meningioma, which was in the differential diagnosis.

Lessons

Intracranial IHs are rare lesions that typically exhibit rapid proliferative growth in the 1st year of life, with 80% of their final size reached by 5 months and a peak at 9 months. This is followed by a gradual involutional phase, in which 90% of lesions regress by 5 years of age.8 However, given the potential for early growth, neurological compromise, and the risk of intracranial bleeding, clinicians may decide to initiate medical treatment even in asymptomatic cases to prevent complications. While propranolol was recognized as a potentially safe and effective therapy, the rapid spontaneous regression justified a conservative, watchful waiting strategy. This case demonstrates that congenital intracranial IHs detected prenatally could undergo rapid spontaneous regression, suggesting the possibility of a conservative approach in select asymptomatic cases.

This case highlights the potential for rapid spontaneous regression of congenital intracranial IHs diagnosed prenatally, like previously reported for postnatally diagnosed intracranial IHs. While IHs can exhibit initial rapid growth and have an inherited risk of spontaneous bleeding, our findings suggest that in asymptomatic neonates, a conservative approach with serial imaging and regular clinical and laboratory follow-up may be a valid and safe alternative. The limitations to this report are the absence of biopsy, the short follow-up time, and the limitations inherent to case reports. Further evidence is needed before establishing a standardized management strategy for these patients (observation vs medical and/or surgical treatment).
