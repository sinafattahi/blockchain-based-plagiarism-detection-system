Abstract

The attitude to self-care of individuals with type 2 diabetes (T2D) is commonly influenced by their time preferences (i.e., long-run discounting and time-consistency), and/or risk preference (i.e., risk tolerance). Tailored preference-based self-care management may improve clinical and cost-effectiveness. This study investigates associations between elicited time and risk preferences with diabetes-related outcomes, i.e., Hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c) and blood glucose level. Time and risk preferences of 396 T2D patients were measured by incentivized tasks. Clinical data was retrieved from hospital information systems for diabetes outcomes (HbA1c and blood glucose levels) and clinical factors. We assessed patient diabetes-associated self-care behaviors and other epidemiological factors by questionnaire. Multivariate linear regressions with stepwise model selection were used to evaluate potential associations with T2D outcomes. We found that participants who favored immediate gratification over greater long-term benefits (time-inconsistent towards present or present-biased) had significantly higher HbA1c levels than time-consistent participants, with a coefficient (95% CI) of 0.41 (0.02,0.80). Neither time nor risk preferences were significantly associated with blood glucose levels. These findings suggest the use of tailored diabetes interventions to help present-biased patients overcome self-control problems. This targeted approach may be more cost-effective but requires formal testing.

Introduction

Diabetes is one of the leading causes of death globally1. It is a well-recognized risk factor for cardiovascular diseases (CVD), such as coronary artery disease and stroke2, blindness3, chronic kidney disease (CKD)4, and others. The higher mortality and morbidity outcomes associated with diabetes lead to greater economic burden. The health costs associated with diabetes for individuals in low- and middle-income countries are considerable5 in both out-of-pocket and catastrophic medical spending (i.e., healthcare costs that individuals pay directly and exceed a certain percentage of a household’s income) compared to individuals without diabetes6. Type 2 diabetes (T2D) is the most common form of diabetes, accounting for almost 90% of cases, largely as a consequence of insulin resistance and/or insufficient insulin production to regulate blood glucose7. Obesity and excess weight are common risk factors for T2D8,9. Therefore, lifestyle modifications (e.g., diet and physical activity) are crucial to reduce body weight and improve diabetes-related health outcomes10.

Despite multiple systematic reviews demonstrating that diabetes self-management education and support can improve diabetes-related health outcomes by reducing associated complications11–13, significant individual variation persists in patients’ ability to sufficiently control their diabetes. This variation can be partly attributed to patient’s time preference (i.e., the relative weight given to the distant future compared to the near future, encompassing both long-run discounting and time consistency)14 and/or risk preference (i.e., the level of risk tolerance versus risk aversion)15. Regarding time preference, two key-dimensions are considered. First, long-run discounting refers to how individuals value the distant future relative to the near future. Second, time consistency refers to whether individuals apply similar weights to future decisions compared to the present, given the same waiting time horizon.

Given the potential future benefits of engaging in diabetes self-management activities contrasts with current adherence costs, decision-making related to time preferences may be influenced by individual discounting that impacts self-management activities14. Those who fully discount the future, i.e., care only for the present, or who care about the future but have self-control problems and are unable to delay immediate gratification (termed time-inconsistent towards present or present-biased), may be less likely to adhere to dietary or physical activity regimens despite the future potential benefits. In contrast, a person who is time-consistent and discounts the future less is more likely to adhere to diabetes self-management through greater sacrifice in the present to benefit from future gains16. Similarly, a person with a higher level of risk tolerance may underestimate or undervalue the harmful consequences of non-adherence to disease self-management, while one who is risk averse may be more motivated to make behavioral changes in the present15.

This insight has important policy implications in that a different set of interventions or strategies to improve adherence could be offered to T2D patients with different time and risk preferences. This may also lead to more efficient use of scarce healthcare resources. For example, a patient who is time-consistent and risk-neutral may only need an educational program, while a present-biased patient will need further complementary interventions based on behavioral insights to help promote stricter adherence to a health improvement plan, which may include some form of financial incentive16.

A systematic review of 12 studies explored associations between measures of time preference and adherence to diabetes self-care activities and diabetes outcomes14. The review found that patients with lower discount rates (also termed as ‘less patient’) were less likely to adhere to self-care activities, leading to worse outcomes than those with more patience. Five of the studies included used either ordinary measures of patience17–21, in contrast to five studies that used continuous measures of discount rates22–26. None of the studies measured time-(in)consistency or present-biasedness. Two studies measured patients’ present bias but did not estimate their discount rate27,28. These studies were conducted in the US, France, Germany, and Japan.

A single study based in Israel used hypothetical choice tasks to elicit risk preference from T2D patients, and found that risk-seeking or risk-tolerant patients were less likely to adhere to healthy eating plans, exercise, blood glucose monitoring, foot care15 and medication-taking29. Another study from Japan used hypothetical risk questions reporting that risk aversion was inversely associated with the presence of diabetic retinopathy or nephropathy25. One study reported no association between ordinally measured risk attitudes and diabetes self-care17.

In summary, there is a lack of consistent evidence of associations between risk and diabetes outcomes, and a lack of evidence of time preferences that differentiates long-run discounting and time-consistency. This study, therefore, aims to investigate associations between elicited time and risk preferences and diabetes outcomes.

Methods

This is a cross-sectional study aiming to investigate associations between elicited time and risk preferences with diabetes-related outcomes, i.e., Hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c) and blood glucose levels, in T2D patients. Figure 1 shows the research protocol, starting with the recruitment of study participants.

Sample and recruitment procedure

T2D patients, treated at the outpatient clinic department (OPD) of the Department of Medicine or Family Medicine, Ramathibodi Hospital, were invited to participate in the study from November 2022 to January 2023. Patients were eligible if they met the following inclusion criteria: Aged older than 18 years, had fasting blood glucose ≥ 126 mg/dL or hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c) ≥ 6.5%, or were using any anti-diabetic medications. Patients with T1D were excluded from the study. The research objectives, procedures, potential risks and benefits were thoroughly explained to all prospective participants, and written informed consent was obtained.

Ethics approval

was obtained from the Human Research Ethics Committee of The Faculty of Medicine Ramathibodi Hospital (COA. MURA2022/485). All methods were performed in accordance with relevant guidelines/regulations.

Elicitation of time and risk preferences

Participants completed two incentivized tasks with real monetary reward using the multiple price list method. [Data-collection instruments and procedures were pre-tested and revised until October 2022.] Task 1 evaluated individual long-run discounting (i.e., how much weight one puts on the far future compared to the near future) and time consistency (i.e., whether the same weight was put on the later time point for both future and immediate timeframes). Participants were classified as time-consistent, present-biased, or future-biased. Task 2 evaluated risk attitude (i.e., levels of risk tolerance or risk aversion).

Time preference elicitation task

Participants were invited to choose between two certain outcomes: a smaller reward at an earlier date or a larger award at a later date based on two time horizons at 1 and 2 months. For example, option A would pay 1000 Thai Baht (THB) in one month, or option B would pay 1050 THB in two months, see Table 1. [Given that the purchasing power parity (PPP) conversion factor (GDP) was 10.91 Thai Baht (THB) per 1 USD in 2022, this amount is equivalent to the purchasing power of 91.66 US Dollar–USD PPP.] This approach has been widely used previously30–34. By varying the amount of later options, the discount rate or the level of long-run discounting can be identified given the participant preference for those amounts. The starting date was varied to evaluate time consistency (i.e., either being time-consistent, present-biased, or future-biased): no front-end delay (today) and a 1-month front-end delay were considered. Therefore, there were three timeframes, i.e., today vs. 1 month, 1 month vs. 2 months, and 1 month vs. 3 months. For each timeframe, there were six decisions with 18 in total. Table 1 describes an example of a set of six decisions for one example timeframe (i.e. 1 month vs. 2 months), and Supplementary Table 1 provides the full choice lists.

Only one decision for this task was randomly selected to be actually paid out. First, long-run discounting refers to how participants valued the far future compared to the near future (from choosing either future time point). Second, time consistency refers to whether participants applied similar weights to future decisions compared to the present, given the same waiting time horizon.

By way of explanation, a participant who cares more about their future health and is time-consistent would be more likely to plan and comply with dietary and exercise advice (the first case). The second case (present-biased) is someone who procrastinates: those who care about their future health but change their mind when it comes to immediate health decisions, so-called time-inconsistent. Participants who lack sufficient care for their future health and behave unhealthily would be considered impatient and time-consistent (the third case). Finally, participants who care more about the near future than the far future or now, could be considered as impatient and future-biased.

For long-run discounting levels, we used the total number of waiting choices in choice sets 2 (1 month vs. 2 months) and 3 (1 month vs. 3 months) to represent the preferences for each participant for future timeframes, see Supplementary Table 1. The scale ranged from 0 to 12, with 0 indicating the participant did not wait for any single decision (extremely impatient), while 12 indicates the participant waited for all 12 decisions in choice sets 2 and 3 (extremely patient).

By comparing each participant’s selections for choice set 1 (today vs. 1 month) and those in choice set 2 (1 month vs. 2 months), participants can be categorized as time-consistent, present-biased, or future-biased, see Supplementary Table 1. Participants were considered time-consistent if they switched to option B (wait) for the same item in choice sets 1 and (2) Participants were considered present-biased if they switched to option B in choice set 1 later than in choice set 2, suggesting they would wait less for a later larger payoff for an immediate payment (today), compared to when the sooner payment would be in the future.

Risk preference elicitation task

Participants were asked to make ten choices over two uncertainty outcomes, Lottery A and B, commonly referred to as binary lottery choices31,32,36. Participants were asked to choose lottery A or B for each of the ten items. Lottery A provided participants with 1200 THB with probability or 1000 THB with probability today. Lottery B provided 2500 THB with probability or 100 THB with probability today. Lottery B is, therefore, a riskier option than A. This series of ten items offered prize sets with ascending probabilities on the highest prize starting from 0.1, then increasing by 0.1 until a final choice between two certain amounts of money. See Table 3 regarding all ten items for the risk preference elicitation task.

After choosing all ten items, only one item was randomly chosen to be paid off by the participant rolling a 10-sided die to determine the chosen Lottery item payoff. Finally, the participant again rolled a 10-sided die to determine the payoff (high or low). Figure 2 shows an example of the first decision. If the participant chose option A (Lottery A), there was a probability of 0.1 to receive 1200 THB when “1” from a 10-sided die was rolled, while there was a probability of 0.9 to receive 1000 THB if “2” to “10” was rolled. If the participants chose option B, he/she would receive 2500 THB when “1” was rolled, but 100 THB when “2” to “10” was rolled. Again, 10% of participants were randomly selected (by rolling a 10-sided die) to actually receive the monetary payoff from this task31,32.

For determination of individual risk tolerance levels, we used the number of riskier options (Lottery B) chosen across 10 items by each participant. A score of ten indicated the participant chose the riskier option, Lottery B, in all items, representing an extreme risk tolerance.

Preference elicitation procedure

A researcher or a trained research assistant clearly described the choice tasks for the time and risk elicitation. Participants were asked control questions to check their understanding of the tasks. Upon completing the elicitation tasks and a questionnaire, participants were compensated based on their performance in the choice tasks. These tasks took on average 33.8 (SD = 12.8) minutes. As a result of random selection (by a 10-sided die), 8.59% and 12.88% of participants received payment from the time and risk preference elicitation tasks, respectively. In addition to the earnings from the elicitation tasks, participants also received 200 THB as a compensation for their time. As a result, the median earning was 200 (IQR: 200, 200) THB.

Outcomes of interest

The primary outcome was fasting serum glycated hemoglobin (% HbA1c), and the secondary outcome was fasting serum glucose (mg/dL), both measured on the day of the interview. These data were retrieved from the hospital information system (HIS). For both tests, venous blood samples were collected after fasting at least 8 h of fasting at the hospital. HbA1c represents a long-term glucose control over approximately 3 months, while fasting serum glucose represents a one-off, immediate blood sugar level.

Covariates

Several covariates were considered following retrieval from the HIS on the interview date: body mass index (BMI), systolic and diastolic blood pressure (SBP and DBP), high-density lipoproteins (HDL), low-density lipoproteins (LDL), triglyceride, estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), glucose-lowering medications (groups, i.e., Biguanides, Sulfonylureas, Thiazolidinediones, Dipeptidyl Peptidase 4 inhibitors (DPP4i), Sodium-Glucose Cotransporter-2 Inhibitors (SGLT2i), Glucagon-like Peptide-1 receptor agonist (GLP1-RA), and insulin). If values were missing on the interview date, corresponding values from the nearest date were taken for which they were available within 1 year before or after the interview date. See Supplementary Table 4 for participant laboratory, physical examination, and medications data on the survey days and with the carried values.

Participants were interviewed to obtain demographic information, including age, sex, years of education, marital status, occupation, number of children and household members; socio-economic characteristics, including wealth, household expenditure, healthcare coverage scheme, family history of diabetes (T1D and T2D); cognitive function was measured by three Raven’s progressive matrices37,38; and health risk behaviors for alcohol consumption and smoking. Frequent alcohol consumers were defined as those who drank alcohol at least once a week, and current smokers as those who infrequently or frequently smoked cigarettes.

We also recorded diabetes self-care behaviors, i.e., dietary control (regarding amount/times of food consumed), adherence to glucose-lowering medication (regarding amount and time taken), and physical exercise. This information was captured through the following question: “How frequently do you undertake the corresponding behaviors as recommended by your healthcare provider?” on a 5-point Likert scale: never (0%), sometimes (~ 25%), about 50%, mostly (~ 75%), and always (100%). The questions were adapted from the self-care inventory-revised39. For the analysis, we considered averaged proper amount and time of diet (scale 1–5 for each domain). The same procedure was applied for medication adherence. See Supplementary Table 2 for descriptive statistics of responses to the questions.

In addition, we recorded the quarter of the year (to check seasonal effects) and the type of OPD clinic (at the Department of Medicine vs. Family Medicine), which the participant visited on the interview date as additional covariates.

Statistical analysis

Summary characteristics were described using mean and standard deviation (SD) or median and interquartile range (IQR) for continuous variables and percentages for categorical variables. For assessing the associations between elicited time and risk preferences and diabetes-related outcomes, multivariable linear regression models were constructed by regressing HbA1c and blood glucose on long-run discounting levels (0–12), time consistency type (either time-consistent, present-biased, or future-biased), and participant’s risk tolerance level (0–10) and covariates whose p-values were less than 0.2 in the univariate step or those whose coefficient was clinically significant (0.3% for HbA1c and 5 mg/dL for blood glucose). A stepwise model with backward selection was applied, and those variables with p-values less than 0.05 were retained within the final model. Results

Participant time and risk preferences

For long-run discounting levels, 35.6% of participants did not wait for either future timeframe (1 month vs. 2 months and 1 month vs. 3 months), while 17.2% waited for all 12 items. The median (IQR) long-run discounting level was 6 (0–9). For time-consistency type, 271 (68.4%) were categorized as time-consistent, 73 (18.4%) as present-biased, and 52 (13.1%) as future-biased. In addition, the median (IQR) for risk tolerance levels was 4 (1–6). For more detailed time and risk preference elicitation results, see Supplementary S3.

Participant characteristics

Participants characteristics are described in Table 4 and Supplementary Table 3. The majority of participants (64.6%) were female, with a mean age (SD) of 61 years (10.1), 73.7% were married, and attended school for an average of 12.2 years (4.6). About one-third (36.9%) of participants were retired, and 37.1% were skilled workers (civil service, company employees, business owners). Almost two-thirds (60.4%) had healthcare coverage from the civil service benefit scheme. For health-related characteristics, 37.9% of participants had a family history of diabetes, and 5.6% and 4.8%, were current smokers and frequent alcohol consumers, respectively.

Factors associated with HbA1c levels

Multivariate linear regression modelling (Table 5) indicated that present-biased compared to time-consistent preference was positively associated with HbA1c levels with a coefficient (95% CI) of 0.41 (0.02, 0.80), while long-run discounting and risk tolerance levels were not significantly associated with HbA1c level with coefficients (95% CI) of − 0.02 (− 0.05, 0.02) and − 0.02 (− 0.08, 0.03), respectively. This suggests present-biased T2D patients had poorer diabetes control compared to time-consistent patients. See univariate analysis in Supplementary S4.

Discussion

This study investigated associations between time preferences, both long-run discounting and time consistency, and risk preferences (risk tolerance level) with clinical outcomes in patients with diabetes. We found that present-biased participants had significantly higher HbA1c (by 3.6%) than time-consistent patients, indicative of poorer diabetes control. This suggests that for patients who are less able to adhere to, or procrastinate over, their prescribed treatment regimen are more likely to have higher HbA1c compared to those who more strongly adhere to their diabetes control plan, given their time-consistent predisposition. The effect of present bias observed compared to time consistence was approximately half of the effect reflected by the combined glucose-lowering medication with standardized coefficients of 0.10 and 0.21, respectively. Long-run discounting and risk tolerance levels were not significantly associated with HbA1c levels, and the magnitude of the coefficients was relatively small (0.02). Neither time nor risk preferences were significantly associated with blood glucose levels.

Association between long-run discounting and time consistency (separately) with diabetes outcomes

We considered both aspects of time preferences in our analysis, i.e. long-run discounting and time-consistency, as well as risk tolerance and other selected covariates in our analysis, which represents a more comprehensive approach than previous studies. Despite this, only time-inconsistency toward the present, i.e. present bias (vs. time-consistent), was significantly associated with HbA1c but not blood glucose levels. This may be a consequence of the fact that HbA1c is considered a more robust measure of diabetes management as it represents a cumulative measure of blood sugar control over the previous three months, while blood glucose fluctuates more dynamically and is sensitive to sugar consumption in the previous six hours40.

Our study adds to the current literature in that we could distinguish long-run discounting and time (in)consistency and show that present bias is associated with HbA1c, a robust diabetes outcome. In contrast, previous studies were unable to distinguish between long-run discounting and time consistency concluding that more patient T2D participants either had better HbA1c levels20,21,24 or no significant associations were detected17.

Only a single study from Denmark investigated T2D patients (n = 79) with similar measures and used hypothetical decision scenarios to elicit time and risk preferences27. They reported present bias was related to worse self-care behaviors and outcomes, including HbA1c (as a binary measure). Our findings, based on an incentivized time and risk preference elicitation method, support their results in a much more robust sample size (396 compared to 79), with consideration of a broader range of potential confounders, from a study based in an upper middle-income country (Thailand) compared to a developed country (Denmark), and with a different service provision and healthcare system.

No association between risk preferences and diabetes outcomes

Risk tolerance was not significantly associated with HbA1c or blood glucose levels, similar to previously reported findings in the US17 and Denmark27. Recent arguments suggest that risk preference is a complex, domain-specific concept41,42. A risk preference elicitation task without specific context, as used in our study, might not be able to capture participants’ risk preferences in the health domain. Similar risk preference elicitation tasks were reported to predict real-world decisions in closely related domains, such as financial decision-making, although not in more remote settings like employment decisions43.

Clinical application

Distinguish between long-run discounting and present bias is crucial when designing appropriate self-care-supporting programs as each requires different intervention strategies. To address present bias, interventions such as commitment devices (e.g., deposit program16, or reminders44 could be highly effective. These alternative interventions, which offer reinforcement for improved daily diabetes self-care behaviour, warrant further consideration and rigorous trialling. In contrast, developing clear strategies to improve long-run discounting on health decisions remains a significant challenge requiring more research.

Our findings raise the question of whether the determination of present bias in T2D patients could allow stratification of diabetes interventions and management programs, utilizing targeted tools (e.g., reminders, commitment devices, or incentive programs) to help present-biased patients overcome self-control problems.

Specifically, reminders are effective by bringing the required action “top of mind”44 making future health goals more salient in the present. Commitment devices empower present-biased patients to make beneficial decisions when they still value future benefits. For example, registering and paying for the gym membership or placing deposit against achieving a successful health goal leverages their future-oriented self to bind their present self to healthier actions16. Incentives might bring future rewards for healthy behaviors feel nearer or even immediate, thereby increasing their perceived value in the present16.

Conversely, time-consistent patients may not require such intensive complementary programs, which may ultimately provide opportunities for cost-savings, in contrast to a universal “one size fits all” program, particularly in resource-limited settings.

Strengths and limitations

This study has several strengths. It is the first study to investigate associations of long-run discounting, time consistency, and risk tolerance simultaneously with objective clinical outcomes, i.e., HbA1c and blood glucose, and use incentivized economic experimental methods to elicit time and risk preferences. This study considered a broad range of potential confounders and used a much larger sample size than previous studies27.

There are some limitations in our study. Firstly, we measured time and risk preferences only at a single time point in a cross-sectional study design, and therefore the regression coefficients can only be interpreted as correlations. It could be that disease severity may affect time preferences and make patients with elevated glycemia more likely to choose immediate rewards45. In addition, this cross-sectional design inherently limited our ability to definitively establish causality or the temporal relationships between variables. For instance, our finding that patients taking more glucose-lowering medications were associated with higher HbA1c levels illustrates this limitation. While medications are intended to lower HbA1c, this observed association likely reflects that patients with more difficult-to-control diabetes require intensified treatment, and despite this, may still exhibit elevated HbA1c. Longitudinal studies would be necessary to disentangle these complex relationships. Future studies might consider measuring both time and risk preferences repeatedly since they might change over time, and researchers could use longitudinal data analysis methods to reduce unobserved variables within a subject. Secondly, the context-free risk preference elicitation method might be less amenable to capturing risk preferences in a healthcare-specific context. Future studies might consider a more context-specific measurement procedure. Lastly, as none of the self-care behaviors are included in the multivariate models of HbA1c or blood glucose levels, there might be some measurement error in self-reported responses of adherence to diabetes self-care behaviors collected during the interview. Participants may wish to report better adherence than actual, which is commonly termed social desirability bias46. Furthermore, we adapted the revised self-care inventory by selecting items broadly applicable to Thai T2D patients. This approach, driven by time constraints for the survey administration and data capture, could potentially affect the psychometric properties of the measure.

Conclusion

This study is the first to investigate associations between long-run discounting, time consistency, and risk tolerance simultaneously with objective clinical outcomes such as HbA1c and blood glucose levels, and incentivized economic experimental methods to elicit time and risk preferences. We considered a broad range of potential confounding variables and a much larger sample size than previous studies27. We found that present-biased participants were more likely to have significantly higher HbA1c than time-consistent participants.

This suggests that categorization of patients according to their time preferences may be important to improve appropriate diabetes education and management programs to better target present-biased patients overcome self-control problems and adherence to their treatment regimens. In contrast, time-consistent patients may be less likely to benefit from similar programs, offering potential cost savings associated with more targeted interventions.
