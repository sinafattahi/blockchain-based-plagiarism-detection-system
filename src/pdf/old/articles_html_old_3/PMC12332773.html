Abstract

This study calculated the bond dissociation energy (BDE), solubility parameter (δ), binding energy (E binding ), and other parameters of 96 diphenylamine antioxidants using molecular simulations to verify the quantitative relationship between the structure and properties of antioxidants and obtained 288 antioxidant performance parameters to build a machine learning (ML) data set. A group-partitioning scheme consisting of 10 group descriptors and 3 molecular connectivity chi index descriptors was established. The antioxidant parameters were combined with the partitioning results to form a complete ML data set. An artificial neural network model (ANN) was used to quantize the structure–performance relationship of antioxidants. The correlation coefficient (R) between the predicted value and the true value was greater than 0.84, the mean relative error (ARE) of BDE was less than 4.53%, the ARE of δ was less than 1.21%, and the ARE of E binding was less than 14.76%. The random forest (RF) model was used to study the contribution of each descriptor to oxidation resistance and analyze the effects of different substituent locations. The results of the chemical and physical analyses showed that the introduction of alkyl chains improved the performance of the antioxidants. An alkyl chain with nine carbons in the main chain was grafted to site 4 as a new substituent, and a new molecular structure of diphenylamine antioxidants was designed. Compared with the skeleton structure, the BDE of the newly designed antioxidant decreased by 1.18%, δ decreased by 6.11%, and E binding increased by 83.44%. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the ML-assisted molecular design of antioxidants.

1. Introduction

In harsh working conditions, lubricating oil is often used to reduce friction and wear on mechanical equipment. In practical applications, lubricating oil is affected by harsh conditions such as oxygen, high temperature, high pressure, high friction, and high metal concentration and is prone to oxidation, resulting in performance failure. Antioxidants are added to lubricants to trap free radicals formed during the preoxidation stage, preventing further reactions and breaking down the peroxides that have been formed. This can effectively delay the oxidation process of lubricating oil, reduce the generation of degradation products, and extend the service life of lubricating oil.

The oxidation of lubricating oil follows the free radical chain reaction mechanism, and atoms, molecules, or ions with unpaired valence electrons are defined as free radicals. Antioxidants are key additives used to protect lubricating oil from oxidative degradation and meet the requirements of industrial applications. It is essential to delay the thermal oxidation aging process of materials and extend the service life. Only a small amount of antioxidants can delay or inhibit the oxidation of materials, which is used in rubber, plastics, lubricating oil, etc., and can improve the thermal oxygen stability of materials. − According to the antioxidant mechanism, antioxidants are divided into main antioxidants and auxiliary antioxidants. Antioxidants that can eliminate free radicals include aromatic amines, phenols, and their derivatives, known as main antioxidants and free radical scavengers. Among them, phenylenediamine antioxidants are often used in mineral oil and rubber products and produce little pollution to the environment. Chao et al. prepared an oil-soluble alkylated diphenylamine antioxidant through the alkylation reaction of diphenylamine. The effect of this antioxidant on the thermal oxidation stability of poly-α-olefin was evaluated by thermogravimetric analysis. For comparison, the thermal oxidation stability and activation energy of poly-α-olefin and zinc dialkyl dithiophosphate were also studied. The results showed that alkylated diphenylamine had better ability than zinc dialkyl dithiophosphate to increase the activation energy, oxidation stability, and oxidation resistance of poly-α-olefin.

Studies on the antioxidant properties of traditional antioxidants mainly rely on experiments, but experimental methods have certain limitations and costs, and there is no quantitative theoretical database that supports the design of new antioxidant molecules. Currently, computational simulation has emerged as the third major method in material research alongside experimental and theoretical science, effectively reducing the process of trial and error. With the convergence of computer technology, physics, and materials science, various simulation methods have been proposed to deeply explore the relationship between molecular structures and properties, including quantum mechanics (QM) and molecular dynamics (MD) techniques. These methods can help researchers bypass complex experimental processes and conduct targeted studies. Although the simulation method can provide new ideas for experiments and save time for trial and error exploration, it is difficult to meet the need of rapid screening of massive data. There are many factors affecting the antioxidant performance of antioxidants, which is difficult to analyze by molecular simulation alone. Machine learning (ML) is a powerful tool to build quantitative relationships between complex data, and the combination of molecular simulation and ML can be used to quantify the impact of different groups on antioxidant performance. Complex and time-consuming calculations in molecular simulations are particularly suitable for ML, and combining ML with molecular simulations can improve computational efficiency, predictive power, and universality. The combination of the two methods offers the possibility of greatly expanding the predictive power. Li et al. developed the deep potential of the molten mixture of LaF3 and Flibe, conducted MD simulations, systematically studied the quantitative relationship between various properties and the concentration of LaF3 using ML methods. The transferability of the deep potential of different formulations on molten Flibe + LaF3 and the predictability of structures and properties are achieved on nanospatial scales and nanosecond time scales.

In computational chemistry, the group contribution method (GCM) can help researchers explore the chemical space of effective antioxidants by splicing groups or structural fragments. GCM can divide a compound into multiple groups (or functional groups, fragments, descriptors, etc.), − which can be recombined into different compounds. The presupposition of GCM is that the properties of a compound can be synthesized by the sum of the contributions of its functional groups to the total properties. GCM mainly uses the types of functional groups in the chemical structure that make up the substance and the frequency of the occurrence of the functional groups to calculate the properties. For example, Mora Osorio et al. proposed a GCM method for calculating the free energy of polypeptide chains. The method uses the sum of the Gibbs free energies of each amino acid, adjusted according to its presence or absence in the chain and its hydrophobic properties. Finally, the equations of 21 peptides were simulated with MD in a dodecane–water rectangular box, where the ARE was 16%. The main advantage of this approach is that all information needed to use by the model is available in the chemical structure.

In artificial intelligence, goal-oriented predictions or decisions are performed by using ML methods. There are many ML methods, such as the artificial neural network (ANN), decision tree (DT), random forest (RF), linear regression (IR), support vector machine (SVM), and K-nearest neighbor. In research, an appropriate ML model should be selected to analyze the relationship between the structure and properties of materials. ML modeling is a more efficient way to explore the broader field of materials chemistry. Trained and evaluated ML models can be used to predict the properties of materials or design molecular structures. Currently, there are very few studies on additives in lubricating oil using ML methods, and most of them are studies on lubricating oil properties (viscosity, tribological properties, etc.). For example, Hariharan et al. developed an ANN model to accurately predict the viscosity changes of nanobiotic lubricants using a multilayer perceptron algorithm. The accuracy of the predicted value is confirmed using experimental studies at the considered concentration of nano silica. Li et al. designed an ML-based surface texture design method for sliding friction pairs by comparing five ML models and selecting ANN and gradient lifting DT as the optimal forward and reverse models, respectively. Results show that the viscosity and friction coefficient of lubricating oil significantly affect the design of texture size, depth, and coverage area.

Barea-Sepúlveda et al. developed an ML model based on partial least-squares regression, SVR, and RF regression to characterize the mixing percentage of two petroleum waxes (paraffin and microcrystalline wax) extracted from lubricants. In addition, strategies for packaging variable selection methods such as the Boruta algorithm and genetic algorithms are used to evaluate whether fewer predictor variables can improve model performance.

Few combine molecular simulations with ML methods to evaluate the combined activity of diphenylamine antioxidants based on GCM. Unlike traditional trial-and-error methods, this study aims to quantify the relationship between the antioxidant structure and its performance using a GCM-based ML method. For this purpose, the models of trimethylol propane trioleate (TMPTO) and 96 diphenylamine derivatives were established. The data set required for ML was constructed using QM and MD simulations. An ANN model was used to determine the quantitative relationship between the antioxidant properties and antioxidant structure. An RF model was used to analyze the contribution of different groups and different substituent sites present in diphenylamine antioxidants to oxidation resistance. A research system based on atomic and molecular levels was established to provide a new method for designing high-performance diphenylamine antioxidants.

2.2. Group Division Scheme

According to the group division scheme proposed using Marrero et al. using GCM, the structures of the 96 diphenylamine derivatives in this study were divided. The group division scheme shown in Table contained 13 descriptors and was used as the input data set of ML. Among them, 1–10 were group structure descriptors, including hydroxyl (−OH) and different types of alkyl groups (−CH3, – CH2–, and > C<). The valence molecular connectivity chi index (valence molecular connectivity chi index for path order 2–4, Chiv2, Chiv3, and Chiv4) was introduced to distinguish isomers. It solved the weak complexity caused by several group descriptors, and as a kind of molecular topological index, it was a two-dimensional descriptor that could describe antioxidant properties. The first group descriptor in the table retained the skeleton structure without further division and completely retained the structural characteristics of the molecule. All chi indices for this study were obtained using the web version of the ChemDes software (http://www.scbdd.com/rdk_desc/index/).

2.2.1. Rational Analysis of the Group Division Scheme

In the group division scheme shown in Table , 96 diphenylamine derivatives were divided, their respective chi descriptors were calculated, and correlation analysis was conducted to prove the rationality of the scheme. A heat map is a graphical tool that visually illustrates the correlation between different variables through the change in color gradient and intensity, and the descriptor with the highest correlation can be found by classification. , Figure shows a heat map of the 13 descriptors in Table . Among them, the correlation between the descriptors shown in light colors (white, light orange, and light red) is strong, and the dark part indicates that the correlation is low, indicating that the inclusion relationship between each group is weak and it is not necessary to further subdivide the group. It also prevents error caused by the repeated calculation of the same group in the calculation process of the ANN model. Through the analysis of R shown in Figure , it was found that the absolute value of 81% of R was lower than 0.5, which further indicates that the correlation between variables is generally not high, and confirms the rationality of this division scheme.

2.3. Construction of Machine Learning Data Sets

Bond dissociation energy (BDE) was introduced to predict the ability of antioxidants to remove free radicals. Diphenylamine antioxidants prevent further oxidation of peroxy radicals by providing active hydrogen atoms. Therefore, BDE is essential for the description of antioxidant activity. The BDE value can be obtained from eq (taking antioxidant RH as an example). Lower BDE values indicate higher antioxidant activity.

ΔGT=[E(R·)+Gcorr(R·)T]+[E(H·)+Gcorr(H·)T]−[E(RH·)+Gcorr(RH·)T] 1

where ΔG T is the free energy of the dissociation reaction at T. Here, E(R·), E(H·), and E(RH) denote the energies of R·, H·, and RH at 0 K, respectively. Gcorr(R·)T is the thermodynamic correction of the corresponding structure at a certain temperature (T), are Gcorr(R‐H)T , Gcorr(R·)T , and Gcorr(H·)T are the free energies of the compound RH and two free radicals R· and H· at a specific temperature (298 K).

Compatibility and interaction between antioxidants and substrates also play an essential role, and incompatibility and a weak interaction lead to antioxidant migration, which is not conducive to sustaining antioxidants. Thus, two other factors, namely, the solubility coefficient (δ) and the binding energy (E binding ), were selected to evaluate the interaction between the two. The key to forming a stable dispersion is the compatibility of diphenylamine derivatives and TMPTO. The intermolecular cohesion energy intensity was characterized by δ. If their Δδ value is less than 1.0 (cal·cm–3)0.5, they are compatible. If Δδ is between 1.0 (cal·cm–3)0.5 and 3.4 (cal·cm–3)0.5, then they are partially compatible. If their Δδ is greater than 3.4 (cal·cm–3)0.5, they are incompatible. − δ is defined as the square root of the cohesive energy density (CED) using eq :

δ=CED=EcohVm=ΔHvap−RTVm 2

where CED denotes cohesion energy density, Ecoh is the cohesive energy of antioxidants or the TMPTO model, and V m is the molar volume. ΔH vap , R, and T denote the heat of vaporization, ideal gas constant, and absolute temperature, respectively.

The migration and solubility of antioxidants in lubricating oil are affected by their interactions with base oil molecules. The negative value of the interaction energy (E inter ) is defined as the binding energy, which can reflect the mixing ability and compatibility between the components. The larger the E binding value, the stronger the interface interaction. If E binding is positive, then the compatibility between the two components is good. To predict the interaction between diphenylamine derivatives and TMPTO molecules, E binding was calculated using eq :

Ebinding=−Einter=Etotal−(Eantioxidant+ETMPTO) 3

where E total is the total energy of the system and E antioxidant and E TMPTO are the energies of antioxidants and TMPTO, respectively.

A total of 96 diphenylamine derivative models were constructed using the MD method. The δ and E binding values of the 96 diphenylamine derivatives were simulated. TMPTO, a lubricating oil with good biodegradability, , tribological properties, and low toxicity, was selected as the base oil, and its molecular structure is shown in Figure . The mixing system of TMPTO and antioxidants was also the simulation object for forming the output data set. QM simulations are based on density functional theory, which states that all ground-state properties of a multiparticle system are a function of its charge density. The BDE of the dissociation reaction of active hydrogen atoms in antioxidants can be calculated by using QM simulation to reflect its antioxidant activity. All QM simulations were performed using the DMol3 module of Materials Studio (MS). MD simulation was performed using the Forcite module of MS to calculate δ and E binding . More details of the QM and MD simulations can be found in the Supporting Information.

The ML data set is an input composed of the descriptors shown in Table . Using QM simulation, 96 diphenylamine derivative models were constructed to calculate their corresponding BDE values, and MD simulation was used to calculate their corresponding δ and E binding to form output data (288 data).

2.4. ML Method

As shown in Figure , to quantify the relationship between molecular structure and performance in 288 antioxidant performance parameters, group partitioning results and three performance parameters were used as ML data sets in this study. The relationship between group descriptors and chi descriptors and antioxidant performance parameters was investigated by using an ANN model. The contribution of each descriptor and substitution site to oxidation resistance was analyzed in detail using an RF model.

Data preprocessing has a significant influence on prediction accuracy. Before establishing the ANN model, the min–max normalization method is used to process the data, which can achieve a minimum error result and avoid abnormal data. Min–max data normalization converts the input parameter value J into a new value J i . On the basis of the minimum and maximum values of this specific parameter, the calculation (eq ) is shown as follows:

Ji=J−JminJmax−Jmin 4

where J i , J, J min, and J max are normalized values, J i is the final value after normalization processing, J is the value of the original input descriptor and output parameter, J min is the minimum value in the data set, and J max is the maximum value in the data set. The range of the original data after normalization becomes [0, 1].

As shown in Figure , this study used a four-layer feedforward ANN model (one input layer, two hidden layers, and one output layer), and the neurons of this model are represented by circles of various colors in Figure . Information was transmitted in the order of input layer, hidden layer, and output layer. In this study, the input layer (the leftmost blue layer in Figure ) represented 10 group descriptors and 3 chi descriptors. The input value of each neuron was equal to the number of groups present in the antioxidant or its molecular connection chi index, which was used to represent structural information. The output layer (yellow layer on the far right of Figure ) represented the three performance parameters of the antioxidant. To determine the optimal number of neurons in the hidden layer (the middle green layer in Figure ), it was necessary to compare and analyze the prediction errors of ANN models with different numbers of hidden neurons.

The optimization algorithm adopted the Levenberg–Marquardt method. The learning rate was set to 0.001. In the ANN optimization process, the mean square error (MSE) was used for evaluation and was the square of the prediction error. The lower the value of MSE was, the fewer was the number of misclassifications, as shown in eq :

MSE=1n∑i=1n(di−yi)2 5

where n is the number of samples, d i is the predicted value of ANN, and y i is the target value of ANN.

In the ANN model, the ML data set was randomly divided into training, validation, and test sets. The training set was used to determine the basic structural parameters of the ANN, the validation set was used to view the training performance of the model, and the test set was used to evaluate the prediction accuracy of the model.

After the training is completed, the average relative error (ARE) should be calculated. The results of ARE can be used to evaluate the prediction accuracy of the model. The calculation (eq ) is as follows:

ARE=100n∑i=1n|di−yiyi| 6

where d i is the predicted value of ANN and y i is the target value of ANN. The smaller the gap between the two is, the closer ARE is to 0, which means higher prediction accuracy. In this study, the training, verification, and testing processes of the ANN model were all completed on MATLAB R2021a software.

To further understand the contribution of various descriptors and different graft sites to antioxidant performance, RF models were constructed in this study when antioxidant performance parameters were analyzed. By assessing the importance of each input variable, the extent to which various descriptors and substitutions contribute to the antioxidant capacity was clarified. In this study, Python’s Scikit-Learn toolkit was used to build RF, which created a bifurcated DT. Figure shows the structure of the RF model for the regression problem. In this study, models named BDE-RF, δ-RF, E binding-RF, and All-RF were established to analyze the contribution of each descriptor to the antioxidant performance parameters of BDE, δ, E binding , and ALL, i.e., the output contains both BDE, δ, and E binding . In addition, the number of decision trees (1–100) in the RF model was optimized using 5× cross-validation, and model construction was based on the optimized data.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Molecular Simulation Results

3.1.1. BDE Analysis of Antioxidants

The BDE of −NH– in 96 diphenylamine derivatives was obtained using a QM simulation. The smaller the BDE was, the easier it was for the hydrogen atoms to dissociate, leading to higher antioxidant properties. Figure shows the BDE of the antioxidants formed by 19 groups at 5 different substitution positions in the skeleton structure. The green dots and lines in Figure represent the baseline BDE value of the skeleton structure (216.15 kJ·mol–1). In other words, the calculated BDE below this reference value indicates that the dissociation capacity of the hydrogen atom is enhanced. The value below the horizontal plane is the expected value in the BDE analysis.

In Figure , if the BDE value is lower than the baseline point, line, and plane, the antioxidant activity has been improved. According to the statistical results, the following conclusions can be drawn: the introduction of electron-donating groups, such as the amine group (Table , no. 4), hydroxyl group (Table , no. 5), and alkyl group (Table , nos. 2, 3 and 12–15), into the skeleton structure decreases the BDE value. By contrast, the introduction of electron-withdrawing groups, such as alkynes (Table , no. 18), is not conducive to reducing the BDE value. Therefore, in the design of antioxidant molecules, if the antioxidant performance needs to be improved chemically, it is necessary to introduce electron-donating groups.

3.1.2. Solubility Parameters of Antioxidants

When the δ value of the antioxidant is closer to the δ value of the matrix, the compatibility is better. The δ values of 96 diphenylamine derivatives were calculated using an MD simulation method. Figure shows the δ values corresponding to the diphenylamine derivative, and the horizontal plane connected by the green dots and lines in the wall diagram indicates the baseline δ value (9.66 (J·cm–3)0.5).

As shown in Figure , when the δ value of the diphenylamine antioxidant is lower than the baseline δ value, its lipophilicity is enhanced. The addition of weakly polar or nonpolar groups, such as alkyl groups with long carbon chains (Table , nos. 12–16), to the skeleton structure can reduce the δ value of the grafted skeleton structure and can narrow the gap between the δTMPTO of the lubricating oil and the antioxidant (8.41 (J·cm–3)0.5). Moreover, when the −C­(CH3)2CH2C­(CH3)3 group was introduced into the skeleton structure, the δ values of the corresponding derivatives at five substitution sites were less than 8.85 (J·cm–3)0.5, which decreased by 8.90%–11.28% compared with the base value. The introduction of functional groups with strong polarity, such as −OH, −NH2, and −COOH, will enhance the polarity and CED value of the antioxidant, resulting in the increase in δ value and the deterioration of oil solubility. For example, after the introduction of the −COOH group, the δ value is increased by 7.35%–12.01%. Therefore, to enhance antioxidant properties using physical factors, the introduction of low-polarity or nonpolar groups, especially alkyl groups with a certain chain length and branched chain structure, should be considered the best choice to improve the compatibility between antioxidants and TMPTO.

3.1.3. Binding Energy of Antioxidants

E binding between antioxidants and TMPTO was calculated using the MD method. Figure shows the statistical results of E binding for diphenylamine derivatives, in which the horizontal plane connected by the green dots and lines in Figure is the baseline E binding value (227.44 kcal·mol–1) between the skeleton structure and the lubricating oil molecules.

As shown in Figure , if the E binding value is in the baseline plane, the interaction between the antioxidant and TMPTO is stronger and alkyl substituents with longer carbon chains can improve the E binding value of the skeleton structure. For example, after grafting −(CH2)8CH3 (Table , no. 14) substituents, the maximum E binding value of the skeleton structure increased by 97.83%, and when introducing −(CH2)13CH3 (Table , no. 15) substituents, the maximum E binding value increased by 86.2%. Therefore, the introduction of alkyl chains with appropriate lengths or substituents with larger molecular weights will increase the E binding value, which will help in reducing the migration rate of antioxidants and stabilizing the dispersion of antioxidants in TMPTO.

3.2.3. Predictive Effect of ANN

The corresponding ANN model was established, and the predicted value and the real value were fitted. Correlation coefficient (R) is a numerical measure of correlation, that is, the linear relationship between the actual output value and the predicted output value. R shows the variability of the data with respect to its mean. A perfect fit indicates that the slope is close to 1, and a slope equal to 0 indicates a random relationship between the actual output and the target output.

The R value is used to evaluate the fitting effect and can represent the prediction accuracy of the ANN model, which can be calculated using eq :

R=∑i=1n(yit−yit®)(yir−yir®)∑i=1n(yit−yit®)2∑i=1n(yir−yir®)2 9

where yit® and yir® are the target and predicted antioxidant performance parameter values, respectively, and yit® and yir® are the corresponding average values.

As shown in Figure , after the statistical fitting analysis of the data in each data set, the R of the data set was above 0.84. As shown in Figure d, the R of the ALL data set was 0.88, which indicates that the fitting effect of the ANN model was good and the results were accurate.

3.2.4. Predictive Effect of RF

As mentioned above, ANN models can accurately predict oxidation resistance parameters, but it is still challenging to analyze the degree of group contribution to oxidation resistance. To explore the contribution of individual descriptors and substituents to antioxidant performance, an RF model was developed to obtain the order of importance of each descriptor.

The number of decision trees was determined using the GridSearchCV method, and the results showed that there were 40, 10, 10, and 20 decision trees in BDE-RF, δ-RF, E binding -RF, and ALL-RF models, respectively. The degree of influence of 13 descriptors on the oxidation resistance is shown in Figure .

In Figure , horizontal numbers 1–13 represent the various descriptors listed in Table . As shown in the results, the chi index (located at 11–13) has a significant contribution to the different antioxidant performance parameters. In particular, the total contribution value of all chi indices (11–13) in the BDE reached 0.87 (Figure a), showing a very high impact. It was 0.52 in δ (Figure b) and 0.86 in E binding (Figure c). In the assessment of the overall antioxidant performance (ALL), the contribution of the chi index was 0.58 (Figure d).

These data highlight the importance of chi descriptors for antioxidant performance evaluation with chi descriptors outweighing group structure descriptors. This further emphasizes that the type of group and its connection mode have an essential influence on antioxidant performance. The specific effects of the 10 group descriptors and 5 substituents on antioxidant performance are analyzed in the following sections to provide more insights for understanding and improving antioxidant design.

3.2.5. Analysis of Influence Degree of Group Descriptors in RF

To investigate in more detail the effect of group descriptors (nos. 1–10 in Table ) on antioxidant performance, chi descriptors were removed from the data set, and an additional RF model was built. The contribution levels of the remaining 10 descriptors were extracted in the same manner as before. The results are shown in Figure .

In BDE-RF (Figure a), the degree of influence of – CH2– (no. 7) was 0.63, accounting for the largest proportion in the 10 groups and indicating that the introduction of −CH2– could reduce the BDE value. The alkyl chain structure (nos. 3 and 7) had a degree of influence of up to 1. As shown in the calculation results, the introduction of the alkyl chain was the most effective method for reducing the BDE value, but when the alkyl chain was very long (see Table , no. 16), the BDE value increased. In the δ-RF model (Figure b), the alkyl chain structure (nos. 3, 7, and 9) had a degree of influence of 0.80, indicating that the introduction of the alkyl chain structure was the key to changing δ. In the E binding-RF model (Figure c), the degree of influence of the alkyl chain (nos. 3, 7, and 9) was 0.85, indicating that E binding was mainly affected by the alkyl chain. Similarly, in the ALL-RF model (Figure d), the degree of influence of the alkyl chain was as high as 0.99, indicating that the alkyl chain was the key affecting the antioxidant performance. ,

As shown in Figure , the contribution degrees of nos. 3 and 7, which were also alkyl groups, were different, indicating that the contribution degrees of alkyl chains with different chain lengths and configurations were also different. In BDE-RF, the influence degree of −CH2– (no. 7) was 0.63, which was higher than that of −CH3 (no. 3), which was 0.37. Combined with the specific simulation data, it showed that extending the carbon chain of the substituent with an appropriate length reduced the value of BDE. In δ-RF, the effect of the −CH3 group on δ was as high as 0.68 and the effect of >C< on δ was 0.02, indicating that branched alkyl chains had an obvious effect on the reduction of δ of antioxidants. In addition, the effect of −CH2– on δ was 0.10, indicating that increasing the length of the alkyl chain could reduce δ, but the effect was not as good as that of branched alkyl chains. In E binding-RF, the contribution degree of −CH2– was 0.83 and the contribution degrees of −CH3 and >C< to E binding were 0.02 and 0.01, respectively, indicating that extending the alkyl chain length was conducive to increasing the E binding value. This further emphasizes that increasing the alkyl chain length is more effective in increasing E binding than increasing branching or introducing an olefin structure. These results indicate that extending alkyl chain length and increasing chain branching degree are two key strategies for optimizing δ and E binding , respectively, in the design of novel antioxidants, thereby improving their overall antioxidant properties. According to the above analysis, alkyl is very effective in reducing BDE (i.e., chemical factors), reducing the δ value and increasing E binding (i.e., physical factors).

3.2.6. Analysis of the Influence Degree of the Substituent Position in RF

Figure shows the effect of the graft sites on the oxidation resistance. Unlike previous analysis methods, the docking sites were labeled directly in the data set. For example, when a group is grafted at position 2, the input vector will be represented as (0, 1, 0, 0, 0). Similar to the RF model building process, the optimal numbers of decision trees in the BDE-RF, δ-RF, E binding -RF, and ALL-RF models were 10, 10, 20, and 10, respectively.

In diphenylamine and its derivatives, the degree of influence of each substitution site is as follows: for BDE, the degrees of influence of positions 1, 4, and 5 were 0.84 (Figure a); for δ, those for positions 2, 3, and 4 are 0.78 (Figure b); for E binding , those for positions 1, 4, and 5 were 0.75 (Figure c); and for ALL, those for positions 2, 4, and 5 were 0.75 (Figure d).

These results indicate which potential substituent sites are beneficial for improving the antioxidant performance. For example, in terms of chemistry, substituent sites 1, 4, and 5 are effective sites to enhance the ability of antioxidants to trap free radicals. For physical factors, such as improving the compatibility of the antioxidant with the substrate, priority should be given to position 4. In short, researchers should select the most suitable replacement sites according to their specific needs and established goals.

3.2.7. Molecular Validation of RF

As for the physical factors affecting antioxidant properties, the effect of alkyl groups on them seems to be complex. For example, in BDE-RF, an appropriate extension of an alkyl chain can reduce the contribution of BDE, −CH3 and −CH2– to 0.37 and 0.63, respectively. In δ-RF, both branched alkyl and extended the substituent backbone are beneficial for reducing the contribution of δ, −CH3 and −CH2– to 0.68 and 0.10. In E binding-RF, the contribution of −CH2– was 0.83, indicating that E binding increased with extension of the substituent backbone. Conversely, according to the results shown in Figure d, the best modification site for improving overall antioxidant performance was site 4.

Based on the above results, alkyl substituents of different configurations were selected from the data set to verify the RF results. Figure shows the selected alkyl structure (Figure a–d) and grafting site 4 (Figure e). The above substituents were grafted at site 4 of the skeleton structure to construct antioxidants. For the convenience of analysis, the substituents in Figure were denoted as C l:m:n , where C denotes carbon, l is the total number of C atoms of alkyl substituents, m is the number of C atoms on the main chain, and n is the number of −CH3 in the substituents.

These findings suggest that increasing the length of linear alkyl substituents helps in improving the physical protective properties of diphenylamine antioxidants, thereby enhancing their antioxidant capacity.

4. Conclusions

The antioxidant parameters and the partitioning results constitute a complete ML data set. Through the comprehensive analysis of the ANN model and RF model, it was proven that there was a quantitative relationship between the structure and properties of antioxidants.

The ANN model was used to quantify the structure-performance relationship. The R of the predicted value and the true value was greater than 0.84, the ARE of BDE was less than 4.53%, the ARE of δ was less than 1.21%, and the ARE of E binding was less than 14.76%. The RF model was used to study the contribution of each descriptor to oxidation resistance and to analyze the effects of different substituent locations. The results of the chemical and physical analyses showed that the introduction of the alkyl chain improved the performance of the antioxidant. Grafting at substitution sites 1, 4, and 5 was beneficial to the reduction of BDE value. Grafting at substitution site 4 was beneficial for improving the antioxidant activity from physical factors.

In summary, an alkyl chain with nine carbons in the main chain was grafted to site 4 as a new substituent, and a new molecular structure of a diphenylamine antioxidant was designed. Compared with the skeleton structure, the BDE of this antioxidant decreased by 1.18%, δ decreased by 6.11%, and E binding increased by 83.44%. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the ML-assisted molecular design of antioxidants. Molecular simulations and ML methods provide a new approach to understanding the structure–performance relationship of antioxidants. This will help us explore the chemical space of antioxidants and screen for molecular structures with improved antioxidants. In the future, the structure–activity relationship of different types of antioxidants can be studied, optimizing and improving the machine learning algorithm and developing an efficient predictive model for antioxidant activity.
