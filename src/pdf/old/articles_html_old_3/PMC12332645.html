Abstract

Polymer coated fertilizers (PCFs) play a crucial role in the sustainable development of precision agriculture. However, conventional petroleum-based coating materials for PCFs are non-degradable and depend on non-renewable resources. Thus, biobased coatings, particularly those derived from vegetable oils, are promising alternatives. This review presents an innovative classification of vegetable oils based on their chemical structure, specifically whether they contain natural hydroxyl groups or require hydroxylation modification. By leveraging data from the Web of Science database, we systematically analyze the research progress of vegetable oil-based polyurethane coated urea from 2005 to 2024. Hydroxyl modification strategies for vegetable oils lacking natural hydroxyl groups are explored. Preparation processes and controlled-release properties of polyurethane coated urea made from both types of vegetable oils are included. Furthermore, aiming at potential coating defects, various modification strategies and their practical effects are evaluated in detail. These insights provide a systematic reference for raw material selection and formulation optimization of vegetable oil-based coated urea. While summarizing the key research achievements, this review comprehensively describes the future challenges faced by the practical application of vegetable oil-based polyurethanes in this field, and proposes potential breakthrough paths based on material innovation and process optimization.

1. Introduction

To meet the global food demand, a substantial amount of fertilizer is used in agricultural production to achieve high crop yields. However, most conventional fertilizers, especially nitrogen fertilizers, suffer from low nutrient utilization, , and their overuse and inappropriate application lead to soil degradation, greenhouse gas emission, and other environmental pollution, which are detrimental to the development of low-carbon and green agriculture. , Coated controlled release fertilizers can adjust the release pattern and timing of nutrients to match the nutrient demand of plants throughout their growth period, thereby improving nutrient utilization and preventing resource waste and environmental pollution. ,

Polymer coated fertilizers (PCFs) have garnered much attention due to their excellent controlled release performances. Polyurethane, the predominant material for PCF production in recent years, is typically derived from non-renewable petroleum resources, leading to resource scarcity and high cost. , In addition, the residual coatings after fertilizer application are difficult to degrade in the soil. More relevantly, their accumulation may pose potential environmental risks.

Given the problems mentioned above, many researchers have focused on the use of renewable resources for the preparation of bio-based coating materials, such as starch, , lignin, agricultural waste, cellulose, , and vegetable oil-based materials. Generally, these materials or their hydroxylated derivatives are used as coatings through curing alone or in combination with chemicals, and modifications are necessary to optimize their performance. Compared with other biomass, the hydroxylation of vegetable oils has a high conversion rate with few by-products, , and the nutrient release pattern and timing of the resulting coating materials can be adjusted using different modification methods. Currently, vegetable oil-based polyurethane is the most promising polymer coating material, and its coating system with adjustable performance is expected to achieve precise nutrient release.

The main component of vegetable oils is triglycerides, and their primary difference lies in the type and content of fatty acids. Specific information about common vegetable oils is provided in Table . , Vegetable oils are categorized into two types based on the presence of natural hydroxyl groups or not. Highly reactive sites, including double bonds and ester bonds, exist in triglycerides, and with the aid of these reactive sites, vegetable oils without natural hydroxyl groups can be converted into polyols through hydroxylation. , Specifically, the main component of castor oil is ricinoleic acid, which accounts for up to 87%, serving as the primary source of natural hydroxyl groups.

Previous reports have systematically reviewed the research progress and practical application of PCFs from different perspectives. Lawrencia et al. analyzed key factors affecting controlled release, release mechanisms, and mathematical models for predicting nutrient release. Similarly, Naz et al. addressed coating material types and controlled release mechanisms. Fertahi et al. provided a comprehensive review of recent advances in biopolymers and their derivatives, release behavior-governing parameters, and impacts on soil and crop growth. Lu et al. comprehensively described crude plant- and animal-based oils, polysaccharides, lignin, and other bio-based coating materials and discussed the technical means to enhance controlled release performances. Cidreira et al. focused on bio-based and smart coatings for coated nitrogen fertilizers, summarizing their controlled release performance in water and soil, water retention capacity, and biodegradability.

A systematic literature search was conducted using the keywords “polyurethane,” “oil,” “fertilizer,” and “release” in the Web of Science database, covering the publications from 2005 to 2024. The annual change trend curve is shown in Figure , and a consistent increase in the research on vegetable oil-based polyurethane coated fertilizers is revealed, with a particularly sharp rise by 2023, approximately a 10-fold growth compared to earlier years. Notably, this surge coincides with a growing contributions from Chinese researchers. Chinese researchers have successfully synthesized fully bio-based polyurethane coated fertilizers using vegetable oils and bio-based isocyanates, and systematically studied the correlations between the composition of vegetable oils and the controlled-release performance. These achievements have not only advanced the development of sustainable materials but also laid a solid foundation for the future application of vegetable oil-based polyurethanes in the agricultural sector. This upward trend also indicates that vegetable oil-based polyurethane coated fertilizers are becoming the research focus in the field of agricultural inputs and have broad prospects for innovation and development in the future.

Unlike most existing review literature, this review is the first to systematically outline the current application status and development prospects of PCFs derived from two types of vegetable oils, based on a binary classification framework of “with or without natural hydroxyl groups.” Specifically, the hydroxylation modification methods of vegetable oils without hydroxyl groups are summarized, the relationships between formulation composition and modification techniques of the coating materials are analyzed and evaluated, and an outlook on the future development trends and potential challenges are provided.

2. Polyurethane Coated Urea from Castor Oil with Natural Hydroxyl Groups

2.1. Pure Castor Oil-Based

Castor oil (CO) with 87% ricinoleic acid has a hydroxyl value of about 160 mg KOH/g and can be directly cured with isocyanate curing agent to form CO-based polyurethane. The resulting polyurethane has been widely used in coatings, foams, and agricultural materials. The earliest attempts to apply CO in the field of PCFs were made by Bortoletto-Santos et al. They synthesized a bio-based coating layer on the surface of urea with a 3:2 mass ratio of CO to 4.4′-diphenylmethane diisocyanate (MDI). Nitrogen release periods of coated urea were 14 days in soil when the coating rate was 2%, and 40 days in both soil and water when the coating rate was 5%. , Liang et al. regulated microstructures and physicochemical properties of CO-based coatings by selecting different isocyanates (polyaryl polymethylene isocyanate PAPI, isophorone diisocyanate IPDI, and toluene diisocyanate TDI) and cyano-hydroxyl ratios. PAPI was the most suitable for the curing of CO, and a large cyano-hydroxyl ratio was conducive to the increase of crosslinking density to obtain excellent controlled release performance. Even at the same coating rate, CO-based polyurethane coated urea with a wide range of nitrogen release periods (12–81 days) was obtained. To reduce the use of petroleum-based resources, Dong et al. used lysine diisocyanate (LDI) to replace a part of PAPI to react with castor oil and butanediol (BDO) for coated urea. The bio-based content reached 77% when the substitution ratio was 1:1, and the release period reached 60 days at a 3% coating rate.

Although CO naturally contains hydroxyl groups, researchers attempted to change its hydroxyl value by esterification, transesterification, and other methods to adjust the coating’s performance or cost. Feng et al. synthesized polysulfide CO-based polyols (PSOPs) with the hydroxyl value of 121 mg KOH/g by ester exchange with methanol using KOH as a catalyst and the addition of sulfur. The release period reached 60 days at 7% coating rate. Tang et al. obtained CO-based polyol (COP) with the hydroxyl value of 247.45 mg KOH/g by ester exchange with propanetriol, and the release period was 70 days at 5% coating rate. Su et al. synthesized CO-based hyperbranched polyol (COHBP) with the hydroxyl value of 233.68 mg KOH/g by reacting with 2,2-di­(hydroxymethyl) propionic acid. Release periods of the corresponding coated urea were extended from 3, 3, and 3 days to 13, 28, and 48 days correspondingly at 2%, 2.5%, and 3% coating rates. Currently, the adjustment of the hydroxyl value seems far from ideal for obtaining coated urea with excellent controlled release performance. 2.2. Modified Castor Oil-Based

2.2.1. Hydrophobic Modification

Despite their advantages, vegetable oil-based coatings often exhibit limited controlled release performance due to inherent hydrophilicity and porosity. Thus, modification of vegetable oil-based coating is common. Researchers have attempted hydrophobic modification using various nanoparticles to improve water-blocking properties, thereby further controlling the nutrient release. Several functional nanomaterials have demonstrated their effectiveness. Sodium-bentonite (Na-BT), modified attapulgite (MAT) by grafting stearic acid onto coupling agent 3-glycidoxypropyl trimethoxy silane KH-560 on attapulgite, and nano-carbon black (Nano-CB) have been used to increase the water contact angle of coatings to above 100°. − Notably, Wang et al. developed a dual-modification approach using hexadecyltrimethoxysilane (HDTMS) and halloysite nanotube (HNT) for CO-based polyurethane coated urea, where HNT functioned as a pore-reducing agent, and HDTMS acted to increase hydrophobicity. Compared with a single modification of HNT, the dual-modified coating achieved an impressive increase of water contact angle from 101.27° to 137.91°. However, as reported, the release periods of the modified coated urea remained limited to about 40 days at 3% coating rate. Interestingly, Pang et al. introduced a hydrolytic condensation reaction between γ-aminopropyl triethoxy silane (KH550) and nano silica to prepare hydrophobic nano silica (KNS), and then modified CO-based coating with it. The water contact angle was increased from 75.9 to 92.1°. The release period was up to 105 days at 3% coating rate, which was 6 times longer than that before modification. Additionally, as shown in Figure , Yang et al. prepared a bio-based polyurethane coated urea (APCU) with CO and bio-based isocyanate (BI) as raw materials, and obtained the nano-modified coated urea (HAPCU) after the modification with nano-laurate copper (Nano-LAC). Water contact angle increased from 59° to 87°, and the release period extended from 3 to 112 days at 5% coating rate. These results reveal an important finding, that is, the water contact angle of the modified coating does not exhibit a direct proportional relationship with the release period of its coated urea. The release period of coated fertilizer is influenced by multiple interconnected factors such as water contact angle, porosity, and cross-linking density of coating, which collectively determine the nutrient release kinetic characteristics of the nutrient.

It is particularly worth mentioning that, Liang et al. achieved a significant advancement in coating efficiency by dual-hydrophobic modification using nano stearic acid-magnesium hydroxide (STA-Mg­(OH)2) and paraffin wax in a CO-based coating. STA-Mg­(OH)2 constructed a micro-nanostructure in the outer layer of the coating to improve hydrophobicity, and paraffin wax helped to maintain the micro-nanostructure and formed a dynamic hydrophobic system, with water contact angle greatly increasing to 151.2°. Release period was as long as 33 days at a coating rate of only 1.5%, which is the lowest value reported so far.

2.2.2. Interpenetrating Polymer Network Modification

Mechanical property of pure CO-based polyurethane coating is not ideal with a tendency to wrinkle at high temperatures, ultimately leading to the peeling and cracking of the coating shell. To address this issue, an interpenetrating network polymer is a unique class of polymer blend, in which a network structure is formed by the interpenetration and entanglement of two or more polymers. , This kind of structure is beneficial to the formation of hydrogen bonds in hard segment, and between the hard segment and the soft segment, which can improve the controlled release properties of the coating material. , Wang et al. synthesized a coating material with this kind of structure after compounding CO and starch-based polyol (SP) to react with IPDI, and optimized by further grafting hydroxyl-terminated dimethyl silicone (HTMS). Release period reached 63 days at 7% coating rate. Tian et al. used PAPI to cure the compounding of SP and CO on the surface of polyolefin wax (PO-Wax) modified urea. Release period was up to 218 days at the PO-Wax content of 16.7% and the coating rate of 5%. Zhao et al. prepared an interpenetrating polymer network by one-pot method using CO as raw material, and sulfur (S) as reinforcing material (Figure ). Tensile strength and swelling resistance of the prepared coating were increased by 455.9% and 46.10%, respectively, and the release period was up to 210 days when the coating rate was 5%. The modifications of PO-Wax and S to the CO-based coating were significant.

2.2.3. Self-Healing Modification

Vegetable oil-based coating materials usually contain non-film-forming substances, which are easy to detach from the matrix to form pores during the nutrient release process, and fertilizers are prone to cracks due to abrasion and collision during preparation and transportation, thus affecting controlled release performance. According to different repair mechanisms, self-healing materials are divided into two categories: externally assisted and intrinsic mainly realized by dynamic non-covalent and covalent bondings. , Tian et al. added bis (2-hydroxyethyl) disulfide (SS) with a self-healing function to a mixture of CO and lignin-based polyol (LG). Disulfide bonds were randomly re-organized to repair the coating micropores, preventing the leaching of non-film-forming substances, reducing the channels for water to enter the coating, effectively delaying nitrogen release, and release period was extended to 63 days at the coating rate of 4%. Yu et al. chose glutaraldehyde (GA) and homemade nano-soybean protein microcapsule (SMC) to form a repairing agent for the self-healing modification of CO-based polyurethane with improved cross-linking degree and coating densification. Release periods were extended from 21, 28, and 49 days to 35, 49, and 70 days correspondingly at 2%, 3%, and 5% coating rates. Although to a certain extent, self-healing is effective to enhance the performance, its advantages are not obvious compared with other modification methods.

3. Polyurethane Coated Urea from Vegetable Oils without Natural Hydroxyl Groups

3.1. Hydroxylation of Vegetable Oil

Although the CO self-modification of can overcome the limitation of fixed hydroxyl value, the performance regulation and cost of a CO-based coating are limited. Trying more vegetable oils is vital. Therefore, it is necessary to consider whether other low-cost vegetable oils can replace CO, in whole or in part.

Other vegetable oils are firstly modified by grafting hydroxyl groups in their backbones, considering most of them do not contain hydroxyl groups. The carbon–carbon double bond and ester linkage of fatty acids are two main reactive sites, which are amenable to chemical reactions to make vegetable oil-based polyols. The main methods include epoxy ring-opening, ester exchange, ozone oxidation, ammonia decomposition, etc. Physical and chemical properties of the other oil-based polyols synthesized by different methods are different. , The first one includes two-step reactions (shown in Figure ), that is, carbon–carbon double bonds are epoxidized by organic peroxide acid, and then followed by grafting hydroxyl groups using nucleophilic reagents such as alcohol and acid. The second one is to obtain polyol with multiple hydroxyl groups by exchanging ester groups between the triglyceride of vegetable oil and small molecular alcohol under the catalysis action as shown in Figure . The former is the most intensively researched and industrially best method at present, and also the most commonly used method to synthesize vegetable oil-based polyol for controlled release coating. 3.2. Soybean Oil-Based

Soybean oil is cheap and readily available. Importantly, it has a high unsaturation degree, with an average double bond of 4.6 in each soybean oil molecule chain, to synthesize soybean oil-based polyol (SOP) with a high hydroxyl value. , Like pure CO-based polyurethane coating, pure soybean oil-based polyurethane coating also needs to be further modified to improve the controlled release performance in order to meet the requirement of crop growth. , Aiming at hydrophobic modification, Feng et al. added siloxane-modified nano-modified gravelly clay (MNA) into SOP for coating urea. Release period reached 70 days at 6% coating rate. Liu et al. only used a kind of siloxane HTMS to modify SOP with triethylenetetramine (TETA) as the cross-linking agent for coating urea. Release period with the addition of 35.33% HTMS at 7% coating rate was extended to 70 days from 7.5 days before modification. Zhao et al. modified soybean oil-based coated urea with polyethylene glycol intercalated bentonite clay (MBT). Coated urea with the MBT addition of 5% at 3% coating rate was extended to 74 days from 23 days before modification. In addition, a self-healing soybean oil-based coated urea with a dynamic covalent network was prepared by adding benzil dioxime (BDO-1) to SOP through a one-step reaction, the reaction and self-healing mechanism are shown in Figure . The release period of coated urea at 4% coating rate reached 49 days. Above all, aiming at interpenetrating polymer network modification, Dong et al. performed SOP and polyester polyol (PP) with PAPI to improve the nutrient release performance of coated urea. Release period reached 137 days at 3% coating rate, increased by 73.42% (79 days for pure soybean oil-based) and 234.15% (41 days for pure polyester polyurethane).

3.3. Palm Oil-Based

Palm oil is produced in large quantities and at a low price, and its production is surpassed by that of soybean oil. In the vegetable oil family, palm oil has a unique compositional structure, with saturated and unsaturated fatty acid contents of about half of each, with an average double bond length of 1.8 in each palm oil molecule chain. It is theoretically an ideal raw material to reduce the amount of costly isocyanate curing agent, and then improve the degradability of coating. Pang et al. developed a high bio-content coated urea by changing the amounts of catalyst and ring opener to palm oil-based polyols (POP) with different physicochemical properties, and then used POP as soft chain segment in combination with in-situ reaction film-forming technology. The release periods at 3% and 7% coating rates reached 22 and 73 days, respectively. For interpenetrating polymer network modification, Liu et al. successfully prepared a bio-based coated urea by converting waste frying palm oil into POP-1 and adding acrylonitrile (AN) to coating. The modified coated urea exhibited excellent swelling ability and effectively delayed the nutrient release. At 7% coating rate, the release period increased from about 50 days (unmodified) to 80 days (modified).

3.4. Other Oil-Based

In addition to the abovementioned vegetable oils, cottonseed oil, kitchen waste oil, and others have also been converted into polyols for the preparation of coated urea, too. Except soybean oil and palm oil, Pang et al. used linseed oil, olive oil, and rapeseed oil to investigate the relationship between the type of vegetable oil and controlled release performance of urea. It was found that linseed oil based coated urea had the longest release period of 56 days at 3% coating rate, indicating that the existence of fatty acids with three CC bonds and a high unsaturation degree helped to produce polyols with multiple hydroxyl groups and high functionality and coatings with good cross-linking density. What’s more, China is a large cotton producer, and cottonseed oil is a non-edible oil, which is cheaper than edible oil, with an average double bond of 3.9 in each cottonseed oil molecule chain, so cottonseed oil has received attention. As shown in Figure , Xie et al. converted cottonseed oil into polyol with the hydroxyl value of 520.94 mg KOH/g via ester exchange to prepare bio-based coated fertilizer (BCF), and then hydrophobically modified BCF by both surface roughening with micro-nano diatomite hydrosols (MN-D) and silanization to fabricate biomimetic superhydrophobic bio-based coated fertilizer (SBPF). The release period of SBCF was more than 2 months at the coating rate of 7%. Turning waste into treasure can not only save resources, but also avoid pollution and protect the environment. Liu et al. purified kitchen waste oil for polyol and polyurethane coated urea and added epoxy resin (EP) to carry out interpenetration of polymer network modification. The EP addition increased crosslinking density by 77.9%, decreased porosity by 28%, and significantly slowed down nitrogen release. The release period was up to 58 days at 7% coating rate. Gutter oil is another waste oil. A large amount of output is very harmful to people’s lives. Wang et al. used both hydroxyl-terminated polydimethyl silicone (HTPMS) and KH550 to modify gutter oil-based polyurethane coating material, and the release period of coated urea was more than 30 days at 5% coating rate. Sun et al. tried a complex MVO of CO and SOP to prepare coated urea with an interpenetrating polymer network structure, which was different from single vegetable oil-based coated urea. Increasing the content of SOP helped to extend the controlled release. The release period of MVO-based coated urea reached 4 months at 6% coating rate, when the 5:4 mass ratio of MVO to curing agent PAPI was used. In summary, exploring the application of other vegetable oils in the field of PCF provides new ideas for obtaining more raw material sources and reducing costs. Hydroxyl-free vegetable oils can be hydroxylated to produce coated urea, and their products often have the same defects as CO-based coated urea and need to be modified to improve the controlled release performance. In general, the modified oil-based coated urea can release nutrients for up to 2 months at a 7% coating rate. To get longer release period, coated urea by mixing different vegetable oils or with PP in a certain proportion even at the same coating rate.

4. Conclusions and Future Perspective

Vegetable oil-based polyurethane coated urea is a fertilizer that meets the need of green agriculture. Various vegetable oils have been used in the field of PCF, and their relative technology has become more mature over the decades. Suitable raw materials can be selected according to specific needs. CO contains natural hydroxyl groups, which can be directly reacted with curing agents to form a polyurethane coating. However, its fixed hydroxyl values and high prices limit performance regulation and cost control of the coating material. Other vegetable oils need to be chemically modified before they are used to synthesize polyurethane coating, but they have a wide range of hydroxyl value intervals, which makes the coating materials have better adjustability of performance. In addition, other vegetable oils enrich the source of raw materials and reduce the cost of the coating. Meanwhile, modification methods provide new ideas and ways for the property improvement of vegetable oil-based polyurethane coated urea. Hydrophobic modification to reduce water contact, interpenetrating polymer network to reduce porosity, and self-healing to repair pore space and damaged area, can all extend the nutrient release period. However, it remains to be explored whether the simultaneous application of the three modifications can have an iterative effect or not. With the continuous development of global agricultural technology, it is foreseeable that vegetable oil-based polyurethane-coated urea is expected to play a great role in agricultural production as well as contribute to environmental protection and sustainable management of resources. In future research, the following points should be emphasized:

  • (1)

    Large-Scale Production Processes: Scaling up vegetable oil modification processes for industrial production may face issues such as reduced reaction efficiency and fluctuation in product quality stability. It will be necessary to optimize process parameters and upgrade equipment to establish a low-cost, high-consistency mass production system, ensuring the homogeneity of modified vegetable oil properties during large-scale production.

  • (2)

    Adaptability to Field Environments: In actual farmland environments, coating materials are susceptible to interference from factors such as soil moisture, pH, and microorganisms, leading to nutrient release curves deviating from design targets. It is necessary to conduct multi-regional field trials to systematically analyze the influence of environmental factors on the nutrient release behavior of coatings and enhance the adaptability of fertilizers to complex field conditions through intelligent coating design.

  • (3)

    Environmental Safety Assessment Systems: Current environmental safety assessment standards for degradable coating materials in agriculture are incomplete, lackingquantitative detection methods for polymer residual toxicity and ecological accumulation effects. Life cycle assessment and bio-toxicity testing should be introduced to systematically evaluate the long-term impacts of coating degradation products on soil ecosystems, providing a scientific basis for environmental risk management and the promotion of fertilizers.

  • (4)

    Development of Functional Specialized Fertilizers: Customized fertilizers with drought-resistant and salt-tolerant functions should be developed by integrating regional soil composition, climatic characteristics, and water resource conditions. For the growth patterns and nutrient requirements of specific crops, specialized coated fertilizers will be needed to match nutrient release patterns with crop nutrient demand rhythms, advancing the implementation of precision fertilization technologies.
