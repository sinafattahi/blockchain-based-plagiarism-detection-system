Abstract

Herein, AlCoFeNiTi high-entropy alloy (HEA) samples were prepared by mechanical alloying using high ball-to-powder (BtP) ratios to investigate the effects of this method on phase formation, magnetic properties, and electrical conductivity. X-ray diffraction revealed that the prepared samples contained amorphous, body-centered cubic (BCC), and face-centered cubic (FCC) phases. The samples were dominated by the amorphous phase, indicating the influence of high BtP ratios on their structure. All the samples exhibited semihard magnetic properties, with the saturation magnetization (M s) value depending on the milling time. The sample synthesized at a milling time of 30 h exhibited the highest M s value of 60.1 emu g–1. The switching field distribution was employed to clarify the contributions of the BCC, FCC, and amorphous phases to the magnetic properties. The results showed that the BCC and amorphous phases contributed to over 95% of the sample magnetism, of which approximately 75% was attributed to the BCC phase. Notably, the magnetic properties of amorphous AlCoFeNiTi HEAs were demonstrated for the first time in this study.

1. Introduction

Metals and alloys have played an important role in the development of human civilization. An alloy typically consists of a base metal with low concentrations of other elements added to enhance its properties. To meet the growing demand for alloys, research in materials science has increasingly focused on high-entropy alloys (HEAs) recently. , HEAs are alloys containing five or more major elements, each with an atomic percentage between 5 and 35%. These alloys are called HEAs because their random solid or liquid solution states have a considerably higher mixing entropy than conventional alloys. Different forms of HEAssuch as powders, thin films, and bulk materialscan be fabricated using three main methods: melting and casting, powder metallurgy, and deposition. Mechanical alloying (MA), a powder metallurgy technique, can produce HEAs using a ball mill system. −

AlCoFeNiTi HEAs have been extensively studied, and their structures depend on the preparation methods. Face-centered cubic (FCC) and body-centered cubic (BCC) phases were observed in alloys produced by MA at 400 rpm for 30 h with a ball-to-powder (BtP) ratio of 25:1. When samples were prepared by MA followed by heat treatment, the FCC to BCC phase ratio depended on the milling time. BCC was the only phase observed in samples prepared by MA at 200 rpm for 35 h with a BtP ratio of 13:1. AlCoFeNiTi ingot and plate obtained by induction melting exhibited a nearly pure BCC phase. Similarly, samples prepared by MA at 300 rpm for 8 h with a BtP ratio of 10:1 showed a pure BCC phase. A BCC phase was also observed in alloys produced by MA followed by spark plasma sintering; however, the sintering process also resulted in TiC contamination. Thus, the BCC phase is the major component in AlCoFeNiTi HEAs, regardless of the preparation process. However, the reported samples were produced by MA at relatively low BtP ratios and with short milling durations.

The properties of HEAs can be readily tuned owing to their compositional flexibility from multiple major elements, making magnetic HEAs an important research focus. ,, An AlCoFeNiTi alloy plate with BCC as the main phase exhibited a saturation magnetization (M s) of 29.83 emu g–1, which was reduced to 1.18 emu g–1 with the addition of Si. An AlCoFeNiTi alloy containing FCC and BCC phases exhibited a high M s value of 53.67 emu g–1, which increased to 70.04 emu g–1 after annealing. The magnetic properties of FeCoNiMnV strongly depend on the milling time. The M s values were approximately 91, 100, 93, and 85 emu g–1 for the samples obtained at milling times of 0, 48, 72, and 96 h, respectively. The M s and coercivity (H c) of milled Co2.0CrCuFeMnNi powders gradually decreased with decreasing powder crystal size and increasing milling time. A mechanically prepared AlFeMgNiTi alloy exhibited high magnetic properties (M s = 54.28 emu g–1 and H c = 43.4 Oe). However, after vacuum annealing at 500 °C for 2 h, its M s value considerably decreased to 7.81 emu g–1. These results show that the magnetic properties of HEAs depend on their components and preparation methods.

In this study, an AlCoFeNiTi HEA was fabricated using the MA method with a high-energy ball mill system to investigate the effects of this process on phase formation, magnetic properties, and electrical conductivity. The samples were prepared at high BtP ratios of 25:4, 25:2, 50:2, and 100:2, with milling times varying from 30 to 100 h. X-ray diffraction (XRD) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) coupled with energy-dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) were employed to determine the structure of the prepared samples. The magnetic properties and electrical resistivity were examined using a vibrating sample magnetometer (VSM) and a four-point probe, respectively. Switching field distribution (SFD) analysis was used to investigate the contributions of the BCC, FCC, and amorphous phases to the magnetic properties of the samples.

2. Experimental Section

Herein, Fe (99.9%, Macklin), Ni (99.99%, Macklin), Co (99.9%, Macklin), Al (>99%, Damao), and Ti (99.99%, Macklin) powders were used without further purification. In a typical batch, the raw materials (2 g) were weighed and mixed using a mortar and pestle for approximately 15 min and then placed in a hardened steel pot (500 mL) with zirconia balls (o̷ 5 mm, 25 g). Toluene (5 mL, analytical reagent grade, Xilong) was then added to the pot to prevent the powder from cold welding, excessive heating, and metal oxidation during milling. The pot was rotated using a Pulverisette 5 (Fritsch) at 250 rpm for different lengths of time to study the effect of the milling time on the phase formation. The received mixture was evacuated at room temperature to eliminate toluene. The obtained powder was used in further experiments without any heat treatment. The BtP ratio was also varied to study its effect on the phase formation.

The structures of the prepared powders were characterized using XRD (Bruker X8), SEM (S4800, Hitachi), and EDS (Ultim Max, Oxford Instrument). The X-ray beam was generated by Cu Ka radiation (40 kV, 30 mA). The XRD data were measured in the 2θ range from 10.0 to 70.0° with a step of 0.02° and scan rate of 2° min–1. The resistivity of the sample was measured using the four-point probe method (CMT Series, Advanced Instrument Technology). The magnetic parameters were measured at room temperature using a MicroSense VSM under different magnetic fields (up to 15 kOe).

3. Results and Discussion

Figure a shows the XRD patterns of the starting metals, and Figure b presents the XRD patterns of samples prepared at a BtP ratio of 12.5:1 for milling times of 2, 4, 5, and 25 h, respectively. An enlarged view of the 2θ region from 43 to 46° is shown on the left side of the figure. The samples prepared with milling times of 2 and 4 h exhibited the characteristic peaks of the raw materials, along with a small shoulder at approximately 35°, indicating that the reaction among the starting metals had commenced but was not yet complete. After 5 h of milling, the features of the starting materials were still detected, but the peak intensities were smaller than those of the samples milled for 2 and 4 h. In contrast, peaks corresponding to the alloy formation were clearly observed. These results suggest that the reaction among the metals was still in progress after 5 h of milling. The XRD pattern of the sample milled for 25 h exhibited no detectable features of the raw materials, indicating that the alloy formation among the raw materials had been completed and the alloy was fully formed.

Figure a–d illustrates the XRD patterns of the samples prepared at different milling times with BtP ratios of 6.25:1, 12.5:1, 25:1 and 50:1. The patterns indicate that all the samples contained a large amount of amorphous phase and small amounts of crystalline phases. where λ is the X-ray wavelength, d hkl is the interplanar spacing, θ is the Bragg angle, and a is the lattice parameter. The value of a 0 was determined from a linear fit derived from a plot of a versus 12[cosθ2sinθ+cosθ2θ] . The results indicated that the prepared samples contained two crystalline phases: BCC and FCC.

Furthermore, the contents of the amorphous, FCC, and BCC phases were calculated from the XRD patterns and plotted as functions of the BtP ratio in Figure e, Figure f, and Figure g, respectively. The amorphous phase accounted for over 50 wt % in all samples prepared at a milling time of 30 h. The amorphous content increased with milling time. After 100 h of milling, the amorphous contents of the samples prepared with BtP ratios of 6.25:1, 12.5:1, 25:1, and 50:1 were 72.9, 76.2, 81.3, and 86.6 wt %, respectively. Among these, the sample with a BtP ratio of 50:1 exhibited the highest amorphous content after 100 h of milling. Notably, the amorphous content exhibited an almost linear dependence on the BtP ratio across all milling times, with larger BtP ratios corresponding to greater amorphous phase content. The slopes of the linear fitting equations were different from each other, indicating that the formation rate of the amorphous phase strongly depended on milling time.

The content of the FCC phase after 30 h of milling increased with the BtP ratio from 6.25:1 to 25:1 and slightly decreased at a ratio of 50:1, exhibiting a quadratic dependence on the BtP ratio (Figure f). After 50 h of milling, the FCC content increased as the BtP ratio rose from 6.25:1 to 12.5:1 but then decreased linearly at higher BtP ratios. For the samples milled for 70 and 100 h, the FCC content almost linearly decreased with increasing the BtP ratio. In contrast, the BCC phase content in all samples decreased exponentially with increasing the BtP ratio (Figure g). These results suggest that the BCC phase transformed into both FCC and amorphous phases as the BtP ratio increased from 6.25:1 to 50:1. Additionally, the FCC phase was also gradually amorphized at higher BtP ratios.

Figure shows the SEM images of the samples obtained after 100 h of milling at BtP ratios of 12.5:1 (a, b) and 50:1 (c, d). Figure a,b shows particle agglomeration in the sample fabricated at a BtP ratio of 12.5:1 due to cold welding among the particles, which was caused by their plastic deformation. The plastic particles underwent initial flattening and subsequent nonfragmentation consolidation during MA, resulting in the formation of lamellar HEA particles. When the BtP ratio was increased to 50:1, the HEA particles exhibited less agglomeration than the 12.5:1 sample. This deagglomeration was due to continuous welding and separation of the powder particles, with their severe plastic deformation owing to their interaction with the jar wall and balls.

Figure a and Figure b illustrate the SEM–EDS images of the samples prepared at BtP ratios of 50:1 after 1 and 100 h of milling, respectively. The SEM and EDS images in Figure a show that the particle sizes were within few micrometers and that the elements exhibited nonuniform distribution, indicating that the alloying process had just started. The SEM and EDS images in Figure b show the flaky particles and the uniform distribution of the Al, Co, Ni, Fe, and Ti phases in the synthesized alloy. The EDS spectrum of the sample in Figure b shows clear peaks of the elements present in the sample, indicating no oxygen contamination (Figure c). Furthermore, there were no detectable peaks indicating contamination from the grinding balls and jars during the milling process.

Figure a shows the magnetic hysteresis curves (measured at room temperature) of the AlCoFeNiTi HEAs prepared at a BtP ratio of 50:1 and different milling times. The Ms values of the samples prepared by milling for 30, 50, 70, and 100 h were 60.05, 42.15, 12.1, and 3.80 emu g–1, respectively. Magnetic saturation was achieved at low applied magnetic fields (∼2000 Oe), indicating that the alloys exhibited high magnetic susceptibility. The M s values of the samples prepared at 30 and 50 h were higher than the reported values for AlCoFeNiTi HEAs synthesized using melting and casting methods. These results indicated a strong dependence of M s on milling time. The sample prepared at 30 h exhibited the highest M s value among the samples synthesized using a BtP ratio of 50:1. Prolonging the milling time led to lower M s values, with the sample prepared at 100 h showing the lowest M s value (3.80 emu g–1). The H c values of the samples prepared at 30, 50, 70, and 100 h were 92.8, 93.5, 133.3, and 116.4 Oe, respectively, consistent with semihard magnetic behavior (Table ). The remanent magnetization (M r) was used to calculate the remanence ratios (M r/M s), which for the samples prepared at 30, 50, 70, and 100 h were 0.32, 0.37, 0.38, and 0.24%, respectively. The M r/M s ratios indicated that the remanent magnetization behavior was nearly independent of the milling time. The sample prepared at 100 h was nearly amorphous, which corresponded to its low M s and M r/M s values, indicating that the amorphous phase of the AlCoFeNiTi HEAs exhibited poor magnetic properties.

Figure b shows the magnetic hysteresis curves (measured at room temperature) of the AlCoFeNiTi HEAs prepared at a BtP ratio of 12.5:1 with different milling times. The M s values of the samples prepared at 30, 50, 70, and 100 h were 60.1, 51.6, 41.2, and 36.5 emu g–1, respectively. Saturation began at low applied magnetic fields (∼2000 Oe), indicating the high magnetic susceptibility of the obtained alloys. The sample prepared at 30 h exhibited the highest M s value among the samples synthesized at a BtP ratio of 12.5:1. Prolonging the milling time led to a reduction in M s, with the 100 h sample showing the lowest M s value (36.5 emu g–1). However, this value was approximately 10 times higher than that of the sample prepared at 100 h and a BtP ratio of 50:1. The sample produced at a BtP ratio of 25:1 exhibited a lower content of the amorphous phase than that prepared at 50:1 and 100 h (Figure e), confirming the extremely poor magnetic properties of the amorphous phase of AlCoFeNiTi HEAs. The H c values of the samples prepared at 30, 50, 70, and 100 h were 91.8, 115.3, 128.3, and 141.9 Oe, respectively, indicating their semihard magnetic properties (Table ). The sample synthesized at 30 h showed better semihard magnetic properties than the reported value of AlCoFeNiTi HEA (M s = 53.67 emu g–1, H c = 88.0 Oe).

Figure c shows the magnetic hysteresis curves (measured at room temperature) of the samples prepared at 100 h and different BtP ratios. The M s values of the samples prepared at 6.25:1, 12.5:1, 25:1, and 50:1 were 40.5, 36.5, 20.4, and 3.8 emu g–1, respectively, and their M r/M s values were 0.32, 0.58, 0.60, and 0.24%, respectively. The H c values of the samples prepared at 25:4, 25:2, 50:2, and 100:2 were 74.2, 141.9, 139.9, and 116.4 Oe, respectively. The BCC phase in the AlCoFeNiTi HEA ingot exhibited M s, H c, and M r/M s values of 28.47 emu g–1, 217.44 Oe, and 26.4%, respectively. The M s value of the ingot was smaller than the samples prepared at 30 and 50 h, while its H c value was higher than all the prepared samples in this study. Figure d shows the plots of the crystalline size and the H c and M s values of the samples prepared at a BtP ratio of 12.5:1 as a function of the milling time. The crystalline size and M s value decreased with the increase in the milling time, whereas the coercive force increased with the milling time. The M s values of the samples prepared at BtP ratios of 12.5:1 and 50:1 were plotted against the crystalline size (Figure e and Figure f, respectively) to examine the roles of the FCC and BCC crystalline phases on the M s of the prepared samples. The M s value exhibited an almost linear dependence on the crystalline size of the FCC and BCC phases. The sloped values of the fitting equation indicated that the crystalline size of BCC played a more important role in the M s than FCC, which was in good agreement with the literature.

The SFD was employed to determine the effect of the BCC, FCC, and amorphous phases on the magnetic properties of the prepared samples. The SFD was defined as the differential of the magnetization (M) with respect to the magnetic field (H). The SFD curves of the samples prepared at a BtP ratio of 12.5:1 and milling times of 30, 50, 70, and 100 h are shown in Figure a–d, respectively. Each curve was deconvoluted into three peaks (peaks 1, 2, and 3), observed at approximately 100–200, −450 to −620, and 1500–1700 Oe, respectively. These three peaks corresponded to the BCC, FCC, and amorphous phases, respectively, in the prepared samples. The switching field (peak position) and normalized peak area were plotted as a function of milling time (Figure e). The area of peak 3 increased with milling time, whereas that of peak 1 decreased. The normalized area of peak 2 slightly increased with milling time, which was consistent with the XRD results of the amorphous phase, indicating that peak 2 should be associated with the amorphous phase in the samples. Notably, the fraction of peak 3 was approximately 4%, while that of peak 1 exceeded 70% in all samples. Thus, peaks 1 and 2 corresponded to the BCC and FCC phases, respectively, as the BCC phase contributed more to the magnetism of the samples than the FCC phase (Figure e). SFD analysis revealed that the BCC and amorphous phases contributed to more than 95% of the sample magnetism, with approximately 75% attributed to the BCC phase.

Figure a shows the digital photo of a pellet prepared under a molding pressure of 560 MPa using the sample synthesized at 100 h and a BtP ratio of 50:1. Figure b, Figure c, and Figure d show the SEM images of the fracture surface of the pellets prepared under molding pressures of 356, 458, and 560 MPa, respectively, using the sample synthesized at 100 h. The small insets show the fracture surface of these samples at higher magnification. These images illustrated that the shape of the AlCoFeNiTi HEAs changed compared to that of the as-prepared particles (Figure c,d). This observation proved that the deformation of the particles occurred under the applied pressure, forming the interface. To investigate the impact of the BtP ratios, the electrical resistivity of the prepared pellets was measured using the four-point probe method. The observed electrical resistivity and conductivity were plotted as a function of the BtP ratios (Figure e). The electrical resistivity values of the samples obtained at BtP ratios of 25:1, 12.5:1, and 6.25:1 were smaller than that of the sample obtained at 50:1. This result suggested that the amorphous phase of the AlCoFeNiTi HEAs exhibited lower electrical conductivity than their crystalline phases.

4. Conclusions

AlCoFeNiTi HEAs were successfully prepared by the mechanical alloying method. XRD results confirmed that the prepared samples contained amorphous, BCC, and FCC phases, with the amorphous phase being dominant. The content of the amorphous phase exhibited an almost linear dependence on the BtP ratios at all milling times. The results suggested that the BCC phase transformed into FCC and amorphous phases, and the FCC phase was gradually amorphized as the BtP ratio increased from 6.25:1 to 50:1. SFD analysis revealed that the BCC and amorphous phases contributed to more than 95% of the sample magnetism, with approximately 75% attributed to the BCC phase. The magnetic properties of amorphous AlCoFeNiTi were demonstrated for the first time in this study.
