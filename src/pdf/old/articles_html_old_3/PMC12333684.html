Abstract

Floquet engineering, featuring time-periodic control of various systems, has enabled observations of many exotic phenomena such as time crystals, breaking of reciprocity symmetry, and exceptional topology. Theoretically, a periodic drive can make the system inherit the time period and generate new harmonics or distort the band structure at the fundamental frequency. Here, by combining such a technique with levitated optomechanics, we present an instructive example of a coherent acoustic frequency comb with microsphere phonon lasers. This work shows that our technique offers the capability of flexibly controlling both the teeth number and the teeth spacing of the resulting coherent acoustic frequency comb. In particular, we confirm that all Floquet-driven comb teeth can reach the truly coherent regime, i.e., satisfying all the characteristic features of phonon lasers. Our work provides a platform to drive the nonlinear phonon laser to a coherent acoustic frequency comb with truly coherent comb teeth.

Floquet engineering drives an optical tweezer phonon laser to a coherent acoustic frequency comb.

INTRODUCTION

As a versatile tool to control diverse systems, Floquet engineering with time-periodic driving (1–3) has been widely used in tuning the properties of optomechanical devices (4–7), atoms (8–11), photonic crystals (12–15), and waveguides (16–20). Unique effects have been observed in Floquet-engineered systems, such as time crystals (21), topological materials (22–25), Floquet solitons (19, 20), Floquet spin amplifications (9), and skin-topological effects (15). Furthermore, by generating new harmonics, Floquet engineering has also been exploited to couple mechanical modes at different frequencies, a key step to realizing mechanical nonreciprocity (4, 5), acoustic bistability (7), and motional quantum entanglement (26). However, as far as we know, Floquet engineering of levitated optomechanical (LOM) systems has not been explored (27, 28), especially the possibility of achieving coherent acoustic frequency combs.

Phonon lasers, as mechanical analogs of optical lasers, with a wavelength shorter than optical lasers of the same frequency, have been realized in many systems including ions (29–33), microresonators (34–40), membranes (41, 42), nanoparticles (43), and photonic crystals (44). These coherent acoustic sources are potentially important for high-precision mechanical measurements and fundamental studies of quantum phononics. In a recent experiment (6), Floquet engineering of phonon lasers was demonstrated with a silicon photonic nanocavity, featuring the appearance of multicolor phonon modes induced by a temporally modulated driving laser (6). Similar results of multicolor phonon lasers have also been observed in active optomechanical systems with a microsphere levitated in an optical tweezer (45, 46). However, these phonon lasers exhibit a distinctly limited number of coherent phonon modes, making them inadequate in achieving coherent acoustic frequency combs which are highly desired for applications (e.g., precision underwater ranging and acoustic imaging) in acoustic metrology.

Here, we show the realization of a coherent acoustic frequency comb with a microsphere trapped in an optical tweezer by harnessing Floquet engineering. We note that in previous works, acoustic frequency combs with thermal phonons (47–56), as acoustic analogs of optical frequency comb (57–61), have been realized by using nonlinear wave mixing (62, 63), bifurcation (48, 51, 64), or a symmetry-breaking method (65). These works, based on nonlinear phononic dispersion relations, usually require high driving power, carefully designed mode frequencies, or engineered mechanical nonlinearities. In contrast, our present work using Floquet engineering is free of these limitations and, in particular, can achieve acoustic frequency combs with coherent phonons instead of thermal phonons.

RESULTS

We consider a microsphere nonlinear phonon laser levitated in an optical tweezer, in which a Floquet pump is used to drive this multicolor phonon laser system to generate new comb teeth on both sides of each phonon lasing mode (see Fig. 1A). More specifically, a sinusoidal signal with frequency Ωmod is applied to periodically modulate the pump power. The power of the fundamental mode Ω0 creates the Floquet states Ω±α at the frequencies Ω0 ± αΩmod with α ≥ 1. For multicolor phonon lasers, this process can similarly occur in all higher-order harmonics, leading to the emergence of additional Floquet states.

When the modulation frequency matches the mode interval Ω0/n, where n is a positive integer, the sidebands of the fundamental phonon laser mode and the higher-order phonon modes overlap, resulting in equal spacing between sidebands. This enables the realization of a coherent acoustic frequency comb with a multiplicative increase in teeth number, by combining the Floquet technique with the nonlinearity of the phonon lasers.

The experimental setup is shown in Fig. 1B (see the Supplementary Materials for details). We use an active LOM system, comprising a SiO2 microsphere trapped by a dual-beam optical tweezer and an active optical cavity pumped by a continuous wave laser (45). The microsphere interacts with the optical cavity through dissipative optomechanical coupling. An optical gain in the cavity compensates for scattering losses from the microsphere, enhancing the photon lifetime and allowing the system to reach the coherent phonon-lasing regime. Because of the nonlinear coupling between the microsphere and the active optical cavity, a multicolor phonon laser emerges, featuring both the fundamental-mode phonon laser and nonlinear higher-order mechanical harmonics. Then, an electronic injection signal at the frequency of Ω0 is applied to the microsphere via an electrode to further improve the quality of such multicolor phonon lasers, including their brightness, stability, lifetime, and quantum coherence (46, 66, 67). This approach can simultaneously lock both the fundamental-mode phonon laser and all higher-order harmonics quite efficiently. With such high-quality nonlinear phonon lasers at hand, we then apply the technique of Floquet engineering (i.e., periodically modulating the pump power), generating more phonon sidebands between the phonon-lasing modes. As a result, the multicolor phonon laser can be switched to a coherent acoustic frequency comb in a well-controllable manner. To the best of our knowledge, this work pioneers such a goal with an LOM system, which thus provides a new powerful tool for metrology applications of phonon lasers, especially in marine environments.

We stress that the unique features of this system that support such a coherent acoustic frequency comb can be listed as follows. First, our system has a strong nonlinearity due to the gain-enhanced dissipative coupling. As a result, we realize nonlinear multifrequency phonon lasing, with both coherent fundamental mode and harmonics, providing a large cardinality of phonon lasing modes before Floquet engineering is applied. Second, the performance of the phonon laser, such as coherence, brightness, linewidth, and frequency stability, is effectively improved through injection locking. Such a high-quality phonon laser is essential to drive strong and numerous Floquet sidebands. Third, strong optical gain in our system can generate strong mechanical gain, enabling the coexistence of multiple phonon laser modes, and thus effectively suppresses mechanical mode competition (6, 68). That overcomes the obstacle of steering the coherent acoustic frequency comb with multiple phonon lasing modes.

The experimental results before and after the Floquet pump are depicted in Fig. 1C, highlighting the differences in the power spectrum between the nonlinear phonon laser and the coherent acoustic frequency comb. In the top case of Fig. 1C, the fundamental mode of the nonlinear phonon laser is measured as Ω0 = 10 kHz. In addition, the higher peaks around 5Ω0, 6Ω0, and 8Ω0 derive from laser noise (see the Supplementary Materials for details). With the introduction of a Floquet pump at a modulation frequency Ωmod = 2 kHz and a modulation depth η = 35%, a coherent acoustic frequency comb with over 40 teeth is excited as depicted in the middle case of Fig. 1C. The teeth strength presents a gradual reduction in the shape of waves. This phenomenon arises from the combination of two mechanisms. First, the power spectrum of the microsphere displacement obeys the Lorentz distribution (43). Thus, the teeth strength gradually decreases for the higher-frequency comb teeth. Second, the energy of the Floquet sidebands derives from the multicolor phonon laser, i.e., Ω0, 2Ω0, 3Ω0,…, and thus leads to lower amplitudes than that of the nearby multicolor phonon lasing mode.

Such a coherent acoustic frequency comb can be modeled by using a resonator described by displacement x(t) with resonance frequency Ω0. We first proceed with the dynamics of the enhanced nonlinear phonon laser (45, 46) and then express the resonance frequency as a variable versus modulation frequency Ωmod and modulation depth η of the pump power. Consequently, the equation of the motion can be written as

mx¨+γx˙+mΩ01+ηsin(Ωmodt)2x=Fth(t)+Fopt(x,t)+Fel(t) (1)

where γ=6πη0R0.6190.619+Kn(1+0.310.785+1.152Kn+Kn2) is the damping rate (43), a function mainly related to the dynamic viscosity of air η0, the radius of microsphere R, and Knudsen number Kn. Fth(t)=W(t)2kBTγ  is the Brownian motion force at the temperature T and W(t) is the independent white noise. Fopt(x,t) = Q(x)P(x,t) is the optical force exerted by the laser in the ring cavity, where Q(x) is the trapping efficiency and P(x,t) is the intracavity power. Since there is a response time τ between the intracavity power change and the displacement, P(x,t) can be expressed as P[x(t − τ)] and transformed into P(x)−dPdxτx˙(t) through Taylor expansion. Then, the optical force can be written as Fopt(x,t)=Q(x)P(x)−Q(x)dPdxτx˙(t) . Fel(t) = Fel−xcos(Ω0x) is the electric force for injection locking (45, 46).

By rearranging the terms in the equation, Eq. 1 can be written as

mx¨+(γ+γopt)x˙+mΩ01+ηsin(Ωmodt)2x=W(t)2kBTγ+Q(x)P(x)+Fel−xcos(Ω0x) (2)

where γopt=Q(x)dPdxτ is the optical damping rate. By numerically integrating Eq. 2, we can obtain the theoretical simulation results, as depicted in the lower case of Fig. 1C, generally aligning with experimental observations. The slight difference between simulation and experiment mainly derives from errors in the parameter estimation, e.g., nonlinearity force, optical damping, etc. Both the experiment and simulation are solid to support the validity of the coherent acoustic frequency comb.

Now, we probe the evolutions of such a coherent acoustic frequency comb by gradually increasing the pump power Pp, under the modulation conditions Ωmod = 1.8 kHz and η = 15% (see Fig. 2A). With the pump power Pp increased from 33.8 to 37.5 mW, we observe an enhancement in the amplitude of the Floquet sidebands. This enhancement arises from the increased nonlinearity of the LOM coupling, which enables the Floquet sidebands to draw more energy from the fundamental-mode phonon laser and its harmonics. Consequently, the number of sidebands increases, and the relative strength distribution of the comb teeth becomes more uniform. Through this process, the LOM system undergoes a smooth transition from the nonlinear phonon-lasing regime to the coherent acoustic frequency comb regime.

To confirm the phonon-lasing state of the emerging comb teeth, we measured the phonon autocorrelation functions at the zero-time delay (69) of the Floquet sideband Ω+1 and 2Ω+1, which can be written as

g(k)(0)=〈b^†kb^k〉/〈b^†b^〉k,(k=2,3) (3)

with the annihilation or creation operator b^ or b^† of the phonon mode. The kth-order correlations of phonons satisfy g(2)(0) = 2 and g(3)(0) = 6 below the threshold Pth, demonstrating the thermal statistics. As the pump power Pp exceeds Pth = 34.7 mW, g(k)(0) decreases and approaches 1, which indicates that the phonon dynamics turns from the thermal state to the coherent state. As depicted in Fig. 2 (B and C), both the second-order and the third-order autocorrelations for Ω+1 and 2Ω+1 are confirmed to approach unity, as one of the essential features of phonon lasers. Compared with previous works of acoustic frequency combs with thermal phonons, the higher-order coherence of our comb teeth holds the promise to enhance the performance in future applications.

The phase coherence of all sidebands within the comb can be proved with a zoom-in on the time domain signal. The measured displacement and velocity of the microsphere, as shown in Fig. 3 (A and B), are in a stable envelope state, where the envelope frequency is consistent with the frequency of the first tooth. Therefore, it can be qualitatively stated that the frequency comb has phase stability, similar to the optical frequency comb. In an ideal situation, each tooth of the frequency comb has perfect phase consistency, but in practical applications, the phase of the comb will fluctuate because of the influence of various noise sources. To further quantitatively analyze the phase stability, we investigate the phase noise of both the fundamental frequency of the nonlinear phonon laser and the first comb tooth of the coherent acoustic frequency comb, as illustrated in Fig. 3C. It is evident that the phase noise of the frequency comb is lower than that of the nonlinear phonon laser, indicating superior phase stability of the coherent acoustic frequency comb.

We note that the teeth spacing of our coherent acoustic frequency comb can be flexibly switched by tuning the modulation frequency Ωmod. Figure 4A presents three representative power spectra for modulation frequencies ranging from 500 Hz to 1.5 kHz, which reveals that the spacing ∆Ω between the mode Ω0 and Floquet state Ω±1 is equal to the modulation frequency Ωmod. It also indicates that the intensity of the Floquet sidebands is weaker than that of the primary phonon-lasing modes. However, when the modulation frequency Ωmod is set equal to the mode interval Ωmod/n with a positive integer n, the Floquet sidebands from two adjacent phonon-lasing modes merge to form enhanced sidebands (see Fig. 4B). For example, when the modulation frequency is

Ωmod=β2−β1α1+α2Ω0,(β2>β1) (4)

the Floquet states Ω+α1 from the mode β1Ω0 and the Floquet states Ω−α2 from the mode β2Ω0 can combine to form a single state. Similarly, other states, such as the Floquet states Ω+α1+1 from the mode β1Ω0 and the Floquet states Ω−α2–1 from the mode β2Ω0 can also yield the same process. This enables the formation of a coherent phonon frequency comb with uniform tooth spacing. Actually, the adjustable range of tooth spacing extends far beyond what is illustrated in Fig. 4B. The maximum teeth spacing is primarily determined by the frequency spacing of the multicolor phonon laser, which depends on the natural frequency Ω0 of the microsphere. Meanwhile, the minimum teeth spacing is constrained by the highest order of the Floquet state. As a result, the teeth spacing in this work can be flexibly tuned across a wide range, from 100 Hz to 10 kHz. Compared to previous experimental works covering only two orders of tunable teeth spacing range (48, 49, 53, 55, 65, 70–72), our approach demonstrates a substantial advancement. It provides a powerful and versatile method for realizing a high-quality, broadband coherent acoustic frequency comb, featuring sufficient comb teeth and highly customizable spacing between the teeth.

We further investigate the effect of modulation depth η on the coherent acoustic frequency comb, as shown in Fig. 4C. With the modulation frequency fixed at Ωmod = 2 kHz, the increase of the modulation depth from 3 to 34% results in a gradual rise in both the number and amplitude of Floquet sidebands, similar to the trends observed in Fig. 2A. To provide further insights into energy transfer, we analyze the intensity variations of the fundamental mode Ω0 and Floquet sidebands Ω+1 and Ω+2 as depicted in Fig. 4D. The amplitude of Ω0 decreases, while the amplitudes of Ω+1 and Ω+2 gradually increase, indicating energy transferring from the fundamental mode to the Floquet sidebands. However, because of energy redistribution effects, the intensity of Floquet sideband cannot be indefinitely enhanced growing with η. Specifically, as η increases beyond critical levels, power from the fundamental mode Ω0 becomes progressively diverted to higher-order sidebands (Ω0 ± nΩmod where n > 1), manifested by the sequential saturation of Ω+1 and subsequent growth of Ω+2 intensity. Even if the sidebands approach saturation, their amplitudes remain lower than that of the fundamental mode. This is consistent with the Lorentzian distribution of the displacement power spectrum (43), which naturally limits the sideband amplitudes relative to the fundamental mode for the same driving energy.

This mechanism of energy transfer not only increases the amplitude of the Floquet sidebands but also enhances their coherence as the modulation depth increases. We calculate the phonon population <N> for the sideband Ω+1 as a function of η, as illustrated in Fig. 4E. The phonon population change aligns with the variation in amplitude, demonstrating that the Floquet sideband Ω+1 transitions from a thermal state to a coherent state as the energy is transferred from the fundamental mode. This transition is further confirmed by calculating the phonon autocorrelation functions g(2)(0) and g(3)(0), as shown in Fig. 4 (F and G). These results verify that the increase in modulation depth facilitates the emergence of coherence in the Floquet sidebands.

DISCUSSION

Last, we emphasize that, although the three core ingredients of our work, i.e., the optical tweezer, the nonlinearity, and the Floquet engineering, are individually well-established powerful techniques, the possibility of combining them to generate coherent acoustic frequency combs has never been demonstrated in previous works. The advantages of this integration are evident not only in Fig. 1, which presents the intuitive result of the coherent acoustic frequency comb, but also in Figs. 2 and 3, which highlight its exceptional coherence. In addition, Fig. 4 confirms the system’s unique correlations and remarkable flexibility in regulation.

Parametric modulation on optically trapped particles may have been extensively investigated in the levitated optomechanics community (43, 68, 73, 74). However, most of these studies are dedicated to feedback cooling or heating the microsphere, without attention paid to Floquet engineering or coherent acoustic frequency combs. Our work diverges significantly from existing studies in levitated optomechanics, both in terms of underlying principles and results. To the case of the experimental operation, they apply the parameter modulation according to the phase of the microsphere, i.e., the modulated signal needs to be locked to the motion of the microsphere (68, 73, 74). In our work, the Floquet sidebands are driven without any need for phase locking. To the case of the experimental result, different from the pioneering work of amplifying or cooling a microsphere, our work develops an instructive strategy to achieve a coherent acoustic frequency comb with Floquet engineering.

Despite significant experimental efforts on acoustic frequency combs based on optomechanical (70), electromechanical (63), and opto-electro-mechanical (55) systems, no method has been demonstrated to enable the thermal phonons in combs convert to coherent regime, i.e., achieve the coherent acoustic frequency comb. For example, Mercadé et al. (6) show two-mode lasing by Floquet engineering. Wang et al. (75) illustrate an optomechanical comb (modulated optical power) based on a two-mode phonon laser. The works as mentioned above realize either multicolor phonon lasers or acoustic frequency combs, which is not yet a coherent acoustic laser comb. In contrast to previous works on acoustic frequency combs, our experiment achieves this powerful tool in a truly coherent regime and thus can significantly enhance the stability and sensitivity of precision mechanical measurements in practice. In addition, our work is intrinsically different from the mode locking of a laser, where the periodic signal is used to lock the phase of multimode lasers, in the absence of the ability to generate new sidebands (6, 76, 77).

In our future work, we will challenge the requirement for vacuum environment by enhancing the quality factor of phonon lasers with new techniques, e.g., structured light (78–80), decorated particles (81, 82). In addition, with the aid of devices like hydroacoustic transducers and acoustic amplifiers (83, 84), our coherent acoustic frequency comb can be transmitted to a targeted object for further detection.

In summary, this work demonstrates an instructive example of a coherent acoustic frequency comb with a microsphere LOM system. The high-order coherence of the emerged comb teeth is verified, and the tunable teeth spacing is demonstrated. Our work not only realizes a flexible switch between high-quality phonon lasers and coherent acoustic frequency combs but also provides a new link between Floquet engineering and phonon laser physics. Our experiment can also serve as a new platform to design and use more coherent phonon devices with LOM systems.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Experimental procedure

The experiment is mainly operated with the following three steps: (i) phonon lasing and electronic injection. Load the microsphere into the trapping region of the dual-beam optical tweezer (green beams in Fig. 1B) and reduce the pressure in the vacuum cavity. Then, adjust the position of the active optical cavity, which is installed on a three-dimensional translation stage. After the phonon laser behavior is observed according to the real-time PSD of the microsphere’s displacement, turn on the signal generator and adjust the amplitude and frequency of the electrical signal. (ii) Periodically pump. Apply a sinusoidal signal to the controller of a semiconductor laser to periodically modulate the pump power of the active optical cavity (see the Supplementary Materials for details). Next, to form an ideal frequency comb, we adjust the amplitude and frequency of the modulated signal, so that the new harmonics generated by different modes of phonon lasers partially overlap. (iii) Data acquisition. We investigate the influence of the Floquet pump on the phonon laser comb, as shown in the Results.

Periodically pump method

The periodic pumping of the active cavity is achieved by the pump laser controller (CLD1010LP) which supports modulation by an external radio frequency (RF) source. It has a 50-ohm input impedance, and the RF signal is ac coupled directly to the laser via a Bias-T network. Then, the required RF voltage can be calculated as URF=50Imod , where the modulation current is Imod=imodsin(Ωmodt) . When the pump current is I0, the modulated depth can then be expressed as η=Imax−IminImax+Imin=imodI0 . In addition, the modulated signal is generated by a function signal generator (DG1402-8750).
