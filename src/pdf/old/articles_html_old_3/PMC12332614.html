Abstract

This study reports the photocatalytic degradation of the RB5 dye by nickel sulfide nanoparticles (NiS NPs) in the presence of salts. Dynamic light scattering has rarely been used to study dye photocatalytic degradation. However, hydrodynamic size (H R) and zeta potential measurements offer insights into RB5 photocatalytic degradation. The hydrodynamic radius of NiS increased from 244.97 ± 31 to 1325.43 ± 531 nm upon interaction with NaCl, with 99% degradation efficiency for RB5 via ·OH, O2 – ·, and Cl· radical mechanisms, which further increased to 1882 ± 385 nm, indicating deposition of dye degraded and mineralized products on the catalyst surface. Least interaction of AgNO3 increased the hydrodynamic radius to 672.6 ± 339 nm with only 40% RB5 degradation. Zeta potential studies confirmed that NiS NPs are negatively charged and stable for RB5 photocatalytic degradation in NaCl, although their overall stability decreased upon interaction with salts. Trapping studies verify the radicals’ generation, and the Langmuir–Hinshelwood model attests adsorption as an active process followed by the RB5 photocatalytic degradation. XRD analysis confirmed an average crystallite size of 7.08 nm with a rhombohedral crystal system. Scanning electron microscopy exhibits agglomerated nanoclusters, and BET analysis demonstrates the mesoporous nature of NiS with a surface area of 161 m2/g. Optical analysis (UV–vis and PL) confirms the interaction of NiS NPs with RB5 and the salts, respectively. HPLC and TOC analyses demonstrate the degradation and mineralization of RB5, respectively. Theoretical investigation reveals that the N–N position is the preferred site for RB5 interaction with NiS NPs, whereas the cage-like structure was found suitable for photocatalysis with a bandgap energy of 2.11 eV. FTIR analysis confirmed the structural stability of NiS NPs up to the 10th cycle, maintaining over 85% RB5 degradation efficiency. These findings highlight the potential of NiS NPs for the elimination of toxic dyes from wastewater and their applicability to practical applications.

Introduction

The rapid growth of industrial sectors has significantly impacted mankind positively but has harmed the aquatic environment. Among various industries, the textile sector is a major contributor to water pollution, discharging large volumes of wastewater containing synthetic organic compounds and azo dyes such as Reactive black 5 (RB5), Acid yellow 23 (AY23), Reactive yellow 145, Direct brown 44, Orange II, and Methyl orange dyes. About 70,000 tons of dyes are consumed globally, with nearly 15% lost in processing. These pollutants resist microbial degradation, leading to global environmental and health concerns. , Textile dyes have been linked to adverse effects on microbial populations, fisheries, agriculture, and human health, including carcinogenicity. Research studies have shown that these dyes can trigger hypersensitivity and can be toxic to living things.

Textile reactive dyes are highly water-soluble and contain polyaromatic molecules that are nondegradable and are not well absorbed by biological solids. Various azo dyes, including Brilliant blue (BB) and Brilliant green (BG), AY23, Methylene blue (MB), and RB5, have been used in the textile, leather, and pharmaceutical industries. Among these dyes, RB5 is one of the most extensively utilized azo dyes, owing to its widespread application in dyeing cotton, polyester, and denim fabrics. Additionally, RB5 finds relevance in biopharmaceutical applications, particularly as an albumin inhibitor and in studies related to enzymatic saccharide metabolism. The dye contains different active groups, including azo (−N=N−) group chromophores, −NH2, −OH, and −SO3 2–, which enhance its adherence property to the textile fibers. Efficient removal and mineralization of RB5 dye from wastewater is crucial to guarantee the safe discharge of treated effluents into natural water bodies.

Various techniques have been used for RB5 dye removal, including adsorption, precipitation, reverse osmosis, flocculation, anaerobic oxidation, and coagulation, which are not much effective because of high costs for infrastructure, lack of engineering expertise, incomplete dye mineralization, and toxic sludge generation. Advanced oxidation processes (AOPs) have attracted significant attention in both scientific and industrial processing, including electrochemical methods, Fenton oxidation, and membrane technology in removing organic pollutants as compared to other techniques. Among AOPs, photocatalysis has emerged as a promising, ecofriendly, and cost-effective alternative for complete dye degradation and mineralization. This technique can be used to treat a wide range of pollutants, including organic and inorganic molecules. It requires low energy as compared to other AOPs by using light sources and solar irradiation.

Different semiconductor nanomaterials act as photocatalysts, including sulfides such as CdS, ZnS, and CuS, and oxides, e.g., ZnO, TiO2, Fe2O3, and CuO for pollutant degradation. Sulfide semiconductors with a suitable band gap demonstrate strong photocatalytic activity under visible light. NiS is a well-known and outperforming metal sulfide due to its high activity, photochemical stability, nontoxicity, low cost, and small band gap, which allows it to work under visible light. Unlike other metal sulfides, which have poor charge separation efficiency and restricted light absorption properties, nickel sulfide nanoparticles (NiS NPs) exploit a larger spectrum of light for improved photocatalytic activity. Because of its ability and potential to work in the visible light, it overcomes a key restriction shared by many photocatalysts that mostly absorb UV light.

NiS is a p-type semiconductor and exhibits complex structural and optical behavior, forming phases such as NiS, Ni3S2, Ni7S6, and Ni9S8, depending on the synthetic conditions. Various morphologies, including nanorods, nanospheres, and flower-like structures, have been reported. NiS NPs can be synthesized by various methods, including the solvothermal, hydrothermal, microwave, ultrasonic, and chemical precipitation methods. Among these methods, chemical precipitation is preferred for its simplicity and efficiency. It is cost-effective and found to be more attractive for the large-scale production of nanomaterials as compared to other complex, low-yield methods.

Dynamic light scattering (DLS) is a key technique for determining the average hydrodynamic size of particles in the nano- to micrometer range by analyzing the fluctuations in scattered light intensity caused by particles’ Brownian motion in the suspension. This instrument also provides insights into colloidal stability in terms of the surface charge at the slipping plane of the particles. Notably, the hydrodynamic diameter obtained from DLS may differ from the actual physical diameter, which is attributed to the formation of a solvation layer and surface-bound species surrounding the particles, e.g., the stability of succinate capping on silver nanoparticles is controlled through DLS studies.

Inorganic salts in textile wastewater may influence the photocatalytic processes of semiconductor nanomaterials based on the variation of stability, surface charge, and hydrodynamic size (H R) of the catalyst. , A study reported the positive effect of inorganic salts on the designed NiS2 and CoS on rGO for Congo red photocatalytic degradation. A study reported the aggregation of TiO2 NPs with NaCl concentration, which increases the hydrodynamic size of TiO2 NPs measured by DLS, and such aggregation influences the RB5 photocatalytic degradation. Another study displayed the interaction of gold nanoparticles with anionic and cationic dyes, which form a monolayer around the nanoparticles’ surface and increase their hydrodynamic size measured by Zetasizer. Salts like NaCl, Na2SO4, CaCl2, Na2S, CH3COONa, and AgNO3 are commonly added to dye baths, contain various anions and cations which can accelerate dye photodegradation by the catalyst through photogenerated radicals and/or act as radical scavengers during photocatalytic degradation or even compete for catalyst adsorption sites, thereby affecting the surface charge and hydrodynamic size of the catalyst. Hence, it is crucial to investigate such “electrostatic interactions” between salt ions and nanoparticles, demonstrating a direct influence on dye adsorption and photocatalytic degradation. Moreover, monovalent and divalent ions affect catalyst surface properties, and understanding their impact on the photocatalytic efficiency is also essential.

In our previous studies, DLS has been reported to monitor the hydrodynamic size (H R) and zeta potential of NPs upon dye adsorption, providing insights into adsorption mechanisms. Our recent study also validates the adsorption phenomenon of RO16 dye by CuO-ZnO nanocomposite through DLS studies by monitoring the changes in H R and zeta potential by increasing the dye concentration at different pHs. Similarly, size distribution and stability studies have been conducted for gold NPs using DLS.

However, H R and zeta potential variations of the NiS catalyst in the presence of various salts in photocatalytic processes are yet to be explored. This is a completely new strategy for confirming RB5 degradation by NiS NPs. Nanoparticle stability is also an important factor for their activity, which can be controlled by the addition of various salts in the NPs suspension. Moreover, how the deposition/interaction of degraded and mineralized products of dye, along with salt ions, affects the hydrodynamic size and surface charge of the catalyst also needs to be investigated.

This study primarily explores the synthesis of NiS nanoparticles and their photocatalytic efficiency against RB5 dye, both alone and in the presence of different inorganic salts. DLS analysis supports the findings by revealing particle size distribution and colloidal stability by surface charge, which are the key findings for the photocatalytic activity of NiS NPs. Density functional theory (DFT) calculations further aid in theoretically analyzing the potential interaction sites within the dye molecule, calculating interaction energies of RB5 dye with NiS alone as well as the NiS-RB5 complex, and confirming the NiS band gap’s suitability for visible-light-driven photocatalysis of RB5 dye.

Materials and Methods

The chemicals utilized in this experiment are nickel acetate tetrahydrate Ni­(CH3CO2)2·4H2O, sodium sulfide (Na2S) anhydrous, poly­(vinyl alcohol) (PVA), NaOH, HCl, NaCl, AgNO3, Na2SO4, CaCl2, CH3COONa·3H20, acetone, isopropyl alcohol (IPA), and polyethylene glycol (PEG), which were purchased from Dae-Jung Chemicals and Metals Co. Ltd., Republic of Korea. RB5, AY23, Reactive Yellow 145 (RY145), Direct Brown 44, Orange II, and Methyl Orange azo dyes were acquired from Sigma-Aldrich, Munich, Germany. All of the reagents and solvents were used as received without any purification. A 0.5 M stock solution of Ni (CH3COO)·4H2O was prepared by dissolving 4.9772 g salt in 40 mL distilled water. A 1 M solution of Na2S was prepared by dissolving 6.2432 g in 80 mL of distilled water. The 1% solution of poly­(vinyl alcohol) (PVA) was prepared by dissolving 1 g in 100 mL.

Synthesis of NiS NPs

The chemical precipitation method was used for the synthesis of NiS NPs, and the synthesis scheme is shown in Figure . Ni­(CH3CO2)2·4H2O and sodium sulfide (Na2S) were used as precursor salts to prepare the NiS NPs. To make 0.5 M of Ni­(CH3CO2)2·4H2O solution, 4.976 g of salt was taken and dissolved in 40 mL of distilled water and stirred at 500 rpm for 20 min using a magnetic stirrer on a hot plate at 60 °C. The pH of the salt solution was 6.5. The salt of Ni­(CH3CO2)2·4H2O was stirred continuously, and a 1% (1 g in 100 mL) 10 mL solution of PVA was added and stirred. To this solution, 1 M Na2S in double volume was added dropwise as a precipitating agent and stirred for 3 h on a hot plate at 60 °C. The precipitate was centrifuged, followed by washing with distilled water; the same procedure was repeated thrice to remove the unwanted residues. After centrifugation, the particles were heated at 100 °C in a hot air oven for 3 h. NiS NPs are thus formed. The NiS NPs synthesized were not further calcined at high temperature and used as such for the photocatalytic degradation of RB5 dye.

Preparation of Various Dyes Solution for Screening Studies

The stock solution of RB5, RY145, AY23, Direct brown 44, Orange II, and Methyl Orange dye was prepared by accurately weighing 1 g of RB5 dye and dissolving it in 1000 mL of distilled water to make a 1000 mg/L solution. From the stock solution, the desired concentrations of each dye were prepared by dilution with distilled water in accurate proportions. NiS NPs were screened against different azo dyes that have been reported to affect photocatalytic degradation. Among them, RB5 was ranked as the best photocatalytic degraded dye with NiS NPs; therefore, RB5 was selected for further optimization and mechanistic studies.

Adsorption Experiments

For adsorption studies, 0.03 g/L NiS NPs were added to 50 mL of dye solutions with varying concentrations (20, 30, 40, and 50 mg/L). The mixtures were stirred continuously, and 5 mL aliquots were withdrawn at predetermined time intervals (5, 10, 15, 20, 25, and 30 min). The solution was filtered through a 0.45 μm syringe filter, and the concentration of residual dye was examined by UV–visible spectroscopy.

RB5 adsorption over time, qt (mg/g), was calculated by using eq .

qt=(C0−Ct)V/m 1

where qt = adsorption over time (mg/g), C 0 = initial RB5 concentration (mg/L), Ct is the concentration after time t, V is the solution volume (L), and m is the adsorbent weight.

Photocatalysis of RB5 Dye

The photocatalytic experiment was performed in a chamber equipped with a halogen lamp (500 W) emitting radiation equal to visible light’s wavelength. A 60 mL of 40 mg/L of RB5 dye solution containing 30 mg of NiS NPs was kept in the dark for 30 min to attain the adsorption/desorption equilibrium, followed by irradiation under a visible light source of 500 W with 30,798 lx light intensity (Halogen lamp, Hi Luminar-Germany) at 25 cm distance for different reaction times ranging up to 1 h. A 5 mL syringe was used to extract, and then a mounting syringe filter of 0.45 μm diameter was used; finally, the dye suspension was filtered. Samples were taken at definite time intervals. The syringe was used because the NiS NPs cannot be easily separated from the dye solution by centrifugation. In both adsorption and photocatalytic experiments, the concentration of residual dye has been monitored by UV–visible spectroscopy at λmax = 598 nm. The percentage of dye molecules undergoing photocatalytic activity was calculated using the following standard expression, eq .

Degradation%=(C0−CtC0)×100 2

The photocatalytic system was optimized based on the RB5 degradation investigations for reaction parameters, including the contact time, pH, NiS dose, and RB5 concentration. Parameter optimization studies were conducted to evaluate the effects of changing the pH, RB5 dye concentration, and photocatalyst dose on the photocatalytic degradation performance of NiS NPs. The pH study was carried out at various pH levels of 2, 4, 6.5, 8, and 10. The pH of the NiS NP suspension was maintained using 0.1 M HCl and 0.1 M NaOH. Dye concentration studies were performed using 20, 30, 40, and 50 mg/L, and the photocatalyst dose influence was monitored at catalyst loadings of 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, and 1 g/L. Additionally, UV–visible spectra were recorded for the RB5 photocatalytic degradation by NiS NPs with predetermined time intervals of 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45, 50, 55, and 60 min, and possible degraded products were monitored.

Photocatalyst Characterization

The NiS NPs were characterized by various physicochemical properties. NiS NP synthesis was confirmed by UV–visible absorption spectroscopy. For this purpose, a double beam UV–visible spectrophotometer was used that is equipped with deuterium and halogen lamps, having a wavelength range of 190–1100 nm and a bandwidth of 1.4 nm (model 200+, Analytik Jena, Germany). Quartz cuvettes were used for analysis with a fixed path length of 10 mm. Fluorescence studies of NiS NPs were conducted on a single-beam LS-45 fluorescence spectrophotometer, model PerkinElmer (Horiba fluoromax-4-P-C). The excitation wavelength used for the NiS NPs was 226 nm. While emission spectral measurements were recorded in the wavelength range of 200–420 nm. All of the fluorescence measurements were taken using a four-sided transparent quartz cuvette. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was employed to scan the morphology of NiS NPs at high resolutions, while energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy was performed to confirm the elemental composition of NiS NPs. The surface area, pore size, and pore volume of the photocatalyst were analyzed using the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) method with a Quantachrome NovaWin instrument, version 11.05. Nitrogen was used as the adsorbate at 77.350 K, with a molecular cross-sectional area of 16.200 Å2 and a liquid nitrogen density of 0.808 g/cm3. A Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) spectrometer (Burker Alpha II ATR FTIR) was used to measure the functional groups of NiS NPs and after RB5 photocatalytic degradation (light reaction) in the wavenumber (cm–1) frequency range of 500–4000 cm–1. For instance, XRD was realized at 40 kV and 40 mA in the scanning angle (2θ) range of 10–80° at a scan rate of 2° min–1 using a diffraction equipped with a Cu Kα radiation source. The standard diffraction data was compared, and unknown components were recognized. The Scherrer formula was utilized for the estimation of average crystallite sizes (D) of nanomaterials, eq .

D=Kλβcosθ 3

The Scherrer constant (K) represents the particle shape, and usually the K value is considered to be 0.9, λ exhibits the wavelength, θ indicates the diffraction angle, and β denotes the full width at half-maximum (fwhm) of the reflection peak. The hydrodynamic size (H R) (nm) and zeta potential (mV) of NiS NPs alone, in various salts, and after RB5 photocatalytic degradation were measured by DLS studies using zeta-sizer Nano ZSP, Panalytical Technologies, UK, with Malvern-Zeta-sizer v7.13 software. This instrument is capable of measuring in the diameter range for the size 0.3 nm to 15 μm and zeta potential range from 3.8 mV to 100 mV. The measurements were performed at 25 °C. Noninvasive 173° backscatter mode was used to measure the H R size at a wavelength of 633 nm. The light source used in the instrument is He–Ne with 10.0 mW of power.

SEM and EDX Analyses

The surface morphology of the NiS nanocrystallites is shown in Figure a. Some larger particles are visible, with some overlapping of the smaller particles. The SEM image shows a randomly distributed agglomerated nanoporous structure with a crystallite size of 7.08 nm (calculated by the Scherrer eq from XRD analysis). Figure b shows the elemental composition of the synthesized NiS NPs studied by EDX microanalysis. It confirms that the nanoparticles are composed of Ni and S. A small amount of oxygen was also detected, possibly due to the oxidation of a small amount of the product in the air. Traces of sodium metal and carbon are also present, which come from the precipitating agent Na2S and precursor salts, respectively.

UV–Visible and Fluorescence Spectra of NiS NPs

Figure a shows the UV–visible absorption spectra of NiS NPs. The absorption peak at 226 nm is sharp and fine, which confirms the successful synthesis of NiS NPs with a fine particle size distribution. The PL emission spectra of NiS NPs were recorded at an excitation wavelength of 226 nm, as shown in Figure b.

A narrow emission peak of NiS NPs was observed at 395 nm, with a small, narrow blue shift at 305 nm. The fluorescence emission peak at 395 nm could be attributed to sulfur vacancies and interstitial sulfur lattice defects. Figure b shows a general decrease in the PL intensity of NiS NPs in 0.1 M salt solutions. AgNO3 causes a lower decrease in the PL intensity, likely due to the minimal surface deposition, as supported by H R data in Figure a. In contrast, NaCl, CaCl2, and Na2SO4 exhibit stronger interactions with the catalyst surface, leading to a greater PL suppression.

Salt cations such as Na+ and Ca2+ form counterion shells around NiS NPs, decreasing zeta potential and increasing H R, leading to NP aggregation, as shown in Figure a,b. These interactions may alter local charge distribution, promoting charge carrier localization at defect sites and reducing fluorescence quantum yield. This property is suitable for the photocatalytic activity because it inhibits charge carriers’ recombination. These salts enhance the adsorption capability of NiS NPs for the RB5 dye and produce additional radicals along with the radicals generated by NiS NPs, which can participate in the RB5 photocatalytic degradation, thereby enhancing the photocatalytic efficiency of NiS NPs.

BET Specific Surface Area of NiS NPs

Figure a illustrates the nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherms of NiS NPs measured at 77 K. The isotherms were obtained using 20 relative pressure (P/P 0) points ranging from 0 to 1. Figure a resembles a type IV isotherm with a noticeable H3-type hysteresis loop at higher relative pressures (P/P 0 > 0.8), which is a characteristic of mesoporous materials with a significant contribution from interparticle voids or slit-like pores formed due to agglomeration of nanostructures, as seen in the SEM micrograph in Figure a.

BET analysis revealed a specific surface area of 161 m2/g for the NiS NPs, along with an average pore diameter of 0.139 nm and a total pore volume of 0.346 cm3/g, as calculated using the DFT method shown in Figure b. The Barrett–Joyner–Hallenda (BJH) analysis also confirmed the presence of mesoporous characteristics, although with limited resolution in the micropore region. Combined with the high surface area observed in BET analysis (161 m2/g), mesoporosity is highly favorable for applications such as photocatalysis, where efficient diffusion and adsorption processes of dye molecules like RB5 are crucial.

Adsorption Studies and Mechanism

Adsorption of the RB5 dye on the photocatalyst surface is a crucial step in measuring the effectiveness of photocatalysts in the RB5 photocatalytic degradation. Optimization Studies for the Photocatalytic Degradation of RB5 in the Presence of NiS NPs pH and Zeta Potential Analysis of NiS NPs

The photocatalytic degradation of RB5 dye molecules is significantly influenced by the suspension pH. Figure a shows that the highest RB5 adsorption, i.e., 58%, occurs on NiS NPs at basic solution pH 10, while acidic solution pH reveals poor adsorption of the RB5 dye on the catalyst surface. Upon irradiation of the reaction mixture for 60 min, NiS NPs reveal RB5 degradation efficiency of 92.07% at pH 10 and 33.3% at pH 2. As the reaction mixture turns more basic, the degradation of RB5 is enhanced, and hence, the rate of RB5 degradation increases in alkaline solutions. Figure b shows that the NPs get negatively charged as the solution pH increases, which increases the electrostatic attraction between NiS NPs and dye molecules because of the positive zeta potential of dye (11.8 ± 5.7 mV). High degradation of positively charged RB5 takes place in the basic medium, as many positively charged RB5 dye molecules can be adsorbed onto the surface of the catalyst via electrostatic interaction, and hence, more degradation takes place.

The zeta potential (surface charge) of a photocatalyst is the main characteristic factor in determining the colloidal stability of the catalyst suspension and also influences the catalytic efficiency of the photocatalyst. The zeta potential of NiS NPs was examined over a wide pH range of 2–10, as shown in Figure b.

The surface charge of NiS NPs can influence the interaction between the RB5 dye molecules and the catalyst’s surface. The zeta potentials of the NiS NPs at pH 2 and 10 are −5.84 ± 1.24 and −18.63 ± 0.53 mV, similar results have been observed in the given work.

It has been observed that the surface charge of NPs becomes more negative with alkaline pH as the adsorption of OH– ions occurs on the photocatalyst surface. Meanwhile, the surface charge decreases with the adsorption of H+ ions present in the NiS NPs suspension on the catalyst surface. Results also confirm more stability of NiS NPs at alkaline pH than under acidic pH.

Figure c shows the effect of variable initial dye concentration (20, 30, 40, and 50 mg/L) on the photocatalytic efficiency of NiS NPs at a fixed catalyst dose (0.03 g/L) and solution pH 6.5. The reaction was first kept in the dark to attain adsorption–desorption equilibrium for 30 min; about 21% (20 mg/L) of the dye was adsorbed on the NiS NPs surface, which increased to 31.7% for 40 mg/L and then decreased to 10.5% at 50 mg/L. In the light reaction, NiS NPs exhibited the highest RB5 degradation efficiency, i.e., 93.88% at 20 mg/L dye concentration, and afterward decreased to 59% at 50 mg/L dye concentration. The decrease in the degradation efficiency of NiS NPs is due to the blockage of the catalyst active sites.

Figure d shows the effect of NiS NP dose (0.015–0.06 g/L) on the photocatalytic efficiency of RB5 dye while keeping a fixed initial dye concentration of 50 mg/L and pH 6.5. Photocatalytic Kinetics Studies

Photocatalytic degradation rate is always linked with pollutant concentration, and this correlation is important to understanding the degradation mechanism. Figure a shows the decrease in photocatalytic degradation capability of NiS NPs as the RB5 concentration increases from 20 to 50 mg/L. This observation can be linked to the highest photogeneration of radicals such as ·OH and ·O2 from the NiS catalyst surface at lower RB5 dye concentration, which results in high RB5 degradation.

At high RB5 concentration, more dye molecules occupy active sites on the photocatalyst surface, which hinders radical generation and reduces the photocatalytic efficiency of nanoparticles. A more intense dye solution can also block the light from reaching the photocatalyst surface, which reduces the photogeneration of active species and subsequently decreases the photocatalytic degradation of the RB5 dye.

The L–H model is the most rigorous framework for heterogeneous photocatalysis, as it combines adsorption and photocatalytic degradation kinetics. The applicability of the L–H model has been confirmed by the linear plot obtained by plotting the reciprocal of the initial rate (1/r 0) against the reciprocal of the initial RB5 concentration (1/[RB5]0) depicted by a straight line (R 2 = 0.96), as shown in Figure b. The L–H analysis confirms that the degradation rate is governed by both adsorption equilibrium (K ads = 25.06 L/mg) and photocatalytic activity (K c = 0.090 mg/L·min), as demonstrated in recent studies. , The higher value of K ads corresponds to the dominant adsorption process, followed by the photocatalytic degradation of the RB5 dye.

The L–H model implies that adsorption of the dye is a significant step in deciding photodegradation rates. A heterogeneous photodegradation process occurs on the photocatalyst surface, where the generated radicals act on adsorbed dye molecules while reducing the electron–hole recombination rate and increasing the photodegradation efficiency. Figure b confirms the decreasing dye degradation rate of NiS NPs with a rising concentration of RB5, which corresponds to the Langmuir–Hinshelwood isotherm model.

Moreover, the adsorption and photocatalytic potential of NiS NPs for the RB5 dye was explored through computing the interaction energy of dye with the catalyst and bandgap calculations.

Theoretical Investigation for Potential Active Sites of RB5 Dye and Bandgap of NiS NPs

The computational study aimed to investigate the photocatalytic behavior of the RB5 dye using NiS as a photocatalyst, focusing on the tautomeric equilibrium between the azo and hydrozo forms of RB5. Azo dyes, including RB5, often exhibit tautomeric equilibrium due to the presence of a hydroxyl group either ortho or para to the azo group, allowing both the azo and hydrozo forms to coexist in aqueous media. This equilibrium is significantly stabilized by hydrogen bond interactions, as shown in Figure a.

A semiempirical method using the PM6 approach was employed to optimize the geometries of both tautomers of RB5 dye and to assess their interaction energies with NiS. Figure b shows the optimized structure of RB5 dye in both azo and hydrazo forms. Both tautomers of RB5 dye, when optimized to their lowest energy states, yielded energies of −2747 kcal/mol for the azo form and −2792 kcal/mol for the hydrozo form. The higher (more negative) energy of the hydrozo form, −2792 kcal/mol, confirms that it is the more stable tautomer. In many cases, the hydrozo form is the most stable and predominant tautomer in aqueous solutions.

It can be seen from Table that, during the interaction of both tautomers of RB5 with NiS NPs, the hydrozo form of RB5 exhibits favorable energetic properties for interaction with NiS. Particularly, the interaction at the N–N position showed a significant interaction energy of −76.19 kcal/mol. Photocatalytic Mechanism Study

The photocatalytic reaction starts once NiS NPs in the RB5 dye solution are exposed to light. NiS NPs have a proper band gap of 2.11 eV between the HOMO and the LUMO orbitals. Electrons from the HOMO transfer to the LUMO, facilitating the formation of electron–hole pairs. These charge carriers show subsequent interaction with the species present on the catalyst surface and in the surrounding environment, such as water and oxygen, to produce radicals, i.e., (·OH) and superoxide radicals (·O2 –) that help in the breakdown and mineralization of the RB5 dye, as shown Figure a. Moreover, the absorption spectra of RB5 after degradation reveal that the dye is degraded completely up to 99%, and the absence of peak shifting confirms that no intermediate compounds are formed at the end of the photocatalytic reaction. Nevertheless, the peaks at 313 and 393 nm were suppressed, which also demonstrated the degradation of the aromatic skeleton of RB5. A degradation reaction was also conducted upon visible light exposure without NiS NPs, where about 5% of RB5 dye was degraded, which testifies that NiS NPs play an important role in the RB5 dye degradation. ,

Moreover, trapping experiments were conducted to verify the role of reactive species generated by NiS NPs under visible light in RB5 degradation. These various quenchers, like PEG and isopropanol (IPA), are added in NiS NPs and RB5 suspension to quench OH·, while AgNO3 traps electrons in the conduction band, which are responsible for the formation of ·O2 –, while h+ in the valence band is responsible for the generation of ·OH. Figure b reveals a reduction in % degradation of RB5 by NiS NPs under PEG, AgNO3, and IPA by 57, 40, and 23%, respectively. This confirms the photogeneration of ·OH and O2 – · by NiS NPs as reactive species for the degradation of the RB5 dye. Figure c reveals the degradation profile of RB5 dye by NiS NPs, which shows that about 99% of the dye was degraded after 60 min of illumination.

Reusability and Stability of NiS NPs

Photocatalyst reusability and stability are key characteristics for practical applications. The reusability performance is typically assessed by monitoring the decline in the photocatalytic degradation efficiency over succeeding cycles.

Figure a shows that NiS NPs maintained high photocatalytic activity, achieving 92% photocatalytic degradation of RB5 dye even until the sixth cycle. A slight decrease in performance was observed in subsequent cycles, with degradation efficiencies of 88% in the seventh cycle and 85% in the 10th cycle, indicating a good reusability performance of NiS NPs.

Figure b shows the FTIR spectra of pure NiS NPs and the spectra after successive cycles for RB5 photocatalytic degradation. The NiS stretching is present at 609 cm–1 as discussed earlier in Figure a. Spectral analysis confirms that NiS NPs retain their structural stability even after 10th cycles of reuse, as no prominent changes were observed in their spectra. Following the first cycle of RB5 dye photocatalytic degradation, the spectra of NiS NPs showed no noticeable alterations, indicating the complete degradation of RB5 dye molecules. However, with successive cycles, minor spectral variations appeared in the 1000–1650 cm–1 region.

These changes correspond to functional groups associated with residual RB5 dye, including sulfoxide (−S=O−) at 1122 cm–1, aromatic hydroxyl (C–OH) at 1045 cm–1, and C–C stretching vibrations of the benzene ring at 1609 cm–1. The absorption peak at 3636 cm–1 in the degraded RB5 sample is attributed to the O–H stretching mode, which is normally caused by adsorbed water molecules.

Conclusions

The photocatalytic degradation of RB5 by NiS NPs is investigated in the presence of various salts such as NaCl, CaCl2, Na2SO4, Na2S, CH3COONa, and AgNO3. DLS was employed to monitor dye degradation by observing hydrodynamic radius and zeta potential changes of NiS NPs in various salts. Variations in these parameters confirmed the interaction of NiS NPs with RB5 dye and indicated its degradation. Increased H R and zeta potential after dye photocatalytic degradation suggest the deposition of degradation products on the catalyst surface. NiS NPs achieved 74.3% dye degradation, which increased to 99.6% with NaCl, as evidenced by an increased H R and reduced surface charge. Photocatalytic degradation efficiency of NiS NPs with salts follows the order: NaCl (99.6%) > Na2S (91.4%) > CaCl2 (89.9%) > Na2SO4 (84%) > CH3COONa (79.4%) > NiS alone (74.3%) > AgNO3 (40%). H R analysis of NiS NPs shows that stronger salt interactions enhance RB5 adsorption followed by degradation. Moreover, zeta potential studies exhibited an overall decrease in stability upon interaction with salts. Control studies showed that salts alone did not affect RB5 photodegradation. Scavengers, i.e., PEG, IPA, and AgNO3, reduce the photocatalytic efficiency of NiS NPs by scavenging ·OH, O2 – ·, and e–/h+. Optimal conditions were pH 6.5, 0.5 g/L dose, and 40 and 50 mg/L dye in 60 mL of solution at 25 ± 1 °C. High RB5 concentration hindered light penetration to the NiS surface, reducing radical generation and photocatalytic efficiency. Further, NiS NPs demonstrate stability and more RB5 degradation at a higher solution pH. HPLC analysis affirmed RB5 degradation, while TOC analysis confirmed dye mineralization. The reusability study showed 85% of RB5 dye was removed after the 10th cycle, suitable for practical applications. FTIR analysis confirmed the presence of degraded products after the 10th cycle on the NPs surface, which is not present in the first cycle. The adsorption of RB5 dye by NiS NPs is better described by the Langmuir isotherm than by the Freundlich isotherm and follows a PFO kinetic model. Moreover, the reaction followed the L–H kinetic expression of heterogeneous photocatalysis, having a higher K ads value (25.06 L/mg) as compared to the K C value (0.090 mg/L·min), which confirms the favorable adsorption of dye followed by its degradation. These findings demonstrate that NiS NPs are effective photocatalysts in the presence of visible light and hold strong potential for practical wastewater treatment applications, even in the presence of inorganic salts. NiS NPs tend to aggregate, reducing their dispersion in water and the availability of active sites, which lowers the catalytic efficiency. Doping NiS with metals or nonmetals and functionalizing its surface may enhance charge separation, prevent aggregation, and increase its dye affinity for further improved photocatalytic performance.
