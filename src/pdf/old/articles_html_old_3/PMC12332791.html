Abstract

Ammonia (NH3) is increasingly recognized as an important sustainable energy carrier, and methane (CH4) is typically added to combustion systems to enhance their combustion performance. Reaction pathways of NH3/CH4 combustion require further clarification. In the present study, the key reactions of NH3 oxidation and NO formation in NH3/CH4 combustion were revealed at the subatomic level. The CCSD­(T)/cc-pvqz//B3LYP/6-311 + G­(d,p) and CBS-QB3 methods were used to calculate the molecular structures, transition states, and energy barriers of all reactions. The rate constants were calculated by the transition state theory with the Wigner tunneling correction, and the results in the present study agree well with previous experimental data and theoretical calculation. Moreover, rate constants were fitted to the Arrhenius equation over the temperature range of 300–2500 K to obtain activation energies. Notably, rate constants and activation energies were determined for the reactions (NH3 + CH3 → NH2 + CH4 and HNO + HO2 → NO + H2O2) that were revealed by the present study. This study will contribute to the understandings of fuel performance in the new era for sustainable energy.

1. Introduction

Ammonia (NH3) is considered a promising alternative fuel to replace traditional hydrocarbon fuels. However, the low flame speed and high autoignition temperature limit its wide applications for direct usage in power systems. − To enhance the combustion performance of NH3, methane (CH4) is usually added to NH3 combustion systems. , A comprehensive understanding of the NH3/CH4 combustion mechanism and the establishment of an accurate chemical kinetic model are vital for accurately predicting the NH3/CH4 combustion performance, controlling NOx emissions, and guiding practical applications.

Since the 1980s, extensive research employing both experimental investigations and theoretical modeling has been conducted to elucidate the high-temperature pyrolysis mechanisms of ammonia. The foundational work of Miller and Smooke, Lindstedt et al., and Bian et al. established the theoretical framework for ammonia combustion and oxidation mechanisms. Notably, Miller and Smooke developed the first detailed ammonia combustion mechanism, which described NH3 oxidation and NO formation mechanisms. Based on the established fundamental NH3 combustion mechanisms, researchers systematically optimized and modified these kinetic models to develop extended mechanisms capable of predicting combustion characteristics for various ammonia-blended fuels.

Early kinetic mechanisms of NH3/CH4 combustion included Skreiberg’s mechanism, Konnov’s mechanism, Gri-Mech mechanism, Tian’s mechanism, and Mendiara’s mechanism. Subsequent studies have developed refined kinetic mechanisms to accurately describe the NH3/CH4 combustion behavior under varying operating conditions. For example, Shu et al. developed a mechanism for high-temperature and high-pressure NH3/CH4 combustion, Glarborg et al. established a mechanism for accurate NOx emission prediction, Li et al. proposed an NH3/CH4/H2 mechanism for combustion product formation at wide equivalence ratios, Zhu et al. proposed a mechanism to predict laminar burning velocity and radical changes in NH3/CH4 combustion, and Zhang et al. employed ab initio calculations for key H-abstraction reactions, achieving good prediction of NH3 oxidation under engine conditions.

In the NH3/CH4 combustion system, NH3 consumption and NOx formation pathways have been widely investigated. The current kinetic mechanisms describe NH3 consumption as proceeding predominantly through thermal decomposition (NH3 + M ↔ NH2 + H) and reactions with radicals/molecules (NH3 + H ↔ NH2 + H2, NH3 + OH ↔ NH2 + H2O, NH3 + O ↔ NH2 + OH, NH3 + HO2 ↔ NH2 + H2O2, and NH3 + O2 ↔ NH2 + HO2). − For NOx formation, the HNO intermediate has been recognized as crucial for fuel NO production. – The established conversion pathways from HNO to NO include reactions with major radicals/molecules, including HNO + O ↔ NO + OH, HNO + H ↔ NO + H2, HNO + OH ↔ NO + H2O, HNO + O2 ↔ NO + HO2, and HNO + NO2 ↔ NO + HNO2. −

In our previous studies, − the reactive force field (ReaxFF) molecular dynamics (MD) method was used to study NH3/CH4 combustion in air, and reaction pathways were proposed by tracing the atomic trajectories. New reaction pathways were identified using the ReaxFF MD method. For example, the CH3 radical promotes NH3 oxidation (NH3 + CH3 ↔ NH2 + CH4) in NH3/CH4 combustion, and the HO2 radical reacts with HNO to generate NO (HNO + HO2 ↔ NO + H2O2). In the previous ReaxFF MD studies, only the reaction pathways were reported. Kinetic data of the new reaction pathways were still lacking. For instance, the molecular structure, energy barrier, and transition states remain unknown for the newly proposed reactions. Meanwhile, the kinetic models of the reactions in previous studies vary significantly, and the reaction pathways of NH3/CH4 combustion was incomplete. Therefore, a systematic study of the combustion process of NH3/CH4 from a kinetic perspective is still needed.

In the present study, the high-accuracy quantum chemistry method is used to calculate the molecular structures, energy barriers, and transition states for reactions of NH3 oxidation and NO formation. The rate constants are calculated by the transition state theory (TST), and the results are compared with previous experimental and theoretical studies. The activation energies in the Arrhenius equations are calculated for all reactions. Furthermore, comprehensive reaction pathways and accurate reaction kinetic parameters are revealed based on the calculations.

2. Methods

Based on density functional theory calculations, the B3LYP hybrid functional has been widely adopted to obtain optimized configurations and energy values via quantum chemistry calculations, and the B3LYP is used to maintain computational cost within an acceptable range while the computational accuracy is guaranteed. − In the present study, a conformational analysis was conducted using B3LYP, with the 6-311 + G­(d,p) , basis set, to discern the minimum energy conformers of chemical species and transition states. Vibrational analysis was used to confirm that each transition state had only one imaginary frequency. IRC calculations were performed to confirm that the optimized transition state structures correctly connected the corresponding reactants and products. For the calculation of electronic single-point energies, coupled-cluster theory CCSD­(T) with the cc-pvqz , basis set was applied due to the high accuracy of the method. The CCSD­(T) is a wave function method, which can be used to accurately calculate electronic energy, and its calculation results are often used as the benchmark accuracy. To further improve the accuracy of the calculations, the CBS-QB3 method was also employed to calculate the energies of all of the optimized species and transition states. CBS-QB3 is a high-accuracy composite computational method that directly yields reaction barriers via basis set extrapolation, widely used in combustion reaction kinetics research. , Finally, the energies were averaged over the results from the CCSD­(T)/cc-pvqz//B3LYP/6-311 + g­(d,p) and CBS-QB3 methods. All of the calculations were carried out on Gaussian 16, and the corresponding sketches were visualized by GaussView software.

The energy generally refers to the Gibbs free energy in the present study, which can be calculated by eq .

G=E+ZPE−TS 1

Here, G is the Gibbs free energy, E is the electronic energy, ZPE is the zero-point vibrational energy, T is the temperature in Kelvin, and S is the entropy. In the present study, the temperature is 298.15 K and the pressure is 1 atm.

The energy barrier was considered the numerical difference of the energy between the reactant and the transition state (as shown in eq ).

Geb=GTS−Gr 2

Here, G eb is the energy barrier, G TS is the energy of the transition state, and G r is the energy of the reactant.

Rate constants were calculated using the TST, and the Wigner tunneling correction was considered for the quantum tunneling effect.

The reaction rate was calculated by eq .

K(T)=k(T)×kTST 3

Here, k(T) is the quantum tunneling effect, and k TST is the approximate reaction rate. k(T) is calculated by eq , and k TST is calculated by eq .

k(T)=1+124(hvmckBT)2 4
kTST=(kBTh)(QTS(T)QR(T))exp(−ΔERT) 5

Here, v m is the imaginary frequency of the transition state, c is the velocity of light, k B is the Boltzmann constant, h is the Planck constant, Q TS is the partition function of the transition state, Q R is the partition function of reactant, ΔE is the energy barrier, and R is the gas constant.

The temperature-dependent rates of the investigated reactions were fitted to the three-parameter modified Arrhenius expression by eq .

k=ATnexp(−Ea/R*T) 6

Here, A is the frequency factor, n is temperature exponent, and E a is the activation energy.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. NH3 Oxidation

In each reaction process, the transition states are denoted as “TS” followed by a number, the intermediates as “IM” followed by a number, and the products as “P” followed by a number.

Figure shows the energy barriers and transition state structures for the reactions of NH3 with various radicals/molecules (OH, H, O, HO2, O2, and CH3). The reaction of reactants begins with the prereactive complex IM and proceeds via the transition state TS and the postreactive complex P to the final products. In these reactions, a hydrogen atom in NH3 is abstracted by the radical/molecule, forming NH2 and a hydrogenated product. For example, OH abstracts a H atom to produce H2O, and H abstracts a H atom to form H2. The energy barrier associated with each transition state determines the hydrogen abstraction ability and the reactivity of the radical species.

Table lists the average energies for the reactions of NH3 with radicals and molecules using different calculation methods. The energy barrier of the NH3 + OH reaction is the lowest (2.48 kcal/mol), which agrees with that reported by Zhang et al. (2.90 kcal/mol). From an energy perspective, NH3 exhibits the highest reactivity with OH under atmospheric pressure conditions. The reaction of NH3 and the OH radical has the lowest energy barrier among all reactions. The energy barrier of NH3 and O is 4.86 kcal/mol, and the energy barrier of NH3 and H is 11.82 kcal/mol. The CH3 radical also contributes to the consumption of NH3. The energy barrier for the reaction between NH3 and CH3 is calculated. The energy barrier of NH3+CH3 is 13.80 kcal/mol, which is lower than the energy barriers of NH3 reacting with HO2 (18.54 kcal/mol) and O2 (54.57 kcal/mol).

3.2. Reaction Pathways of NO Formation by HNO

HNO is a primary intermediate in the formation of fuel NO. − In addition to fuel NO, thermal NO is formed at high temperatures (>1800 K) through the oxidation of N2. The formation mechanism of thermal NO is explained by the extended Zeldovich mechanism. Figure shows the energy barriers for the reaction of HNO with radicals and molecules such as H, OH, HO2, and NO2 to generate NO. The H radical abstracts a hydrogen atom from HNO to produce NO and H2. The OH radical abstracts hydrogen to form NO and H2O. The reaction with HO2 generates NO and H2O2 via hydrogen abstraction. The reaction with NO2 proceeds through hydrogen transfer, producing NO and HONO.

The reaction energies calculated by different methods are listed in Table . Among all of the reactions regarding NO formation by HNO, the OH radical has the highest reactivity and is most likely to attract H atoms. The energy barrier for the reaction between HNO and the OH radical is the lowest, which is −2.95 kcal/mol. The energy barrier of the reaction between HNO and H is 0.53 kcal/mol, and the energy barrier of the reaction between HNO and HO2 is 1.48 kcal/mol. The reaction between HNO and NO2 produces two forms of HONO, namely, trans-HONO and cis-HONO. The HO–NO dihedral angle in trans-HONO is 180°, and the HO–NO dihedral angle in cis-HONO is 90°. The transition state structures for the formation of cis-HONO and trans-HONO are different, and their energies also vary. The energy for generating NO and cis-HONO is 4.64 kcal/mol, which is lower than the energy for generating NO and trans-HONO (9.92 kcal/mol).

3.3. Reaction Pathways of NO Formation by H2NO and HNO2

HNO2 and H2NO are important intermediates in NH3/CH4 combustion, participating in direct fuel NO formation pathways. ,, HNO2 has five isomers, namely, cis-HONO, trans-HONO, cis-HNOO, trans-HNOO, and HNO2. The NH–OO dihedral angle of 90° is cis-HNOO, and 180° is trans-HNOO. Figure a shows the energy barrier for the conversion of cis-HONO to HNO2. The energies of different calculation methods and their average values are listed in Table . The torsional energy barrier between cis-HONO and trans-HONO is 10.32 kcal/mol, which is consistent with that reported by Chen et al. (10.6 kcal/mol). Due to the sufficiently large torsional energy barrier between cis-HONO and trans-HONO, the two rotational isomers can maintain obvious differences at high temperatures and show different reactivity. The energy of trans-HONO to generate HNO2 is 55.70 kcal/mol, in which the H atom is transferred from the O atom to the N atom to form a nitrogen and oxygen double bond (NO). Figure b shows the energy barrier diagram for the generation of NO by cis-HNOO. The reaction process includes the following three steps: the dihedral angle between H–O and O–O is twisted from 90° to 180° (cis-HNOO → trans-HNOO), the H atom is transferred from the N atom to one of the O atoms, then the H atom moves away and generates OH with another O atom, and the N and O atoms generate NO. The step with the highest energy in the whole process is the second transfer of H atoms (trans-HNOO → IM1), which is also the most difficult step in the process of HNOO generating NO.

The accurate activation energies of H2NO and HNO2 isomer conversion and the reaction of NO generation were calculated at a higher level of theory in the present study, which can accurately predict NO emissions.

4. Conclusions

In this study, the kinetic mechanism for the key reaction pathways of NH3 oxidation and NO formation was systematically studied. High-accuracy quantum chemistry method was used to calculate the molecular structures, transition states, and energy barriers of the reactions. The B3LYP/6-311 + G­(d,p) method was used for geometry optimization and frequency calculations, and the CCSD­(T)/cc-pvqz and CBS-QB3 methods were used for energy calculations. The results indicate that NH3 is oxidized by radicals/molecules including OH, H, HO2, CH3, O, and O2 to generate NH2. The barrier energy of the reaction between NH3 and OH is the lowest, and the barrier energy of the reaction between NH3 and O2 is the highest among all of the NH3-rerlated reactions. HNO reacts with OH, H, HO2, NO2, and O2 radicals/molecules to generate NO. The reaction between HNO and OH has the lowest energy barrier, and the reaction between HNO and O2 has the highest energy barrier with respect to O2. The energy barriers for the formation of NO from HNO2 and H2NO were also calculated. Temperature-dependent rate coefficients for all reactions were calculated based on the TST and corrected for tunneling using the Wigner tunneling correction factor. The rate constants calculated in this study agree well with the experimental data and previous theoretical calculations in the literature. The rate constants of the new reaction pathways discovered by this study were calculated as well. The activation energies in the Arrhenius equation for individual reactions were determined in the temperature range of 300–2500 K. Given the increasing interest in NH3 as a sustainable fuel, the findings from this study will add knowledge to our understandings about the performance of alternative fuels in the energy transition period.
