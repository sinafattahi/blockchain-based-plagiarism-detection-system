Abstract

Regarding the incomplete understanding of the influence mechanisms of microstructural differences in coal on outburst propensity, this study selected typical outburst and nonoutburst coal samples for comparative experimental investigations. The microchemical and pore structure characteristics of coal samples were systematically characterized through analytical techniques including Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR), Raman spectroscopy, X-ray diffraction (XRD), and low-temperature liquid nitrogen adsorption experiments to elucidate how these microstructural features influence their outburst propensity. The results of the study show that outburst coal samples possess a higher degree of aromaticity, with hydrogen atoms migrating to new chemical environments that give rise to abundant, low-crystallinity nascent primary aromatic hydrocarbons and substantial CH4 generation. Concurrently, the number of oxygen-containing functional groups in the coal is less, and the coal samples have a stronger adsorption capacity for gas. In outburst coal samples, the spatial structure of macromolecular structures is chaotic and disordered, and discontinuities or defects in the microcrystalline framework lead to an increased micropore content. Comparison of the pore structure characteristics of the outburst and nonoutburst coal samples revealed that the outburst coal samples had more total pore volume, more pore content of the coal body per unit volume, and a thinner coal body skeleton, which made the coal body less resistant to damage. The proportion of micropores in the outburst coal samples is even higher and is dominated by fine-necked bottleneck holes and wedge-shaped holes, which increases the resistance to gas desorption and the residual gas content of the coal body and increases the risk of coal and gas outburst. This study provides a theoretical basis for understanding the influence of coal microstructure parameters on coal outburst characteristics.

1. Introduction

Gas is an associated gas resource in the coal formation process and consists mainly of methane. Since the early 19th century, gas has been recognized as a major source of hazards affecting the safety of coal mining. , Gas accidents mainly include sudden gas outbursts, coal and gas outbursts, gas explosions, etc. According to statistics, gas accidents account for 45% of the total number of coal mine accidents in China. Among them, coal and gas outburst (hereinafter referred to as outburst) is a kind of complex dynamic disaster in the process of coal mining, which has been threatening the safe production of coal mines. Inherent properties of coal samples play an important role in coal and gas outbursts. ,

In recent years, scholars have carried out a large number of studies on macroscopic mechanical properties and seepage characteristics for both outburst and nonoutburst coal samples. , The elastic limit of outburst coal is less than that of nonoutburst coal. The yield stress of outburst coal is smaller than that of nonoutburst coal. The smaller the yield stress is, the easier it is for the combined action of the ground stress and the gas to reach the yield stress of the coal rock and destabilize and damage the coal sample, and the more prone to coal and gas outburst. , Nonoutburst coals have higher permeability, and gas is more easily transported through the seams. Gas escapes from the coal seam to the roof and floor. It is not easy to produce a gas concentration in the coal seam, which reduces the gas content in the coal seam. Therefore, a small amount of gas accumulated in coal seam mining is difficult to reach the energy required for coal and gas outburst, and the risk of coal and gas outburst is low. , The mechanical and seepage macroscopic characteristics of coal samples are the environmental basis of the coal and gas outburst. The microstructure characteristics of coal samples are the fundamental factors affecting whether coal samples have outburst characteristics. Lin found that the permeability characteristics of coal are closely related to the microscopic pore characteristics. The larger the pore volume of coal samples, the larger the specific surface area and adsorption/desorption characteristics of coal with strong capacity. The permeability characteristics of the coal is relatively poor, the strength of the coal body is lower and more prone to damage, and the risk of outburst is greater. Sun et al. , analyzed the effects of pore structure and chemical functional groups on CH4 and CO2 adsorption of coals with different degrees of metamorphism by Raman spectroscopy and molecular simulation techniques and found that the microporous volume and specific surface area were positively correlated with the adsorption amount, which reveals that the microscopic adsorption mechanism of coals and the degree of metamorphism of coals have a significant effect on the distribution of the pore structure and functional groups, which in turn affects the adsorption performance. Bin et al. used FTIR, XRD, and DFT simulation to study the effect of coalification on the microstructural characteristics of coal, including aromaticity, aliphatic structure, and functional group changes, and found that the degree of coal metamorphism is closely related to its microstructural characteristics. Further research scholars carried out studies on the structural characteristics of outburst and nonoutburst coal samples. Outburst coal samples were mostly dominated by tectonic coals, and tectonics increased the total pore volume and specific surface area of the coals, , while the adsorption/desorption capacity and diffusion coefficients of tectonic coals were generally higher, while the compressive strength and modulus of elasticity were generally smaller. Wang et al. , investigated the chemical structure and hydrocarbon production characteristics of tectonic and primary coals with different degrees of metamorphism and their effects on gas adsorption capacity and found that tectonics changed the chemical structure of the coals, reduced the hydrocarbon production potential, and promoted the evolution of macromolecular structure, and the chemical structure parameters were closely related to the gas adsorption capacity.

In summary, scholars have carried out a lot of related research on the microstructure differences and gas adsorption and desorption capacity of different coal types, but there is a lack of systematic analysis of the microstructure differences between outburst and nonoutburst coal samples, and there are few studies on how the microstructure affects the outburst risk. Therefore, in this paper, Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy, laser Raman spectroscopy, X-ray diffraction spectroscopy experiments, and low-temperature nitrogen adsorption experiments were used to study the microstructure changes of outburst coal and nonoutburst coal and to explore the microstructure characteristics of outburst coal so as to provide reference for the prevention and control of outburst disasters.

2.2. Experimental Methods

  • (1)

    Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy

A Bruker VERTEX-80v infrared spectrometer manufactured by Bruker, Germany, was used. Spectral acquisition parameters were set with a 400–4000 cm–1 wavenumber window, achieving 0.06 cm–1 spectral resolution through 64 coadditions for signal optimization.

  • (2)

    Raman Spectroscopy

The instrument used was a Bruker Senterra confocal Raman microspectrometer. The Raman system was configured with a 532 nm diode laser operating at 5 mW incident power, 2 s acquisition time per spectrum, and 10-scan coaveraging protocol, covering 0–3700 cm–1 stokes shift range with variable spectral resolution (9–18 cm–1) across the detector array.

  • (3)

    X-ray diffraction spectroscopy

The D8 Advance powder crystal X-ray diffractometer was used. The test parameters were set as follows: the test angle range was 4–70°, the test power was 3 KW, the anode target material was Cu, the radius of the tester was 250 mm, the emission slit was 0.6 mm, the antiscattering slit was 8 mm, the scanning speed was 0.1 s/step, and the sampling interval was 0.01945 step.

  • (4)

    Low-temperature liquid nitrogen adsorption experiment

Nitrogen is a chemically stable inert gas at low temperatures and is often used in adsorption experiments. Nitrogen can be adsorbed on the surface of a solid, and desorption occurs when the gas molecules can overcome the force field on the solid surface. Adsorption equilibrium is reached when the rates of adsorption and desorption are the same. The curve of the adsorption equilibrium as a function of pressure is called an adsorption isotherm. By studying the adsorption isotherm, structural parameters such as surface area, pore volume, and pore size distribution of the coal sample can be obtained.

3. Results

3.1. Analysis of Experimental Results of Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy

Functional groups of coal are atoms or groups of atoms on the edge of the basic unit of chemical structure, which are the key factors dominating the physicochemical properties of the coal body. The characteristic IR absorption bands originate from quantized vibrational modes of functional groups, whose spectral positions are governed by harmonic oscillator parameters derived from bond dissociation energies and molecular geometry. Therefore, in this paper, the characteristic information on vibrational absorption peaks in FTIR is utilized to explore the microstructural differences between outburst and nonoutburst coals. Based on the positions, intensities, frequencies, and vibrational modes of different absorption peaks in the FTIR spectra, the types and numbers of functional groups in coal were analyzed. It was shown that the FTIR spectra of coal can be divided into four parts, namely, hydroxyl groups (OH) (3600–3000 cm–1), aliphatic hydrocarbons (CH3, CH2) (3000–2700 cm–1), oxygen-containing functional groups (CO, COOH, C–O) (1800–1000 cm–1), and aromatic hydrocarbons (benzene rings) (700–900 cm–1). Compared with nonoutburst coals, the ratio of fat chain length and degree of branched chainization (I 1) of the outburst coals decreased, indicating that the number of methylene groups in the outburst coal samples decreased, and the length of the fat chain of the coal samples became shorter, producing a large number of short-chained aliphatic hydrocarbons with a flimsier skeleton. The ratio of Aryl lipid ratio (I 2) of the outburst coal increased, and the degree of aromatization of the coal sample was higher; i.e., the hydrogen in the coal was transferred to the chemical environment so that the content of aliphatic hydrogen was relatively reduced, and a large number of nascent primary aromatic hydrocarbons with low crystallinity were produced, which made the structure of the sample disordered and unorganized. The decrease in the degree of aryl ring condensation (I 3) value of the outburst coal indicates that the degree of polymerization of aromatic rings in the coal structure is reduced, and there are more unpolymerized or low polymerization degree aromatic ring structures in the coal molecules. The results of previous studies show that the amount of aromatic hydrocarbons in coal samples is directly proportional to the adsorption capacity of the coal body for gas. As the relative content of aromatic hydrocarbons increased, the reactive groups related to the affinity for gas increased, leading to a decrease in the surface energy of the coal and an increase in the adsorption capacity. , The increase in the number of gas molecules adsorbed by the coal body provides a gas power source for coal-gas outburst, which further corroborates the difference in functional group structure between outburst and nonoutburst coal samples.

3.2. Analysis of Raman Spectroscopy Experimental Results

Raman spectroscopy can detect the crystalline, molecular, and amorphous structures of substances and is an effective complement to infrared spectroscopy because of its simple sample preparation, high resolution, and high sensitivity. The Raman spectrum of coal can be divided into three absorption peak bands: wedge peak (0–1000 cm–1), the first level of spectral peak order region (1000–2000 cm–1), and the second level of spectral peak order region (2200–3400 cm–1). There are generally two distinct characteristic peaks in the primary order region D and G peaks, which have been widely studied because their spectral peak characteristics are closely related to the structural composition of macromolecules and the degree of ordering. , In this paper, in order to study the degree of defects and order of the coal lattice in the outburst and nonoutburst regions, the spectral peak features of the first-order region of Raman spectroscopy are selected to be discussed. As depicted in Figure , which shows the Raman spectra of three types of coals after baseline correction, it is distinctly observable that within the range of 1000–2000 cm–1, the two primary characteristic peaks of the first-order spectral peaks, namely the D-peak and the G-peak, are situated at the positions of the D-peak (1300–1400 cm–1) and the G-peak (1560–1620 cm–1).

From Figure , it is evident that the Raman spectra of the three coal samples exhibit comparable characteristics, which demonstrates the overall similarity of the coal structure. The differences in the positions of the D and G peaks between the outburst and nonoutburst coals are small, but the peak heights and sharpnesses are large. The peak heights of the outburst coal samples are lower and the peak widths are larger, while the nonoutburst samples, on the contrary, have the double-peak heights increased and the peaks are sharper and sharper. The G peaks are related to the expansion and contraction vibration of the carbon–carbon (CC) double bond (respiratory vibration of the aromatic ring) in the coal macromolecular structure, which corresponds to the previously mentioned fact that the CC bonds account for the largest proportion in the XQ coal. As the Raman spectral characteristic parameters of the double peaks need to be calculated, the Raman spectral first-order modes of the coal samples were fitted to the peaks using Peak Fit 4.0 and Origin software to quantitatively analyze the molecular structures. In the first-order region, for all five coal samples, the absorption peaks were identified as the D and G peaks. Through split-peak simulation, the following parameters were obtained for each coal sample: the peak position of the D peak (denoted as WD/cm–1), the peak position of the G peak (WG/cm–1), the difference in peak positions between the D and G peaks (PG-D/cm–1), the full width at half-maximum of the D peak (fwhm-D), the full width at half-maximum of the G peak (fwhm-G), the peak intensity of the D peak (I D), the peak intensity of the G peak (I G), and the ratio of the peak intensities of the D and G peaks (I D/I G). The analyses were developed based on Figure .

    Peak position difference (G–D)

It has been shown that with the increase of coalification degree, the disordered small molecules in the coal body gradually polymerize to the stable and ordered aromatic ring structure. In Raman spectra, this is reflected in the right shift of the G peak position and the left shift of the D peak position, and the peak position difference in PG‑D between the D and G peaks can reflect the thermal maturity of the coal body. From Table , it can be seen that the peak position difference PG‑D of the outburst coal is small, 210.81 cm–1 and 223.13 cm–1, while that of the nonoutburst coal reaches 233.99 cm–1, which indicates that the molecular structure of outburst coal is more disorganized, with a higher proportion of impurity molecules, which is easy to occur coal and gas outburst. However, the small molecules of the disordered outburst coal body gradually begin to polymerize and transform to the stable and orderly aromatic ring structure, which makes the thermal maturity of the coal body gradually increase and the risk of outburst decrease. Therefore, the nonoutburst coal samples are characterized by a larger peak position difference.

From the gas outburst point of view, nonoutburst coal exhibits fewer microcrystalline structure defects, whereas outburst coal has a greater number of such defects. This phenomenon can be accounted for by the more disordered nature of the microcrystalline structure in outburst coal, which has a lower level of order, and the gas is easier to store and prone to outburst, while the microcrystalline structure defects of nonoutburst coal are fewer, and the structure is tighter with fewer micropores, which hinders the outburst of the gas to a certain extent.

3.3. Analysis of X-ray Diffraction Spectroscopy Results

Coal receives the combined effect of many factors during its formation, which makes the aromatic microcrystalline structure of coal molecules vary, which is manifested in the XRD patterns as differences in the diffraction peaks. The XRD diffractograms of the two coal samples, as presented in Figure , exhibit a distinct graphite diffraction peak (peak 002) positioned close to 2θ = 25° in the spectrum, which mainly reflects the stacking height of the aromatic lamellae of the coal samples and the degree of directional arrangement of the aromatic lamellae spatially; an obvious γpeak emerges at approximately 2θ = 21°, and this peak is associated with the aliphatic carbon structure within the coal molecules.

3.4. Differences in the Microscopic Pore Structure of Coal Samples

Pore structure is one of the important microscopic properties of coal samples. The isothermal adsorption curves of coal samples are shown in Figure by comparing the pore structure characteristics of outburst and nonoutburst coal samples through low-temperature nitrogen adsorption experiments to analyze the pore structure variability of different types of coal samples. The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) classified the adsorption isotherms of porous materials into 6 major categories, while 2 subbranches were added later, and the type of desorption hysteresis can be classified into 5 major categories. The adsorption isotherm of the coal sample conforms to the type IV isotherm.

By the interpretation of the BET equation and Kelvin equation, monolayer adsorption of N2 on the coal surface occurs when the relative pressure P/P 0 is less than 0.2. The isothermal adsorption curve tends to flatten when the relative pressure of P/P 0 is between 0.2 and 0.8. Multilayer adsorption of N2 is mainly manifested in this stage. When the relative pressure P/P 0 is greater than 0.8, the isothermal adsorption curve rises rapidly, and capillary coalescence of N2 in coal occurs. As shown in Figure , the isothermal adsorption–desorption curves of the coal samples have obvious desorption hysteresis phenomena. The type of hysteresis loop is related to the pore structure of the coal. The rapid desorption phenomenon of the nonoutburst XQ coal sample when the relative pressure P/P 0 is 0.45 indicates that the pores in the coal sample are dominated by open pores. When a certain relative pressure was reached inside and outside the pores, all adsorbed gases were desorbed. However, the adsorbed gas was not completely desorbed after the desorption hysteresis phenomenon in prominent coal samples PBW and PBJ, and the adsorbed gas was retained. This indicates that the narrower bottleneck pores and wedge-shaped pores in the outburst coal samples PBW and PBJ are dominated by the adsorbed gases, which are not easy to desorb, and the adsorbed gases in the pores are released rapidly after being disturbed, which increases the risk of coal-gas outburst.

Based on the liquid nitrogen isothermal adsorption curves, the relationships between pore diameter, pore volume, and specific surface area were calculated for both outburst and nonoutburst coal samples. IUPAC defines less than 2 nm pores as micropores, 2–50 nm as mesopores, and more than 50 nm as macropores. As shown in Figure , the proportion of micropores in coal samples to the total pore volume of coal samples is small, in which the proportion of micropores in nonoutburst XQ coal samples is the lowest, and there are two peaks in their mesopore segments; the mesopore pore volume dominates in the total pore volume. Therefore, the microporous structure of the outburst and nonoutburst coal samples is characterized by the transfer from micropores to mesopores. At the same time, the total pore volume of the outburst coal samples is larger, so the skeleton of the coal body becomes thinner per unit pore volume, which makes the antidestructive capacity of the coal body decrease and the susceptibility of the coal body to damage increase.

As shown in Figure , the specific surface area distribution of the three coal samples has similarity; the specific surface area corresponding to micropores in the coal samples has the highest percentage, and the specific surface area of micropores plays a dominant role in the specific surface area of pores in the coal samples. The specific surface area of the outburst coal samples PBW and PBJ is much larger than that of the nonprotruding XQ coal samples, and the percentage of the specific surface area of the microporous segments of the outburst coal samples is larger than that of the nonoutburst coal samples. Previous studies have shown that the pore surface is the main place for gas adsorption, and the higher specific surface area of the protruding coal samples provides the possibility for a large amount of gas adsorption. , The presence of a large number of micropores and semiclosed bottleneck pores leads to poor permeability and connectivity of the coal body, which further increases the risk of coal-gas protrusion.

4. Discussion

In this paper, with the help of Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR), laser Raman spectroscopy (Raman), X-ray diffraction spectroscopy (XRD), and low-temperature liquid nitrogen adsorption test, the differences between the outburst and nonoutburst coal samples were systematically investigated from a microscopic point of view. The FTIR experimental analysis showed that the amount of methylene in the outburst coal samples was less and the length of the aliphatic chain of the coal samples became shorter, forming short-chain aliphatic hydrocarbons. The degree of aromatization of the outburst coal samples is higher, and the hydrogen in the coal undergoes chemical environment transfer so that the content of aliphatic hydrogen is relatively reduced, and a large number of nascent primary aromatic hydrocarbons with low crystallinity and main hydrocarbon gases, such as CH4, are produced. The amount of aromatic hydrocarbons in coal samples is proportional to the adsorption capacity of the coal body for gas. , With the increase of the relative content of aromatic hydrocarbons, the reactive groups related to the affinity of coal samples for gas increased, which led to the decrease in the surface energy of coal and the enhancement of gas adsorption capacity, which provided conditions for the high gas endowment of coal seams.

The Raman spectroscopy experiments revealed the differences in the structural composition of macromolecules in the outburst and nonoutburst coal samples. The peak position difference P G–D of the outburst coal samples is smaller; the molecular structure is more heterogeneous, and the impurity molecules account for a higher percentage. He half-peak width ratio (D/G) of the D and G peaks of the nonoutburst coal samples increased gradually, and the size of the graphite microcrystals in the coal was increasing, and the microcrystalline structure was more ordered and compact. The closely ordered microcrystalline structure makes the number of micropores in the coal samples decrease, the comparative area of pores decreases, the gas adsorption decreases, and the risk of coal and gas outburst is reduced. The outburst coal samples have smaller I D/I G values, weaker graphitization, and more structural defects in the coal, and structural defects are an important component in the formation of micropores. As shown in Figure , compared and analyzed with the X-ray diffraction spectroscopy results, the aromatic layer spacing (d 002) value of the outburst coal samples increased, the aromatic hydrocarbon structure of the outburst coal samples was less crystalline, the molecular layer spacing increased, and the arrangement of the aromatic layer structure was more disordered and lax. The stacking height (L c) of the aromatic lamellae in the vertical direction decreases, squeezing the pore space and causing the pores in the coal samples to transition from microporous to ultramicroporous. The directional arrangement of the carbon structure becomes less organized, and the range of ordered structures gradually decreases. Increased aromaticity (f a) and greater spacing between molecular layers disrupt the coal molecular arrangement and can lead to an increase in amorphous disordered carbon structures. The influence law of the microstructural difference of coal samples on the outburst characteristics of coal samples was verified by the difference of microporous structure between outburst and nonoutburst coal samples. The outburst coal is characterized by disordered microstructure, loose structural arrangement of aromatic layers, as well as a high percentage of impurity molecules, disordered and chaotic molecular arrangement, low crystallinity, and large microcrystalline structure. The disordered arrangement between molecules and microcrystalline structural defects leads to an increase in the number of micropores in the coal samples. Therefore, the outburst coal samples are dominated by micropores with more pore volume and specific surface area and are dominated by fine-necked bottlenecks and wedge-shaped pores. The micropores provide a large amount of adsorption space for the gas; it is difficult to desorb the gas from the fine-necked bottlenecks and wedge-shaped pores of the outburst coal samples, and the residual gas content of the coal body is high, which significantly increases the risk of outburst of the coal body.

The macroscopic characteristics of outburst and nonoutburst coal samples are also significantly different, and the research results show that the coefficient of robustness and strength of outburst coal samples are uniformly smaller than those of nonoutburst coal samples. The lower deformation resistance of outburst coal samples is importantly linked to the arrangement of the molecular structure in the coal. The closer the molecules are arranged and the fewer the structural defects, the greater the resistance to compression. Through Raman spectroscopy experiments and X-ray diffraction spectroscopy experiments, it was found that the molecular structure of the outburst coal samples was disorganized, the molecular arrangement was disordered, the orderliness of the directional arrangement of the carbon structure became lower, and there were more defects in the crystal structure of the molecular composition, which explains why the outburst coal samples have a lower resistance to deformation in terms of the microchemical structure. At the same time, liquid nitrogen adsorption experiments show that the pore content in the outburst coal samples is higher, and the more the pore content of the coal body per unit volume, the thinner the coal body skeleton is, which makes the antidestructive capacity of the coal body lower and the susceptibility of the coal body to be destroyed increase.

5. Conclusion

  • (1)

    The outburst coal samples have a higher degree of aromatization, and the hydrogen in the coal undergoes chemical environment transfer, which produces a large number of nascent primary aromatic hydrocarbons with low crystallinity and generates a large amount of CH4. At the same time, the number of oxygen-containing functional groups in the coals is low, which increases the adsorption capacity of the samples to the gas, and this provides an environmental basis for the protruding coal samples to accumulate the gas.

  • (2)

    The spatial structure of the macromolecular structure of the outburst coal samples is disorganized and disordered, the impurity molecules account for a higher percentage, and the discontinuity of its microcrystalline structure or the structural defects make the content of micropores in the coal samples increase. At the same time, the stacking height (L c) of the aromatic lamellae of the outburst coal samples in the vertical direction decreases, which says the increase of the number of micropores or ultramicropores in the coal species from the perspective of molecular composition.

  • (3)

    Comparison of the pore structure characteristics of the outburst and nonoutburst coal samples revealed that the outburst coal samples had more total pore volume, more pore content of the coal body per unit volume, and the thinner the coal body skeleton, which made the coal body less resistant to damage. The proportion of micropores in the outburst coal samples is higher and is dominated by fine-necked bottleneck pores and wedge-shaped pores, which makes gas desorption difficult and the residual gas content of the coal body high, increasing the risk of coal and gas outburst.
