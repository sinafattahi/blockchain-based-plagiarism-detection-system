Abstract

Current proposals to achieve carbon reduction through the co-firing of ammonia and coal in fluidized bed boilers necessitate further research into the combustion characteristics of ammonia under fluidized bed conditions. Ammonia co-firing has primarily been explored using either detailed combustion mechanisms in pulverized coal boilers or coal combustion reactions in fluidized beds. In this study, we performed experiments and detailed mechanism simulations of coal and ammonia co-firing in a bubbling fluidized bed (BFB) combustion apparatus (operating temperature: approximately 1123 K) and conducted experiments at various primary air (PA) and secondary air (SA) ratios. We constructed a 1-D simulation reactor, mimicking a fluidized bed reactor, based on an equivalent reactor network. We also used three chemical reaction models, the Okafor, Nakamura, and modified mechanisms, to simulate NH3 combustion reactions in the BFB. Under variable PA and NH3 ratios, NO emissions in both models were primarily controlled by NH2, O, and HNO radical reactions. Reaction pathway analysis revealed that both mechanisms exhibited similar characteristics for NO formation and reduction. These results indicate that during the initial NH3 oxidation reaction, HNO production is governed by NH2 and O radicals, as illustrated by reactions, such as NH2 + O = HNO + H and NH2 + NH2 = N2H2 + H2, which affect NO formation. Thus, the advanced model for NH2 and HNO reactions can better simulate ammonia oxidation at lower temperatures. This enhancement aids in bridging the gap between experimental results and simulations, particularly under fluidized bed conditions, providing important theoretical support for planned ammonia/coal co-firing in fluidized bed boilers.

1. Introduction

The increasing recognition of global environmental crises has led to the implementation of proactive, environmentally friendly policies in the energy sector. In power generation, various measures are being explored to reduce greenhouse gas emissions, such as CO2 and N2O emissions, with a strong emphasis on achieving carbon-free power generation. In this global context, research is being actively conducted to achieve net-zero emissions and reduce or neutralize carbon. , Notable initiatives include the European Green Deal (2021), which established rigorous standards for air pollutant emissions from internal combustion engine vehicles, and the 2020 directive of the International Maritime Organization, which introduced stringent emission standards for marine operations. Converting fossil fuels to carbon-neutral or carbon-free fuels in thermal power plants is a key strategy for achieving net-zero targets and can be expedited by using existing facilities. In this regard, coal co-firing with ammonia is recognized as a rapid and effective approach for reducing CO2 emissions, with research being conducted in countries, such as Japan, China, the United Kingdom, Spain, and others. −

In the Republic of South Korea, the government has announced policies to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by approximately 290 million tons (40%) from baseline levels by 2030. These policies include expanding renewable energy generation and introducing low-carbon fuels, such as hydrogen and ammonia, for combustion. In line with the recent trend toward achieving carbon neutrality in the energy sector, the South Korean government is actively implementing measures, such as adopting carbon-free power sources, enhancing energy efficiency, and promoting electrification. These initiatives aim to ensure a sustainable and environmentally friendly power generation sector. Specifically, a target has been established to increase the use of hydrogen and ammonia for energy from 2.1% in 2030 to 7.1% in 2036. Additionally, an ongoing government project in thermal power generation aims to achieve carbon reduction through 20% ammonia co-firing in coal-fired power plants, thereby leveraging this important carbon-free fuel source. The Sam-check Power Plant operates a 550-MWe ultrasupercritical circulating fluidized bed boiler, , the largest among the existing circulating fluidized bed power plants. Fluidized bed power plants typically operate at temperatures ranging from approximately 1123 to 1173 K, which is lower than the temperatures of pulverized coal-fired boilers. − Thus, investigating the combustion reaction of ammonia under these low-temperature conditions is crucial. Moreover, given current plans to implement ammonia co-firing in fluidized bed boilers, evaluating the detailed combustion mechanism during ammonia co-firing with coal under these conditions is essential (1100 K).

Fluidized bed simulations have been investigated using both 3-D simulations, such as computational fluid dynamics (CFD) and computational particle fluid dynamics (CPFD), and 1-D simulation approaches. The use of CFD and CPFD in simulations provides deeper insights into coal combustion processes, − predicting velocity distributions, hydrodynamic behaviors, such as bubble size, species concentrations, and other hydrodynamic parameters within a BFB. − However, the chemical kinetic mechanisms involved in CFD and CPFD simulations require significant computational resources, and, the flamelet model could therefore also be considered as an alternative. Recently, with the widespread availability of massive parallel computing, carrier-phase direct numerical simulations (CP-DNS) have become feasible and been applied to pulverized coal combustion. In addition, the use of global reactions in CFD simulations to minimize computational costs is a widely adopted practice. − Furthermore, 1-D models can be used to simulate the gasification reactions of coal and biomass in fluidized beds, helping to reduce resource consumption. − Some studies have used tools, such as Aspen Plus, which incorporate hydrodynamics based on two-phase theory in 1-D simulations. − Notably, other studies have employed a chemical equivalent reactor network (ERN) method as a tool for simulating fluidized beds in 1-D. − In this approach, fluidized bed reactors with complex flow behavior are represented by using ideal reactor units interconnected in a network. Regions with strong mixing are modeled as a perfectly stirred reactor (PSR), while straight flows without back-mixing are represented by plug flow reactors (PFR). In previous research on the fluidized bed gasification of solid fuels, including coal and biomass, − fluidized bed reactors were simulated using hydrodynamics through PSR and PFR reactors, similar to the approach used in this study. However, these studies primarily focused on the reactions of solid fuels and did not specifically investigate ammonia combustion.

Ammonia gas combustion has been widely investigated. Among the studies on detailed ammonia chemistry, the Okafor model accurately represents the current methane (hydrocarbon-based fuel) and ammonia co-firing characteristics, providing precise simulations that have been verified against other models and experimental data under gas-burner conditions. However, this model assumes a combustion temperature of approximately 2000 K, which differs significantly from the conditions in a fluidized bed furnace. Therefore, Nakamura et al. proposed enhancing the oxidation reaction by utilizing the NxHy radical at a lower temperature (1400 K). Their detailed chemical model for ammonia, which improved the Konnov model, successfully simulated ammonia combustion at relatively low temperatures. Nevertheless, this temperature remains higher than the combustion temperature in the fluidized bed region, making the direct application of these models to fluidized bed ammonia combustion challenging.

Research on the co-firing of ammonia and coal is a recent field of growing interest, and associated studies are ongoing. − However, research on the co-firing of ammonia with coal using detailed ammonia chemistry remains limited. Although Taniguchi et al., along with similar studies, ,, investigated ammonia combustion through experiments and detailed mechanisms, these studies were conducted in pulverized coal boilers at temperatures higher than those typically found in fluidized beds. Sousa Cardoso et al. and Kim et al. conducted experimental studies on coal and ammonia in pilot-scale fluidized bed reactors; however, these studies lacked detailed chemical analyses.

Therefore, the aim of this current study was to investigate the combustion characteristics of NH3 during the co-firing of NH3 and coal in a fluidized bed reactor. Initially, we conducted experiments using a laboratory-scale bubbling fluidized bed (BFB) setup. Subsequently, we performed a simulation study using the Chemkin-Pro program, based on previous research, − , and NO and N2O emissions during ammonia combustion were analyzed. Aside from the previous study by Dai et al., no other research has addressed coal combustion in these fluidized beds. Given that the objective of this study was to identify the direction and improvement areas for the detailed ammonia chemistry model in coal co-firing using 1-D simulations, some errors in the coal combustion simulation were anticipated. Thus, a simulation study was conducted using the Chemkin-Pro program with previous work serving as a reference. Although the Chemkin-Pro program is limited in accurately simulating hydrodynamic conditions compared to other 1-D models, our study primarily aimed to explore an improved method for the detailed chemical reaction mechanism of ammonia during co-firing with coal under fluidized bed temperatures and conditions. Previous 1-D studies , simulated the devolatilization process and char combustion model (coal → char + C x H y + light gases), assumed to form through the reaction, and the present study is based on the same assumptions. Gas combustion model used a detailed chemical model for ammonia combustion, , and sensitivity and reaction pathway analyses were conducted based on the previous model at low temperatures (approximately 1100 K). Based on these results, we enhanced the model and proposed an advanced model. These simulation results have significant implications for comprehensively understanding the chemical reactions of ammonia and predicting ammonia oxidation in a fluidized bed boiler.

2. Methods

2.1. Experimental Methods

KIDECO, a commercially available imported coal from Indonesia, was selected as the coal sample for this study. This sub-bituminous coal is widely used as a fuel in power plants, particularly in fluidized bed boilers. Analysis of fuel physical properties was conducted according to the American Society for Testing and Materials (ASTM) standards. Proximate analysis was performed using a commercial industrial analyzer (TGA701; LECO Co., St. Joseph, MI) following the ASTM D3172 standards. Ultimate analysis was carried out using an elemental analyzer (6850 GC/5975 MSD; Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA) following the ASTM D5373-16 standards. The calorific value, a key fuel parameter, was also measured for each sample using a calorimeter analyzer (5E-C5508, CKIC Co., Ltd., Hunan, China) according to ASTM D5865-19 standards. −

The experiment was conducted using a BFB combustion apparatus under fluidized bed conditions at 1123 K. A diagram of the experimental equipment is presented in Figure . The BFB apparatus consists of an electrically heated BFB constructed from a stainless steel (SUS) 304 tube, a coal feeder, a gas supply system, and a gas sampling and analysis system. The inner diameter and height of the SUS tube are 50 and 1250 mm, respectively. The BFB is divided into three segments: the injection section, where gas is introduced; the reaction section, where combustion occurs; and the freeboard zone, where gas reactions and capture occur. As depicted in Figure , PA (primary air) and NH3 were injected from the bottom, while coal was injected from the side. The equipment also allows for the injection of secondary air (SA) and NH3 through a side port. Exhaust gas is directed into the gas analyzer through the upper part, with an FR-IR type gas analyzer selected for combustion gas analysis.

Mixed air (with a constant equivalence ratio of 1.2 at the PA port) was introduced into the combustion chamber through the PA port at the bottom of the reactor to create bubbling conditions in the bed. Combustion experiments were conducted once the flow inside the reactor stabilized. The NH3 co-firing ratio, defined as the ratio of NH3 input to the total thermal input of coal and NH3, as expressed in eq , ranged from 0 to 20%:

The fuel and experimental conditions were as follows: first, coal combustion experiments were conducted with a primary air/secondary air (PA/SA) ratio ranging from 1:0 to 0.8:0.2. Subsequently, experiments were carried out under 20% ammonia co-firing, using the same PA/SA ratio range of 1:0 to 0.8:0.2. Finally, experiments were conducted by varying the R NH3 from 0 to 20% at a fixed PA/SA ratio of 1:0. 2.2. Numerical Method

To simulate each experiment, laboratory-scale BFB equipment was designed using an ERN. A 1-D simulation reactor was then constructed to mimic a circulating fluidized bed reactor based on the ERN reactor described in a previous study. , Figure presents a schematic illustration of an ERN-based BFB.

As the detailed chemical composition of volatile matter is not yet completely understood, it is generally treated as a hypothetical substance represented by C x H y O z or a combination of different species. In this study, the species composition in the volatile matter was calculated using thermodynamic equilibrium and kinetic models based on FLASHCHAIN, incorporating data from the PC Coal Lab. − The calculated chemical species from the volatile components of coal included tar, CH4, H2, CO, CO2, H2O, HCN, and NH3, following a similar approach to that used in a previous study to investigate coal devolatilization. The compositions of the devolatilization gas and char obtained from the program were then used as inputs to a perfectly stirred reactor (PSR1) to perform devolatilization reactions using Chemkin-Pro. A simplified schematic diagram of the coal–ammonia mixture is presented in Figure . The volume of PSR1 was set to 2% of the total reactor volume, following a previously described methodology. Subsequently, a bubbling bed was established as the combustion zone in PSR2, where air was supplied to facilitate combustion reactions. Coal pyrolysis was precalculated using the PC Coal Lab program, as previously described. PSR consists of a small, thermally insulated chamber with inlet and outlet ducts. A steady flow of fuel and oxidizer was introduced in such a manner that high-intensity turbulent mixing ensured that the contents of the reactor remained nearly spatially uniform.

Thus, the conversion from reactants to products is primarily controlled by chemical reaction rates rather than by mixing processes. The reactor requires rapid mixing, and the residence time is determined based on the flow rate and reactor volume. The hydrodynamic considerations of PSR are characterized by parameters, such as reactor volume, residence time or mass flow rate, heat loss or temperature, incoming temperature, and mixture composition.

In the above-mentioned method, bed hydrodynamics, external and internal (within the particle) heat transfers, and intrinsic pyrolysis chemical kinetics have not been comprehensively elucidated. However, pyrolysis is assumed to be complete within the PSR, and the solid bed material in the freeboard zone exchanges heat and mass with the solid particles. When the ERN model was constructed, the following assumptions were made:

  • (1)

    The pyrolysis zone was modeled using a combination of thermodynamic equilibrium and kinetic models. ,,, The oxygen supplied from the bottom of the fluidized bed reactor did not reach the fuel entry point, as the carrier gas (N2) injected during coal suppresses the oxidation reactions.

  • (2)

    The volume of the pyrolysis zone was assumed to be 2% that of the total volume of the gasifier, based on scale analysis and the fact that pyrolysis occurs within a very short period after coal enters the fluidized bed reactor, as observed in previous studies. The small volume of the pyrolysis zone and the flow turbulence resulted in a well-stirred situation during the kinetic reaction, modeled using a PSR.

  • (3)

    The coal reaction occurs in a fluidized bed with rapid mixing due to the fluid dynamics of the bed, allowing it to be effectively simulated using a PSR, as demonstrated in previous studies.

  • (4)

    The freeboard was modeled as a PFR due to minimal axial mixing in the flow direction, with mass diffusion considered negligible.

  • (5)

    The temperature of the combustion device was externally controlled and measured using an experimental thermocouple to maintain a constant temperature; thus, the experimental set temperature was used.

In this study, solid-phase coal was simplified into gas fuel, which differs from the heterogeneous reactions in coal combustion. Various material interactions and nonlinear reactions occur in real reactors; however, these processes can be simplified in models, potentially leading to errors. Furthermore, under actual flow conditions, particle size, shape, and interactions, such as those between Char and NO or Char and N2O, can be influenced by flow dynamics. These variations in reaction rates and the resulting changes may be overlooked in the models, potentially causing deviations, as demonstrated in the validation results in Figure . However, as this study focused on the qualitative comparison of coal and ammonia co-firing, the experimental and simulation deviations were generally deemed acceptable based on the coal prefiring experiment. Therefore, the results can be regarded reliable for qualitatively investigating nitrogen oxide emissions during coal/ammonia combustion using the proposed mechanism and model.

According to Okafor et al., the following reactions are involved in the ammonia oxidation pathway: NO + NH2 = NNH + OH, NH2 + O = HNO + H, and HNO + H = NO + H2. Among the other elementary reactions in the ammonia oxidation path, these three have the most significant effect on the concentrations of H and OH radicals in methane–ammonia flames. , In fluidized bed co-firing of coal and ammonia, HNO, H, and OH radicals are expected to considerably influence NH3 combustion. −

The second model employed was the Nakamura model. Most existing reaction mechanisms do not predict the formation of weak flames: only the reaction mechanism developed by Konnov predicted the formation of a weak flame at low temperatures. However, the predicted location of the weak flame significantly deviates from the location of the experimental weak flame. Building on the Konnov model, ,– Nakamura et al. extensively updated their chemical kinetic modeling, focusing primarily on N2Hx chemistry at low temperatures (approximately 1400 K), as N2Hx species are expected to be produced from NH2 + NH2 reactions. Nakamura et al. then performed sensitivity and reaction-flux analyses to assess the importance of N2Hx chemistry in the reaction zones of weak flames at low temperatures. The Nakamura model includes 33 species and 232 elementary reactions. Therefore, sensitivity and reaction-flux analyses of the Okafor and Nakamura models during co-firing are necessary to develop a comprehensive NH3 model for the fluidized bed. In this study, both models were used and their results were compared.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Fuel Composition

In this study, we investigated the effects of the PA/SA ratio on NO x emissions when NH3 was co-firing with coal in a BFB reactor. The LHV of the coal used in the experiment was 4450 kcal/kg, while that of NH3 was 4446 kcal/kg. According to the proximate and ultimate analysis data for coal (Table ), the fuel characteristics were classified as sub-bituminous. Coal particles were sieved to a size range of 400–600 μm, and sand was sieved to a size range of 200–400 μm. The LHV values for coal and ammonia were comparable as were the calorific values per unit mass. The mass input during co-firing was expected to be similar; however, as solid coal fuel had a different volume per unit mass compared to gaseous NH3, the total gas mass flow input varied between the 100% coal combustion and 20% co-firing conditions.

3.2. Effect of the PA/SA Ratio on NO Formation and Reduction

Preliminary experiments and validation experiments were conducted on coal combustion and ammonia co-firing using the BFB device. We performed co-firing experiments with a coal-to-ammonia ratio of 20% and analyzed and compared the combustion NO emissions with the 1-D simulation results. The conditions used in each experiment are listed in Table . To validate the coal combustion simulations, we conducted BFB experiments under various PA/SA ratios. Figure depicts a comparison of the gas emission results between the experiments and the Nakamura and Okafor models.

During the experiment, when the PA/SA ratio was 1, NO x emissions were 170 ppm, and NO tended to decrease as the PA/SA ratio decreased. However, the simulation results of the previous models show somewhat different values. The Okafor and Nakamura model simulations predicted a greater NO x reduction effect due to the air-staging effect. In addition, N2O emissions exhibited characteristics that differed from the experimental results, highlighting the need for further research, as noted by Nakamura et al.

However, the simulation results for H2O, CO2, and O2 were similar to the experimental results. Therefore, despite some differences in gas emissions during coal combustion, similar overall trends were observed. In Figure , the comparison of NO production and reduction from coal char using different models reveals that the formation and depletion of NO from char vary progressively with changes in the PA/SA ratio. These results indicate that the simulation effectively captures and represents the NO emissions behavior from coal.

Through these results, coal combustion was verified using a 1-D simulation; based on these findings, we established a basis for conducting co-firing simulations by incorporating an ammonia gas reaction model into the coal combustion process. Figure depicts a comparison of the gas emission results between the experiments and the Nakamura and Okafor models for 20% ammonia co-firing. During the experiments, the NO x emissions were 356 ppm when the PA/SA ratio was 1:0.

As the PA/SA ratio decreased, NO x emissions gradually decreased from 356 at a PA/SA ratio of 0.8:0.2 to 301 ppm, indicating a 15% reduction. This decrease suggests that as the proportion of PA decreased, the amount of excess oxygen required for combustion was reduced, leading to more NH3 being consumed by reduction reactions rather than by NO formation at the low temperature of 1100 K. , The N2O emissions predicted by the Nakamura and Okafor models were significantly higher than those observed experimentally. This discrepancy arises because the mechanisms of both models were developed for gas combustion conditions, which occur at a higher temperature than those found in the fluidized bed reactor. Consequently, the N2O + H = N2 + OH reaction, as well as the N2O + (M) = N2 + 0.5O2 and N2O + O = N2 + O2 (Figure ) thermal decomposition reactions, could not accurately simulate the decomposition of N2O under the reactor temperatures. Both models exhibited significant deviations from the experimental results under this temperature condition.

For an in-depth investigation of this aspect, NO formation and reduction were analyzed using the total rate of production (ROP), as shown in Figure . Contrary to the experimental result that NO reduction would increase as PA decreased, the results revealed that NO reduction was progressively suppressed as the PA/SA ratio decreased in the Nakamura model. This result can be attributed to the abundance of oxygen, as demonstrated by the pathway analysis in Figure . At a low temperature, O radicals and OH radicals generated NH2 at a high rate. NH2 exhibited a high reaction rate with HNO due to its elevated radical concentration. However, NO reduction reactions, including NO + NH2 → N2 + H2O, NH2 + NO → NNH + OH, and N2O + H, were insufficient at a low temperature. As a result, NH2, which was rapidly produced in large quantities, was converted to N2H2 and consumed in large quantities in the hydrogen production reaction. Afterward, NH2 required for NO reduction was consumed in this way; when the PA/SA ratio decreased, there was a continuous decrease in the level of NO reduction. Although these reactions were enhanced in the development of the Nakamura model, further refinements are required to improve their accuracy under low-temperature conditions (approximately 1100 K). Thus, a larger proportion of NH2 was consumed in NO production through HNO than the amount available for the NO reduction reaction.

Additionally, NO emissions were reduced at a PA/SA ratio of 0.8. The formation of NO derived from NH3 was decelerated due to the consumption of H and O radicals as the O2 fraction decreased and the formation of HNO also slowed as O and H radicals were reduced. Furthermore, the NH2 + NH2 → N2H2 + H2 and NH2 + NO → N2 + H2O reactions were facilitated, followed by the N2H2 → NNH, NNH + M → N2 + H + M, and NNH → N2 + H reactions, promoting N2 formation. Thus, this reduction in HNO emissions led to a decrease in NO emissions. − As the PA/SA ratio decreased, the NH2 + O → HNO + H reaction declined, whereas the NH2 + NH2 → N2H2 + H2 reaction increased. This observation suggests that at a lower ROP value, the NH2 + NH2 → N2H2 + H2 reaction significantly impacted the NO x results. According to the Nakamura mechanism, as the concentration of O2 relative to NH3 decreases, the conversion of NH3 to HNO is inhibited, leading to a reduction in NO production and a corresponding decrease in the ROP of NO reduction in the simulations. Moreover, the final NO emissions were correlated with the net ROP of NO and the formation of HNO. −

Compared with the experimental results, the Nakamura mechanism highlights the need for further enhancement of the NO reduction reactions involving NH2 and NH radicals.

In contrast, according to the Okafor simulation results, the NOx emissions were as low as 2 ppm in all cases. This is because unlike in the Nakamura model, the reactivity of NH3 was lower in the Okafor model, causing it to react more slowly throughout the reactor. This is evident from the ROP values presented in Figure , where the Okafor model exhibits ROP values that are approximately one-tenth of those in the Nakamura model. Consequently, most of the NH3 was utilized for the NO reduction reactions, as depicted in Figure . Additionally, considering the reactivity of NO during this period, the reaction rate in the Okafor model was 10% of that in the Nakamura model. Thus, the NH3 reaction occurred more slowly over a longer period in the Okafor model owing to its lower reactivity at the low temperature of 1100 K. During NO reduction, a large amount of NO was converted to N2O.

Under low-temperature conditions (1100 K), NH3 was converted to NH2, with a greater amount being converted to N2H4, NNH, and N2H2 rather than to HNO, which generates NO x through NH2 oxidation. Additionally, a significant amount of NH2 was consumed for NO reduction. According to the Okafor model, most NH3 reactions at low temperatures (1100 K) involved NO reduction. At lower PA/SA ratios, the concentration of the O radicals decreased with increasing NH3/O2 ratios, which led to reduced HNO formation. This is because many of the OH radicals were consumed by the dehydrogenation reactions of NH3, which consequently inhibited the NO formation reactions between NH2/HNO and OH radicals, ultimately leading to reduced NO emissions. Thus, the detailed chemical mechanisms governing the NO and N2O concentrations during the ammonia oxidation process under low-temperature conditions need to be further elucidated. It will therefore be necessary to accurately simulate the discrepancies between the experimental results and 1-D model simulations.

To achieve this, the reaction model should be adjusted to enhance NH2 and HNO reactivity using experimental data, as previously suggested. ,, Under low-temperature conditions in the 1-D simulation, N2O (e.g., the N2O + H = N2 + OH reaction) was not reduced as observed in the experiments, leading to high N2O concentrations. The elevated N2O concentration further inhibited the reduction of NO through the NH + NO = N2O + H reaction, resulting in increased levels of both NO and N2O. Consequently, to accurately simulate ammonia reactions at low temperatures, further improvements to the N2O reaction model are essential.

The results indicate that the initial rapid reactivity of ammonia and the oxidation reaction of NO were enhanced in the Nakamura model. However, as the temperature (1100 K) was lower than the conditions for which the model was originally developed, the reduction reactions of NO and N2O were insufficiently simulated, leading to high NO emissions. In the Okafor model, the NO oxidation reaction was subdued owing to the lower reactivity of ammonia at 1100 K, which is below the temperature considered during the development of the model. At this temperature, a significant amount of NO was converted to N2O due to a relatively high NO reduction reaction. However, as discussed in a previous study, the reduction reaction of N2O to N2 was not sufficiently simulated owing to inaccuracies in the N2O reaction model, resulting in high N2O emissions.

3.3. Effect of the NH3 Ratio on NO x Formation and Reduction

To analyze the characteristics of NO formation and reduction in each model according to the ammonia co-firing ratio, we conducted experiments and simulations for NH3 co-firing values ranging from 0 to 30%. The experimental results indicated that NO emissions increased significantly from 169 to 308 ppm as R NH3 increased from 0 to 10% under a PA/SA ratio of 1:0. These emissions further increased to 325 ppm at 20% R NH3.

The simulation results in Figure reveal that the Nakamura model slightly overpredicted NO, similar to the previous simulations involving the PA/SA ratio.

Moreover, the N2O emissions predicted by the Nakamura and Okafor models were significantly higher, consistent with previous experiments with varying PA/SA ratios. This discrepancy arises because, as previously described, the mechanisms of both models were developed for gas combustion conditions that exceed those in fluidized bed reactors. Consequently, the N2O decomposition reactions in these models could not accurately simulate the decomposition of N2O at the temperatures relevant to fluidized bed conditions.

ROP and pathway analyses of NO were performed based on the R NH3. The simulation results indicated that NO emissions initially increased at an R NH3 of 20% and subsequently decreased at 30%. To further investigate this trend, an ROP analysis of NO was performed (Figure ), which revealed that NH3 reduction reactions continued to decrease as the NH3 ratio increased. In the Nakamura model, it was observed that as the total amount of NH3 increased, conversion of the initial NH3 to NH2 and N2H2 accelerated. , Thus, the consumption of OH and O radicals also increased, leading to reduced NH3 reactivity. Thus, the reduction reaction of NO further slowed at 30% R NH3 compared with that at 20%, resulting in lower final NO emissions. The R NH3 increased beyond 20%, and the amount converted to HNO also increased. However, when more than 30% NH3 was added, the NH3 reaction was suppressed. This suppression occurred due to the increased consumption of radicals for converting NH3 to NH2. Consequently, NO reduction and its conversion to N2H2 became more dominant than the conversion of NH2 to HNO. The net rate of NO ROP and the formation of HNO were more strongly influenced by NH2 and O radicals than by the previous results associated with the PA/SA ratio.

In the Okafor model, the NO emission results exhibited a trend consistent with the experimental results at a concentration of 10% R NH3. However, as the R NH3 increased beyond 20%, the NO reduction effect became excessive, resulting in minimal NO emissions, which was consistent with the trends observed in the results presented in Section . This trend was also supported by the ROP analysis results for NO (Figure ). Additionally, in the Okafor model, NH3 slip was observed to begin at 875 ppm for 20% co-firing and increased to 8320 ppm at 30% co-firing. These results indicate that consistent with previous simulation results, the Okafor model exhibited lower reactivity than did the Nakamura model at low temperatures (1100 K).

In the pathway of the Okafor model shown in Figure , with an increase in R NH3, the reaction between NH2 and O radicals to produce HNO was inhibited while the reaction generating N2H4 was promoted. This occurred because O radicals were consumed during the conversion of NH3 to NH2, leading to unburned NH3 at 20 and 30% R NH3 due to a shortage of O radicals. Overall, the simulation results revealed differences in the NO emissions. However, the ammonia co-firing simulations indicated that the models exhibited similar trends concerning the PA/SA ratio, resulting in comparable NO formation and reduction.

Analysis results in both models of the effect of the NH3 ratio on NOx formation and reduction revealed that the formation of NO was primarily governed by elementary reactions involving N-containing intermediate species, such as NH2, O radicals, and HNO radicals. At a high R NH3, the concentration of the O radicals decreased with an increasing NH3/O2 ratio, which led to decreased HNO formation. This decrease occurred because many of the O/OH radicals were consumed by the dehydrogenation reactions of NH3, which consequently inhibited NO formation reactions between NH2/HNO and O/OH radicals, resulting in reduced NO emissions. , According to the BFB simulation results under these conditions (PA/SA and NH3 ratios), an enhancement in the reaction between NH2 and HNO is required to further elucidate the detailed chemical mechanisms and better simulate ammonia oxidation at low temperatures.

The overall simulation analysis indicated that the trends in NO emissions simulated by the Nakamura model differed from those of the experimental results. Similarly, the Okafor model demonstrated low NO oxidation reactions due to the lower ammonia reactivity at 1100 K, where the generated NO was converted to N2O through a relatively high NO reduction reaction. Consequently, both the Nakamura and Okafor models failed to accurately represent the NO emissions. Furthermore, N2O emissions also showed significant discrepancies, indicating that improvements in low-temperature models are required to accurately simulate the co-firing of ammonia and coal at low temperatures.

As presented in Figure , after the conversion of NH3 to NH2 in the Nakamura model, the radicals generated in the NxHy reactions led to increased NH2 production. In the temperature range for which the Nakamura model was originally developed, these reactions work together to promote NH2 consumption and suppress high NO production.

Based on the current pathway analysis, the primary factor for the discrepancy between the current experimental and simulation results for NO was identified as the rapid NH2 + O = HNO reaction and the slow NH2 + NO reaction, which was refined using the Glarborg model. When NH3 is oxidized under conditions with a high excess air ratio, a substantial amount of HO2 radicals can form, promoting the oxidation of NO to NO2. We incorporated this reaction, NO + HO2 = NO2 + OH, into the model and adjusted it. To better simulate the low N2O emissions observed, we advanced the existing N2O reaction rates were advanced. Specifically, to reflect that N2O is reduced to N2 more rapidly in the presence of O2, we adjusted the frequency factor coefficients for the reactions of N2O + O = N2 + O2 and N2O + H = N2 + OH, consistent with a previous report.

The advanced model demonstrated a prediction accuracy higher than that of the existing model, as presented in Figures and . As discussed in Figure of Section and Figure of Section , the NH2 + NO reaction, which initially exhibited a low reactivity, was enhanced in the Glarborg model. Additionally, the NH2 + NH2 = N2H2 + H2 reaction, which also had a low reactivity, was improved to ensure more efficient consumption of NH2 in thermal decomposition and DeNOx reactions, particularly during high NO production. These improvements enabled the simulation to more accurately predict NO emissions, aligning closer with experimental data. Similarly, the N2O reactions indicated that the reduction and thermal decomposition processes in the existing Nakamura and Okafor models were slower under the fluidized bed temperature of 1100 K, leading to significant discrepancies from the actual experimental results. By refining these reactions, the simulation provided a more realistic representation of the process. These modifications effectively controlled excessive oxidation reactions, allowing ammonia oxidation, DeNOx, and thermal decomposition effects to be more accurately simulated under the current fluidized bed reactor conditions.

However, we emphasize that the high accuracy of this advanced model is limited to the specific fluidized bed reactor used in this study and the R NH3 currently considered (10–30%). The primary focus of this study was to provide insights into advanced ammonia models through pathway analysis in coal–ammonia co-firing, rather than to develop a fully refined model. These insights can inform and guide the subsequent development of a more comprehensive and refined model in future studies. Thus, the reliability of the model presented may decrease when the R NH3 or experimental conditions change. A combustion device was constructed in a simplified manner based on the previous study and was compared with the experimental data from the previous study. The results demonstrated that the new model exhibited a high degree of accuracy across different devices, confirming its reliability. According to the simulation results for coal combustion and Run 4, the new model demonstrated a high level of accuracy across various devices, confirming its reliability. However, in Run 3, discrepancies in the NO and N2O results were observed. This discrepancy arose because, under the experimental conditions, when ammonia was injected into the SA section; rather, variations in the injection port shape and ammonia injection rate resulted in ammonia being directly introduced into the furnacefactors that were not considered in the 1-D simulation. Consequently, the Nakamura and Okafor models exhibited high levels of unburned carbon and ammonia slip. Additionally, the advanced model showed slight differences in the NO and N2O values. According to the results, the new model showed a relatively high level of accuracy even in the experimental devices of other prior papers, which confirmed the reliability of the advanced model.

Therefore, in future research, we plan to develop an NH3 oxidation mechanism suitable for a low-temperature (1100 K) fluidized bed by enhancing the NH2 and HNO reactions, similar to a previous study. 4. Conclusions

In this study, we developed a chemical reactor network model of a 1-D BFB to investigate the influence of low temperatures (approximately 1100 K) on NO x formation and the reduction reaction pathways of ammonia/coal co-firing using developed detailed chemical kinetics. The main points and conclusions are as follows:

  • 1.

    Ammonia co-firing with coal was simulated in a BFB device under low-temperature conditions (1100 K) with various PA and NH3 ratios, using the coal combustion reactions from previous studies , , and detailed reactions from the advanced Nakamura and Okafor models. To validate the simulation, we compared the results of coal combustion experiments conducted under varying PA/SA ratios to the simulation results. Subsequently, we conducted fluidized bed ammonia co-firing experiments and verified the simulation model under different ammonia co-firing conditions, ranging from 0 to 20%.

  • 2.

    NO emissions simulated by the Nakamura model tended to be higher than the experimental results with varying PA/SA and R NH3. The Okafor model excessively simulated NH2-driven NO reduction, failing to accurately represent NO emissions. The simulation results from the two models indicated that previous models cannot sufficiently simulate the oxidation reactions of ammonia in a fluidized bed condition (low temperatures).

  • 3.

    NO emissions were primarily influenced by the reactions between NH2 and O radicals, as well as those involving HNO, suggesting that improving the reactions of NH2 + NO → NNH + OH, NH2 + NO → N2 + H2O, and NH2 + NH2 → N–H2 + H2 would enhance the simulation of ammonia mixing reactions in the fluidized bed. Additionally, the reduction reaction of N2O (e.g., N2O + H = N2 + OH reaction) exhibited a tendency for excessive emissions due to low reactivity at low temperatures. Thus, our study results confirm that improving these reactions (NH2, HNO, and N2O reactions) could enable a more accurate simulation of NO formation, even under low-temperature conditions (1100 K).

  • 4.

    The accuracy of the model was significantly enhanced by improving the NH2 + NO reaction, NH2 + NH2 = N2H2 + H2 reaction, and N2O reaction in the Nakamura model. The advanced model now exhibits almost identical agreement with the current fluidized bed reactor experimental results; similar values were also observed for N2O emissions.

This study has implications for future simulations of ammonia/coal co-firing in fluidized bed boilers and provides important theoretical support for planned carbon reduction projects based on ammonia co-firing. Furthermore, we plan to improve the reaction model to more accurately predict N2O reactions, with further experimental data from PFRs.
