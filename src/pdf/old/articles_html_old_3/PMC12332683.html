Abstract

Luffariellolide (1) was previously isolated in useful quantities (>200 mg) from the marine sponge Suberea sp., collected from the Philippines. The intrinsic antibacterial activity of 1 was insignificant; however, 2-(pyridinyl)­pyridazinone 2, derived from 1, exhibited considerable activity against . In this study, 12 natural-product-like pyridazinones 3–14 were synthesized by modifying the γ-hydroxylbutyrolactone core of 1. Among them, 2-(5-fluoropyrimidinyl)­pyridazinone 11 was 64-fold more potent than 1 against , with a MIC of 2 μg/mL. Using a convergent five-step synthesis, the diterpene side chain of 11 was simplified to a C12 lipid chain and 2-methyl-2-pentene to obtain 20 and 22, respectively, both of which retained the same MIC value as 11. The N-phenylnaphthalen-1-amine (NPN) uptake assays of 20 and 22 revealed their effective permeability to the outer membrane of , comparable to that of the positive control, polymyxin B. This study provides a foundation for future structural optimization of the novel antibacterial 2-(pyrimidin-2-yl)-5-alkyl-pyridazin-3­(2H)-one scaffold targeting .

Introduction

The value of natural products (NPs) has been extensively demonstrated in the field of drug discovery. According to a survey of the sources of approved drugs from 1981 to 2019, more than half of small-molecule drugs were developed from NPs or NP-derived molecules. Bioactive NPs can serve as hit compounds to identify novel modes of action and molecular targets for specific disease models. NPs with sufficiently good pharmacological properties that fulfill all the requirements can ideally proceed to approval and commercialization as drugs. Throughout history, the unique structures of NPs with inherent biological activities have inspired the design of novel drug-like small molecules. ,

In successful cases of NP drug development, the initial lead natural compounds have often been replaced with NP-like synthetic analogs derived through a series of structural optimizations. These modifications can improve pharmacological properties, such as stability at low pH, modulation of toxicity in normal cells, affinity to binding sites, potency enhancement, and reduced molecular complexity. , For example, structure–activity relationship (SAR) studies motivated by the potent antitumor activity of halichondrin B, which was isolated from a marine sponge, resulted in the development of eribulin (Halaven). This drug was a structurally simplified cyclic ketone of the parent NP, retaining only the macrocycle segment, while exhibiting enhanced in vivo efficacy. , Additionally, the development of dolastatin 10 as an anticancer agent led to the identification of less toxic synthetic analogs, monomethyl auristatins E and F (MMAE and MMAF), which serve as payloads in the approved antibody–drug conjugates Adcetris and Blenrep, respectively. Hence, the structural diversification of NPs provides a valuable tool for identifying pharmacophores and improving efficacy and selectivity toward target diseases.

In our previous study, luffariellolide (1) was isolated from the marine sponge, Suberea sp., in a sufficient quantity (>200 mg) for chemical derivatization. Although the intrinsic antibacterial activity of 1 was insignificant, 2-(pyridinyl)­pyridazinone 2, which was synthesized by modifying the γ-hydroxylbutyrolactone core of 1, exhibited considerable activity (MIC = 4 μg/mL) against (Figure a). is a Gram-negative bacterium implicated in a range of infections, including pneumonia and hospital-acquired infections such as urinary tract infections, bloodstream infections, and liver abscesses. The characteristic dual-membrane structure of Gram-negative bacteria, consisting of an outer membrane rich in lipopolysaccharides and an inner cytoplasmic membrane, presents a substantial barrier to the effective penetration of antibiotics into the cell. Consequently, bacterial cell permeability constitutes a critical consideration in the development of novel classes of antibacterial agents targeting . ,

In this study, the preliminary findings were extended toward the development of novel luffariellolide analogs containing pyridine or pyrimidine moieties, which exhibit significant antibacterial activity and effective cell permeability against . To achieve this, elucidating the role of the C-5 diterpene residue within the scaffold was essential; however, the absence of functional groups on the side chain limited opportunities for direct modification. Therefore, a convergent five-step synthesis starting from tetronic acid was developed, enabling the late-stage diversification of the side chain. This process established a platform for the generation of structurally diverse antimicrobial derivatives inspired by 1, but independent of the natural supply.

Results and Discussion

Our synthetic design leveraged the native reactivity of the γ-hydroxylbutyrolactone moiety in 1. Inspired by the structure of 2, we synthesized two types of derivatives, 2-(pyridin-2-yl) (type I) and 2-(pyrimidin-2-yl)­pyridazin-3­(2H)-one (type II), which are prevalent in a broad array of important bioactive molecules (Figure b). ,− These NP-like molecules were simply prepared from 1 through the condensation of the γ-hydroxylbutyrolactone core with readily available aryl hydrazines under acidic conditions. , The incorporation of a halogen atom into the derivatives was considered advantageous, as it has been shown to enhance antibacterial potency and broaden the spectrum of activity, exemplified by fluoroquinolones and fluorocyclines. − Thus, halogen-containing arylhydrazines were selected as the primary reactants for the synthesis of NP-derived pyridazinones.

Type I derivatives 3–8 were prepared through chemical modification of 1 using 2-hydrazineylpyridines to yield 2-(pyridinyl)­pyridazinones (Scheme ). Although a relatively large quantity of luffariellolide (1) was isolated from the marine sponge, the derivatization of 1 was performed on the microscale due to limited access to the source. Reaction conditions were evaluated to ensure operational simplicity and reproducibility; optimal conditions were achieved using AcOH as a solvent at 80 °C. Compounds 3, 5, 6, and 8 were synthesized under the optimal conditions using the corresponding 2-hydrazineylpyridines, proceeding without noticeable side reactions to afford moderate-to-high yields (73–46%). However, the syntheses of 4 and 7 were accompanied by the decomposition of 1, resulting in lower yields. Derivatives 3–7 were used to investigate the p- or m-substitution effects of methyl and halogens on the antimicrobial activity. Compound 8, which contains a 3-pyridinyl moiety, was used to evaluate the effects of the nitrogen atom location in the pyridine moiety.

Type II compounds were developed to examine the effects of an additional nitrogen atom present in the N-2 heterocyclic residue of the pyridazinone core. 2-(Pyrimidinyl)­pyridazinones 9–13 were synthesized using 2-hydrazineylpyrimidines bearing identical p-substituents to those used for compounds 2–6 (Scheme ). The optimal reaction conditions for type I derivatives were compatible with an array of 2-hydrazineylpyrimidines, efficiently yielding desired 2-(pyrimidinyl)­pyridazinones 9–14.

The antibacterial activities of the NP-like derivatives 3–14 against several Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria were evaluated. Consistent with previous findings, 3–14 exhibited weak-to-moderate activity toward most strains; however, selectively significant antimicrobial activity was observed against (Table ). Compared with 1, compounds 3–6 and 8 bearing a p-substituent on the pyridine residue exhibited enhanced antibacterial activity in the 8- to 16-fold (MIC = 8–16 μg/mL) range. However, none of the type I compounds 3–8 exhibited a potency superior to that of 2 (MIC = 4 μg/mL), which was set as the baseline. The MIC values of 5, 7, and 8 indicated that the position of the substituent has a greater influence on the antibacterial activity than the position of the nitrogen atom within the pyridine moiety.

The beneficial effects of the pyrimidinyl group on the MIC values were generally observed when comparing types I and II. The pyrimidinyl analogs 10, 11, and 13 exhibited 2- to 4-fold lower MIC values compared with the corresponding pyridinyl analogs 3, 4, and 6, whereas 12 exhibited the same level of activity as 5. However, compound 9, the pyrimidinyl variant of the most potent type I derivative 2, completely lost its activity. In addition, cases 7 and 14 demonstrated the detrimental effects of m-substituents, regardless of the 2-pyridinyl and 2-pyrimidinyl derivatives. Among compounds 1–14, 2-(5-fluoropyrimidinyl)­pyridazinone 11 was the most potent derivative (MIC = 2 μg/mL), with 64- and 2-fold improvements in MIC values over 1 and 2, respectively.

Chemical modification of the γ-hydroxylbutyrolactone core of 1 revealed its potential to enhance antimicrobial activity; however, the role of the diterpene side chain remains unclear. Therefore, we performed additional SAR studies on the lipophilic side chain of the most potent derivative 11, while leaving the 2-(5-fluoropyrimidinyl)­pyridazinone part intact. Because the side chain possessed few functional groups for direct derivatization, we designed a convergent synthetic route that enables the late-stage introduction of diverse C-5 side chains. The strategy outlined in Scheme features the use of 5-bromopyridazinone 15, the core scaffold of 11, as the key intermediate for diversifying the C-5 residue under Negishi coupling conditions.

The synthesis of 15 was initiated by preparing 4-bromofuran-2­(5H)-one 17 from tetronic acid 16 using oxalyl bromide (Scheme ). The treatment of 17 with TIPSOTf afforded silyl enol ether 18 in a quantitative yield. Selective epoxidation of the C-4 double bond in 18 was performed with a freshly prepared DMDO solution at −78 °C, followed by sequential desilylation and epoxide opening with Amberlyst-15 to afford γ-hydroxylbutyrolactone 19 in 88% yield. However, the condensation reaction of 19 with 5-fluoro-2-hydrazinylpyrimidine yielded only trace amounts of 15 under the same conditions used for type I and II derivatives. After monitoring various acids and temperatures, we found that microwave irradiation (180 °C, 150 W) in the presence of AcOH most efficiently produced the key intermediate 15, but only in a low yield.

5-Alkyl pyridazinones 20–28 were synthesized through the coupling reaction of 15 with various alkyl or aryl halides under Negishi conditions (Scheme ). , To investigate the effects of replacing the diterpene moiety of 11 with a simple lipid chain, 15 was coupled with 1-bromododecane to produce 20 in 51% yield. During the synthesis of 20, debromination of 15 was accompanied to afford byproduct 21 in 6% yield. This byproduct 21, devoid of the side chain, was employed to elucidate the structural requirement of the side chain for antimicrobial activity. To explore the effects of side chain length, 5-bromo-2-methyl-2-pentene and methyllithium were used to synthesize 22 and 23, respectively, each containing one isoprene unit and a methyl group at C-5. The coupling reaction of bromocyclohexane proceeded with unidentified side reactions, resulting in a low yield of 24 (22%). Considering the effects of additional halogen substituents on the side chain, derivatives 25 and 26 were synthesized by using the corresponding alkyl bromides. However, the lithium–halogen exchange of 4-bromo-1,1,1-trifluorobutane was problematic, yielding 25 with a low reaction efficiency.

For aryl bromides, the coupling reactions of 15 proceeded with side reactions caused by t-butyllithium to yield unexpected products. The exposure of 2-bromothiazole to the reaction conditions resulted in the replacement of bromine with the t-butyl group, followed by a conjugated addition at the C-5 position, affording 27 in 12% yield. The Negishi coupling of 4-bromodiphenyl ether was unexpectedly followed by a conjugated addition of t-butyllithium, resulting in the formation of 5,5-disubstituted pyridazinone 28 rather than the expected product.

The effect of modifying the C-5 side chain on the antibacterial activity against is summarized in Table . Comparison between 15 and 11 revealed that bromination at C-5 of the pyridazinone core had a detrimental effect on the antibacterial activity (MIC = 64 μg/mL). Compound 20, with a C12 lipid chain, exhibited the same level of antibacterial activity (MIC = 2 μg/mL) as 11, indicating that the side chain could be simplified without a loss of potency. Compound 21 revealed that the pyridazinone core itself possesses some activity (MIC = 32 μg/mL); however, a lipophilic side chain is essential for significant antibacterial activity. For 22, substitution of the side chain with a single isoprene unit afforded a comparable MIC value (2 μg/mL) to those of 11 and 20. However, reducing the chain length to methyl, as in 23, resulted in a diminished activity. As shown in 24, substitution of the cycloalkyl group at C-5 was unfavorable for antibacterial activity. Comparison of 25 with 22 revealed that additional fluorine substituents on the side chain were not beneficial; similarly, in the case of 26, the antibacterial activity was completely lost. Additionally, derivatives 27 and 28, which contained a thiazole and phenyl group at C-5, respectively, were inactive against .

Through diversification of the C-5 substituent on 11, we identified several structural features that exhibited antibacterial properties. First, the length and complexity of the diterpene chain at C-5 can be reduced to some extent without a loss of activity (20 and 22). Second, although the pyridazinone core was identified as a pharmacophore, a lipophilic side chain with a length of C5 to C12 was required to retain significant activity (21 and 23). Third, the presence of a cyclic scaffold at C-5 was not beneficial to antimicrobial potency (24, 26, and 27). Overall, 20 and 22, which are structurally simple and easily synthesized, showed potential as lead compounds to develop novel antimicrobial 2-(pyrimidin-2-yl)-5-alkyl-pyridazin-3­(2H)-ones.

Cytotoxicity of the most potent derivatives 20 and 22 was evaluated using the normal cell lines Vero (African green monkey kidney epithelial cells) and HaCaT (human keratinocyte cells). Compound 20 displayed low toxicity, with CC50 values of 316 and 226 μM for Vero and HaCaT cells, respectively. Notably, 22 was not cytotoxic within the concentration ranges evaluated for the cell lines (CC50 > 500 μM) (Table , Figure S1).

Outer membrane permeability is a key factor influencing the potency of antibacterial agents against Gram-negative bacteria. To assess this property, the permeability of 20 and 22 in was measured using the NPN uptake assay. ,− After treating 20 and 22 at concentrations eight times higher than the MIC, fluorescence was measured every 5 min for 30 min to calculate the NPN uptake and compared to that of a positive control, PMB. As shown in Figure A, both compounds rapidly disrupted the bacterial outer membrane within the first 5 min (NPN uptake factor = 0.8), achieving permeability levels comparable to that of PMB. The average NPN uptake over 30 min suggested that the observed antibacterial activity of 20 and 22 resulted from the disruption of the outer membrane of (avg. 20 = 90.8%, avg. 22 = 91.0%, avg. PMB = 100%) (Figure B).

Checkerboard assays of 20 and 22 combined with kanamycin against were used to estimate the FICI as a criterion for synergistic effects (Figure ). An FICI ≤ 0.5 indicates a synergistic effect between two agents used together, while 0.5 < FICI ≤ 4 suggests indifference. Based on their MIC values, kanamycin was used at a concentration range of 0.125–8.0 μg/mL, and compounds 20 and 22 were assessed at a range of 0.015–8.0 μg/mL. The FICI values of 20 and 22 were calculated to be 1.0, indicating no significant synergistic or antagonistic interaction caused by its combination with kanamycin (Table ).

Conclusions

In summary, the γ-hydroxybutyrolactone core of 1 was chemically modified into pyridazinones to obtain potent antimicrobial derivatives against . The synthesis of NP-like 2-(pyridinyl) and 2-(pyrimidinyl)­pyridazinones 3–14 from 1 identified 2-(5-fluoropyrimidinyl)­pyridazinone 11 as the most potent derivative. Notably, 11 exhibited an MIC value of 2 μg/mL, which represented a 64-fold increased antibacterial activity over that of 1. The SAR study on the C-5 substituent of the pyridazinone core revealed that derivatives 20 and 22, which possess decyl and 2-methyl-2-pentenyl residues at C-5, respectively, were equally as potent as those of 11. Furthermore, the NPN uptake assays of 20 and 22 exhibited a cell permeability almost identical to that of PMB, demonstrating a significant advantage in disrupting the outer membrane of .

Although extensive SAR and mechanistic studies are required to develop a more potent derivative, this study provides a novel antibacterial 2-(pyrimidin-2-yl)-5-alkyl-pyridazin-3­(2H)-one scaffold, which was inspired by NPs, as well as a practical synthetic route for diversifying the left and right fragments of the pyridazinone core. These results will be useful for further structural optimization to develop a new class of antibacterial agents against .

Experimental Section

General Experimental Procedures

All of the reagents were purchased from commercial suppliers and used without further purification. 1H and 13C NMR spectra were recorded in CDCl3 by using a Bruker 600 MHz spectrometer (Bruker BioSpin GmbH, Rheinstetten, Germany) at 297.1 K. Chemical shifts were reported in parts per million (ppm) by using CHCl3 as an internal standard. High-resolution mass spectra were obtained using a Sciex X500R Q-TOF spectrometer (Framingham, MA) equipped with an ESI source. Infrared spectra were recorded using a Bruker Alpha II FT-IR spectrometer (Bruker Optics, Ettlingen, Germany). Reactions were monitored by TLC on silica gel, with a combination of hexane and ethyl acetate as eluents. Silica gel 60 (0.040–0.063 mm) from Merck was used for column chromatography. All compounds are >95% pure by HPLC analysis.

Synthesis of Compounds 3–28

Detailed synthetic procedures and spectral information, including 1H and 13C NMR data for compounds 3–28, are provided in the Supporting Information.

Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC) Assay

(KCTC 2690) was used for the antimicrobial activity tests. Bacteria were grown for 16 h in Mueller-Hinton broth (MHB) at 37 °C, then streaked onto a tryptic soy agar (TSA) plate and cultured for an additional 16 h to isolate a pure strain. The turbidity of the bacterial suspensions was adjusted using the McFarland standard of 0.5–1.5 × 108 CFU/mL by adding sterile 1X phosphate-buffered saline (PBS). Pure compounds were dissolved in DMSO and dispensed into 96-well plates at a concentration of 128 μg/mL. The compounds were serially diluted 2-fold, from 128 to 0.25 μg/mL. Subsequently, the bacterial suspensions were inoculated in 96-well plates and incubated for 16 h at 37 °C. The lowest concentrations that inhibited bacterial growth were recorded as the MIC values. Kanamycin (K1377) was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO) and was used as a positive control.

Cytotoxicity Assay

Cell Culture

All of the cell lines used (Vero and HaCaT) were obtained from the Korean Cell Line Bank (Seoul, Korea). Minimum Essential Media (MG-63), Rosewell Park Memorial Institute 1630 (SK-BR-3, AGS, and H1299), and Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (Hep3B and Vero) were used as cell culture media; the culture media were supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS), 1% streptomycin (100 μg/mL), and penicillin (100 units/mL). The cells were maintained at 37 °C in a 5% CO2 incubator.

Cytotoxicity and Cell Growth Inhibitory Assay

The cytotoxicity of the samples was evaluated in a normal Vero cell line, and the cell growth inhibitory effect of the samples was evaluated in various human cancer cell lines, including MG-63, SK-BR-3, AGS, Hep3B, and H1299, by measuring the cell viability. Briefly, the cells were seeded in 96-well plates at a concentration of 1.4 × 104 cells/well (MG-63, AGS, Hep3B, and Vero) and 2 × 104 cells/well (SK-BR-3 and H1299), respectively. After being incubated for 16 h, the cells were treated with different concentrations of the samples and further incubated for 48 h. Cell viability was measured using the 3-(4,5-dimethyl-2-thiazolyl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT) assay.

NPN Uptake Assay

The N-Phenyl-1-naphthylamine (NPN) uptake assay was performed following the procedure from Kim et al. with minor modifications. (KCTC 2690) was inoculated into 10 mL of MHB and grown at 37 °C with shaking at 250 rpm until the culture reached mid-logarithmic growth phase. The cell culture was centrifuged at 2500 rpm for 5 min and resuspended in 5 mL of buffer A (5 mM HEPES buffer, 5 mM glucose, pH 7.2). After centrifugation, the collected cells were resuspended in 2.5 mL of buffer A to obtain a bacterial working suspension with an OD600 = 0.5. A total of 100 μL of the cells and 50 μL of buffer A containing 10 μM NPN were added to each well of a 96-well black plate. Subsequently, 50 μL of either a chemical compound (16 μg/mL), the positive control polymyxin B (PMB, 10 μg/mL), or buffer A was added to each well. Fluorescence was then immediately measured using a SpectraMax M2e microplate fluorometer (excitation 350 nm, emission 420 nm) for 30 min. At each time point, the NPN uptake factor was calculated as (Fobs – F0)/(F100 – F0), where Fobs is the measured fluorescence of the sample, F0 is the baseline fluorescence of NPN with bacteria, and F100 is the fluorescence of NPN with bacteria after adding 10 μg/mL PMB. The results were represented in two aspects: (1) the progression of NPN uptake factors measured every 5 min after adding compounds and NPN to the bacteria; (2) the average NPN uptake percentage measured over 30 min. The experiment was carried out in triplicate.

Checkerboard Assay

The checkerboard assay was carried out following the procedure from Bellio et al. with a few modifications. The compound stock solution was serially diluted 2-fold in column 1 from wells A to G on the plate, while kanamycin was serially diluted 2-fold in row H from columns 12 to 2, generating a 2D gradient concentration with 77 possible combinations of two agents. (KCTC 2690) was grown for 16 h in MHB at 37 °C, streaked onto a TSA plate, and cultured for another 16 h to isolate a pure strain. The turbidity of the bacterial suspensions was adjusted to the McFarland standard of 0.5–1.5 × 108 CFU/mL by adding sterile 1X PBS. The plate was then sealed and incubated in a stationary incubator at 37 °C for 16 h. Bacterial growth was monitored by using the same procedure as the MIC test, and optical density (OD) was measured at 600 nm. The experiment was carried out in triplicate. The percentage of growth in each well was calculated as (ODdrug combination – ODbackground)/(ODdrug free well – ODbackground) x 100. The fractional inhibition concentration index (FICI) was calculated as FICI = FICA + FICB, where the FIC of each antibiotic is defined as the MIC of the antibiotic in combination divided by the MIC of the antibiotic alone. FICI values were interpreted as follows: synergy when FICI ≤ 0.5, antagonism when FICI > 4, and indifference when 0.5 < FICI ≤ 4.
