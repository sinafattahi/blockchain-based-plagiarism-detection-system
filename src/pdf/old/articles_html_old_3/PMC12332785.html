Abstract

A comprehensive study on the dynamic mixing process of biomass particles and inert particles in a cylindrical fluidized bed is conducted. The particle mixing parameters at multipositions inside three-dimensional fluidized beds are obtained by a specially designed measurement system. The local particle mixing ratios and dispersion coefficients are analyzed. Particle mixing mechanisms, including the convection mechanism and diffusion mechanism, are analyzed based on variations of mixing ratios and dispersion coefficient at multipositions inside the beds. Results show that the averaged radial dispersion coefficient at half-radius ranges from 0.0038 to 0.026 m2/s, which is about 1.5 times of that near the wall. The averaged axial dispersion coefficient ranges from 0.004 to 0.056 m2/s, which is about 2.3 times the radial dispersion coefficient. The particle mixing at the center and top is mainly driven by a convection mechanism; meanwhile, particle mixing at the bottom and near the wall is mainly driven by a diffusion mechanism.

1. Introduction

Because of the advantages in heat and mass transfer and fuel flexibility, fluidized bed has been widely used in many processing industries (Hirano et al. and Jang et al.). In most cases, particles of different sizes or densities are used for various purposes. Typical examples of such processes are biomass fluidized bed gasifiers using inert particles to assist fluidization (Johnsson et al. and Amiri and Movahedirad). The mixing process of biomass particles and inert particles in fluidized beds is closely related to the chemical reaction in the gasifier (Johnsson et al.). A quick and uniform mixing between biomass particles and inert particles is very important for the complete gasification of biomass. Therefore, detailed knowledge about the dynamic mixing process of biomass and inert particles is very needed.

Many experimental studies on particle mixing and segregation have been reported in the literature. According to the measurement method utilized, these experimental researches can be generally classified into two types, namely, static research and dynamic research. In static research, the mixing or segregation result of particles is evaluated after a steady mixing state is reached. In dynamic research, the overall mixing process of particles is studied.

In previous static researches, the mixing or segregation result of particles was usually obtained by the so-called “bed frozen method” (Huilin et al., Szentannai and Szücs, and Dalai et al.). Gas supply is suddenly cut off after the mixing reaches a steady state and the particle mixtures settle down. Then, the static bed is separated into a certain number of layers, according to the height. Particle mixtures at different layers are removed from the fluidized bed, and their components are analyzed by sieving or weighting. The axial and lateral distribution of biomass loading from 5 to 30% by weight in silica sands wwre analyzed by Emiola-Sadiq et al., and they found that the volume fraction of light particles toward upper part of the fluidized bed increased with the increase of gas velocity. Shao et al. studied the mixing behavior between silica sands (0.28 mm, 2650 kg/m3) and large nonspherical particles (diameter ranging from 6.2 mm to 10 mm) in a rectangular fluidized bed. They found that the effect of particle shape on mixing was less important than that of particle density, and the improvement of particle mixing with gas velocity was significant at a lower gas velocity. Steady mixing can be generally reached at 12s when superficial velocity is between 0.78 and 0.94 m/s. In addition, the mixing index decreased with the increase in biomass loading, and the use of densified biomass was preferable. Barcelos et al. found that the axial mixing index of sands (1.55 mm) and cocoa shell particles (2.58 mm) varies significantly from bottom to top of the bed in three levels. It was found that better mixing can be realized when 40 wt % of coconut shells and 75 wt % of cocoa shells are used. Pablos et al. used an Uno-sample device to obtain the particle samples inside a conical spouted bed after the bed is frozen. Minimal segregation was formed close to the wall and at the bottom; meanwhile, complete mixing can be reached at the rest part of the bed. Segregation may be avoided by increasing the gas velocity to twice the minimum spouting velocity. In the bed frozen method, the measurement would interrupt fluidization, which makes the evaluation of the whole particle mixing process in fluidized beds difficult (Shao et al.).

In previous dynamic research, the whole mixing process of particles in fluidized beds was usually reviewed. Many dynamic measurement methods have been developed in recent years (Olaofe et al., Fotovat et al., Bai et al., Umekawa et al., and Wirsum et al.). Olaofe et al. marked two groups of particles when the diameter was 2.5 and 3.5 mm with different colors. The particle behavior was observed by using a digital camera. Binary mixtures segregate slightly at about 1.1 u mf, but ternary mixtures become well-mixed. The phosphorescence tracing method was used by Bakhurji et al. to study the influence of the distributor on the particle mixing process. Particle mixing when a 90°-hole distributor was used was increased to two times when a distributor with a 30° inclined-hole distributor was used. Tracer particles coated with fluorescence were used by Sette et al. to measure the lateral dispersion coefficient at the bed surface, which ranged from 0.015 to 0.04 m2/s. Sette et al. studied the lateral mixing of wood chips, pellets, and pellet char in a scale-up fluidized bed by collecting samples of particle mixtures containing a bulk bed material and a tracer agent over time at the particle outlet. The result showed that the lateral dispersion coefficient increased almost linearly with height; the fuel type and the distributor have no effect on the dispersion coefficient. The lateral dispersion coefficients were in the order of 10–2 m2/s. Another new tracing method was designed by Zhang et al., in which special silicon carbide tracer particles were heated by microwaves and detected by infrared imaging equipment. The result showed that particle mixing improved with the increase in mean gas velocity and the difference in density of particles.

The particle mixing behavior has also been studied by analyzing some parameters that are related to the distribution of special “tracer particles” in fluidized beds (indirect method). Martinez Castilla et al. analyzed the temperature field over the surface of a bubbling fluidized bed using finite volume analysis and found that lateral dispersion coefficients were about 1 × 10–3 m2/s when the excess gas velocity was less than 0.68 m/s. In the experiments conducted by Niklasson et al., the moist particles were used in a large fluidized bed and the water concentration at the surface was analyzed. The lateral dispersion coefficient of fuel particles was calculated on the basis of finite volume analysis of water concentration, and the result was about 0.1 m2/s. Schlichthaerle and Werther analyzed the particle mixing and segregation behavior in fluidized beds using solid carbon dioxide as tracers. The concentration field of CO2 at the surface of the bed was measured to reflect the particle mixing process.

The literature mentioned above mainly concentrated on particle mixing behavior in 2D fluidized beds or at the surface of 3D fluidized beds. However, a comprehensive understanding about the particle mixing behavior inside three-dimensional fluidized beds is still lacking, which is due to the difficulty in measuring mixing parameters inside the beds. Specifically, the color tracing, phosphorescent tracing, fluorescent tracing, and thermal tracing methods are applicable only to two-dimensional fluidized beds and a limited number of particles. The indirect measurement methods are also not suitable for the measurement of particle mixing parameters inside a three-dimensional fluidized bed (Liu and Chen). Moreover, as the size, density, shape, and surface property of particles can primarily affect particle mixing behavior, marking of particles may cause error in measuring the dispersion coefficient or mixing index (Huang et al. and Sette et al.).

Another important concern for particle mixing research is that the reported dispersion coefficients range from 0.0001 to 0.1 m2/s, which is a very wide span (Sette et al.). Even for particle mixing under similar conditions, the difference in the measured dispersion coefficients may be still large wherein the values span two orders of magnitude (Liu and Chen). One of the major causes of the large difference in the previously reported dispersion coefficients is measurement error. An appropriate method that enables direct, accurate, continuous measurement of particle mixing parameters in 3D fluidized beds is still lacking at present.

For this problem, a special measurement system for particle mixing behavior in three-dimensional fluidized beds is developed based on the capacitance probe method. In previous researches, the capacitance probe method was mainly applied to obtain the local particle concentration when a single kind of particle was used (Sutton and Chen, Werther, Vogt et al., and Wiesendorf and Werther). In this work, the capacitance probe method was utilized to measure particle mixing parameters when different groups of particles are used in the bed. On this basis, a comprehensive study on the particle dynamic mixing process in a cylindrical fluidized bed (inner diameter of 160 mm) is conducted.

2. Experimental Setup

2.1. Fluidized Bed System

The experimental system of the fluidized bed is shown in Figure . A cylindrical column with an inner diameter of 160 mm and a height of 1000 mm was used as the main test facility. The material used for the cylindrical column was organic glass. The gas distributor was a perforated plate with an open fraction of 3.5% and was characterized by a high pressure drop. In addition, a frequency converter and a valve were used to regulate the inlet gas velocity. The inlet gas velocity was monitored using a vortex flow meter produced in China. The distribution of measurement points is also shown in Figure. . Five measurement points that are coded from A to E were distributed at different heights at the axial line of the fluidized bed. Two measurement points that are coded from F and G were distributed at half-height of the static bed, as shown in the figure. The measurement point F is near the wall, and the measurement point G is at the half-radius of the fluidized bed. Two sets of binary mixtures were used. The first set of binary mixtures was composed of walnut shell particles (light) and quartz sands (dense), and the density ratio was 0.54. The second set of binary mixtures was composed of quartz sands (light) and aluminum oxide particles (dense), and the density ratio was 0.736. The height of the fixed bed was 160 mm, in which the dense particles were at the lower half part and the light particles were at the upper half part of the bed. The variation of mixing ratios at all measurement points with time during the overall mixing process was recorded simultaneously at a frequency of 1 kHz.

2.2. Measurement System for Particle Mixing in Three-Dimensional Fluidized Beds

The capacitance probe measurement system consisted of three parts, namely, a sensor (in-house fabricated), an amplifier (MTI Instrument AS-500), and data acquisition (NIDAQ-9174) as shown in Figure . The capacitance probe sensor consisted of three mental coaxial electrodes, namely, the central electrode, active guarded electrode, and a ground shell from the inside out. Two insulting layers were mounted between three electrodes. The electrodes of the capacitance probe were made of stainless steel, and the insulating layers were made of ceramic. Low-noise coaxial cables were used to connect these parts. The diameter of the coaxial cable was 3.5 mm. The core wire and shielding layer of the coaxial cable were made of copper, and the insulating layer was made of polyvinyl chloride. The BNC connector was a coaxial fitting, which was used to connect the capacitance probe sensor and the coaxial cable. In detail, the sensing electrode was connected to the core wire of a coaxial cable, and the active guarded electrode was connected to the shielding layer of the coaxial cable. The response of the capacitance probe system was 1 × 10–3 s.

The output signal of the capacitance probe system was a joint result of particles and air in the measuring field of the sensor. Based on previous reports, the output signal of the capacitance probe system shows a linear variation with the volume fraction of gas or particles in the sensing field of the probe sensor (Li et al. and Huang et al.). 2.3. Signal Processing Method

The data acquisition time was picked out manually by analyzing the output signal second by second. As shown in Figure , when a bubble passes by the measurement point, a peak of the output signal is obtained, and this part of the signal is removed. The emulsion phase signal was identified when the signal decreased to bottom after the peak caused by bubbles. This part of the signal (inside the blue frames illustrated in Figure ) was averaged to calculate the local mixing ratio using eq when walnut shell and quartz sand particles were used or eq when the other set of binary mixtures were used.

It has been proven by Maurer et al. that the presence of a capacitance probe in a fluidized bed (inner diameter of 14 cm) has nearly no influence on particle behavior. When the capacitance probe sensor was in a fixed bed, the signal noise of the capacitance probe system was about 1.6 × 10–3 V, which is three orders lower than the output signal. Therefore, the effect of probe intrusion and signal noise on measurement accuracy can be reasonably neglected. It is worth mentioning that the use of the capacitance probe method for particle mixing is feasible in a steady bubbling fluidized bed. Therefore, the measurement can be operated when there is a low gas velocity in the bed. If turbulent fluidization occurs, then the concentration of particles in the emulsion phase will change and the precondition of using the capacitance probe method for particle mixing is not met.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Validation of the Novel Measurement System

Binary mixtures are fully mixed based on a preset volumetric ratio in the three-dimensional fluidized bed. The inlet gas velocity used in the calibration test is about 0.95 u mf. The state of binary mixtures in the fluidized bed is close to the fluidization state but not fluidized. In this way, the particle mixing ratio will not change. The averaged measured mixing ratio at seven parts is compared with real values as shown in Figure . The relative errors between the real and measured values are 5.6 and 6.5% for walnut shell and quartz sand particles as well as quartz sand and aluminum oxide particles, respectively. The measured mixing ratios fit well with the actual values, and the uncertainty is small.

3.2. Dynamic Mixing Behavior of Particles in Three-Dimensional Fluidized Beds

The distributions of mixing ratios of dense particles with height when the walnut shell and quartz sand particles are used are shown in Figure . The gas velocity varied from 1.15 to 1.38 u mf. The figures show that at the start of mixing (t = 2 s), the dense particles accumulate at the lower part, whereas the light particles accumulate at the upper part. As the mixing time increases, the dense particles gradually disperse to the upper part, whereas light particles disperse to the lower part.

Figure illustrates the vertical distribution of f d at different gas velocities and mixing times when quartz sand and aluminum oxide particles are used. With the increase in gas velocity, the axial distribution of f d becomes flat, which indicates that uniform mixing between binary mixtures is reached. At higher gas velocity, more dense particles are carried to the surface of the bed by rising bubbles. In addition, more light particles flow downward to fill the gap caused by rising bubbles. Therefore, higher gas velocity results in a more uniform distribution of dense particles in the bed.

The effect of the density ratio on the particle mixing process is shown in Figure . The mixing ratio is measured when the particles are fluidized for 5 s with a gas velocity of about 1.26 u mf. The vertical distribution of dense particles becomes more uniform when the binary mixtures with a density ratio of 0.736 are used. When there is a larger difference in the density of particles, a more segregated state is formed in the three-dimensional fluidized bed, which is in accordance with the findings of previous research (Haghgoo et al.).

3.3. Particle Mixing Mechanism in Three-Dimensional Fluidized Beds

In bubbling fluidized beds, the mixing of particles results from a highly convective bubble flow (convection) and random motion of particles (diffusion). Convection mixing is due to the rising bubbles that carry a large amount of particles up together in its wake and simultaneously push emulsion solids aside, which results in ascending and descending emulsion solids (Sette et al.). By contrast, diffusion mixing is due to the local relative movement of particles because the motion of particles in fluidized beds is random.

In previous researches, the particle mixing process in fluidized beds was usually assumed to be a random isotropic dispersion process, which was mathematically described by Fick’s second law (Sette et al. and Olsson et al.). Overall, the mixing process was represented by a single dispersion coefficient, D. This is inadequate for understanding the physical mechanism governing the mixing process. The mechanism governing particle mixing remains unclear (Sette et al.).

For this problem, this article separately evaluated the effect of convection and diffusion on the particle mixing process. After the local mixing ratios of particles in the bed are measured, the mixing mechanism is analyzed as follows. At one measurement point, if the local mixing ratio fluctuates largely during mixing, then the large fluctuation may be due to the macro relative flow of emulsion solids containing different particles. Therefore, the governing mechanism of particle mixing is convection. On the contrary, if a small fluctuation of the local mixing ratio is observed, then mixing is primarily accomplished through the local relative motion of particles. Then, the governing mechanism of particle mixing is diffusion.

The variation in the local mixing ratios in the three-dimensional fluidized bed is shown in Figure . Walnut shell and quartz sand particle mixtures are used, and the superficial gas velocity is set to 1.38 u mf. The STD of f d is calculated using the mixing ratio in each 4 s to illustrate the fluctuation of the mixing ratio. The standard deviation (STD) of f d is given in the figure to evaluate the fluctuation of the local mixing ratio during mixing. The standard error represents the amplitude of fluctuation of the local mixing ratio, which can be also used to reflect the mixing mechanism. A threshold STD value is defined to distinguish the convection and diffusion mechanism. It can be seen from Figure that the STD of the local mixing ratio is highest (about 0.4) at the center and top of the fluidized bed, while the STD of the local mixing ratio at the bottom and near the wall is generally below 0.1. Considering the above analysis, an appropriate threshold STD value to distinguish the convection and diffusion mechanisms is set as 0.1. If the STD of the local mixing ratio is less than 0.1, then the governing mechanism for particle mixing is diffusion. Otherwise, the governing mechanism for particle mixing is convection.

As shown in Figure a, f d decreases slowly from approximately 1.0 to 0.53 at the lower part of the three-dimensional fluidized bed. The STD of f d is generally below 0.08. The fluctuation of the mixing ratio at the bottom is the smallest among these measurement points in the three-dimensional fluidized bed. Therefore, the governing mechanism of mixing at the bottom of three-dimensional fluidized beds is diffusion. On the one hand, the effect of bubbles on mixing is weak because bubbles have been generated. No coordinated motion of large particle groups is present in this part. On the other hand, from the microscopic point of view, the mean free path of particles is short because the volume fraction of particles at the bottom is high. Particles at the bottom are accelerated by the inlet gas, which results in a high collision frequency (Jin et al.). Therefore, the governing mechanism of particle mixing at the bottom of the 3D fluidized bed is diffusion.

By contrast, the particles at the center show different mixing behavior. As shown in Figure b, the STD of f d at the center rapidly increases to 0.36 at the beginning. The fluctuation of the mixing ratio at the center is the largest among these measurement points. Therefore, the governing mechanism of particle mixing at the center is convection. The mixing behavior of particles is primarily influenced by the bubble dynamic properties in the bed. At the center of three-dimensional fluidized beds, the bubbles grew after coalescence. The large bubble carries a large amount of dense particles when rising up. In addition, the light particles flow downward to fill the gap created by the bubbles, thereby resulting in the relative motion of the emulsion solids. Moreover, the local mixing ratio at the center of the bed shows a large fluctuation during mixing. Therefore, the governing mechanism of particle mixing at the center of three-dimensional fluidized beds is convection.

The mixing process of particles at the surface is shown in Figure c. The fluctuation of the local mixing ratio is larger than that at the bottom, which may be due to the relative motion of emulsion solids induced by large bubbles. Although the bubbles at the top are larger than those at the center of the bed, the fluctuation of the mixing ratio at the top is smaller than that at the center. The particle trajectory will change because of the lateral thrust exerted by bubbles, which leads to the relative motion of solids at the particle levels. Consequently, diffusion also has an important effect on mixing at the surface.

The variation in the local mixing ratio near the wall during mixing is shown in Figure d. The fluctuation of the mixing ratio is smaller than that at the center and top, but similar to that at the bottom. Therefore, diffusion is the main mechanism of particle mixing near the wall. It was found that there is a large velocity gradient of particles near the wall (Li et al.). The relative motion of solids at the particle level is enhanced, and the mixing process is primarily accomplished by diffusion.

The power spectral density of the mixing ratio at different positions is given in Figure . Dominant frequencies of local mixing ratios are observed at the center and top, which are 0.44 and 0.27 Hz, respectively. The power spectral magnitude of the mixing ratio at the center is higher than other parts of the bed. The dominant frequency of f d at the center and top is due to the heterogeneous structure of a large scale (bubble) in the fluidized bed. However, finding the main frequency during power spectral analysis at the bottom and near the wall is difficult, probably because of the absence of a large heterogeneous structure at these parts. The power spectral magnitude at the bottom and near the wall is lower than that at the center, which indicates that the variation of the local mixing state is smaller during mixing.

Based on the above-mentioned discussions, particle mixing at the center and surface is similar to “macro mixing”, which can improve the mixing of bed materials in a larger scope. Meanwhile, the mixing at the bottom and near the wall is similar to “micro mixing”, which can improve the mixing of bed materials in a smaller scope. The difference among local mixing processes at different parts of the three-dimensional fluidized bed provides useful guidance for the design of the feeding point of the fluidized bed reactor.

3.4. Particle Dispersion Coefficient in Three-Dimensional Fluidized Beds

The aforementioned local mixing processes show that the mixing mechanism of particles at various positions of the three-dimensional fluidized bed is different. Here, a new micro dispersion coefficient was proposed. Given the local mixing ratio in a three-dimensional fluidized bed, the local mass concentration of dense particles, F mj, is calculated as follows:

Fmd=fdρd(1−Fa) 13

The definition of the micro radial dispersion coefficient (J r) and vertical dispersion coefficient (J v) is given as follows:

Jr=Dmr(∂Fmj∂r) 14
Jv=Dmv(∂Fmj∂y) 15

where D mr and D mv are the radial and vertical micro dispersion coefficients, respectively; J r and J v are the local radial and vertical flux of dense particles per unit area in unit time, respectively; r is the distance from the axial line; y is the height of the measurement point. J r near the wall is evaluated on the basis of the increase in mass concentration of dense particles in unit time and unit area because the wall serves as the boundary of mixing in the lateral direction. J r near the wall is the time-averaged increase in the mass concentration of dense particles every 5 s. Then, J r at half-radius is evaluated by summing up the time-averaged increase in mass concentration of dense particles at half-radius and two times the J r near the wall. J v values at different vertical positions are calculated in the same manner.

The micro radial dispersion coefficient is shown in Figure . The density ratio is 0.54. Figure a shows that at the half-radius, the radial dispersion coefficient ranges from 0.0038 to 0.0157 m2/s, from 0.0075 to 0.0195 m2/s, and from 0.0097 to 0.026 m2/s when the gas velocities are 1.15, 1.26, and 1.38 u mf, respectively. Despite the scattered distribution of the radial dispersion coefficient in the figure, the radial dispersion coefficient increases quickly with the STD of f d. Considering that the higher STD of f d at the measurement point is due to the relative flow of emulsion solids, faster radial mixing is acquired when convection happens. In addition, high radial dispersion coefficients are found with the increase in gas velocity. Figure b shows that the radial dispersion coefficient near the wall is different from that at the half-radius. D mr primarily ranges from 0.0068 to 0.0113 m2/s, from 0.007 to 0.0148 m2/s, and from 0.0098 to 0.0171 m2/s when the gas velocities are 1.15, 1.26, and 1.38 u mf, respectively. In general, D mr at half-radius is 1.5 times that near the wall in the 3D fluidized bed.

Sette et al. found that the lateral dispersion coefficient ranged from 0.0074 to 0.00744 m2/s when the gas velocity increased from 0.33 to 0.5 m/s. A slow increase in the dispersion coefficient was found. In present research, the averaged value of the measured radial dispersion coefficient increases from 0.0088 to 0.0144 m2/s when the gas velocity increases from 0.36 to 0.46 m/s. The measured radial dispersion coefficient is close to the value acquired by Sette et al. The difference is that we found a faster increase in the dispersion coefficient with gas velocity.

The micro vertical dispersion coefficient of particles in the three-dimensional fluidized bed is shown in Figure . The density ratio is 0.54. Figure a shows that at the lower part, D mv changes from 0.004 to 0.027 m2/s. At a low gas velocity, a higher vertical dispersion coefficient is found when the STD of f d is small, indicating that the governing mechanism of particle mixing is diffusion. The particles are primarily influenced by the inlet jetting gas. At higher gas velocity, D mv increases with the STD of f d, and a higher D mv is obtained. Therefore, at higher gas velocity, the governing mechanism for vertical mixing in the three-dimensional fluidized bed is convection.

The micro vertical dispersion coefficient at the higher part is shown in Figure b. In general, the vertical dispersion coefficient at the higher part primarily falls between 0.022 and 0.056 m2/s, which is nearly three times that at the lower part of the bed. For mixing at different gas velocities, the vertical dispersion coefficient increases with the STD of f d, which indicates that the main mechanism of vertical mixing is convection.

The averaged measured radial dispersion coefficients are 0.0088, 0.0108, and 0.0144 m2/s when the gas velocities are 1.15, 1.26, and 1.38 u mf, respectively. Meanwhile, the vertical dispersion coefficients are 0.0213, 0.0244, and 0.0333 m2/s when the gas velocity are 1.15, 1.26, and 1.38 u mf, respectively. The ratios between the averaged vertical and radial dispersion coefficient are 2.42, 2.26, and 2.31, respectively, when rising the gas velocity. An obvious variation of the ratio with gas velocity is not found. In general, the vertical dispersion coefficient is about 2.3 times of the radial dispersion coefficient in the three-dimensional fluidized bed.

3.5. Particle Mixing Quality in Three-Dimensional Fluidized Beds

Higher dispersion coefficient does not always represent better mixing quality (Hoomans et al.). Thus, evaluation of the local mixing quality of particles is necessary. In the literature, several methods are identified to quantify the mixing quality of particles in fluidized beds, namely, the average-height method (Deen et al.), nearest-neighbor method (Deen et al.), Lacey’s method (Lacey), and particle concentration method (Sahoo et al.). In average-height and nearest-neighbor methods, the mixing index is calculated on the basis of the coordinates of all particles. Lacey’s method is used to calculate the mixing index knowing the concentration of dense particles in all cells in simulation. Evaluating the local mixing quality of binary mixtures inside three-dimensional fluidized beds using the aforementioned methods is difficult.

In addressing this problem, a new mixing index is defined in the present research. The mixing index used to evaluate local mixing quality is developed based on the concept of entropy in mathematical theory of communication introduced by Shannon. In the present research, the entropy is standardized to evaluate the mixing quality of particles in a fluidized bed. The expression of the new mixing index is given in eq .

The variation in the local mixing index with mixing time at different heights of the three-dimensional fluidized bed is shown in Figure . The density ratio is 0.54, and the gas velocity is 1.26 u mf. Fast mixing between dense and light particles is acquired at the surface. Meanwhile, the mixing at the bottom is significantly slower than that at the center and top. After about 12 s, complete mixing of binary mixtures is formed at different heights of the three-dimensional fluidized bed. The difference among local mixing qualities at different heights of the three-dimensional fluidized bed is primarily due to the difference in the bubble dynamic property and mechanic characteristic of particles. In detail, bubbles at the bottom of the bed are smaller, and the relative motion among particles is weak. With the increase in bed height, bubbles will grow because of coalescence. Consequently, more particles are carried up by large bubbles, and the relative motion of particles is remarkably enhanced.

The variation in the local mixing index with time at different radial positions is shown in Figure . Fast mixing is acquired at the axial line, and mixing at half-radius is slightly slower than that at the axial line. As shown in the figure, complete mixing is acquired at about 6 and 8.2 s at the axis and half-radius, respectively. The mixing of particles near the wall is the slowest, and complete mixing is reached at about 12 s. Therefore, the volume fraction of particles near the wall is higher, but the gas velocity is lower than that in the main flow region. The mobility of particles near the wall is poor, and the mixing is slower than that at the axial line and half-radius.

4. Conclusions

A novel measurement system for particle mixing behavior in 3D fluidized beds was developed. The mixing parameters of binary mixtures at multipositions inside the beds are acquired. The particle dynamic mixing process, dispersion coefficient, and mixing index at different positions are discussed. The main conclusions are drawn as follows:

  • 1.

    A large fluctuation of the local mixing ratio is found during the mixing process at the center and top of the bed. The governing mechanism of particle mixing at the center and top is convection. Meanwhile, weak fluctuation in a local mixing ratio is found at the bottom and near the wall of the three-dimensional fluidized bed, which indicates that the governing mechanism of particle mixing is diffusion. The dominant frequencies of the local mixing ratio at the center and top are 0.44 and 0.27 Hz, respectively, but finding the main frequency at the bottom and near the wall is difficult.

  • 2.

    At 0.5 H 0, the radial dispersion coefficient of particles at the half-radius primarily ranges from 0.0038 to 0.026 m2/s. The radial dispersion coefficient at the half-radius is 1.5 times of that near the wall in the fluidized bed. The vertical dispersion coefficient is about 2.3 times the radial dispersion coefficient in the fluidized bed. It is worth mentioning that the above conclusions are acquired based on the assumption of symmetry of a three-dimensional fluidized bed.

  • 3.

    Fastest mixing of particles is found at the top of the three-dimensional fluidized bed; meanwhile, the slowest mixing of particles is found near the wall. Good mixing can be reached when the particles are fluidized for about 12 s.
