Adeno-associated virus serotype 9 (AAV9) is currently considered the benchmark vector for clinical cardiac gene transfer applications but can entail severe hepatotoxicity because of its strong hepatotropism.1,2 Against this background, we report the discovery of a novel adeno-associated virus (AAV) capsid (cap) isolated from human myocardium that uniquely combines cardiospecificity with cardiotropism in vivo and bears the potential to affect future cardiac gene therapy developments.

Harnessing bulk genomic DNA isolated from human left ventricular myocardial biopsies (n=64), we identified AAV9, AAV6, and 7 yet undescribed AAV caps (C1–7, cap-sequences National Center for Biotechnology Information/GenBank: PP736786-PP736792) (Figure [Ai]). Complete cap-sequences were bioinformatically assembled from cap-gene fragments, which were amplified from cardiac DNA by high-fidelity/ultra-low-error polymerase chain reaction, using 2 AAV-cap consensus primer pairs.3 Subsequent amino acid sequence comparisons indicated high similarity of AAV6 with AAVC1–4 and AAV9 with AAVC5–6, whereas AAVC7 showed the least homology (Figure [Aii]). A phylogenetic sequence assessment suggested closer similarity of AAVC7 with AAV13 and AAV3 serotypes (Figure [Aiii]).

To assess in vivo biodistribution characteristics, AAV sequences were cloned, produced, and affinity-purified for tail vein coinjections in mice (Figure [Bi]). As each vector was distinguishable by a 15-nucleotide barcoded cytomegalovirus promoter–driven yfp (yellow fluorescent protein) reporter, AAVC7 was identified as the only vector with a positive in vivo heart-liver ratio by barcoded vector genome amplicon next-generation sequencing (Figure [Bii]). Against AAV9, AAVC7 displayed a significantly reduced tropism in noncardiac organs (Figure [Biii]). This instigated a single-vector study focusing on cardiac and hepatic transduction and immunogenicity of AAVC7 and AAV9. (Figure [Ci]). Both vectors exhibited equivalent cardiotropism assessed by real-time polymerase chain reaction analysis of the cytomegalovirus-yfp reporter. However, compared with AAV9, AAVC7 yfp mRNA levels were almost undetectable in the liver and significantly lower in quadriceps muscle (Figure [Cii]). AAVC7 also induced a 3-fold lower anticapsid IgG antibody response than AAV9 without capsid cross-reactivity (Figure [Ciii]), potentially ascribing low immunogenicity to AAVC7.

We further characterized AAVC7 transduction properties in human induced pluripotent stem cell–derived (hiPSC) cardiomyocytes (CMs) and hepatocytes, respectively. Compared with AAV6, AAVC7 showed equally effective transduction capacity of hiPSC-CM–derived engineered heart tissue, as determined by YFP-fluorescent microscopy and fluorescence-activated cell sorting–based quantification of enzymatically isolated iPSC-CMs (Figure [Di]). Unlike AAV6, which infected an average of 59% of albumin-counterstained iPSC hepatocytes in vitro, AAVC7 did not transduce iPSC hepatocytes (Figure [Dii]). To ascertain the potential clinical relevance of these findings, both AAVC7 and AAV9 were coinjected into German landrace pigs with the barcoded cytomegalovirus-yfp system (Figure [Ei]). The amplicon next-generation sequencing–based organ-wide vector genome biodistribution analysis corroborated AAVC7’s cardiospecificity and extracardiac detargeting versus AAV9 (Figure [Eii]) as well as equally strong transduction of the right and left porcine ventricle by AAVC7 (not shown).

This prompted a dose-dependent transduction in vivo study (Figure [Fi]), using vector genome copy dosages and CM-targeted expression systems currently used in clinical AAV9-based cardiomyopathy trials.1 AAVC7 and AAV9, both carrying a TNNT2 (cardiac muscle troponin T) promoter–driven yfp reporter, exhibited a homogeneous and strong YFP protein signal in left ventricular tissue, as shown by immunohistochemistry, immunoblotting of the highest vector dose with YFP immunofluorescent detection in CMs of AAVC7-transduced hearts (Figure [Fii through Fiv]). In contrast, AAVC7 did not ectopically express YFP protein in the liver, but AAV9’s strong hepatotropism entailed an approximately 1000-fold higher dose-dependent hepatic transduction compared with any AAVC7 dosage, ultimately disabling TNNT2’s transcriptional transgene silencing (Figure [Fii through Fv]). This highlights the detrimental mechanisms for AAV9 leading to hepatic immunotoxicity in patients caused by massive vector overload and ectopic transgene expression.2

Of note, subsequent single-nucleus RNA sequencing from left ventricular tissue of an AAVC7-treated mouse heart (National Center for Biotechnology Information/Gene Expression Omnibus GSE290777) (Figure [Gi]), using the ubiquitously active cytomegalovirus-yfp reporter, showed in vivo CM-specific expression among the expected transcriptionally inferred myocardial cell populations (Figure [Gii and Giii]).4 Last, we tested whether clinically dosed AAVC7 may safely affect cardiac function in vivo using the previously published relaxin peptide–regulatable huRXFP1 (human relaxin family peptide receptor 1) inotropic reporter system (Figure [Hi]).5 Baseline cardiac contractile performance and heart rate were unchanged between sham, AAVC7-luciferase, and AAVC7-huRXFP1–treated hearts (Figure [Hii]). Intraperitoneal administration of 50 μg relaxin peptide yielded a similar chronotropic response in all groups, but only AAVC7-huRXFP1–treated hearts showed a substantial increase in contractile performance, as assessed by left ventricular catheterization (Figure [Hii]).

Overall, our identification of AAVC7 as a naturally evolved heart-directed AAV capsid might have far-reaching implications for the development of cardiac gene therapies. Its unique in vivo CM-targeted profile with proven ability to transduce human hearts, profound liver detargeting, potentially low immunogenicity, and excellent producibility (not shown) may enable safe and effective in vivo delivery of gene addition, silencing, or editing strategies to diseased hearts with superior characteristics to other AAVs that are currently used or being considered for the treatment of human heart diseases.1
