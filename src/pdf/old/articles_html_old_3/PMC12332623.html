Abstract

This paper studies the adsorption energy, differential charge density, work function, energy band, density of states, optical properties, and recovery time of intrinsic GaN and transition metal Ni-doped GaN in adsorbing reducing gases H2, H2S, and NH3 based on the first principles. The results show that the adsorption of H2, H2S, and NH3 on the intrinsic GaN surface all belongs to physical adsorption. The adsorption of the three gases by Ni-doped GaN is all chemical adsorption, and the adsorption energies are −0.94, −1.45, and −1.41 eV, respectively, making the adsorption more stable. Ni doping enhances the charge transfer between the GaN surface and the gas, reducing the work function and bandgap width of the adsorption system. The maximum absorption coefficient of the doped system in the visible-light range is approximately 30% higher than that of the intrinsic GaN. The recovery times of Ni-doped GaN adsorbing H2, H2S, and NH3 can be adjusted to 5.2, 3.4, and 7.2 s, respectively, by controlling the temperature. This study provides theoretical support for the detection and sensing of H2, H2S, and NH3 gases based on GaN substrates.

1. Introduction

Hydrogen (H2), hydrogen sulfide (H2S), and ammonia (NH3) are common reductive gases. H2 is highly flammable and can easily cause fires or explosions when exposed to ignition sources. − H2S and NH3, when present in the atmosphere, can react with other pollutants to form acid rain or other toxic compounds, leading to soil, water, and air pollution. , From an environmental safety perspective, it is crucial to develop suitable sensors for detecting these gases. Traditional semiconductor gas sensors often suffer from low sensitivity, poor optical response, and long recovery time, − making real-time and rapid detection of H2, H2S, and NH3 challenging. Therefore, developing gas sensors with high sensitivity, , good optical response, and ideal recovery times is of significant scientific and practical importance.

Gallium nitride (GaN) has shown great potential in optoelectronics and high-temperature, high-frequency applications due to its excellent photoelectric properties and thermal stability. − However, the gas adsorption capability of intrinsic GaN is limited, which hinders its use in high-efficiency sensor design. To enhance GaN’s gas adsorption capacity, metal doping is commonly used to modify its electronic structure and surface properties. For example, Li et al. used Be, Mg, and Zn doping to reduce the bandgap and work function of GaN. Chen et al. studied Mn-doped GaN for the adsorption of H2S and SO2, finding that doping enhanced the interaction between GaN and gas molecules, improving gas adsorption capacity. While these metal dopants have improved GaN’s adsorption capabilities, their impact on optical properties has been limited. Nickel (Ni), as an abundant metal, is often used as a dopant to enhance material optical properties. Kanwal et al. demonstrated that Ni doping in CdS resulted in a red shift in the absorption spectrum, significantly improving its optical performance. Alhassa et al. showed that Ni doping in ZnS reduced the bandgap and enhanced optical properties in the visible-light range.

Therefore, to further improve GaN’s gas adsorption capacity and optical properties, this study employs first-principles calculations to investigate the electronic properties, optical properties, and recovery time of Ni-doped GaN for the adsorption of reductive gases (H2, H2S, and NH3), providing a theoretical basis for developing high-performance gas sensors with high sensitivity, good optical response, and short recovery times.

2. Model Construction and Computational Methods

This study uses the CASTEP software package within Materials Studio to calculate the adsorption of reductive gases (H2, H2S, and NH3) on GaN. To ensure accurate results, the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) with the PBE exchange-correlation functional and DFT-D2 dispersion correction was employed for geometric optimization. , The Kohn–Sham equations and energy functionals were solved self-consistently. A 4 × 4 × 1 supercell model containing 32 atoms was constructed, with a vacuum layer of 20 Å to avoid interlayer interactions. The k-point grid was set to 4 × 4 × 1, and the cutoff energy was 400 eV. The energy tolerance during iteration was less than 2 × 10–5 eV/atom, with convergence criteria of 0.01 eV/Å for atomic forces, 0.1 GPa for internal stress, and 0.001 Å for atomic displacement. The optimized adsorption sites for intrinsic and doped GaN are shown in Figure . The GaN surface exhibits four distinct adsorption sites: Ga atom top (TGa), N atom top (TN), bridge site (B), and hollow site (H). The initial distance between the reductive gas molecules (H2, H2S, and NH3) and the GaN surface was set to 3 Å. Through computational studies comparing these four adsorption sites, the hollow site (H) demonstrated the most favorable gas adsorption performance. For specific content, please refer to the Supporting Information. Consequently, this work focuses exclusively on analyzing the adsorption behavior of H2, H2S, and NH3 at the H site of the GaN surface.

The formation energy serves as a critical indicator for evaluating system stability and doping feasibility. The formation energy of the doped system is calculated as follows:

EformN=Edoped−Epure+nμGa−nμNi 1

Here, E doped and E pure represent the total energies of the optimized Ni-doped GaN and pristine GaN, respectively. n denotes the number of dopant atoms, while μGa and μNi correspond to the chemical potentials of Ga and Ni, respectively. The calculated formation energy for the Ni-doped system is −3.3 eV. The negative value confirms the thermodynamic stability of the doped system.

The adsorption energy (E ads) is calculated as

Eads=Egas+GaN−(EGaN+Egas) 2

where E gas is the energy of a single gas molecule, E GaN is the energy of GaN without adsorbed gas, and E gas+GaN is the total energy of the adsorption system. A positive E ads indicates an endothermic process, while a negative E ads indicates an exothermic process, making the system more stable and adsorption more likely.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Adsorption Energy and Adsorption Distance

After geometric optimization, the atomic structure of the GaN surface adsorbing H2, H2S, and NH3 gas molecules is shown in Figure .

Table presents the adsorption distances and energies of H2, H2S, and NH3 on both pristine GaN and Ni-doped GaN surfaces. The adsorption energy of all the adsorbed gases on the GaN surface is negative, and the energy of the system after adsorption is lower and more stable. Notably, H2S exhibits an increased adsorption distance on pristine GaN, suggesting low sensitivity, whereas H2 and NH3 show decreased distances with adsorption energies of −0.11 and −0.08 eV, respectively, indicating weak physical adsorption. In contrast, Ni-doped GaN demonstrates significantly higher adsorption energies of −0.94, −1.45, and −1.41 eV for H2, H2S, and NH3, respectively, accompanied by reduced adsorption distances, implying a strong interaction between Ni-doped surfaces and gas molecules. The substantial changes in both adsorption distance and energy suggest a chemisorption mechanism between Ni-doped GaN surfaces and gas molecules.

3.2. Differential Charge Density

The total charge density (TCD) and electron density difference (EDD) for intrinsic and Ni-doped GaN with adsorbed gases are shown in Figure . Compared to intrinsic GaN, Ni-doped GaN exhibits greater charge transfer. The TCD plots (Figure a,c) show no orbital overlap between intrinsic GaN and gas molecules, indicating weak interactions. However, Ni-doped GaN shows a clear orbital overlap with gas molecules, suggesting the formation of chemical bonds and strong interactions, consistent with the adsorption energy analysis. The EDD plots (Figure b,d) show that charge transfer between intrinsic GaN and gas molecules is weaker than that between Ni-doped GaN and gas molecules, indicating that Ni doping enhances the interaction between GaN and gas molecules.

3.3. Work Function

The work function (φ) is defined as the minimum energy required to move an electron from the material’s Fermi level to the vacuum level, expressed as

ϕ=Evacuum−EFermi 3

where E vacuum represents the vacuum level and E Fermi denotes the Fermi level of the material. The work functions of GaN before and after doping during gas adsorption are illustrated in Figure . Variations in work function critically determine the sensing performance of sensors. After Ni doping, the work functions of all three gas adsorption systems on GaN decreased, indicating that Ni doping effectively modulates the electronic structure of the GaN surface. This modification reduces the electron affinity of the GaN surface, facilitating electron emission. The introduction of metal atoms enhances the interaction between the substrate and gas molecules, consistent with the conclusions drawn from differential charge density analysis. Furthermore, the consistent trend observed across all three gas adsorption systems demonstrates the universality of Ni doping in regulating the electronic properties of GaN, providing a theoretical basis for designing high-efficiency gas sensors.

3.4. Band Structure

As shown in Figure , the band structures of intrinsic GaN and Ni-doped GaN with adsorbed gases are presented. E = 0 eV corresponds to the Fermi level. From Figure a, the intrinsic GaN exhibits an indirect bandgap structure, where the conduction band minimum (CBM) is located at the high-symmetry point Γ, and the valence band maximum (VBM) resides at point M, yielding a calculated bandgap of 2.194 eV, consistent with previous literature reports.

For the adsorption systems of intrinsic GaN with H2, H2S, and NH3 (Figure b–d), all configurations maintain an indirect bandgap. Upon Ni doping, significant electronic interactions occur between the Ni 3d orbitals and the N 2p orbitals of GaN. This hybridization generates new impurity energy levels near the Fermi level. Concurrently, the Fermi level shifts toward the conduction band, indicating increased electron concentration and enhanced n-type semiconductor characteristics in Ni-GaN compared to intrinsic GaN. This elevated electron concentration strengthens gas adsorption capabilities. The Ni doping induces a reduction in bandgap width across all gas adsorption systems, effectively modulating the energy band structure and facilitating electron transitions. These modifications are critical for optimizing the electronic properties of GaN-based gas sensors.

3.5. Density of States

To further analyze the electronic energy states of intrinsic GaN and Ni-doped GaN surfaces after adsorbing H2, H2S, and NH3 molecules, we calculated the density of states (DOS) for gas-adsorbed GaN systems before and after doping, as shown in Figure . Here, E = 0 eV corresponds to the Fermi level. For the Ni-doped system, the total DOS curve shifts significantly toward lower energy levels, indicating that spontaneous exothermic reactions occur between the gas molecules and the GaN surface. In the Ni-doped system, new peaks emerge near the Fermi level, primarily contributed by the Ni 3d orbitals. These Ni 3d orbitals form localized states near the Fermi level, leading to a noticeable reduction in the bandgap of the doped system. This facilitates easier electron transitions, consistent with the band structure analysis results.

In the energy range of −6 to −1 eV, a significant overlap between the Ni 3d and N 2p orbitals is observed, forming strong hybridization effects. Within the range of −10 to −5 eV, Ni doping induces a prominent overlap between the 1s orbitals of H2, 2p orbitals of H2S, and N 2p orbitals of NH3 with the Ni 3d orbitals. This suggests that Ni-doped GaN exhibits stronger chemical adsorption of gas molecules, likely due to hybridization between the Ni 3d orbitals and gas molecular orbitals, forming chemical bonds. Additionally, hybridization peaks between Ni 3d and gas molecular orbitals appear in this range, and new peaks emerge at the conduction band edge after gas adsorption, indicating electron redistribution caused by chemical adsorption.

In summary, the introduction of Ni dopants enhances the interaction between the material and gas molecules through electronic hybridization effects, enabling more effective gas adsorption. However, the overall total DOS changes are not substantial, implying that structural modifications at the material surface during gas adsorption are reversible, allowing for material reuse. Furthermore, numerous impurity peaks appear in the visible-light energy range (1.62 to 3.11 eV) for the doped adsorption systems, which may enhance light absorption capabilities within this spectral region.

3.6. Optical Properties

To further verify the previous speculation, the following text calculates the optical properties of GaN when it adsorbs gases. The dielectric function is presented in complex form as ε(ω) = ε1(ω) + iε2(ω), which describes the electron transition process. Here, the real part ε1 and the imaginary part ε2 are ε1 = n 2 – k 2 and ε2 = 2nk. The real and imaginary parts can be used to describe the process of material absorbing and releasing photons. By using the Kramers–Kronig dispersion relation, not only can the real and imaginary parts of the dielectric function be derived but also the absorption coefficient I (ω) and the reflectivity R (ω) can be obtained, where ω represents the angular frequency. The dielectric function formula is as follows:

I(ω)=2ω[ε12(ω)−ε22(ω)−ε1(ω)]2 4
R(ω)=|ε1(ω)+iε2(ω)−1ε1(ω)+iε2(ω)+1|2 5

The dielectric functions for intrinsic and Ni-doped GaN with adsorbed gases are shown in Figure . Ni-doped GaN shows increased static dielectric constants for H2, H2S, and NH3 adsorption, indicating higher polarization strength and carrier mobility. The imaginary part of the dielectric function shows a red shift and increased absorption in the visible-light range, suggesting improved light absorption due to Ni doping.

The absorption spectra in the visible light range are shown in Figure . The absorption coefficient of the doped system increases by approximately 30% compared to intrinsic GaN, with a trend of increasing absorption from low to high energy. The introduction of impurity levels reduces the energy required for electron transitions, enhancing the material’s light absorption capability.

3.7. Recovery Time

The recovery time for Ni-doped GaN with adsorbed gases is calculated using transition state theory. The recovery time is defined as

τ=A−1e−Eads/(KbT) 6

where A is the attempt frequency (A = 1012 s–1), E ads is the adsorption energy, K b is the Boltzmann constant (8.62 × 10–5 eV/K), and T is the temperature. The recovery times for Ni-doped GaN at different temperatures are shown in Figure . At 370 K, the recovery time for H2 is 5.2 s; at 580 K, the recovery time for H2S is 3.4 s; and at 550 K, the recovery time for NH3 is 7.2 s. These results suggest that the recovery time can be controlled by adjusting the temperature, allowing for selective desorption of H2, H2S, and NH3.

Conclusions

This study uses first-principles calculations to investigate the adsorption of H2, H2S, and NH3 on intrinsic and Ni-doped GaN. The results show that intrinsic GaN exhibits physical adsorption for H2 and NH3 but is insensitive to H2S. Ni-doped GaN shows chemical adsorption for all three gases, with reduced adsorption energy and enhanced structural stability. Ni doping reduces the work function, increases carrier density, and introduces impurity energy levels near the Fermi level. The static dielectric constant and absorption coefficient of the doped system increase, with the maximum absorption coefficient rising by approximately 30% in the visible-light range. The recovery time analysis suggests that temperature control can be used to achieve selective desorption of H2, H2S, and NH3, facilitating gas detection in complex environments. This study deepens the understanding of gas adsorption on GaN and provides a theoretical basis for developing high-performance gas sensors with enhanced optical properties.
