Abstract

This study presents hybrid sponges composed of zinc oxide and chitosan, engineered via atomic layer deposition (ALD), as highly efficient adsorbents for the removal of textile dyes from aqueous environments, including complex dye mixtures. ALD is a vapor-phase strategy to conformally deposit photocatalytically active zinc oxide within the porous chitosan matrix, enabling enhanced water stability and improved structural integrity. The main aim is to elucidate the influence of key physicochemical parameters on the adsorption process using Direct Blue-78 as a model dye. The effects of the adsorbent amount, initial dye concentration, solution pH, ionic strength, and temperature were systematically investigated. The process occurred slightly influenced by changing pH values and ionic strength, revealing the contribution of both electrostatic forces and hydrophobic interactions. Interestingly, the increased amount of adsorbent and the reduced concentration of pollutants favored dye removal from water. A very high maximum adsorption capacity of 2000 ± 400 mg/g was observed, denoting the great performance of the proposed material that retained the same behavior if in the presence of azo-dye mixtures composed also by Direct Red 83:1 and Direct Yellow 86. Kinetic modeling was carried out, and several adsorption isotherm models, including Langmuir, Freundlich, Temkin, and Dubinin–Radushkevich, were used to fit to the experimental data. The results suggested that the proposed adsorbent had a heterogeneous adsorption surface and a single mathematical model cannot be able to fit experimental data. Furthermore, thermodynamic parameters were derived, highlighting the spontaneous and endothermic nature of the adsorption process.

1. Introduction

Clean water is essential for a healthy community; however, over the past century, due to the increased industrialization and urbanization, the water bodies have been largely affected by environmental pollution. Indeed, the large presence of contaminants in water poses important environmental and health risks and deteriorates the water quality, making it unfit for use. Textile dyes, heavy metals, and emerging contaminants are commonly detected in water sources, with dyes representing one of the primary contributors to water pollution. A significant number of industries use dyes in their processes, and the untreated effluents they discharge often contain substantial quantities of different organic dyes, frequently in complex mixtures. , So, the need to remove dyes from water arises, highlighting the great impact that these pollutants have on the whole ecosystem. Dyes are of particular concern due to their carcinogenic nature and their potential to cause various waterborne health issues, including allergic reactions, dermatitis, genetic mutations in humans, nasal septum perforation, and severe irritation of the skin, respiratory tract, and mucous membranes. Among the proposed approaches, ion exchange, membrane technologies, physicochemical processes, and biological methods are particularly noteworthy. Extensive research efforts have therefore been dedicated to the development of materials for the treatment of dye-contaminated water, with adsorption techniques emerging as being particularly advantageous. Notable benefits of adsorption include simplicity in design, operational flexibility, reusability of adsorbent materials, cost-effectiveness, ease of implementation, and the limited production of secondary waste. , For instance, natural adsorbents derived from vegetables, clay, biowaste, natural polymers etc., have been employed for this purpose. − Among natural polymers, chitosan (CH) has been extensively studied in the form of films and sponges for applications in water remediation. − While films based on chitosan generally show good stability in aqueous environments, sponges tend to revert to a hydrogel state upon water exposure, limiting their practical effectiveness. , To overcome this limitation, several studies have explored structural modifications and cross-linking strategies aimed at improving water resistance and mechanical strength. For instance, Wang et al. reported the use of sodium hydroxide solutions to stabilize sponges based on chitosan after their formation. Under alkaline conditions, the reduced solubility of chitosan promotes collapse of the polymer network, leading to increased hydrophobicity. Similarly, previous work by some members of the current research group demonstrated that the combination of biochar and sodium hydroxide improved both the stability and adsorption capacity of chitosan sponges, enhancing their ability to remove a broader range of pollutants. However, wet-chemical stabilization strategies often suffer from limited precision and may compromise the porous architecture or surface functionality of the material. In contrast, atomic layer deposition (ALD) offers a vapor-phase approach that enables precise engineering of porous biopolymer matrices with nanometer-scale control and excellent conformality. , In a recent study, Lotito et al. reported the fabrication of millimeter-thick sponges based on chitosan and conformally coated with zinc oxide via ALD, yielding a multifunctional adsorbent with enhanced water stability. The material was characterized by using different techniques in synergy, such as spectroscopic ellipsometry, XPS, TGA, UV–vis, photoluminescence, and ATR-FTIR, analyzing the effect of varying the number of ALD cycles, resulting in different ZnO thicknesses in the range of 5–30 nm on the chitosan matrix. So, the deposition process allowed a uniform integration of ZnO throughout the porous scaffold, significantly improving adsorption performance and introducing photocatalytic activity under UV irradiation.

Interestingly, surface reactions between ALD precursors and the amino and hydroxyl groups of chitosan induced structural rearrangements within the polymer matrix, resulting in a denser, more rigid, and less hydrophilic network. This chemisorption behavior effectively mimics a form of inorganic cross-linking, enhancing mechanical resistance and limiting swelling in aqueous environments. − While most ALD studies to date have focused on cellulose-derived materials, recent attention has shifted toward chitin and chitosan due to their renewable origin, biocompatibility, and the presence of functional groups that readily interact with ALD precursors. Their adaptability and suitability for large-scale processing further support their potential for sustainable water treatment technologies. − Following detailed structural and compositional analysis, Lotito et al. demonstrated that the ZnO-functionalized chitosan sponge was highly effective in removing the textile dye Direct Blue-78 (DB-78) from water, exhibiting also photocatalytic degradation under UV light. Based on these results and the careful characterization of the adsorbent already available in the literature, the present work provides an in-depth investigation of the adsorption mechanism, including kinetic and thermodynamic modeling. The maximum adsorption capacity, determined experimentally, reached 2000 ± 400 mg/g. The value is significantly higher than those reported for other natural and synthetic adsorbents, including chitosan-based systems in various physical forms, as summarized in Table S1. ,− Values that ranged from a few to 990 mg/g have been reported; however, those studies did not provide evidence of the removal of a textile dye mixture from water, which in the present study has been successfully demonstrated, showing that the hybrid adsorbent can remove other dyes, even when in complex mixtures. For this purpose, Direct Red 83:1 and Direct Yellow 86 were adopted, evaluating also the adsorption capacities. Moreover, the lack of competitive effects between the studied dyes has been observed. Novel horizons into the environmental field are thus opened with this paper, giving details to treat industrial water contaminated with pollutants. ,−

2. Experimental Section

2.1. Chemicals

The textile azo-dyes tested during this study, Direct Blue (Color Index No. 78, chemical formula: C4 2H2 5N7Na4O1 3S4, MW: 1055.1 g/mol, ε605 nm = 21,760 L·mol–1·cm–1), Direct Red (Color Index No. 83:1, chemical formula: C3 3H20N6Na4O17S4, MW: 992.77 g/mol, ε5 27 nm = 22,400 L·mol–1·cm–1), and Direct Yellow (Color Index No. 86, chemical formula: C3 9H30N10Na4O1 3S4, MW: 1066.94 g/mol, ε3 90 nm = 22,800 L·mol–1·cm–1) were supplied by COLORPRINT Fashion SL (Avenida Francisco Vitoria Laporta, S/N-17, 03830, Muro de Alcoy, Alicante, Spain) and used without further purification. Commercial-grade chitosan powder derived from crab shells (high viscosity, degree of deacetylation ≥75%, MW ≈400 000 Da), acetic acid (99.9%), and various inorganic salts (NaCl, NaBr, NaClO4, NaNO3, LiCl, KCl, MgCl2, and CaCl2), as well as HCl and NaOH for pH adjustment, were all purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (Milan, Italy). Dye stock solutions (100 mg/L) were prepared in deionized water and further diluted according to the need. pH adjustments were performed by adding either HCl or NaOH. For the atomic layer deposition process, diethylzinc (DEZ, Dock Chemicals, Inc., ≥95%, CAS 557-20-0) was used as the metal–organic precursor, Milli-Q water served as the oxygen source, and high-purity argon (99.9%) was employed as the carrier and inert gas. All experimental measurements were conducted in triplicate with error bars representing the standard deviation from the mean.

2.2. Chitosan Sponges’ Preparation Protocol

A 1% (w/v) chitosan-based solution was prepared by dissolving chitosan in 0.8% (v/v) acetic acid to enhance the polymer solubilization. The mixture was magnetically stirred for 24 h at room temperature to ensure complete dissolution. The resulting hydrogel was subsequently filtered to remove any undissolved residues and degassed for 1 h to eliminate trapped air bubbles.

The degassed hydrogel was then poured into plastic Petri dishes, which served as molds for the formation of solid-state sponges. The filled dishes were frozen at −20 °C for 24 h, followed by freeze-drying for another 24 h using a Thermo Scientific Heto LyoLab 3000 freeze dryer.

The resulting porous sponges were then subjected to Atomic Layer Deposition (ALD) for surface modification.

2.3. Atomic Layer Deposition of ZnO on Chitosan Sponges

A custom-built atomic layer deposition (ALD) reactor described by Lotito et al. was used adopting the same experimental conditions. DEZ (diethylzinc) and Milli-Q water were used as reactants, and argon (Ar) was used as carrier gas. The number of deposition cycles was set to 200. Zinc oxide was deposited on substrates including silicon wafers with native oxide and glass slides (Diamond Microscope slides, 1.2 cm × 2 cm), depending on the intended analysis.

2.4. UV–Visible Measurements

A Shimadzu UV2600i UV–vis–NIR spectrophotometer, Shimadzu Corp. was employed to acquire the absorption spectra of the dyes in the wavelength range of 200–800 nm, set a scan rate of 1 nm/s. The pollutants’ concentrations were determined by measuring their absorbance and applying the Lambert–Beer law.

2.5. Determination of the Hybrid Chitosan-Based Sponges’ Point of Zero Charge (pHPZC)

The point of zero charge (pHPZC) of the studied adsorbent was experimentally determined. ,, NaCl was dissolved in 30 mL of water to reach a 5.0 × 10–2 M solution. The pH of this solution was adjusted within the range of 2–12 (pHi) to prepare different samples. A small amount of adsorbent was then added to each sample, and the mixture was stirred at 298 K for 48 h. Afterward, the final pH values (pHF) were measured. By plotting pHi against pHF, the point where the two curves intersect represents pHPZC, which is the pH at which the adsorbent has an equal number of positive and negative charges. So, the adsorbent surface becomes positively charged at pH values below the pHPZC and negatively charged at pH values above it.

2.6. Specific Surface Area Analysis

The specific surface area of the different samples was determined by N2 adsorption (BET method). The nitrogen adsorption curves were acquired by a Sorptomatic 1990 instrument (Thermo Electron Corporation, operating with the static volumetric principle).

2.7. In Batch Adsorption Experiments

The adsorption experiments were performed after placing the obtained sponges in water properly contaminated with DB-78, adopted as a model, and/or other dyes in the mixture. The dye removal was thus obtained under continuous stirring until adsorbent saturation.

The role of parameters affecting the dye removal was carefully investigated, and for the purpose the sponges and DB-78 amounts, temperature, pH, and ionic strength were changed. 3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Overview

Our previous paper discussed the possibility of using the proposed adsorbent to remove DB-78 from water with the objective of identifying optimal conditions for fabricating hybrid CH/ZnO sponges via ALD. Following thorough characterization using a combination of complementary techniques, the key properties of the material were successfully demonstrated. The CH/ZnO sponges exhibited high water stability in contrast to unmodified chitosan sponges, which rapidly dissolved upon immersion. Preliminary results on the removal of DB-78 from an aqueous solution were also reported. In detail, building on these findings, the present work aims to investigate the kinetics and thermodynamics of DB-78 adsorption, with a particular focus on elucidating the interaction mechanism. It is worth mentioning that the quantitative analysis was obtained by monitoring over time the dye’s visible spectrum in a range of wavelengths 450–800 nm. Indeed, this dye has a characteristic band at 605 nm, attributed to a π-π* transition, which can be used to infer its amount in water. , For example, as can be observed in Figure S1, by adopting a DB-78 solution 25 mg/L, pH 6, in the presence of 15 mg of the adsorbent, it removed at room temperature (rt) around the 100% of the dye in 120 min, and accordingly, the absorption band significantly reduced its intensity. Equation was used for the % of Ads calculations. As can be observed in Scheme , the adsorbent changed from white (Scheme A,B) to blue (Scheme C) due to the dye removal from the water solution containing it (see the molecular structure at the top of Scheme ). At the same time, the latter turned from blue (Scheme D,E) to transparent (Scheme F) color at the end of the process, confirming the clear performance of the adopted adsorbent. Accordingly, the maximum adsorption capacity (q max) of CH/ZnO was also experimentally evaluated by using eq , confirming the finding. For the purpose, adsorption experiments were realized until obtaining adsorbent saturation, restituting a q max of 2000 ± 400 mg/g, a very great value if compared with other works. ,− Accordingly, the specific surface area of chitosan sponges was determined to be 500 ± 10 m2/g, with a pore volume of 0.20 ± 0.02 cm3/g. It is important to highlight that the q max value was greater than the one obtained by Gubitosa et al. referred to CH sponges (q max: 1000 mg/g) not subjected to ALD but treated only with NaOH to render them stable in water.

3.2. Effect of Adsorbent and Adsorbate Amounts

Various amounts of adsorbents and pollutants were tested to investigate their effects on the adsorption process. Specifically, adsorbent amounts ranging from 5 to 15 mg were used, while the dye’s initial concentration was fixed at 25 mg/L, with pH set to 6 and temperature at 298 K (room temperature, r.t.). To compare the results, both the adsorption percentage of adsorption and adsorption capacities, at different contact times, were calculated by using eqs and , respectively. The results indicated that the adsorption of DB-78 was strongly influenced by the amount of used CH/ZnO sponge. As the amount of adsorbent increased, the percentage of adsorption also rose (Figure A). This suggested that the available active sites of the adsorbent played a crucial role in the process. ,, At the beginning of the process, a larger number of free active sites were available, and increasing the adsorbent amount further enhanced the adsorption capacity. ,, This observation was supported by the adsorption capacity values shown in Figure B. Interestingly, the equilibrium adsorption capacity at 120 min decreased with increasing adsorbent amount. However, when the contact time needed to reach equilibrium was considered, a reverse trend was observed. This behavior can be explained by the fact that as the amount of adsorbent increased, the available adsorption sites did not get fully saturated, leading to a decrease in q t values under these conditions. According to eq , where the weight of the adsorbent appears as the denominator, the unoccupied sites also contribute to the overall adsorption capacity, lowering its value. However, the process reached equilibrium faster when an excess of active sites is present, as experimentally herein observed. To further investigate this aspect, the concentration of DB-78 varied between 6.5 and 50 mg/L, with the adsorbent amount fixed at 5 mg. Again, the percentage of adsorption (Figure C) and q t values (Figure D) were calculated. As the DB-78 concentration increased, the percentage of adsorption decreased while the q t values increased. This confirmed the importance of active sites: with lower concentrations of DB-78, more free active sites were available, resulting in a higher percentage of adsorption. However, the increase in q t values suggested that the higher dye concentration enhanced the initial removal efficiency as the presence of more DB-78 molecules favored collisions with the adsorbent, thus improving the adsorption rate. As the process progressed, the reduced number of available active sites led to a slower approach to equilibrium, which required longer contact time. , Therefore, during the removal of DB-78 by CH/ZnO hybrid sponges, both diffusion and adsorption processes should play a significant role, according to the contact time. It is worth noting that the same behavior was observed by Gubitosa et al. when chitosan sponges treated with NaOH were used, suggesting that the use of ALD did not alter the whole features of chitosan, with the important increase of the maximum adsorption capacity and water stability. Wang et al. during their work about the removal of Rose Bengal retrieved also this finding highlighting the role of active sites and available surface.

3.3. Kinetic Studies

To gain insight into the mechanism of the DB-78 removal, the PFO and PSO kinetic models were used by applying eqs and , respectively. Specifically, the data obtained when the adsorbent and dye amounts were rearranged accordingly (Figure A–D). So, the linear fitting was applied, and Tables S2 and S3 report the obtained kinetic parameters. To determine the most suitable model for the data, the correlation coefficient (R 2) was used, alongside a comparison between the experimental adsorption capacity at equilibrium (q e,exp) at 120 min of contact time and the theoretical adsorption capacity at equilibrium (q e,cal), which was obtained by applying the model. ,,, From this comparison reported in Tables S2 and S3, it was determined that the dye removal process involves a complex interplay of multiple mechanisms, making it difficult for a single kinetic model to accurately describe the experimental data. Specifically, the PFO (pseudo-first-order) kinetic model is based on the assumption that the adsorption rate is directly proportional to the number of available sites, with DB-78 diffusion playing a significant role. Conversely, the PSO (pseudo-second-order) model suggests that the adsorption rate is proportional to the square of the unoccupied sites, with the rate-limiting step being surface adsorption driven by physicochemical interactions between the dye and the adsorbent. So, since both PFO and PSO models well described the DB-78 removal by using the proposed adsorbent, the finding confirmed the kinetic relevance of both physisorption and diffusion of the pollutant from the solution toward the adsorbent. On the other hand, Wang et al. showed that the removal of Rose Bengal by chitosan sponges followed PSO kinetic, highlighting that the addition of ZnO in our proposed adsorbent favored multiple mechanisms of adsorption. To infer other kinetic details, the Weber–Morris model was also applied to the experimental data, as shown in eq . Linear regression was performed to evaluate the model’s applicability, with the assumption that intraparticle diffusion was the rate-determining step. According to the model, a straight line passing through the origin should be obtained when plotting the adsorption capacity (q t) versus the square root of time. , Conversely, if fitting the experimental data requires multiple linear segments, it may indicate that the dye adsorption process occurs in two or more distinct stages. Specifically, the results shown in Figures S2A,B were obtained. The DB-78 adsorption process can be interpreted involving two main mechanisms: (i) initial diffusion of the dye from the solution to the external surface of the adsorbent, followed by (ii) intraparticle diffusion and adsorption. This two-step behavior was reflected by the experimental data, which can be clearly divided into these two phases (Figure S2A,B), corresponding to the two straight lines necessary to describe the obtained data. Furthermore, during the second phase, as the concentration gradient of dye decreased, the rate of adsorption was also reduced, leading to the equilibrium. Tables S4 and S5 show the kinetic constants referred to the intraparticle diffusion stage. The enhanced kinetics observed further support the hypothesis that ALD-ZnO treatment introduces accessible active sites and improves dye-accessible porosity while maintaining sponge integrity. On this ground, it is possible to assess that multiple rate-limiting steps occurred during the process, confirming the results obtained during the applicability of the PFO and PSO models.

3.4. Isotherm of Adsorption

Langmuir, Freundlich, Temkin, and Dubinin–Radushkevich (D-R) isotherms were applied to the experimental data obtained by varying the concentration of DB-78. Using eqs –, different values of adsorption capacity (q e) and equilibrium concentration (C e) were used and arranged, accordingly. The results and inferred parameters are reported in Figure A–D and Table S6, respectively. The R 2 coefficients were analyzed to assess which models best described the adsorption process. All models successfully described the DB-78 removal process using CH/ZnO sponges. Again, a single model was not able to describe the process, confirming that the dye removal followed multiple pathways of interaction. , This indicated that the adsorption was heterogeneous, meaning that the distribution of adsorption sites on the adsorbent surface was not uniform in terms of energy as already reported by Wang et al. Specifically, the energy associated with the adsorption process varied as the surface of the adsorbent was covered. The conformal ZnO layer introduced by ALD may also contribute to the observed heterogeneity in the surface energetics. Finally, the D-R isotherm applicability was particularly useful for inferring the energy parameter (E), which, in this case, was found to be less than 8 kJ/mol. This suggests that the adsorption of DB-78 onto the CH/ZnO sponges mainly involved weak physical interactions between the dye molecules and the adsorbent, with the minor contribution of chemisorption and other stronger interactions.

3.5. Thermodynamic Study

Adsorption experiments were carried out by changing the temperature of the DB-78 solutions within the range of 276–303 K while keeping constant the adsorbent amount at 5 mg, the initial DB-78 concentration at 25 mg/L, and the pH at 6. The results, shown in Figure A, illustrate the adsorption capacity at different contact times. An increase in DB-78 removal was observed as the temperature rose, indicating that the process was endothermic. To further investigate this aspect, the enthalpy change (ΔH 0 2 9 8K) was determined by plotting ln­(K eq) against 1/T, as shown in Figure B. Specifically in Table S7, the values of K eq, expressed as K L, are reported and derived from the Langmuir model (eq ). This approach also allowed for the calculations of the entropy change (ΔS 0 2 9 8K) using eq . The resulting positive ΔH 0 2 9 8 value (+60 kJ/mol) confirmed the endothermic behavior of the process, while the positive ΔS 0 2 9 8 (+250 J/(mol × K)) indicated an increase in system disorder. With these thermodynamic parameters, the Gibbs free energy change (ΔG) was calculated at various temperatures. The negative values of ΔG, which became more negative as the temperature increased, demonstrated that the adsorption process was spontaneous. The detailed thermodynamic parameters are summarized in Table S8.

3.6. pH Effect

Further insights into the adsorption process were obtained by varying the pH of the DB-78 dye solutions. Three experiments were performed at pH values of 4, 6, and 12 while keeping constant the initial dye concentration at 25 mg/L and the adsorbent amount at 5 mg as well as maintaining room temperature. The results, presented in Figure A,B, display the % of adsorption and adsorption capacities as a function of pH and contact time. For the sake of comparison, Figure C,D reports the results obtained at equilibrium. A decrease in pH from 6 to 4 resulted in an increase in q t or % of Ads, whereas increasing the pH from 6 to 12 led to a slight decrease in the dye removal, though the adsorption process was not entirely inhibited. These findings can be interpreted by considering the point of zero charge (pHPZC) of the adsorbent and the molecular characteristics of the dye. As shown in Figure S3, the pHPZC of the adsorbent was approximately 6.5, which is consistent with values typically reported for chitosan-based materials. , This implies that CH/ZnO had a positive surface charge at pH < 6.5 and a negative charge at pH > 6.5, due to the protonation of the amino groups in chitosan. On the other hand, the dye molecule, owing to its sulfonate groups with a pK a < 2, had a permanent negative charge. Additionally, the dye also has tertiary amino groups that can be protonated at lower pH, while the primary and secondary amine groups were protonated in neutral conditions, starting to deprotonate only above pH 9. On this ground, it is possible to assess that, at pH values below 6.5, strong electrostatic attraction occurred between the negatively charged SO3 – groups of the dye and the positively charged amino groups of chitosan. However, the gradual decrease in adsorption efficiency from pH 4 to 6 can be attributed to reduced electrostatic interactions due to a partial deprotonation of chitosan. , The protonated amino groups of DB-78 should also contribute, favoring the repulsion. Additionally, in acidic environments, chitosan undergoes significant swelling, which can enhance dye uptake. At pH values above 6.5, both the dye and the adsorbent were mainly negatively charged, resulting in electrostatic repulsion that lowered the adsorption efficiency. To better highlight this finding, ATR-FTIR analyses were performed on CH/ZnO sponges before and after removal of DB-78 (Figure S4). Typical vibration bands of chitosan were retrieved. In detail, the amide I band, attributed to the −NHR–CO– stretching vibration, was observed at 1640 cm–1, while the amide II band, corresponding to the −NH2 bending vibration, overlapped with the carboxylate stretching mode of the acetate anions at 1541 cm–1. The broad absorption band in the range 3300–3400 cm–1 was ascribed to the combined contributions of O–H and N–H stretching vibrations. The doublet peaks at 2923 and 2875 cm–1 corresponded to asymmetric and symmetric C–H stretching modes, respectively. Characteristic peaks at 1410 and 1378 cm–1 were indicative of acetate anions and C–N bond vibrations. The signals at 1151 and 1059 cm–1 were attributed to the asymmetric and symmetric stretching of the C–O–C linkage, whereas the band at 1031 cm–1 was due to C–O stretching vibrations within alcoholic moieties. After the DB-78 removal, significant spectral modifications were evident within the 2000–1000 cm–1 region of the chitosan spectrum, with a general decrease in band intensity and ratios, suggesting reorganization of hydrogen bonding. Notably, the peak at around 1410 cm–1 reduced its relative intensity, and the C–O–C vibration modes were slightly modified. The intensity of the O–H and N–H stretching bands also decreased and occurred less defined. According to these results and changes in the amide I and II bands, it is possible to confirm the involvement of amino groups in interaction with the adsorbate, as evidenced by the shift of the C–N peak from 1378 to 1370 cm–1.

Nevertheless, since adsorption was not completely suppressed, it is likely that hydrophobic interactions and H-bonds largely contributed to the overall uptake, with the slight role of protonated amino groups of DB-78 that should be attracted by the negative adsorbent. These results are also consistent with the known behavior of ZnO-based materials in aqueous environments. As demonstrated by Xia et al., ZnO films deposited by atomic layer deposition maintain surface charge stability and integrity under neutral to mildly basic conditions, but exhibit reduced chemical stability under acidic environments. This behavior supports the observed trend in dye adsorption across the investigated pH range and highlights the importance of carefully tailoring working conditions when ALD-modified materials are deployed in water treatment applications.

3.7. Effect of Salt

To evaluate the possible influence of ionic strength on the adsorption process, a series of experiments were performed by increasing the salt concentration present in the DB-78 solution. Sodium chloride (NaCl) was initially selected as the model electrolyte to study the process. The % of adsorption was thus calculated and is presented in Figure S5A. To compare the results, the values obtained at equilibrium are reported. Increasing NaCl concentrations from 0.001 to 1 M did not significantly affect the dye removal, confirming that electrostatic interactions were not the lone predominant forces governing the adsorption process, and screening of DB-78 and chitosan charges did not affect the process. ,, To further explore the role of ionic species, the nature of the salt was varied by using lithium chloride (LiCl) and potassium chloride (KCl), thereby changing the cation nature while keeping the anion concentration (Cl–) constant. Experiments were conducted fixing the salt amount at 0.001M, and the results are shown in Figure S5B, again referring to equilibrium conditions. The lack of change in the % of DB-78 removal corroborated the previous results, further strengthened when using divalent cations, i.e., calcium chloride (CaCl2) and magnesium chloride (MgCl2), that favored an increased ionic strength value. These results confirmed that the process was carried out not only by electrostatic interactions but also by the involvement of additional forces. The large presence of H-bonds and Wan der Walls forces was thus proven, without excluding the salting out effect of dye induced by ions in solutions when present in high amounts. Finally, due to the clear presence of interaction involving SO3 – groups of DB-78 and protonated amino moieties of chitosan, the potential for competitive interactions between dye and anions for adsorption sites on the positively charged adsorbent surface was clarified. It is plausible that Cl– ions competed with the dye molecules, possibly screening the protonated amino groups of chitosan. Additional experiments were thus carried out by varying the type of anion while keeping Na+ as the constant cation. Specifically, NaCl, NaBr, and NaNO3 and NaClO4 were tested at concentrations of 0.001 M, with the results shown in Figure S5C; once again, the results refer to % of Ads calculated at equilibrium. The lack of significant changes in DB-78 removal efficiency were observed, indicating that anion competition was likely less significant than the slight shielding effects induced by the cation in the DB-78 solution. The results obtained are very significant since real wastewater should contain a large amount of electrolyte that usually inhibited the removal of dyes.

3.8. Removal Performances toward Textile Dye Mixture

To further validate the efficiency of the proposed adsorbent, the removal of two additional dyes DR 83:1 and DY 86 was investigated. Their molecular structures are presented in Figure A,B. UV–visible spectroscopy was once again employed to monitor the adsorption process given the strong absorbance of these dyes in aqueous media. Figure A,B shows the UV–vis spectra of these dyes, initially at 25 mg/L, recorded before and after 120 min of contact with 5 mg of adsorbent at pH 6 and room temperature. The percentage of adsorption was calculated and is reported in Figure C comparing the results with the DB-78 removal obtained under the same conditions of work. Both DR 83:1 and DY 86 were successfully removed from solution. Specifically, if DR 83:1 was removed to a lesser extent than DB-78, on the other hand, DY 86 was efficiently removed from water. This variation in performance was attributed to differences in the chemical structure and functional groups present in the chemical structures of the adopted dyes. Previous studies, including works by some of the authors of this paper have highlighted the influence of molecular planarity and electronic delocalization on adsorption efficiency. For instance, the presence of hydroxyl groups in DR 83:1 should promote hydrogen bonding with the adsorbent, which may alter the adsorption kinetics, as previously observed in similar systems. On the other hand, DY 86 probably favored the formation of stronger hydrophobic interactions. Accordingly, the maximum adsorption capacities for these dyes were also calculated and occurred 800 ± 300 for DR 83:1 and 2500 ± 700 for DY 86, confirming the different affinity toward the adsorbent. Starting from this consideration, a mixed-dye system was also tested, and the results are shown in Figure D. Each dye exhibits a distinct absorption spectrum, which contributes to the overall spectral profile of the mixture. As a result, a complex absorption envelope formed due to the superimposition of individual spectra was observed. Measurements were taken over a contact period of up to 1440 min, observing the important removal of dyes also if present in the mixture. Moreover, these data confirmed that DB-78 continued to be effectively removed even in complex systems, underlining the robustness conferred by vapor-phase ZnO nanoengineering.

4. Conclusions

In this study, sponges composed by chitosan and ZnO deposited via ALD were investigated, evaluating their efficiency for water remediation, specifically targeting to synthetic textile azo-dyes removal. While our previous work focused mainly on the characterization of the adsorbent, this study offers a comprehensive investigation of the textile dye Direct Blue-78 (DB-78) adsorption, systematically evaluating the influence of key physicochemical parameters on the process. The results revealed that both the quantity of adsorbent and the initial pollutant concentration influenced the adsorption efficiency. Specifically, the increase in the adsorbent amount and the dilution of DB-78 solutions favored the removal. Kinetic modeling indicated that both pseudo-first-order and pseudo-second-order models effectively described the process, implying the combined importance of dye–adsorbent collisions, availability of active sites, and diffusion phenomena. The applicability of the Weber–Morris intraparticle diffusion model further suggested that adsorption was not governed by the lone internal diffusion mechanisms. Adsorption isotherms provided additional insights into the surface heterogeneity. All applied models yielded good agreement with the experimental data, indicating a distribution of adsorption energies likely influenced by the ZnO layer introduced via ALD. Thermodynamic analysis confirmed the spontaneous and endothermic nature of the process, as evidenced by negative ΔG° and positive ΔH° and ΔS° values. Further investigation into the effects of pH and ionic strength highlighted the minor role of electrostatic interactions and the presence of strong hydrophobic forces between the dye molecules and the adsorbent. The use of atomic layer deposition (ALD) was fundamental to achieving the material performance observed in this study. This vapor-phase technique enabled the uniform incorporation of ZnO within the porous chitosan matrix, resulting in a conformal coating that chemically bonded to amino and hydroxyl groups present in the polymer network. Such interaction enhanced water stability, introduced photocatalytic functionality, and improved the mechanical integrity of the sponge by mimicking a form of inorganic cross-linking. These results demonstrate the ability of ALD to finely tune the structure and surface chemistry of biopolymer-based materials, supporting their use in scalable and regenerable systems for advanced wastewater treatment. These findings pave the way for a broader environmental use of the material, particularly in treating water contaminated with structurally different pollutants. Indeed, the material demonstrated its capacity to efficiently remove mixtures of dyes without significant competition effects, demonstrating its potential for real wastewater treatment applications.
