Abstract

Preformed particle gels (PPGs) have been introduced as an efficacious chemical technology to alleviate or reduce unwanted water production from heterogeneous mature oil fields. However, there are some limitations to the existing developed PPGs, such as mechanical robustness, temperature stability, and formation fluid resistance. To address these limitations, a nanomaterial-reinforced preformed particle gel was introduced. In this current research work, the authors have reported a new type of novel nanomaterial-reinforced PPG using the reinforcement of zinc oxide (ZnO) nanomaterial, methacrylic acid, N,N-dimethyl acrylamide as a monomer, and N,N-methylene bis acrylamide as a cross-linker. The newly developed reinforced PPG, termed RF-PPG, shows high mechanical strength and exceptional long-term thermal resistance, salinity tolerance, and pH resistance. The resulting PPG (RF-PPG) exhibited superior performance compared to conventional PPG (without nanomaterial), i.e., WRF-PPG. Preformed particle gel with reinforcement (RF-PPG) has shown the equilibrium swelling of 119 and 32 in distilled water and 30,000 ppm of NaCl brine solution, respectively. The elastic modulus of RF-PPG is up to 2100 Pa, which is significantly higher than the particle gel without reinforcement (WRF-PPG), i.e., 600 Pa. Moreover, the swelling characteristics of RF-PPG, in high salinity and pH, show less variation, although the swelling capacity of RF-PPG is less in different solvents as compared to WRF-PPG. The nanomaterial reinforcement significantly improved the long-term thermal resistance for more than 185 days in 30,000 ppm of NaCl brine solution at 100 °C and shows an increment in swelling at higher temperatures. Laboratory sandpack flooding study demonstrated that RF-PPG provides exceptional plugging capacity of more than 90% and residual resistance factor of 11.92 and 15.03 for high permeable sandpacks at 100 °C. Considering the unique characteristics of the engineered preformed particle gel, RF-PPG is ideally suited for water shutoff jobs in heterogeneous reservoirs.

1. Introduction

Globally, an increase in the energy needs prompted the oil and gas industry to search for new reservoirs and improve production from existing wells. Diverse techniques are employed to enhance production from existing wells and the lifespan of oil and gas fields, such as advanced reservoir characterization, optimization of artificial-lift systems, water shut-off strategies, and different enhanced oil recovery techniques. , When improving production from existing wells, it often comes with an unintended companion water. On average, for every three barrels of water produced, there is an accompanying production of one barrel of oil. The major challenge the oil and gas industry faces is managing this water influx. Sources for this water production can be due to reservoir-related problems (coning, reservoir heterogeneity, high permeability streaks, and induced fractures) or due to near-wellbore problems (casing leaks, channeling behind casing, and water coning). This excessive water production reduces the efficiency of hydrocarbon recovery, corrosion, and scaling, and increases water treatment costs. Also, the economic viability of reservoir survival is jeopardized by excessive water effluent, leading to diminished oil recovery. Moreover, this excessive water production poses significant economic, environmental, and operational hurdles (oil/water separation and pumping), leading to the ultimate shut-in of wells. Therefore, addressing this challenge leads to extensive investigation and development efforts in the field of water shut-off technologies. Water shut-off methods aim to reduce water production, allowing for increased oil or gas production from the well. Furthermore, two extensively employed methods to address this excessive water production are (a) Mechanical: Management of fluid flow through use of casing patches and liners, cement plugging, packers and bridge plugs, straddle packers, downhole water sink technology, perforation modifications, and use of smart wells completions, and (b) Chemical technology: Polymer gels (in situ gels, cross-linked polymer gels, and particle gels) are used to curb this undesired water production. , This chemical technique helps reduce excess water production by sealing the induced fractures and high permeability streaks, thereby increasing the oil sweep efficiency. Chemical techniques are extensively used due to their cost-effectiveness and high efficiency. Broadly, polymer gel systems are classified as in situ gels and gels prepared at surface conditions, i.e., PPG (preformed particle gel system). An in situ gel system is comprised of the injection of polymer and cross-linker into a targeted location to form a three-dimensional (3D) gel structure at the reservoir condition. − PPGs were developed to address the limitations associated with in situ gel systems. − A comparative analysis of polymer gel technologies for water shutoff applications is summarized in Table . Among the latest methods in polymer gel systems is preformed particle gel (PPG). PPGs are created by a free radical polymerization technique under surface conditions as bulk gels, which are subsequently dried and crushed into the appropriate particle sizes and then injected at desirable locations in the reservoir. When these gels come into contact with formation fluids, their hydrophilic qualities enable them to swell multiple times their initial size and stop water from diverting into high permeability streaks. , Moreover, PPGs are elastic and deformable when swelled, and they can spread through a reservoir’s porous material with ease. Furthermore, different reservoir features can be used to determine particle sizes for the production of dried PPGs, which can range in size from micrometers to centimeters. Many instances of PPG implementation in oil fields have demonstrated success, effectively managing excess water production with minimal risk and mitigating environmental footprint. − Researchers have developed sophisticated formulations to improve PPGs’ mechanical and thermal stability. PPGs that can withstand high temperatures have been created by employing tetraallylammonium chloride as a cross-linking agent. In order to increase gel strength, 2-Acrylamido-2-methyl-1-propanesulfonic acid (AMPS) or N-vinylpyrrolidone (NVP) monomers were also investigated. High-strength PPGs have also been made using divinylbenzene (DVB) as a cross-linker. , However, the soft and pliable nature of swelling gels can cause shear failure under high-temperature reservoir conditions, which would lead to ineffective plugging. Although PPGs have shown promising results and have a number of advantages over in situ gels, their efficacy in challenging reservoir settings is still restricted. Acrylamide as a monomer, N′-methylenebis (acrylamide) (NMBA) as a cross-linker, and potassium persulfate (KPS) as an initiator are commonly used in the synthesis of conventional PPGs. However, under harsh reservoir conditions and high temperatures, these formulations have the tendency to deteriorate. Some limitations include (a) Thermal: lack of sufficient thermal resistance to endure extreme reservoir conditions leading to shorter degradation periods; (b) Mechanical: insufficient strength and toughness; (c) Elasticity: inadequate gel elasticity; and (d) Swelling and Compatibility: insufficient swelling ability under high-temperature and high-salinity reservoir conditions. Thus, it necessitated finding a technology that overcomes these limitations of conventional PPGs. The latest advancement within the realm of PPGs is the incorporation of nanomaterials into the polymer matrix, enhancing various characteristics such as viscoelasticity, pH tolerance, thermal resistance, mechanical durability, and resilience to high salt concentrations. Thus, the present work aims to enhance the conventional PPG by incorporating Zinc Oxide (ZnO) nanoparticles, expecting improvements in mechanical strength, degradation resistance, and plugging efficiency through a structured synthesis and characterization process. Although various nanomaterials have been explored for reinforcing PPGs (refer to Table ), no comprehensive research or reported studies have specifically investigated the application of ZnO-based PPG for controlling excessive water production in the oil and gas industry. Moreover, ZnO is more easily available and cost-effective than other reported nanomaterials used for reinforcement. This gel is synthesized using N,N-dimethylacrylamide as the polymer due to its high water absorption and retention capacity. Its ability to bind colloidal particles enhanced the bridging effect, which played a crucial role in flocculation. N,N’-Methylenebis­(acrylamide) is used as the cross-linker. Following synthesis, ZnO-reinforced PPG underwent thorough characterization and evaluation. The effects of salinity, pH, and temperature on the swelling capacity of RF-PPG are also investigated, while rheological properties are analyzed to assess the mechanical robustness of the engineered PPG in comparison with the conventional PPG (WRF-PPG). Additionally, single sand pack flooding experiments are conducted to evaluate performance in reducing water production and improving reservoir profile conformance in heterogeneous formations. The findings confirm that incorporating ZnO nanoparticles strengthens the polymer network, minimizes premature gel breakdown, and enhances the longevity and performance of the PPG under harsh reservoir conditions.

2.3. Methodology for Preformed Particle Gel Preparation

The procedure for synthesizing nanomaterial reinforced-preformed particle gel (RF-PPG) via free radical polymerization is mentioned below, and the schematic representation of the synthesis is illustrated in Figure :

  • a.

    Initially, 0.8 g of Zinc Oxide (ZnO) nanomaterial was dispersed in 100 mL of distilled water in a beaker and stirred for 10–15 min to ensure thorough mixing. Ultrasonication was then performed using a TAKASHI Model: U250 sonicator to achieve complete dispersion and prevent chunk formation.

  • b.

    The prepared mixture was transferred into a flat-bottom flask, and 10.30 mL of N,N-dimethylacrylamide (DMA) was added, followed by stirring for 30 min.

  • c.

    Separately, a solution was prepared by dissolving 4 g of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) in 20 mL of distilled water, which was then used to neutralize 7 mL of methacrylic acid. This neutralized solution was then transferred into the flask and mixed for another 30 min.

  • d.

    Subsequently, 0.20 g of the cross-linker, N,N’-methylenebis (acrylamide) (NMBA), was added to the solution and mixed.

  • e.

    Nitrogen purging was then initiated to create an inert atmosphere for efficient polymerization, and the solution was stirred for 45 min. The mixture was transferred into a flat-bottom flask and placed in an oil bath at 60 °C.

  • f.

    To initiate free radical polymerization, potassium persulfate (KPS) was added to the solution, which was maintained at 60 °C for 20–30 min to ensure complete polymerization.

  • g.

    After the formation of a bulk gel, it was sliced into smaller pieces.

  • h.

    The gel pieces were then dried in an oven at 60 °C until completely dehydrated, followed by grinding into powder form using a blending machine. In this manner, RF-PPG, i.e., particle gel reinforced with ZnO nanomaterial, was successfully synthesized.

To evaluate the developed RF-PPG, the PPG size was selected to fall within the standard ASTM (American Society for Testing and Materials) sieve size range of 40 to 50, specifically between 0.3 and 0.425 mm. Similarly, a PPG without ZnO reinforcement (WRF-PPG) was synthesized, and both gels were compared to assess the effect of reinforcement on various properties. Moreover, the comparative characterizations of reinforced PPG and nonreinforced PPG reveal the effect of the incorporation of nanomaterials into the polymeric chain. However, the reinforcement of zinc oxide nanomaterials within the polymer matrix can occur through both physical and chemical interactions. Physical interactionssuch as van der Waals forces and electrostatic attractionscommonly take place between ZnO nanomaterials and polymer chains without requiring any functional group modifications. These non-covalent interactions promote a uniform dispersion of nanomaterials throughout the polymer matrix, a phenomenon often referred to as physical crosslinking. In addition to these physical interactions, ZnO nanomaterials can also be integrated into polymer chains via hydrogen bonding and chemical bonding. The monomers used in the formulation of the polymeric gels contain functional groups such as −COOH, −OH, and −NH2, while the surface of ZnO nanomaterials presents hydroxyl (−OH) groups. These surface hydroxyl groups enhance hydrogen bonding with the polymeric matrix, significantly contributing to water absorption. Furthermore, covalent bonding may occur when the functional groups present in the polymer or modified ZnO nanomaterials undergo chemical reactions, resulting in stronger and more permanent interactions.

2.5. Swelling Measurements

Swelling Ratio: This refers to the extent to which a polymer gel absorbs a solvent and expands upon exposure. The swelling ratio is a critical parameter, as it determines the gel’s ability to block or reduce the permeability of reservoir zones. A higher swelling ratio indicates a greater fluid absorption capacity, leading to improved fluid diversion and profile modification within the reservoir. The swelling ratio for a polymer gel is typically calculated using the following formula:

SwellingRatio=[(Wswollen−Wdry)Wdry] 1

W swollen is the weight of the polymer gel when it has absorbed the solvent.

W dry is the initial dry weight of the polymer gel.

For the determination of the swelling ratio of synthesized PPGs at different concentrations, a known weight of dried RF-PPG (W dry) was soaked in predetermined brine concentrations in a measuring cylinder and left at ambient temperature for 24 h to achieve maximum swelling capacity. Afterward, residual water was separated using filtration, and the swollen PPG was collected, weighed (W swollen), and recorded. To ensure repeatability, swelling tests were performed at least three times, and an average swelling ratio was reported within a 5% experimental error margin.

The swelling capacity of the synthesized PPGs was evaluated under varying conditions, including different brine concentrations, pH values (1–14), salts (NH4Cl, MgCl2, KCl, CaCl2), temperatures (60–120°C), and swelling time (0–540 min). Figure presents images of both dried and swollen gels.

2.6. Rheological Studies

Rheology involves investigating how materials flow and deform under applied force or stress, providing valuable insights into their behavior across various conditions, such as stress, temperature, and pressure. Gel rheology is often characterized by analyzing the elastic and viscous moduli (G′ and G″), which indicate the gel’s structural integrity and resistance to deformation before failure. The storage modulus (G′) represents the ratio of elastic stress to strain, showing how much energy is stored in the gel’s structure and its ability to recover during deformation. In contrast, the viscous modulus (G″) reflects the ratio of the viscous component to stress, indicating the energy lost in the material. When the elastic modulus is greater than the viscous modulus, the material behaves primarily as an elastic solid.

To assess the rheological properties of the synthesized PPGs, an MCR502 Rheometer (Anton Paar Co.), as shown in Figure , with parallel plate geometry and a 2 mm gap, was employed. All rheological experiments were conducted at room temperature to determine the viscoelastic behavior, specifically focusing on the elastic/storage modulus (G′) and the viscous/loss modulus (G″). The strength of the synthesized PPGs (both reinforced and nonreinforced) was evaluated by measuring the viscoelastic response.

The testing process involved an amplitude sweep test to identify the linear viscoelastic (LVE) region at a fixed angular frequency (ω) of 10 radians per second, covering a strain range of 0.001 to 1. Subsequently, a frequency sweep test was conducted in oscillation mode, exploring a frequency range of 0.1 to 100 radians per second at a strain rate of 0.005 s–1.

2.7. Long-Term Thermal Stability

The ampule sealing test was conducted to evaluate the long-term thermal stability of the synthesized PPGs. A 30,000 ppm NaCl solution was added to separate ampules containing measured amounts of dry WRF-PPG and RF-PPG samples. Figure illustrates the glass-blowing apparatus used to seal the glass ampules. It consisted of an oxygen cylinder, an LPG (liquefied petroleum gas) cylinder, and a customized nozzle. The long-term thermal stability test was then conducted by placing the sealed ampules in an oven at 100 °C using ampule holders. To evaluate the thermal stability of the developed RF-PPG and WRF-PPG, visual inspections were conducted.

3.6. Thermogravimetric Analysis (TGA) and Differential Thermogravimetric Analysis (DTG)

For the evaluation of the thermal stability of synthesized PPGs, TGA (thermogravimetric analysis) and DTG (differential thermal analysis) were performed. The comparative TGA and DTG results for RF-PPG and WRF-PPG are shown in Figure , while the analyzed data are summarized in Table . The degradation of synthesized PPGs occurred in distinct stages, as observed in Figure . In the first stage (30–120 °C), the TGA curve data shows a mass loss of 3% for RF-PPG and 12% for WRF-PPG, which can be attributed to the evaporation of free water, bound water, and volatile matter present within the particle gel. In the second stage (120–350 °C), mass loss increases to 15% for RF-PPG and 40% for WRF-PPG, likely due to the release of entrapped water molecules from the 3D polymer matrix. In the third stage (350–550 °C), a significant mass loss is observed, with 70% loss for WRF-PPG and 60% loss for RF-PPG, indicating the degradation of the cross-linked network structure, polymer branches, and primary chains. The DTG curve in Figure highlights that WRF-PPG and RF-PPG experience maximum degradation at 360 and 390 °C, respectively. These results demonstrate that the thermal decomposition stages of RF-PPG shift to higher temperatures compared to WRF-PPG, confirming that the incorporation of ZnO nanomaterials enhances the thermal stability of RF-PPG.

3.7. Rheological Studies

Analysis of the graph (Figure a) suggests that in the case of RF-PPG, the elastic modulus (G′) is greater than the viscous modulus (G″). Similarly, for WRF-PPG, the elastic modulus dominates over the viscous modulus until a crossover point, beyond which the viscous modulus surpasses the elastic modulus. This observation suggests that both gels exhibit viscoelastic characteristics; i.e., they display a combination of viscous (flow-like) and elastic (recovery-like) behavior when subjected to deformation. The frequency sweep test (Figure b) shows that the dynamic moduli (elastic and viscous) remain relatively constant up to an angular frequency of 100 rad/s for both WRF-PPG and RF-PPG, indicating the formation of a stable 3D network structure in both synthesized PPGs. Furthermore, there is a noticeable enhancement in the mechanical strength of the ZnO-reinforced PPG compared with PPG without reinforcement (WRF-PPG). This increase in strength is attributed to the higher elastic modulus of RF-PPG, as illustrated in Figure a. A higher elastic modulus indicates that the material is stiffer and more resistant to deformation under an applied load. Consequently, it can be concluded that the reinforcement of the ZnO nanomaterial significantly enhances the strength of the PPG.

3.8. Swelling Ratio Evaluation

3.8.1. Impact of Salinity on the PPG Swelling Ratio

Figure illustrates the effect of salinity on the swelling ratios of RF-PPG and WRF-PPG. The swelling ratio decreases as the brine concentration increases. This behavior can be explained by the charge balance and electrostatic repulsive forces. The negative charges of carboxylate (COO–) and N,N-dimethyl (−NMe2) groups in the polyacrylamide chain generate electrostatic repulsive forces, which create more space within the polymer network, allowing greater water absorption and subsequent expansion. , However, as the NaCl concentration increases, more Na+ ions (counterions) penetrate the polymer network, screening the anion–anion repulsive interactions. This charge shielding effect reduces the repulsive forces, thereby decreasing the available space within the polymer structure and limiting further water absorption. It can also be seen from the graph (Figure ) that the swelling ratio of WRF-PPG is greater than that of RF-PPG with an increase in saline concentration due to the incorporation of nanomaterials, which increases the cross-linking points and acts as a physical cross-linker, leading to the creation of more hindrance in the polymeric gel chain. Moreover, the extra cross-linking leads to a reduction in the contribution of osmotic pressure of the fabricated gel. Hence, it is suggested that the ZnO-nanomaterial is successfully incorporated into the polymeric gel and leads to the enhancement of the stability of the gel. −

3.8.2. Impact of pH on the PPG Swelling Ratio

The swelling behavior of PPG is also influenced by the ionic strength. This investigation explores how variations in pH impact the swelling ratio of PPG, as pH affects the ionization of functional groups within the polymer network, thereby influencing water absorption and gel expansion. The pH of the solutions was adjusted within the range of 2–12 using hydrochloric acid (HCl) and sodium hydroxide (NaOH). The variations in pH and their effects on the swelling ratio are illustrated in Figure . Under acidic conditions, an increase in pH up to neutral levels leads to an increase in swelling capacity. However, at higher pH values (alkaline conditions), the swelling capacity decreases, and the polymer absorbs less water. At pH < 5 (acidic conditions), the carboxylate (COO–) groups present on the polymer chain transform into carboxylic acid (COOH), significantly reducing the negative charge repulsion along the polymer chain. As a result, water absorption by the polymer network decreases. In an alkaline environment (pH > 9), the carboxylate ions (COO–) undergo a transformation into sodium carboxylate (COONa). The elevated pH due to the addition of NaOH increases the penetration of sodium ions into the polymer network, where they interact with carboxylate (COO–) ions. This interaction induces a screening effect on the polymer chains and reduces anion–anion electrostatic repulsion. , Consequently, water absorption decreases, leading to a lower swelling ratio.

3.8.3. Impact of Temperature on PPG Swelling

To study the impact of temperature, a known weight of dried synthesized PPGs was soaked in a 30,000 ppm of NaCl brine solution in sealed containers and kept at ambient temperature for 24 h. Initially, the swelling of the particle gel was measured at ambient temperature. Subsequently, the sealed containers were placed in an oven set at 60, 70, 80, 90, 100, and 120 °C, and the swelling capacity was recorded. From Figure , it can be observed that at temperatures below 80 °C, there is a slight increase in the swelling ratio, indicating that RF-PPG experiences minimal swelling below this threshold. However, for both synthesized PPGs, significant changes in swelling behavior are noted at temperatures above 80 °C. This increased swelling is attributed to the phenomenon of thermoswelling, which leads to the expansion of the 3D cross-linked polymer network.

3.8.4. Impact of Different Salts on the PPG Swelling Ratio

Moreover, the impact of multivalent ions on the absorption and swelling characteristics of RF-PPG and WRF-PPG was evaluated by measuring the equilibrium swelling of the fabricated samples in a brine solution containing different ions (i.e., monovalent, divalent, and multivalent ions) at a concentration of 30,000 ppm using CaCl2, KCl, MgCl2, and NH4Cl salts at ambient temperature and pressure. Figure indicates a reduction in the equilibrium swelling due to the presence of divalent ions (Mg2+ and Ca2+) compared to the monovalent ion salt solutions (K+, NH4 +) at ambient temperature and pressure. − This reduction occurs because multivalent ions have multiple bonding sites and form stronger electrostatic interactions with opposite charges, thereby decreasing the swelling capacity of PPGs.

3.8.5. Impact of Time on PPG Swelling

The swelling characteristics of the developed gels play a crucial role in assessing their effectiveness over time. The swelling behavior of RF-PPG and WRF-PPG in a 30,000 ppm NaCl brine solution over time is shown in Figure . A particle size of 30–40 mesh was used under ambient conditions to determine the swelling ratio of RF-PPG and WRF-PPG. From Figure , it can be observed that, initially, both WRF-PPG and RF-PPG absorb brine rapidly due to their hydrophilic nature. However, as the cross-linked polymer matrix becomes saturated with brine, the absorption rate slows down. The equilibrium swelling ratio is reached in less than 10 h, with values of 29 for WRF-PPG and 25 for RF-PPG. Furthermore, RF-PPG exhibits a lower swelling capacity compared to WRF-PPG due to the incorporation of ZnO nanomaterials into the polymer matrix. As the ZnO concentration increases, the degree of cross-linking also increases, leading to a reduction in the absorption capacity of the reinforced PPGs.

3.9. Long-Term Thermal Stability

The long-term thermal stability of the synthesized RF-PPG was assessed by using the ampule sealing test, as shown in Figure . This test evaluates the aging effect on (a) WRF-PPG and (b) RF-PPG swollen gel at an elevated temperature of 100 °C in a NaCl brine solution with a concentration of 30,000 ppm. The change in swelling ratio was regularly monitored, and visual signs of degradation were noted. Figure a,b depicts the swollen WRF-PPG and RF-PPG, respectively, in NaCl brine solution (30,000 ppm) at 100 °C. Degradation of WRF-PPG was observed after 55 days, whereas RF-PPG remained stable for 185 days before showing signs of degradation. The aging process of RF-PPG demonstrated superior thermal stability, with no observable degradation for more than 185 days at 100 °C. Thus, it can be concluded that RF-PPG exhibits significantly better long-term thermal stability compared to WRF-PPG, making it a more effective candidate for water shutoff applications.

3.10. Sand-Pack Studies

3.10.1. Single-Sand-Pack Experiment

A sand pack flooding test was conducted to assess the plugging efficiency of the synthesized RF-PPG, which was selected based on thermal stability, long-term thermal stability, and rheological evaluation tests. This test also provided insights into the performance of the fabricated RF-PPG in water control by determining its plugging efficiency and residual resistance factor (RRF). A single sand pack was filled with sand (30–40 mesh size) as per the characteristics mentioned in Table to perform the core flooding experiment. Uniform-sized sand particles were selected to create the sand pack, and constant pressure was applied to ensure effective compaction. The applied injection pressure (1500–2000 psi) ensured effective penetration of the RF-PPG into the porous medium, while a back pressure of 500 psi prevented premature gel breakthrough and simulated subsurface reservoir flow conditions. The temperature of 100 °C was used in the test by placing the sand-pack holder into the oven to replicate conditions found in high-temperature reservoirs. From Table , the plugging efficiency of RF-PPG was found to be 91.61% in sand pack 1 and 93.34% in sand pack 2, demonstrating the strong plugging capability of RF-PPG in sealing highly permeable zones within the formation. The core flooding test of RF-PPG was preferred over that of WRF-PPG due to its superior long-term thermal stability, mechanical strength, and swelling behavior. These characteristics suggest that RF-PPG can provide prolonged and efficient plugging, making it a suitable candidate for water control in heterogeneous reservoirs.

3.10.2. Evaluation of Flow Behavior and Applicability of Darcy’s Law in Sand-Pack Studies

The applied injection pressure ensured effective penetration of the RF-PPG into the porous medium, while the back pressure prevented premature gel breakthrough and simulated subsurface reservoir flow conditions. The temperature of 100 °C was selected to replicate conditions found in high-temperature reservoirs.

3.10.2.1. Applicability of Darcy’s Law in This Case

At 100 °C, water and vapor can exist in two phases, potentially leading to deviations from Darcy’s law, which is typically valid for single-phase, incompressible flow in porous media. However, in our specific experimental setup, despite the high temperature (100 °C), our experimental conditions were controlled such that liquid water was the dominant phase during the RF-PPG injection. The presence of back pressure helped suppress significant vapor formation, keeping the system largely in the liquid phase during flow measurements. As a result, the influence of gas-phase flow was minimal, reducing the impact of the two-phase flow complexities. Under such conditions, water behaves as an incompressible fluid, making Darcy’s law a reasonable approximation for describing permeability and fluid flow behavior.

4. Practical Applicability and Injection Challenges of ZnO-reinforced PPG in Reservoirs

Successful field implementation depends not only on material performance but also on injection feasibility, cost considerations, and reservoir-specific factors, such as rock type and water salinity.

4.1. Injection Challenges

The injectivity of PPGs depends on factors such as particle size, gel strength, and permeability of the reservoir rock. For low-permeability formations, ensuring proper gel propagation without excessive pressure buildup can be challenging. To address this, ZnO-reinforced PPGs can be optimized by controlling particle size distribution and preswelling conditions to improve injectability while maintaining effectiveness in water shutoff applications. However, injection challenges are mostly lithology-specific.

  • 1.

    Sandstone Reservoirs: Sandstones generally exhibit relatively uniform pore structures, but permeability variations due to heterogeneity can impact gel placement. The developed ZnO-reinforced PPG has been optimized to maintain a controlled particle size distribution, allowing for effective transport through high-permeability zones while preventing premature plugging. The enhanced mechanical strength helps the gel withstand shear forces during injection, ensuring deeper penetration into thief zones and fractures.

  • 2.

    Carbonate Reservoirs: Carbonate formations often contain secondary porosity, which includes vugs, fractures, and complex pore networks, which can again lead to uneven gel placement. Additionally, the surface chemistry of carbonates can influence gel adhesion. The presence of ZnO nanoparticles in the PPG formulation improves cross-linking density, reducing gel washout in highly fractured zones. However, due to potential adsorption and interaction with carbonate surfaces, preflush treatments are imperative.

4.2. Effect of Rock Type and Water Salinity

4.2.1. Rock Type

The gel’s ability to penetrate and effectively plug high-permeability channels depends on the pore structure of the reservoir rock. In carbonate reservoirs, surface interactions between the gel and rock may affect gel placement, requiring additional considerations such as preflush treatments to enhance gel adhesion.

4.2.2. Water Salinity

High salinity can impact swelling behavior and gel stability. The ZnO-reinforced PPG in this study was tested in high-salinity conditions, demonstrating improved thermal and mechanical stability. However, further optimization may be required for extreme salinity environments, which will be explored in our future work.

4.3. Cost Implications

The economic viability of PPG synthesis is crucial to its adoption in the oil and gas industry. A comprehensive cost analysis and efficiency evaluation are essential for optimizing water shutoff methods. Scaling up from laboratory to industrial production is necessary to assess feasibility under real-world conditions and ensure sufficient supply for field trials. Tables and present a cost estimation for both particle gel systemswithout reinforcement and with nanomaterial reinforcementamounting to U.S. dollars 2.32 and 2.93, respectively. Considering the efficiency of RF-PPG and its financial justification, an average oil well produces approximately 125 bbls (∼20,000 L) of oil and 754 bbls (∼120,000 L) of associated water. Under laboratory conditions, RF-PPG achieves a 90% reduction in water production, resulting in only 75 bbls (∼12,000 L) of water while maintaining oil production at 125 bbls (∼20,000 L). Additionally, on an industrial scale, a reduction of 679 bbls (∼108,000 L) of water could lead to an additional 67 bbls (∼10,800 L) of oil. Considering reservoir heterogeneity, if RF-PPG achieves 70% efficiency in real-field applications, water production would reduce to 226 bbls (∼36,000 L) instead of 754 bbls (∼120,000 L), with an additional oil recovery of 22 bbls (∼3,600 L). Thus, the incorporation of ZnO into PPGs slightly increases material costs compared to conventional PPGs. However, this increase is justified by the improved performance, leading to longer-lasting water shutoff efficiency and reduced frequency of retreatments. Additionally, ZnO is relatively cost-effective compared to other nanomaterials like graphene oxide, making it a viable choice for field applications.

4.4. Reservoir-Specific Factors

Successful field application depends on factors such as the temperature, pressure, and fluid composition. Pilot-scale studies under representative reservoir conditions are essential to validate lab-scale findings.

5. Conclusions and Future Perspective

  • 1.

    An RF-PPG (reinforced preformed particle gel) has been engineered , with the successful incorporation of zinc oxide (ZnO) as a reinforcing material (indicated by the findings from FESEM studies, P-XRD, and EDX), and subsequently assessed for its efficacy in water shutoff applications.

  • 2.

    Rheological studies have suggested better mechanical strength in synthesized reinforced PPG (RF-PPG) as compared to PPG without the reinforcement (WRF-PPG).

  • 3.

    The TGA and DTA graphs disclose that the deterioration temperature of the main polymeric backbone chain in RF-PPG rises to 390 °C, whereas in WRF-PPG, it occurs at 360 °C. It indicated an enhancement in the thermal stability of the synthesized RF-PPG due to reinforcement.

  • 4.

    Moreover, RF-PPG has also shown higher long-term temperature stability at 100 °C for around 185 days, while WRF-PPG has indicated stability for about 55 days.

  • 5.

    Single-sandpack experiments have shown improved water shutoff performance, as evidenced by a reduction in permeability following RF-PPG injection.

  • 6.

    Further large-scale field applications of ZnO-reinforced PPGs will provide deeper insights into their long-term performance, reservoir adaptability, and economic feasibility. Additionally, multiphase flow studies, numerical simulations, and environmental impact assessments should be conducted to evaluate their interaction with heterogeneous reservoirs and ensure sustainable oilfield applications.
