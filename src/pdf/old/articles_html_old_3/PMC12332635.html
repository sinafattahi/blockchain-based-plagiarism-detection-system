Abstract

There is little research on the strain characteristics of ultradeep dolomite rocks with deep burial, high stress, and developed fractures and macropores. This article comparatively studies the influence of stress and fracturing on the pore-throat structure of different types of ultradeep dolomite rocks by setting up stress experiments and fracturing experiments and using computed tomography (CT) scanning. In the stress experiment, the fluid–solid coupling effect on pore space was considered. The study shows that unlike shallow to medium carbonate reservoirs, when the stress is high but lower than the rock yield stress and in a flowing state, the studied ultradeep dolomite rock has a higher permeability after recovery from compression. When the stress is high, brittle deformation occurs inside the rock sample, and dolomite particles in the macropores are crushed and detached, blocking the middle pores with the gas flow; the two adjacent mesopores are merged into one macropore. In this way, the average pore size of the rock sample increases and the proportion of macropore volume increases. The increase in macropores plays a leading role in improving the reservoir’s permeability. The edges of dolomite crystals are compressed open under high stress, resulting in more developed intercrystalline fractures, larger fracture widths, and strengthened communication between fractures. Besides the effect of stress, the fractures generated by fracturing greatly expand and connect the existing fractures, and then improve the connectivity of the reservoir, which is the main factor leading to the improvement of reservoir permeability after fracturing. In addition, rock mechanical parameters were obtained through triaxial mechanics experiments. Dolomite rock samples that have undergone multiple cycles of stress cycling have more developed microfractures and macropores and weakened axial compression resistance and are more prone to be fractured. This study can provide a theoretical basis and guidance for the efficient development of ultradeep dolomite.

1. Introduction

Microcomputed tomography (Micro-CT) imaging offers nondestructive pore structure analysis with superior spatial resolution compared to thin-section petrography, scanning electron microscopy (SEM), and high-pressure mercury intrusion (HPMI) techniques. , Usually, thin-section analysis and SEM remain standard for lithology identification and pore classification, while combined HPMI and borehole logging data enable reservoir classification. − However, HPMI-derived pore structure parameters exhibit limited capacity to resolve ≥1 mm pore spaces in carbonate reservoirs. , Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR)-based investigations of stress-altered pore systems in tight sandstones and coals commonly rely on HPMI data inversion, − and inherent HPMI measurement constraints may compromise NMR interpretation accuracy. Micro-CT imaging uniquely enables simultaneous evaluation of rock matrix connectivity and heterogeneity with micrometer-scale fidelity, surpassing the qualitative limitations of conventional petrophysical methods.

Extensive experimental investigations have revealed a progressive understanding of stress-dependent pore evolution in carbonate and clastic reservoirs. Comparative studies demonstrate that cyclic stress loading (up to 28 MPa) induces distinct pore throat responses in differing lithologies: Salimidelshad et al. observed differential fracture closure behaviors between dolomite (φ/porosity: 27%, K/permeability: 228 mD) and calcite (φ: 24%, K: 6 mD) at 3100m depth, where compaction-dominated porosity reduction (Δφ > 15%) in dolomite contrasted with fracture network reactivation (permeability increase of 120%) at lithological boundaries exceeding yield stress thresholds. Advanced pore-scale simulations by Yang et al. − reconstructed stress-induced microstructural evolution in digital cores, revealing that fracture-dominated carbonate systems (φ: 10%, K: 16 mD) exhibit 40% pore throat connectivity loss during 28 MPa cyclic loading, while tight sandstone specimens (K: 80 mD) showed accelerated permeability decline (ΔK/K 0 > −30%) associated with throat plugging mechanisms. It is found that the extension, connection, and disconnection of fractures in fracture-type carbonate rocks are more significant during loading and unloading processes.

Micro-CT based characterization has emerged as the prevailing methodology for resolving stress-pore interactions. Comparative studies demonstrate that medium-high permeability sandstones (K: 600 mD) undergo 25–35% pore radius reduction under 20 MPa confinement, whereas tight sandstones (K: 80 mD) experience 50% connectivity loss through stress-sensitive microfracture networks (Fu et al.). The heterogeneity-stress sensitivity relationship was quantified through pore-network modeling of digital cores (φ: 4–14%, K: 160–26410 mD), showing stress-induced permeability reduction rates escalating with pore size inequality (Zhao et al.). These findings were corroborated by in situ X-ray tomography observations of limestone (φ: 38–49%) where stress cycling (0–20 MPa) triggered pore coalescence (throat count ↓40%) and vug isolation (porosity ↑12%), with subsequent core-flooding confirming 25% hysteresis in permeability recovery (Zhang et al.). performed X-ray microcomputed tomography on carbonate minerals at different effective stresses (0 MPa, 20 MPa) using high resolution to determine the microscopic pore structure, permeability, and porosity of carbonate rocks as stress changed. The study found that in limestone (calcite accounts for 97%), under the stress of 20 MPa, the large interconnected pores were disconnected and became small pores. Core flooding could lead to changes in the microstructure of rock samples, fine-grain migration, and redeposition in weakly consolidated zones near large pores, which might lead to increased porosity and pore connectivity.

Parallel advancements in triaxial testing protocols have elucidated stress-path dependent mechanical responses (Lu et al.). Experimental data indicate that deep formation rocks (Zhou et al.) exhibit 20–25% compressive strength enhancement under combined thermoporo-mechanical loading (150 °C, 30 MPa), while subcritical crack propagation models (Li et al.) quantify shear strength degradation (Δτ > 35%) as a function of fluid pressure gradients. The coupling between seepage-induced crack growth (Liu et al.) and stress-sensitive permeability (Wang et al.) establishes a mechanistic linkage between microfracture closure dynamics (permeability sensitivity coefficient >0.8 MPa–1) and hydro-mechanical system evolution.

Current research on strain behavior in ultradeep carbonate reservoirs under coupled high stress (>120 MPa) and elevated temperature remains limited, − particularly regarding multiphysics coupling effects in highly heterogeneous pore systems. , Characterized by extreme formation stresses (up to 120 MPa) and unprecedented pore-space heterogeneity with well-developed fracture-cavity networks, these reservoirs exhibit distinct stress-dependent pore evolution mechanisms that are poorly understood. A “great change” in the stress state during a production process can lead to differences in the pore-throat structure and strain characteristic parameters of a reservoir before and after the loading and unloading stress processes. In addition, in the actual production process, the flow of gas and water can cause the movement of rock particles in the pore space and then have an impact on the pore throat structure. Conventional studies neglect the dynamic interplay between gas–water two-phase flow and mechanical deformation during production, which exacerbates the pore-throat structure transformation through stress cycling.

Based on the above literature review, this study establishes a novel experimental framework combining the stress experiment, fracturing experiment with micro-CT characterization, and true triaxial mechanical testing to quantify stress-induced pore collapse and fracture reactivation mechanisms. Through stress experiments, the influence of gas and water flow on the pore/throat structure is considered. The target ultradeep carbonate samples before and after stress experiments and fracturing experiments are studied by CT scanning to nondestructively obtain the distribution characteristics of pores and throats, the connectivity, and the development of fractures and macropores before and after strain. In addition, triaxial mechanics experiments are carried out to obtain the mechanical properties of ultradeep dolomite rocks to further analyze the fracturing experiment results from the perspective of rock mechanics. Finally, through multiscale analysis of ultradeep dolomite specimens before/after cyclic loading and fracturing, coupled fluid–solid effects on pore connectivity are resolved, the characteristics of pore space changes with stress in the target ultradeep dolomite gas reservoir are analyzed, and theoretical suggestions for on-site ultradeep gas reservoir development are given.

2. Geological Background

The Dengying IV Member gas reservoir in the Gaoshiti-Moxi block belongs to the Weiyuan-Longnvsi structural group in the gentle tectonic area of the central Sichuan ancient uplift in the Sichuan Basin. It is located at the eastern end of the Leshan-Longnusi ancient uplift and is a large hidden structure in the background of the ancient uplift. The research area has undergone multiple tectonic movements, mainly ascending and descending movements, with relatively gentle structures. ,,

The Dengying Formation is a product controlled by multilevel sea level rise and fall cycles. The regional transgression regression cycle divides the Dengying Formation into the first, second, third, and fourth members (referred to as the Dengying I, II, III, and IV Member) from bottom to top. The Dengying IV Member (Figure ) is a set of carbonate rock formations developed on a platform background, continuously deposited with the Dengying III member in a conformable contact, and also in an unconformable contact with the Maidiping Formation and Qiongzhusi Formation. The western part of the research area is the Deyang Anyue Rift Trough, with the Dengying IV Member rapidly pinching out. The residual thickness of most target strata is generally 280–380m, with a thinning trend from north to south and from west to east.

The lithology in the study area is relatively complex, mainly composed of algal agglomerate dolomite, algal laminated dolomite, and algal sand debris dolomite. Affected by the uplift of the Tongwan Movement, the Dengying IV Member underwent varying degrees of leaching and erosion, forming high-quality reservoirs. The effective reservoirs are mainly developed in the algal dolomite and sandy dolomite in the upper part of the Dengying IV Member. The reservoir is mainly composed of secondary intergranular dissolved pores, intergranular dissolved pores, and medium- to small dissolved caves. The average porosity of the Dengying IV Member reservoir plunger sample is 3.87%, and the average permeability is 0.51 mD. The average porosity of the entire diameter rock sample is 3.97%, and the average horizontal permeability is 2.89 mD, exhibiting low porosity and low permeability characteristics.

The core and imaging data show that fractures are generally developed in the Dengying IV member, mainly consisting of structural fractures, pressure solution fractures, and expansion solution fractures. Unlike other karst carbonate gas reservoirs, the main storage space of the Dengying IV gas reservoir is a small-scale fracture-cave structure, with the small karst caves with a diameter of 2–5 mm being the main storage space and the development of fractures significantly improving the permeability of the reservoir. The combination of fractures and various effective pores constitutes a high-quality reservoir type for gas reservoir development. According to the pore type and the matching relationship between pores, fractures, and pores, the reservoirs in the Dengying IV Member can be divided into three types of reservoirs: fracture-macropore type, macropore type, and micropore type. Fracture-macropore-type and macropore-type reservoirs are currently the types of reservoirs that can be effectively developed, while micropore-type reservoirs cannot be effectively utilized under existing development technology.

3. Experimental Samples and Methods

3.1. Experimental Samples

Six rock samples were selected from the Dengying IV Member gas reservoir in the Gaoshiti-Moxi block, among which four full-diameter cores were scanned by CT, of which DS4 corresponds to the fracturing experiment, the remaining three cores correspond to the stress experiment, and two plunger rock samples were used for the triaxial mechanics experiment under formation conditions. 3.2. Basic Experiment

Porosity and permeability tests were conducted on plunger and full diameter rock samples using the OPP-1 high-pressure porosity and permeability tester and the TC-180 ultrahigh pressure multifunctional displacement system (as shown in Figure ) for gas reservoirs, respectively. The TC-180 displacement system can withstand a maximum pressure of 180 MPa and a temperature of 200 °C.

The D8 Discover Diffractometer was used for X-ray diffraction (XRD) quantitative analysis of total minerals and clay minerals in rock samples. During the testing process, the X-ray source emits X-rays, which undergo diffraction after passing through the sample. The diffracted light is received by the detector and converted into an electrical signal, ultimately generating a diffraction pattern. The professional XRD analysis software was used for data processing, and the mineral composition and crystal structure information on the sample were finally obtained.

SEM equipment Quanta F200 and energy-dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) were used to qualitatively characterize the pore throat structure of target reservoir rock samples. Their resolution was 5 nm, the magnification range was 20–150,000, and the scanning voltage was 15 kV. Before conduction of a test, gold particles were coated on their scanned surfaces to improve the conductivity of the sample surfaces.

3.3. Stress Experiment

Stress experiments were used to reflect the fluid–solid coupling effect on the pore space of the target ultradeep dolomite reservoir. The experimental steps and data processing refer to the core analysis method and the reservoir sensitivity flow experimental evaluation method, − and three full-diameter cores were subjected to stress experiments under formation conditions. The stress sensitivity experiments were conducted on a TC-180 displacement system. The experimental temperature is 110 °C, and the confining pressure is 130 MPa. The stress experiment of each core is divided into a pore pressure reduction process and a pore pressure increase process, which simulate the changes in porosity and permeability during the production process and the shut-in pressure recovery process in actual production, respectively. In the process of reducing the pore pressure, the pore pressure was gradually reduced from 56 to 15 MPa, and the gas-phase permeability of the rock sample was measured at each pore pressure point; in the process of increasing the pore pressure, the pore pressure was increased from 15 to 56 MPa gradually, and the gas-phase permeability of the rock sample was measured at each pore pressure point. The net stress varied between 74 and 115 MPa. Before and after the stress experiment, CT scans were performed on each core at the same resolution. The rock sample will not be broken during the stress experiment because the stress is lower than the rock yield stress.

3.4. Fracturing Experiment

Fracturing experiments are conducted to simulate the differences in pore space, permeability, and porosity before and after rock samples are fractured. In the fracturing experiment, the target core was placed vertically in the axial direction and the axial pressure above 150 MPa was evenly loaded on both sides of the end face until the core was fractured. Before and after the fracturing experiment, CT scans were performed on the core at the same resolution. By comparing the fracturing experiment and the stress experiment, the difference in the change of the pore space and the reason for the change in the seepage capacity are analyzed when the stress is higher than the yield stress of the rock. During the fracturing experiment, the rock will be broken.

3.5. CT Scan Experiment

The CT test instrument used in this experiment is the phoenix v|tome|x m micrometer CT scanner produced by General Electric Company of the United States. The full-diameter cores before and after the stress and fracturing experiment were placed on the stage of the CT instrument, and the equipment parameters were adjusted for scanning (as shown in Figure ). The CT scanning resolution before and after the stress experiment and the fracturing experiment was 37 μm. After the CT scan, the professional data processing software VOLUME GRAPHICS STUDIO MAX and FEI AVIZO were used to process the reconstructed 3D model data before and after the experiment. − The Mask method was used to access valid voxels and to clean the invalid voxels. After denoising the original CT images and after enhancing their contrast, an ant colony tracking algorithm was used to track a cracked body. A fracture space was extracted after manual screening and correction. A watershed algorithm was used for image segmentation to extract a rock pore space. The connectivity of a digital core model was analyzed by using the connected domain analysis method. , The same threshold was used to segment the rock matrix and pore space of different types of cores before and after the experiment, and digital cores for the entire full-diameter cores were obtained. After the analysis of the full-diameter core was completed, the two small areas of each core, the pore-developed area and the fracture-developed area, were selected for further processing and contrast before and after the experiment. These two regions represent the areas where pores and fractures are relatively developed, respectively. The maximum sphere method was used to extract the pore network models of two small areas, of which the voxel values are both 500 × 500 × 500. ,− Please refer to the ref for specific CT scanning and pore network modeling methods. The pore size distribution of a full diameter rock sample before and after the stress and fracturing experiment and the pore-throat size distribution in the pore-developed and fracture-developed areas before and after the experiment were compared to obtain the influence of stress and fracturing on pore space of the target ultradeep dolomite reservoir.

3.6. Triaxial Mechanics Experiment

Strain characteristic parameters such as the elastic modulus, Poisson’s ratio, and triaxial compressive strength of the different types of plunger samples were obtained by measuring the longitudinal and lateral deformation of the plunger rock sample, with which the fracturing results of different types of rock samples in fracturing experiments were further analyzed from the perspective of rock mechanics. The triaxial mechanical test of the rock samples was carried out on a plunger rock sample using the RTR-2000 high-pressure rock triaxial dynamic test system of GCTS company in the United States. The system complies with ISRM (International Society for Rock Mechanics) and ASTM (American Society of Testing Materials) standards. − The experimental temperature was 80 °C. The stress range was about 0–350 MPa until the rock sample was fractured. Due to the existence of cracks and cavities on the surface of the rock sample, the confining pressure cannot be loaded too high; otherwise, it will lead to oil inflow and the test will fail. Rocks were broken in triaxial stress experiments.

4. Results and Discussion

4.1. Lithology and Physical Properties of Rock Samples

XRD analysis reveals that the six rock specimens exhibit nearly identical mineral compositions, consisting predominantly of dolomite (95.7–99.1%), with fluorite (0.2–3.9%), quartz (0.4–3.2%), and calcite (<0.5%). Notably, clay minerals are essentially absent in all samples.

SEM characterization demonstrates that all samples comprise medium- to fine-crystalline dolomite. The reservoir pore structure, influenced by tectonic activity and subsequent diagenetic processes, displays a pore network dominated by intercrystalline pores and intercrystalline dissolution dissolved pores. Secondary pore structure features including dissolved vugs and microfractures are well developed, shown in Figure . EDS analysis confirms the presence of minor asphaltene infillings and fluorite precipitates along dolomite crystal surfaces, shown in Figure b–d.

Core analysis data (Table ) show porosity ranging from 1.5 to 12% and permeability spanning 0.03–43 mD, reflecting the pronounced heterogeneity characteristic of ultradeep dolomite reservoirs. All rock physical property results represent averages from multiple experimental replicates with relative standard deviations (RSDs) maintained below 5%, ensuring data reliability. Following the industry standard SY/T 6110-2016 “Gas Reservoir Description Method” and the Dengying Formation Reservoir Type Classification Criteria, the cores were categorized into three reservoir types: micropore-type (Sample 17(1)), macropore-type (Samples DS2, DS4), and fracture-macropore-type (Samples 14(2), DS5, DS6).

4.2. Seepage Capacity Before and After the Stress Test

The experimental results (Figure ) show that, different from previous experimental results on shallow and medium carbonate gas reservoirs, all types of cores exhibited better seepage capacity during the pore pressure increase stage. ,,− At each pore stress point, the permeability of the macropore type DS2 after the stress experiment is 1.1–6.1 times higher than that before the stress experiment; for the fracture-macropore type DS5, it is 2.0–21.0 times, and for the fracture-macropore type DS6, it is 1.7–9.1 times. After one round of depressurization, the permeability of DS2 became 105.6% of the initial permeability, 199.1% for DS5, and 174.2% for DS6. Figure shows the analysis of accuracies and uncertainties of all permeability sensitivity values based on standard deviations (SDs). Data are shown as mean ± SD, and several replicates were performed per experiment. Most of the data collections and experimental operations were done by a computer, and the high-precision and high-repeatability ISCO pumps and electronic mass flowmeters were used to measure experimental data. Therefore, the analysis results show that all RSDs were less than 5.0%, which verified the reliability of the experimental data.

This may be due to the increase in stress leading to further opening of the boundaries of the dolomite crystal, the generation of new fractures that communicate the seepage channel, and the crushing of the dolomite crystal. Fluid migration leads to dolomite particle migration and changes in the microstructure of pore throats and improves connectivity of pore throats. This conjecture must be supported by the following CT results.

4.3. CT Scan Experimental Results

The CT scanning experiment results of four different types of full-diameter cores before and after the stress experiments and fracturing experiments were obtained. The photos of four different types of full-diameter cores after the stress and fracturing experiments are shown in Figure . The pore space characteristics of the same location area before and after stress were mainly analyzed. The same location includes the full-diameter area, pore development area, and fracture development area. Due to the CT scan experiment resolution, the experimental results mainly reflect the pore spaces of mesopores, macropores, small throats, microfractures, and above, which have a major contribution to the permeability of the target rock sample. The experimental results mainly include pore size distribution, throat size distribution, pore-fracture-macropore ratio, number of fractures, pore-throat ratio, connected pore volume ratio, coordination number, etc. The purpose of the experiment is to study the effect of stress on the distribution of pore size and throat size, the development of fractures, and the connectivity in the target ultradeep dolomite gas reservoirs.

4.3.1. Development of Pores and Throats

Figure shows the CT scans of the pores and throats developed inside the rock samples before and after the stress and fracturing experiment. The CT scan images before and after the experiment were segmented using the same threshold for pores and matrix. As shown in Figure a,e for the macropore-type rock sample DS2. After the stress experiment, the micropores at 1′ and 2′ became larger, the three mesopores at 3′ converged into one macropore, and the fractures at 4′ became denser and wider. As shown in Figure b,f, for fracture-macropore type rock sample DS5, after the stress test, the particles in the pores corresponding to 1′ appeared to fall off, and the corresponding dissolution pores at 2′ and 3′ became larger. As shown in Figure c,g, for fracture-macropore rock sample DS6, after the stress test, the micropores at 1′ gathered into mesopores, the original mesopores became larger, the micropores at 2′ became mesopores, the mesopores at 3′ were squeezed and became micropores, and the corresponding fractures at 4′ extended and became wider. As shown in Figure d,h, for the macropore-type rock sample DS4, after the fracturing experiment, the two mesopores at 1′ merged into one macropore, the particles in the mesopore at 2′ fell off, the mesopore at 3′ was squeezed into micropores, and obvious fractures were formed at 4′. Different from previous understanding, after the pores of ultradeep dolomite rocks were compressed and restored, most pores did not become smaller, but bigger. This is because the boundaries of the dolomite crystal were opened by high stress, resulting in the crushing of the dolomite crystal; then the original particles in the pores fell off due to the influence of gas flow. In addition, the two adjacent dissolution pores were merged into one larger pore under the stress and are also the reason. The fractures also became wider and denser under high stress. After fracturing, the obvious fractures occurred in the studied ultradeep dolomite rocks, which greatly improved the connectivity of the rock sample.

4.3.1.1. Pore Development Extent

Analysis of the full-diameter cores, pore-developed areas, and pore-developed areas before and after the experiment (Table ) shows that under the action of stress, the number of pores decreased, the average pore radius increased, and the pore volume would increase or decrease. Under the action of fracturing, the number of pores decreased, the average pore radius increased, and the pore volume should increase. Due to the small scale of the pore development area and fracture development area, their results are used as a reference and cannot be used as the change characteristics of the overall rock sample.

4.3.1.2. Throat Development Extent

Analysis of the development of throats before and after the experiment of different types of cores (Table ) shows that under the action of stress, the number of throats decreased, the average throat radius increased or decreased, the throat length increased, and the throat volume increased or decreased. Overall, stress caused the throats in both the pore-developed area and fracture-developed area to become less and longer. After the fracturing experiment, the variation trends of the number of throats, the total throat volume, and the average radius of the throats at the pore-developed and fracture-developed areas are opposite. Although the average length of the throat at the pore-developed area increased, the number and average radius of the throat at the pore-developed area decreased more greatly, resulting in a decrease in the total throat volume at the pore-developed area. The number of throats, the average throat length, and the average throat radius at the fracture-developed area all increased, resulting in an increase in the total throat volume at the fracture-developed area. Overall, fracturing caused the throats at the pore-developed area to become less, smaller, thinner, and longer and throats at the fracture-developed area to become more, bigger, wider, and longer.

4.3.2. Distribution of Pores and Throats

4.3.2.1. Full Diameter Core

First, the pore-throat structure of the full-diameter core before and after the experiment was analyzed, and the corresponding digital core was obtained (Figure ). It can be found that after the stress experiment, the pores of DS2, DS5 and DS6 tended to become larger, and the fractures and macropores were more developed; the pores of DS4 after fracturing were more developed, and the fractures in the axial direction were very obvious.

The pore size distributions of different types of full diameter cores were analyzed (Figure ). Analysis shows that, after stress increased and decreased, the number and volume of mesopores in both macropore-type and macropore-fracture type rock samples decreased, while the number and volume of macropores increased. This is due to the brittle deformation of rock particles caused by high stress, where particles on macropores detached and slipped with the fluid, leading to the blocking of mesopores by rock debris particles (Figure b,f). It may also be due to the close pores being squeezed into a macropore (Figure d,h). This indicates that the pressure reduction in ultradeep dolomite reservoirs may not cause significant damage to reservoir permeability. After the fracturing experiment, compared to the stress experiment, the number and volume proportion of macropores increased more and the size of mesopores in the rock sample also increased. This indicates that more brittle deformation occurs in the rock sample after fracturing and the degree of particle detachment inside the pores is higher.

4.3.2.2. Pore Development Area

The corresponding pore network models were extracted further from the digital cores of pore-developed areas in each type of core. The number distribution and volume distribution of pore sizes and throat sizes at the same pore-developed area before and after the stress and fracturing experiments were obtained, and the results before and after the experiments were compared. From the digital cores and pore network models (Figure ) of the pore-developed area before and after the stress experiment or the fracturing experiment of various types of cores, it can be intuitively found that the size of some pores and throats increased significantly.

Combined with the size distribution of pores and throats (Figures and ), after bearing stress, the number proportion of pores in the pore-developed area generally decreased within the mesopore size range while increasing at the macropores. This is consistent with the variation law at the entire diameter sample. This is because under stress, particles inside the rock sample slip with the fluid, blocking some mesopores, resulting in an increase in the number of macropores. The volume proportion of pores, number, and volume proportion of throats in the pore-developed area of different rock samples did not change uniformly within the range of full-scale throats, which is caused by the small size of the observed area.

4.3.2.3. Fracture Development Area

It can be intuitively found from the digital cores and pore network models (Figure ) of fracture-developed area of various types of cores before and after stress experiments that there are more connected large pores and large throats in the fracture development area of DS2, DS5, DS6, and DS4 after the stress and fracturing experiments.

Combined with the size distribution of pores and throats where fractures developed in various types of cores before and after the experiment (Figures and ), after bearing stress, the number and volume proportion of pores and throats in the fracture-developed area of different rock samples did not change uniformly within the range of full-scale throats, which is caused by the small size of the observed area. After the fracturing experiment, the number and volume proportion of mesopores in the fracture-developed area decreased, while the number and volume proportion of macropores increased. The number and volume proportion of microthroats and small throats decreased, while the number and volume proportion of small and medium throats increased. This indicates that fracturing enhances the permeability of rock samples by increasing the number and volume of large throats and large pores in the fracture-developed area. More large throats make it easier to connect pores and improve the connectivity between pores. The larger the pore size, the stronger the storage and seepage capacity of the reservoir.

4.3.3. Development of Macropores and Fractures and Connectivity Evaluation

Comparing the connectivity of different types of cores before and after the stress test (Table ), after the stress test, the proportion of the number of mesopores in the full-diameter macropore type core DS2 decreased by 51.78%, the proportion of the number of macropores increased by about 78 times, the proportion of the number of fractures increased by 21.47%, and the proportion of the volume of connected pores and throats decreased by 46.07%. The proportion of the number of mesopores in the full-diameter fracture-macropore type core DS5 decreased by 41.27%, the proportion of the number of macropores increased by about 55 times, the proportion of the number of fractures decreased by 44.25%, and the proportion of the volume of connected pores and throats increased by 9.21%. The proportion of the number of mesopores in the full-diameter fracture-macropore core DS6 decreased by 56.29%, the proportion of the number of macropores increased by about 109 times, the proportion of number of fractures increased by 33.29%, and the proportion of the connected pore and throat volume decreased by 5.68%, After the fracturing experiment, the proportion of the number of mesopores in the full-diameter macropore type core DS4 decreased by 75.35%, the proportion of the number of macropores increased by about 26 times, the proportion of the number of fractures increased by about 25 times, and the volume proportion of the connected pores and throats increased by 66.85%.

It can be concluded that after the stress experiment, regardless of the macropore type or fracture-macropore type cores, under high stress, the edges of dolomite crystals were opened and broken, gas flow carried away broken particles, mesopores fused into macropores, and the number and volume of mesopores decreased in dolomite, resulting in an increase in the proportion of the number and volume of macropores; therefore, the seepage capacity of the cores was greatly improved. Not all rock samples have an increase in the proportion of the connected pore space volume after being subjected to stress. The volume of connected pores and throats in the macropore-type core decreased significantly due to poor connectivity, while the volume of connected pores and throats in the fracture-macropore cores decreased not much or even increased slightly due to the development of fractures. This further indicates that the increase in the number of large pores or large throats in ultradeep dolomite reservoirs is the main reason for the increase in permeability after stress. Compared to the macropore-type core DS2 under stress, the proportion of the number of fractures in the macropore-type core DS4 after fracturing increased more and the volume ratio of connected pores and throats increased more. This proves that fracturing improved the permeability and pore-throat structure connectivity of ultradeep dolomite rock samples mainly by increasing the proportion of fractures in pore space.

Comparing fractures at the same location before and after the experiments in different types of rock samples, results are shown in Figure and Table . After the stress experiment, for the macropore type rock sample DS2, the fracture volume and fracture width both decreased; for the fracture-macropore type rock samples DS5 and DS6, the fracture volume decreased but the fracture width increased. After the stress experiment, the increase in the fracture width of the fractures promoted the improvement of the seepage capacity of the rock samples. This indicates that as the stress increased, brittle deformation occurred inside the rock sample, causing inter particle fractures and the creation of new fractures. The boundaries of the dolomite crystals were further opened, creating new intercrystalline fractures connecting the seepage channels. The new connecting channels were established between existing fractures, and the fracture width increased, leading to an increase in the permeability of the rock sample. After the fracturing experiment, for macropore type rock sample DS4, the fracture volume and fracture width both greatly increased, which significantly improved the seepage capacity of the rock samples. This indicates that fracturing mainly relies on increasing the proportion of the fracture width and volume to improve the permeability of rock samples. When the rock sample is fractured, the stress is higher than the yield change stress, and a large number of fractures are generated. The fractures communicate with each other, resulting in a significant increase in the fracture width and volume and a significant increase in seepage capacity.

4.4. Triaxial Mechanics Experiment Results

The triaxial mechanics experiment further explains the conditions under which the fracturing experiment occurs; that is, the axial pressure exceeds the rock compressive strength, usually above 200 MPa for dolomite. The photos of the two rock samples after the triaxial mechanics experiments are shown in Figure . The rock mechanical parameters and physical parameters of the two rock samples are listed in Figure and Table . Combined with rock sample surface observation (Figure ), rock mechanical parameters, and petrophysical parameters, the influence of fractures and macropores on rock mechanical parameters of ultradeep dolomite rock samples was analyzed. The rock sample 14(2) has high porosity and high permeability, that is, macropores and fractures are relatively developed in rock sample 14(2). The rock sample 14(2) has low failure axial compressive strength and low Poisson’s ratio and Young’s modulus. This shows that the reservoirs with developed fractures and macropores have poor axial compression resistance, large lateral compression elasticity and weak plasticity, and are easy to fracture. The rock sample 17(1) has low porosity and low permeability, that is, the macropores and fractures are less developed in rock sample 14(2). The rock sample 17(1) has high failure axial compressive strength and high Poisson’s ratio and Young’s modulus. This shows that the reservoirs without developed fractures and macropores have strong axial compression resistance, poor lateral compression elasticity and strong plasticity, and are not prone to be fractured. , The above indicates that the target reservoir is more susceptible to be fractured after frequent well opening and closing; that is, frequent stress increases and retreats, accompanied by more developed large pores and microfractures.

5. Conclusions

This study employed micro-CT imaging and triaxial mechanical testing to investigate stress-dependent pore structure evolution, postfracturing alterations, and mechanical properties of ultradeep dolomite reservoirs in the Dengying Formation in Gaoshiti-Moxi block. The following conclusions could be drawn:

  • 1.

    Contrasting mid-to-shallow layer carbonate reservoirs, both high stress and fracturing significantly induced pronounced pore reduction in the target ultradeep dolomite reservoir. The number and volume proportion of mesopores were reduced, the number and volume proportion of macropores were increased, and the average pore radius was increased.

  • 2.

    Both high stress loading and fracturing elongated throat channels in the target ultradeep dolomite reservoir. Because the size of the target area selected for throat analysis is small, resulting in uncertainty in the results of the throat radius and throat volume.

  • 3.

    The ultradeep dolomite reservoir studied is dominated by dolomite. Under the action of high stress, brittle deformation occurs inside the target ultradeep gas reservoir, and the particles inside the macropores break down and slide with the fluid, blocking the mesopores, thereby decreasing the number of mesopores and increasing the number of macropores. Simultaneously, high stress triggers intercrystalline fracture development along dolomite crystal boundaries, accompanied by fracture widening and enhanced interconnectivity. Poststress analysis reveals that macropores exhibit a greater volumetric expansion compared to fractures. The increase in macropores is the main reason for the increase in permeability of ultradeep dolomite reservoirs after bearing high stress. This indicates that the pressure reduction in ultradeep dolomite reservoirs may not cause significant damage to reservoir permeability.

  • 4.

    During fracturing (where applied stress exceeds the rock’s yield strength), extensive fracture networks of varying scales are generated, with notable increases in fracture aperture and volume. These fractures significantly amplify reservoir connectivity. Postfracturing, fractures demonstrate a higher volumetric expansion rate relative to macropores, establishing fracture proliferation as the key factor enhancing reservoir permeability. Furthermore, repeated well shut-in and restart operations (cyclic stress exposure) are projected to amplify macropore and fracture development within the reservoir, thereby increasing its susceptibility to subsequent fracturing.
