Abstract

With the continuous development of science and technology, research and application of new materials are becoming more and more important. MXene, with its layered two-dimensional structure, is widely used as a new functional filler in rubber, offering excellent electrical conductivity, thermal conductivity, and mechanical properties, and in electronic devices, sensors, and energy storage. However, due to its high surface energy, it is not quite compatible with most polymer substrates. Therefore, it is necessary to consider an appropriate method to compound it with polymer materials, and the emulsion method is one of the better methods. In this paper, Ti3C2 MXene was prepared by etching the M’AX phase (Ti3AlC2) powder with the hydrofluoric (HF) acid solution. Then, MXene was added to a nitrile latex (NBR) solution in the emulsion method to prepare MXene/NBR nanocomposites. Compared with pure nitrile butadiene rubber, the addition of MXene significantly improves the mechanical strength, wear resistance, and electrical conductivity of rubber nanocomposites. When the MXene content was 0.75 phr, the mechanical strength of NBR nanocomposites was optimized, the Akron wear was 0.1855 cm3, the thermal conductivity was 0.3216 W/m·K, and the volume resistivity was 0.13 × 108 Ω·cm. The study also shows that MXene has a uniform dispersion, unique structure and properties, and great potential in the preparation of high-performance rubber nanocomposites.

Introduction

With the discovery of graphene, the tribological properties of composites have entered a new stage. Two-dimensional (2D) materials with a similar structure to “graphene” have attracted extensive attention due to their outstanding mechanical properties, low shear strength, good adsorption performance, and high specific surface area and are used as fillers to improve polymer properties. − In the field of tribology, researchers found that two-dimensional graphene-like materials, such as boron nitride, molybdenum disulfide, etc., were coupled by weak van der Waals force between layers, which can form a transfer layer under shear force, thus the friction and wear properties of polymers filled with 2D materials can be effectively improved. − As a novel graphene-like two-dimensional material, MXene was still in the research stage. − It was obtained by selectively etching the A atomic layer of the ternary carbonitride MAX phase. The general formula of MAX was M n+1AX n , where M was an early transition metal element, A was mainly a IIIA or IVA group element, and X was the carbon or nitrogen element. − The mechanical properties of MXene (their stiffness) were among the first to be studied by first-principles studies. MXenes’ Young’s modulus (e.g., ∼334 GPa for Ti3C2T x ) places them at the top of solution-processed 2D materials beyond BN, graphene oxide, and reduced graphene oxides. − Furthermore, MXenes exhibit excellent bending rigidity in addition to their high stiffness. Added to MXene stiffness is the MXene bending rigidity while being flexible as 2D sheets with high conformality to the substrate and ability to wrap around particles of polymers, metals, and ceramics. − More recently, MXenes have been investigated in friction, wear, and lubrication studies. − The carbide and nitride core is held together by weak interface bonds, resulting in reduced sliding resistance, and the reactivity of MXene makes them a prospective material in tribology, as has been reviewed recently. MXenes’ negative surface charge and hydrophilicity, combined with MXenes’ mechanical properties, make them candidates for application as additive-free reinforcement materials in composites and for conformal coatings for wear reduction.

Consulting relevant information has shown that MXene is widely used in energy storage, catalysis, semiconducting, and other fields. However, to the best of our knowledge, there have been few reports of MXene being used as a filler to improve the friction and wear properties of the material. For the application of wear-resistant materials, some reports have investigated the noncovalent or covalent functionalization of Ti3C2T X -based polymer composites, , using physical mixing of Ti3C2T X with polymer materials. As a category of polymers, rubber has excellent elasticity and flexibility and is commonly used in many engineering sealing systems to prevent the entry of dirt and the leakage of sealing media. Due to the presence of strong polar groups (−CN) in its molecular chain, NBR had good oil resistance (especially resistance to mineral oil, animal and plant oils, liquid fuels, and solvents) and was one of the most widely used and low-cost rubber sealing materials in the automotive and aerospace industries. , However, under sliding contact modes with relatively high speeds and high loads, NBR exhibits high friction coefficients and severe wear. Additionally, the heat generated by friction cannot be effectively dissipated, which accelerates energy dissipation and failure of the sealing system. So far, two common methods could be used to reduce the friction and wear of rubber: lubricating oil and advanced lubricating coating. − Lubricating oil or grease was often used to improve the friction of rubbers, but it had deadly drawbacks such as swelling behavior, degradation of lubricants, and environmental pollution problems. Therefore, the design and development of a 2D filler with excellent performance may be a positive strategy to improve rubber properties.

In this study, multilayered MXene nanosheets were synthesized via selective etching of a Ti3AlC2 MAX phase powder using HF acid. MXene/NBR nanocomposites were subsequently fabricated through emulsion blending, where the MXene content was systematically varied to investigate its critical role in enhancing mechanical properties, tribological performance, electrical conductivity, thermal conductivity, and thermal stability. The underlying reinforcement mechanisms, including interfacial interaction optimization and energy dissipation pathways, were elucidated through morphological and spectroscopic analyses. These findings provide fundamental insights for designing high-performance elastomeric composites in sealing applications under extreme operational conditions.

Experimental Section

Materials

Titanium aluminum carbide (Ti3AlC2) powder (purity of 98%, 600 mesh) was supplied by Jilin 11 Technology Co., Ltd. Hydrofluoric acid (HF) and anhydrous calcium chloride (CaCl2) were purchased from Fuchen (Tianjin) Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd. NBR latex (60%) was provided by NANTEX Industry Co., Ltd. Rubber additives were commercial products. Other reagents were chemically pure and used as received.

Preparation and Surface Treatment of MXene

The MAX phase powder (Ti3AlC2) was etched by an HF acid solution (HF, 40%). First, 10 mL of 40% HF acid solution was added to a PTFE container, followed by adding 1 g of Ti3AlC2 powder slowly. The mixture was stirred at 40 °C with a magnetic stirrer for 48 h. After the reaction was fully completed, an appropriate amount of deionized water was slowly added to the mixture to obtain a diluted suspension. The suspension liquid was put in a centrifuge tube at 4000 rpm for 15 min. After centrifugation, the liquid was rinsed with deionized water repeatedly to remove excess HF acid until the pH value of the washing liquid was 7. Finally, MXene was obtained by drying it in a vacuum drying oven at 80 °C for 7 h.

MXene was organically modified by in situ polymerization technology: polypyrrole (PPy) was dissolved in isopropyl alcohol, then added to the aqueous solution of MXene to form a uniform PPy/MXene suspension, and freeze-dried by a freeze-drying machine to obtain a modified organic PPy/MXene. Moreover, MXene modified by PPy has excellent dispersibility and stability.

Preparation of MXene/NBR Nanocomposites

We adopted the emulsion method to prepare MXene/NBR, and the scheme is shown in Figure . Two hundred forty milliliters of butadiene latex (solid content 41.5%) was put into a beater, followed by adding the dispersion of MXene into the latex. After adding a certain proportion of deionized water into the latex, the hybrid emulsion was mechanically stirred for 1 h. The MXene/NBR mixture was obtained by flocculating nitrile latex with 15% anhydrous calcium chloride solution during stirring with a glass rod. The flocculating rubber master batch was rinsed repeatedly with deionized water to remove the residual calcium chloride cation and then dried for 24 h in an electric drying oven at 60 °C.

The flocculation mixture was put into a laboratory two-roll mill for plasticizing, and the roller temperature was 60 °C. After mastication, zinc oxide, stearic acid, accelerator M, accelerator DM, and sulfur were added successively. The formula is shown in Table . And then, the scorch time (ts1), cure time (tc90), and torque differences were evaluated by using an Oscillating disk rheometer (ODR GT 70–70-S2; GOTECH Testing Machine, Inc., Taiwan) whose procedure followed the ASTM D2048–11 standard testing at a temperature of 170 °C. The determined cure time was used for vulcanizing the NBR and MXene/NBR blends in a hydraulic press for every individual compound.

Characterization Techniques

Mechanical Properties

The tensile strength and elongation at break for the NBR and MXene/NBR nanocomposites were studied by using a Universal Testing Machine (GOTECH AI-7000M; GOTECH Testing Machine, Inc., Taiwan), according to ASTM D412–06 standard testing. The tensile testing speed used for all specimens was 500 mm/min. The hardness values for all of the NBR and MXene/NBR nanocomposites were tested according to ASTM D2240–05 using a hardness durometer (Shore A) model GS-719G (GOTECH Testing Machine, Inc., Taiwan). The conductivity of MXene/NBR nanocomposites was measured by a four-probe method (RTS-8 four-probe instrument, Guangzhou 4Probes Tech Ltd.). The thermal conductivity of MXene/NBR nanocomposites was tested with a thermal constant analyzer (TC 3100, Xi ’an Xiaxi Electronic Technology Co., Ltd.) at 25 °C, with a sample size of 30 mm in diameter and 2 mm in thickness. The volume resistivity of Mxene/NBR nanocomposites was measured with a digital high-resistance meter (PC68, Shanghai No. 6 Electric Meter Factory), with a sample diameter of 60 mm and a thickness of 2 mm. The abrasion volume of MXene/NBR nanocomposites was tested by an Akron abrasion tester (GOTECH Testing Machine, Inc., Taiwan), respectively. Meanwhile, the friction coefficient was measured by using a multifunctional friction coefficient tester (GOTECH Testing Machine, Inc., Taiwan).

Structural Characterization

Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR) was carried out on a Tensor 27 spectrometer (Bruker) from 4000 to 400 cm–1. X-ray diffraction (XRD) spectra were recorded on a Smartlab (9 kW, Rigaku) diffractometer with Cu–Kα radiation over the 2θ range of 5–90° at room temperature. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out on a TG 209 F3 thermogravimeter (Netzsch). The micromorphology of Ti2AlC3, MXene, and MXene/NBR nanocomposites was observed on a JSM-7200F scanning electron microscope (SEM, JEOL) at an acceleration voltage of 15.0 kV. The ultrathin transmission electron microscopy (TEM) sample was cut using a cryogenic ultramicrotome, Leica ultracut EM UC6 (Germany), at −60 °C, and the microstructure of MXene and MXene/NBR nanocomposites was observed by JEM-F200 TEM (JEOL). The XPS measurement was carried out on ESCALAB 250 Xi (Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc., Waltham, MA) with the X-ray source of Al Kα (1486.6 eV). During the XPS measurements, a flood gun was applied to compensate for the surface charge; further, the binding energy of the C–Ti bond was corrected to 282.0 eV. The angle step used in angle-resolved XPS measurement was 5°. The accuracy of the angle-resolved XPS was about ±0.1°. The dynamic mechanical properties of MXene/NBR nanocomposites were analyzed on a DMA-Q800 dynamic thermomechanical analyzer (DMA, TA), scanning from −40 to 60 °C at 3 °C/min under a nitrogen atmosphere. Thermal transitions were analyzed by Q20 differential scanning calorimetry (DSC, TA) under N2 purge (50 mL/min) at a heating rate of 10 °C/min across the temperature range of −50 to 120 °C.

Results and Discussion

Structural Analysis of MXene

As shown in Figure , MXene had a wide and strong O–H absorption band at 3233 cm–1. It indicates that Al in the Ti3C2 structure has been etched by HF acid, and it is replaced by −OH. There was an absorption peak at 1716 cm–1, which was the vibration absorption peak of the carbonyl group (CO) generated by oxidation during etching. There was one peak at 709 cm–1, which may be the characteristic absorption peak of Al2O3 generated by Al oxidation.

Meanwhile, the infrared spectra of polypyrrole modified MXene (PPy-MXene) show that the characteristic peaks at 1444 cm–1 were attributed to the CC stretching vibration in the pyrrole ring, and the characteristic peaks of polypyrrole also appeared in other wavenumber segments. In comparison to MXene, PPy-MXene displayed a characteristic PPy absorption band at 3435 cm–1 with a red-shift in wavenumber. This red-shift phenomenon implies a possible hydrogen-bond-mediated interfacial coupling between PPy and MXene. The infrared spectroscopy of PPy-MXene clearly reveals that PPy nanoparticles were loaded onto the surface of MXene nanosheets. It was worth noting that when PPy was combined with MXene, the oxygen-containing groups in PPy-MXene, such as O–H and CO, their characteristic peak intensity was significantly weakened, which indicates that pyrrole monomer can reduce the oxygen-containing groups by in situ polymerization on the surface of MXene nanosheets.

According to Figure , the (002) and (004) characteristic peaks of the MAX phase powder (Ti3AlC2) are located at 9.5, 18.9, 38.9, 41.7, and 56.6°. In the MAX phase, Ti3AlC2 has the characteristic 2θ peak of the Al atom at 38.9°. After the etching reaction, the corresponding characteristic peak is significantly weakened, and some amorphous wide peaks appear, indicating that after HF etching, Al in the MAX phase powder is successfully etched away, while the peaks at 9.5 and 18.9° were wider and the peak strength was weakened. Also, the deviation to the left indicates that the MXene material is prepared. The position of the characteristic peak corresponding to MXene (Ti3C2) obtained by etching changes to a low angle, which was located at 8.8 and 18.2°, respectively. All of these changes indicate that the MXene (Ti3C2) prepared has good quality. And the XRD pattern of PPy-MXene showed that the characteristic peaks of MXene were shifted significantly, indicating that after PPy modification, the interlayer spacing of MXene was increased, and the PPy-MXene complex was obtained.

Figure shows the micromorphology of MAX phase powder (Ti3AlC2) and MXene (Ti3C2). As could be seen from Figure a,c, the particle size of the MAX phase powder (Ti3AlC2) is 2–6 μm, and its blocks have a layered textured structure, with dense morphology between layers. It could be seen from Figure b that after etching, the “accordion” morphology of MXene appeared layer by layer, but the particle size did not change significantly. Figure d is a larger version of MXene, which shows that the spacing between the layers was different. From the comparison between parts b and d of Figure , it is visually illustrated that the etching effect is obvious. The TEM image in Figure e reveals a homogeneous dispersion of few-layer MXene nanosheets (average lateral size: 1.5 ± 0.5 μm) within the aqueous phase, with minimal aggregation (<2% area coverage), achieving agglomeration-free dispersion of MXene within the NBR latex.

As shown in Figure a, the XPS spectra indicated that MXene and PPy-MXene are composed of Ti, O, and C elements. In the high-resolution C 1s spectra (Figure b), the MXene sample exhibits two distinct peaks at 284.8 and 281.8 eV, corresponding to C–C and C–Ti bonding configurations. Following PPy modification, no significant alterations were observed in either the integrated intensity or the binding energy shift of the C–C component. Conversely, the C–Ti peak area demonstrates a pronounced reduction, suggesting that the partial dissociation of C–Ti bonds occurred during the functionalization process. This structural evolution implies that while the sp2-hybridized carbon framework remains largely intact, the interface between MXene layers and modifying polymer undergoes controlled degradation at titanium–carbon sites. The O 1s spectra (Figure c) of MXene exhibit deconvolution into two components at 529.6 eV (Ti–O bonding) and 531.8 eV (C–Ti–OH x species). Upon PPy functionalization, a notable reduction in the C–Ti–OH x peak intensity occurs concurrently with observable broadening of the Ti–O spectral feature, indicating surface chemical environment modifications that alter oxygen-containing functional group distributions. Figure d illustrates the modified Ti 2p spectra exhibiting four characteristic peaks at 454.9, 459.6, 461.2, and 464.9 eV, assigned to Ti 2p3/2 (Ti+) and Ti 2p1/2 (Ti4+) spin–orbit components. This spectral configuration reveals partial oxidation of titanium species during PPy functionalization, as evidenced by the emergence of higher oxidation state features (Ti4+) alongside residual lower valence states (Ti+). The observed peak intensity ratios and binding energy shifts indicate controlled interfacial redox reactions that modulate the electronic structure of MXene through selective valence state transformation. , Combined with the results of FT-IR (Figure ), it was further confirmed that PPy was bonded to MXene.

Mechanical Properties of MXene/NBR Nanocomposites

The mechanical properties of the samples filled with MXene were evaluated by tensile testing (Figure ). The addition of the MXene as a sole reinforcement as well as the addition of it causes an increase in the tensile strength and wear-resisting property of the NBR nanocomposites. With the increase in the amount of MXene added, the tensile strength of the composite first increased and then decreased slowly. When the additive amount was 0.5, the tensile strength of the composite reached a maximum, increasing by 97.08%. The enhanced tensile properties of the nanocomposites demonstrate effective stress transfer across the interface between the NBR matrix and MXene nanosheets. During deformation, MXene interlayers sequentially absorb impact energy through controlled delamination, creating tortuous crack paths that require higher fracture energies to propagate. The breakdown of crack propagation inevitably reduces the local applied stress and improves the fracture resistance. In addition, the presence of PPy facilitates the formation of functional groups on both edge sites and basal planes of MXene nanosheets, thereby enhancing interfacial interactions with the NBR matrix. However, as the amount of MXene filling continued to increase, the tensile strength decreased slightly. At high filling, the distance between MXene nanosheets decreases, resulting in local and extensive fracture surfaces, leading to tensile strength shrinkage of the MXene/NBR nanocomposites.

However, this improvement does not imply a deterioration of the elastic properties of the material: all composites exhibit a higher elongation at the break in relation to pristine rubber. As can be seen from Table , with the increase of MXene dosage, its tearing strength, 100 and 300% elongation at break, hardness, and other mechanical properties of the composite materials continue to improve. Progressive incorporation of MXene induces dilution of the NBR cross-link network packing density. This structural reorganization enables greater network extensibility, ultimately enhancing the nanocomposites’ ultimate tensile strain while improving their ductility. Furthermore, Table demonstrates 31.25% (0.5 phr loading) higher tensile strength in PPy-MXene nanocomposites compared to pristine polymers, confirming their enhanced mechanical performance. It indicates that the interfacial bonding improvement induced by PPy-MXene leads to enhanced mechanical performance of nanocomposites.

Figure shows that the Akron abrasion volume of pure NBR is 0.2147 cm3 and those of MXene/NBR nanocomposites containing 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, and 1.0 phr MXene were 0.1996, 0.1761, 0.1855, and 0.1993 cm3, respectively. When the MXene content is 0.5 phr, the nanocomposite exhibits the lowest Akron abrasion wear loss. Figure also shows that the friction coefficient (COF) of pure NBR was 4.692 and that of MXene/NBR composite materials containing 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, and 1.0 phr MXene were 3.396, 3.172, 1.946, and 1.584, respectively. Compared with that of pure NBR, the COF of MXene/NBR nanocomposites decreased obviously. Experimental data reveal that MXene introduction notably reduces friction coefficients in NBR composites, mainly resulting from intensified molecular-level interactions at the MXene/NBR interface.

Thermal Conductivity of MXene/NBR Nanocomposites

Figure shows the relationship between thermal conductivity (k) and MXene content. The thermal conductivity of pure NBR was 0.157 W/(m·K). It was a poor conductor of heat, and the heat gathered during the use of the material could not be dispersed in time, causing the product to heat and thus affecting the service life. When MXene nanosheets were filled, the thermal conductivity of the composite increased and then decreased. After 0.75 phr of MXene was added, the thermal conductivity of the composite was 0.325 W/(m·K), which was a thermal conductivity material, and the thermal conductivity of the composite was increased by 107% compared with that of pure NBR. Heat was released along the homogeneous isolated network structure formed by self-assembly of MXene nanosheets bonded together, and the two-phase interface interaction caused by physical cross-linking of MXene nanosheets with proteins around the NBR molecular chain further improves the thermal conductivity of thermal interface materials. , However, when the addition of MXene continued to increase, the thermal conductivity of the composite decreased slightly, which may be caused by the uneven dispersion of MXene in the composite

The Electrical Conductivity of MXene/NBR Nanocomposites

As presented in Figure , the electrical properties of MXene/NBR nanocomposites exhibited concentration-dependent behavior. Pristine NBR demonstrates a volume resistivity of 0.167 × 108 Ω·cm, while the incorporation of MXene nanosheets progressively reduces this value with increasing filler loading, consistent with MXene’s inherent conductivity. A marginal resistivity decline, potentially linked to hydroxyl groups on MXene surfaces enhancing interfacial charge transfer, did not alter the composite’s fundamental insulating behavior; all formulations retain resistivity magnitudes exceeding 109 Ω·cm (the critical threshold for insulation materials). This optimized balance of controlled conductivity and retained dielectric integrity positions MXene/NBR nanocomposites as viable candidates for precision electronic packaging applications.

Thermal Stability of MXene/NBR Nanocomposites

Figure comparatively presents the TG, DTG, and DSC curves for pristine NBR vulcanizate and MXene/NBR nanocomposites under an argon atmosphere. It could be seen from Figure a that with the increase of the amount of added MXene, the thermal stability of NBR was slightly improved. There was only one mass loss stage in the temperature range of 300–500 °C, and the mass loss rate was 79.6–88.07%. However, it is worth noting that both the maximum thermal loss temperature and the residual mass of the MXene/NBR nanocomposite were higher than those of the pure NBR vulcanizate. The peak thermal decomposition temperature (T max) of NBR vulcanizate demonstrated a marginal elevation from 362.9 to 366.5 °C in TGA (Figure b), slightly improving thermal stability. It was concluded that MXene nanofillers predominantly mediate carbonaceous matrix restructuring pathways with minimal interference in macromolecular chain scission dynamics during thermal decomposition processes. Moreover, MXene acted as a barrier in the NBR matrix to block volatile thermal degradation products. The presence of MXene can effectively limit the movement of NBR molecular chains, thus requiring higher energy for the thermal degradation of NBR. This was because of the high molecular chain saturation and good thermal stability of NBR matrix materials; therefore, the introduction of MXene did not significantly improve the thermal stability of composite materials.

Figure c reveals a singular glass transition temperature (T g) for all MXene/NBR nanocomposites, confirming homogeneous phase structure development through effective filler–matrix interfacial compatibility. While pristine NBR exhibited a T g at −10.3 °C, MXene incorporation induces a controlled elevation of this transition temperature (ΔT g = +1.1 °C at 0.75 phr loading). This thermal behavior originates from hydrogen-bond-mediated interfacial coupling between MXene hydroxyl groups and NBR cyano moieties.

Dynamic Mechanical Thermal Analysis of MXene/NBR Nanocomposites

The temperature-dependent variations in both storage modulus (E′) and loss factor (tan δ) of NBR nanocomposites were systematically characterized in Figure . As shown in Figure a, the initial E′ value exhibits a concentration-dependent enhancement phase, reaching maximum reinforcement at 0.5 phr MXene, followed by a progressive reduction beyond 0.75 phr nanofiller content. This critical threshold (0.75 phr) signifies the onset of filler network destabilization, overriding interfacial reinforcement effects. Figure b shows the damping parameters of MXene/NBR nanocomposites. Upon the incorporation of MXene into the NBR matrix, both the tan δ peak value (denoted as tan δmax) and the loss peak area exhibit progressive increases. These observations demonstrated that the addition of MXene enhances the damping performance of NBR nanocomposites, which originates from robust interfacial interactions between MXene and the NBR matrix. Specifically, the improved damping properties were attributed to the formation of additional hydrogen bonds at the MXene/NBR interface, thereby optimizing energy dissipation capabilities in the composite system.

Further analysis shows (as shown in Table ), the tan δ of the composite material at 0 °C presents a slight increase, while the tan δ at 60 °C fluctuates with little change as the amount of added MXene increases, indicating that the wet slip resistance of the composite material is improved, while the rolling resistance does not change significantly. The analysis shows that part of the rubber molecular chains in the composite material interpenetrate into the hollow structure of MXene during the process of mixing and vulcanization, forming a physical interlock structure, which restricts the movement of rubber molecular chains and reduces the internal friction between MXene, so the tan δ does not increase significantly.

Microstructure Analysis of MXene/NBR Nanocomposites

As illustrated in Figure , when the content of MXene was 0.5 phr, the characteristic peak of the nanocomposite was at 6.5°. With the increase of the amount of MXene added, the characteristic peak position of the MXene/NBR nanocomposites did not change, and the peak intensity increased, indicating that the addition of MXene had no effect on the matrix structure of NBR.

However, the uniform dispersion of nanomaterials within the matrix remains a critical factor governing the performance of nanocomposites. In order to evaluate the dispersion of MXene in NBR vulcanizate as well as the interfacial interactions, SEM and TEM were utilized to observe the morphology of MXene/NBR nanocomposites. As shown in Figure a–e, the dispersion state of MXene nanosheets in the nanocomposites was examined by SEM. In a certain range of MXene addition, it was well distributed in the NBR matrix, and the fracture surface of nanomaterials was smooth, but at a high filling amount of 1 phr, MXene was slightly agglomerated, which may lead to a decrease in the performance of the composite, which was consistent with the test results of the relevant properties of the composite.

As demonstrated in Figure f, the TEM micrographs of MXene/NBR nanocomposites with 0.5 phr MXene loading exhibit single or several layers of stacked MXene uniformly dispersed in the NBR matrix and a separated network morphology, in which submicron NBR latex particles were covered by MXene nanosheets through a self-assembly process.

Conclusions

The principal conclusions can be summarized as follows: This study has successfully developed homogeneous phase-structured MXene/NBR nanocomposites via the interfacial bridging effect of polypyrrole (PPy) serving as a barrier between MXene fillers and the NBR matrix. XRD, SEM, TEM, and XPS characterizations confirmed the successful synthesis of MXene. TGA results suggested that the nanocomposites had good thermal performance (T max ≈ 366.5 °C). The thermal conductivity and electrical conductivity of the nanocomposites were investigated, and it was found that the nanocomposites exhibited optimal thermal and electrical performance when the MXene content reached 0.75 phr. Moreover, because of the reinforcing effect of MXene nanosheets, the mechanical properties of the nanocomposites were enhanced, including increased tensile strength (up to 14.7 MPa) and elongation at break (up to 360%). Meanwhile, the wear resistance of the nanocomposites has also been improved, with the lowest Akron abrasion loss reaching 0.1855 cm3 and the friction coefficient reduced to below 1.6. With improved thermal, electrical, mechanical, and wear resistance properties of the nanocomposites, this work offers a new approach for the preparation of polymer-based dielectric nanocomposites with practical application values.
