Signal Peptide of Human Serum Albumin (residues 1–18) Forms Amyloid-like Aggregates

Abstract

Signal peptides (SPs) are essential tools for sorting or translocating synthesized proteins. However, except for a few examples, their studies in isolation are quite limited. Here, we asked a question about the aggregation propensity of signal peptides (residues 1–18) of Human Serum Albumin (HSA). Molecular dynamic simulations were used to observe the mechanism through which signal peptides of HSA self-aggregated under physiological conditions. Further, we confirmed our results by combining dye-based assays, atomic force microscopy, and transmission electron microscopy techniques. It was noted that the signal peptide of HSA (regions 1–18) forms typical amyloid-like fibrils.

Introduction

Human serum albumin (HSA), the most abundant protein in the body, performs essential biological functions and binds to various ligands, including amino acids, fatty acids, ions, and hormones. − The half-life of HSA is approximately 20 days, and they are present in lymph, saliva, cerebrospinal fluid, intestinal fluid, and plasma. , Human serum albumin has worked as a molecular chaperone whose major function is restricting the aggregation of other proteins. They also protect enzymes from thermal degradation and aggregation. The signal peptides (SPs) are short peptides located at the N-terminus of proteins, involved in protein transport and release. They are present in all eukaryotes and prokaryotes and have diverse applications in various industrial and scientific fields. They are first identified by the signal recognition particle (SRP), a ribonucleoprotein complex, and subsequently enter between the twin transmembrane domain of Sec 61a. In eukaryotes, the process starts when the SRP allows the N-terminal domain of a secretory protein. This episode permits the co-translational shift of the protein into the lumen of the endoplasmic reticulum. Post transfer of the SPs into the lipid membrane, they are dissected by the membrane-bound SPs and subsequently broken down by intermembrane proteases.

The HSA signal peptide (residues 1–18) plays a crucial role in the folding and activity of recombinant proteins folded by 17 disulfide bonds. Signal peptides are important molecular entities with a canonical function in the targeted secretion translocation of proteins. They are also significantly involved in the production of recombinant proteins. Most of the aggregation-associated studies have demonstrated the aggregation propensity of HSA protein in the presence of metal ions or alone. , However, the misfolding and self-aggregating-like activity of signal peptides of HSA in isolation still pose unanswered questions. Understanding the aggregation potential of signal peptides of HSA may provide valuable insights into the pathophysiology of related diseases. The formation of amyloid, a multistep process that can lead to the production of highly toxic intermediates in biological systems, is associated with various diseases such as Alzheimer’s, systemic amyloidoses, synucleinopathies, and type 2 diabetes mellitus. − Analyzing the aggregation propensity of the signal peptide of HSA can contribute to a better understanding of disease pathophysiology.

The aggregation of the signal peptide of HSA protein after its delivery to the intended destination raises questions about the consequences if the protein quality control system fails to degrade this cleaved-off signal peptide. We aimed to investigate the fate of the signal peptide if it is not degraded by the protein homeostasis machinery or the protein quality control system, and we hypothesized that it may aggregate. Due to its solubility only in DMSO, studying the monomer was challenging. To test this hypothesis, we used in-silico tools, MD simulations, dye-based spectroscopy, and microscopy to predict and confirm the self-aggregation propensity of the HSA signal peptide (regions 1–18) under physiological conditions.

2. Materials and Methods

2.1. Reagents

Signal peptide (residues 1–18) “MKWVTFISLLFLFSSAYS” of HSA protein was obtained from NCBI, chemically synthesized, and purchased from Genscript, USA, with 91.5% purity. A TEM grid was acquired from Ted Pella Inc., USA. Thioflavin T (ThT), Congo red (CR), 8-anilinonaphthalene-1-sulfonic acid (ANS), and 1,1,1,3,3,3-hexafluoro-2-propanol (HFIP) were acquired from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA). Other chemicals used in this study were of the highest purity, acquired from regional sources.

2.2. In Silico Prediction of Fibril Formation

The aggregation score (aggregation propensity region) of the signal peptide of HSA protein was predicted using different freely accessible data servers such as FoldAmyloid, Metamyl, TANGO, AGGRESCAN, and CamSol. − In short, parameters such as physiological setup (temp 37 °C and pH 7.4) were used to run the TANGO software. However, other databases, namely, CamSol, Aggrescan Foldamyloid, and NHSA, were used in default settings. We used Peptide 2.0 (www.peptide2.com) to predict hydrophobicity, and the physiological parameters of the signal peptide were calculated with the help of the PROTPARAM database (www.expasy.org/protparam).

2.3. MD Simulation of HSA Signal Peptide (Residues 1–18)

The simulations utilized the GROMACS software package (version 2022.3) along with the GROMOS96 54a7 force field. The SPC (simple point charge) water model was used, and periodic boundary conditions (PBC) were implemented using a cubic simulation box. To ensure a minimum initial distance of 1 nm between proteins and the box boundaries, the system was set up accordingly. Nonbonded interactions were truncated at a 1.2 nm cutoff, and long-range electrostatics were computed using the Particle-Mesh Ewald (PME) summation method. The temperature was maintained at 300 K using a modified Berendsen thermostat (V-rescale), while the pressure was regulated at 1 bar with the Parrinello–Rahman barostat. Counterions were added to neutralize the system, and additional salt was included to achieve the desired concentration. Before the main simulations, energy minimization employing the steepest descent algorithm was conducted, followed by equilibration stages consisting of a 10 ns NVT simulation and a subsequent 10 ns NPT simulation. During equilibration, the protein backbone was restrained to maintain stability. These preliminary simulations were performed to generate the initial configurations necessary for aggregation simulations. The starting conformation for the aggregation simulations was obtained from the 2-μs monomer simulations of HSA_SP (1–18). In an aggregation simulation, six monomers were randomly placed in a periodic cubic box with an edge length of 10 nm, corresponding to a concentration of 0.15 M. The systems were solvated, and appropriate ions were added to neutralize the system and achieve the desired salt concentration. Before the production run, the aggregation simulations were further equilibrated using a 30 ns NVT simulation, followed by a 30 ns NPT simulation. The aggregation simulation was run for 2000 ns after the equilibration. ,

2.4. Thioflavin T Assay

In vitro, aggregation was estimated using fluorescent-dye-based assays such as ANS and ThT. A 200 μM ThT stock solution was prepared in distilled water, while a final concentration of 20 μM was used for a dye-based binding assay. Data were acquired using a black 96-well flat-bottomed plate through a microplate reader (TECAN Infinite M200 PRO). The excitation wavelength of 450 nm and emission spectra from 470 to 700 nm were used. Experiments were performed at least in triplicate.

2.5. ANS-Dye-Based Fluorescence Assay

ANS-dye-based binding analysis of the signal peptide of HSA protein was conducted using a 20 μM concentration. A final concentration of 30 μM for both the monomer and aggregated signal peptide of HSA protein was used in sodium phosphate buffer (20 mM; pH 7.4). Data were acquired in a TECAN Infinite M200 PRO microplate reader. The excitation wavelength of 380 nm and emission spectra ranging from 400 to 650 nm were used. The control used in the experiment was ANS dye with only with buffer. Experiments were performed at least in triplicate.

2.6. Congo Red Shift Assay

Congo red (CR) dye is amyloid specific and binds with cross-β-sheet-rich amyloid-like aggregates and shows a characteristic red shift in spectra. Mili-Q water was used to prepare a stock solution (0.1% w/v) of CR. Then, CR solution with a concentration of 25 mM was added to the aggregated sample of the signal peptide of the HSA protein (residues 1–18). A Tecan Infinite M200 microplate reader was used to record the absorption spectra at a 400–450 nm wavelength using a transparent 96-well plate. Experiments were performed at least in triplicate.

2.7. High-Resolution Transmission Electron Microscopy (HRTEM)

For TEM analysis, 7 μL of the aggregate sample (diluted 10× in Milli-Q) was placed on 200 mesh grids of copper and incubated at RT for 60 s. The grid was rinsed softly, swirling in water droplets for at least 30 s three times. Afterward, an aqueous solution of ammonium molybdate (3% w/v) was used to stain the grids negatively. In the end, the grids were allowed to dry overnight, and then a snapshot was captured in HRTEM (FP 5022/22-Tecnai G2 20 S-TWIN, FEI) at a 200 kV accelerating voltage.

2.8. Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM)

The aggregate sample was first diluted 30 times with Milli-Q and then drop cast using 7 μL on a newly dissected mica sheet. After 2 min of incubation, the specimen was rinsed with deionized water and dried overnight in a desiccator. In the end, a snapshot was captured on AFM in tapping mode (Dimension Icon from Bruker).

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Prediction of Aggregation Propensity of the Signal Peptide (Regions 1–18) of HSA Protein

The aggregation tendency of every protein is modeled by evolution according to its actual presence in the organisms. HSA is an abundant protein in the human body and displays excellent ligand-binding potential and is also a carrier of various exogenous and endogenous proteins. Protein aggregation is affected by several factors, such as the protein structure (primary, secondary, or tertiary), processing, and the environment of the protein in which the protein exists. Aggregation or misfolding may reduce or entirely alter the biological characteristics. Therefore, protein aggregation remains a hurdle in the commercialization and development of biotechnology products. Various in silico-based platforms that predict APR from the polypeptide sequence of signal peptides of HSA based on biochemical and physicochemical characteristics have been available. In the present study, we have used five freely available web-based high-precision server tools for APR prediction in the signal peptide of HSA (TANGO, AGGRESCAN, FoldAmyloid, CamSol, and AGGRESCAN). Our in-silico server-based results revealed greater prediction scores for aggregation propensity of signal peptide regions 1–18, especially the residue between the second through 15th (Figure A–E). Human serum albumin involves transporting various exogenous and endogenous chemicals such as metals, drugs, amino acids, bile acids, toxic metabolites, hormones, and fatty acids. , Signal peptides are key tools that control the function and folding of recombinant proteins, which are folded by disulfide bonds. HSA is a multifunctional protein with wide application in clinical settings.

The physicochemical properties of our signal peptide sequence were investigated utilizing Protparam and www.peptide2.com for the hydrophobicity index. We observed mostly hydrophobic amino acids, with an overall 11 of them allocated across the whole signal peptide of the HSA sequence (hydrophobicity index: 61.11%; as shown in Figure ).

3.2. Prediction of the Aggregation of the Signal Peptide of HSA at the Atomistic Scale

Protein aggregation of signal peptides of HSA is a discussion of great concern to researchers due to its involvement in the protein homeostasis system and protein translocation. Numerous in-silico tools are available to predict aggregation propensity from the analysis of amino-acid residues in a polypeptide chain. Similarly, an investigation of the structural rearrangements during the aggregation and disaggregation process through numerous MD simulation techniques has been reported earlier. In this study, we therefore performed an MD simulation of the HSA signal peptide (regions 1–18) to elucidate its aggregation behavior at the atomic level in the presence of multiple monomeric subunits. The GROMOS96 54a7 force field was applied to record the aggregation of signal peptides of HSA under physiological temperature. MD simulation was run for 2000 ns, and a snapshot of signal peptides is illustrated in Figure . After the run of 50 ns, the signal peptides converted into dimers and then converted to hexamers at 100 ns and in the stable confirmation to 2000 ns. So far, any study related to MD simulation that showed the propensity of self-aggregation behavior of signal peptides of HSA (regions 1–18) under physiological conditions has not been reported to date.

3.3. Oligomer Formation of the HSA (Residues 1–18) Peptides

To study the aggregation propensity of HSA (regions 1–18), five independent exploit-solvent atomistic MD simulations were studied for HSA (regions 1–18). Each replicate was established from multiple monomers in unsystematic spots that were recognized to aggregate in the absence of constraints. The average highest oligomerization state such as monomer, dimer, etc as a function of simulated time is illustrated in Figure A. The oligomerization state of HSA (regions 1–18) increased on average by 2 and 3 during the 100 ns. HSA (regions 1–18) peptide simulations reached the highest hexamer state at approximately 100 ns, and it showed a uniquely stable state under hexamer conditions. HSA (regions 1–18) has a limited amount of β-sheet. Because β-sheets are properties of amyloid fibrils, we performed a 2000 ns simulation to acquire entirely formed β-sheet aggregates. At the end of the simulation, a limited number of β-sheets is noticed. However, the highest α-helix content was observed in HSA (regions 1–18). This is an interesting finding because HSA (regions 1–18) aggregates into fibrils, and they have the highest number of α-helices compared to normal amyloids. To validate if this alteration in monomer confirmation had any impact on aggregation behavior, we performed the aggregation simulation, beginning from random monomeric confirmation of the HSA peptide from the last few seconds of the 2 μs. The aggregation is faster, as can be noticed in Figure . The contact plot for the oligomers is depicted in Figure B. This verified the aggregation kinetics based on the monomer confirmation. Specifically, it seems that the termini are key for connections between different monomers, and when they interact intermolecularly they are not accessible for the intermolecular connections that lead to aggregation.

3.4. Binding of Signal Peptide Sequence of HSA (Residues 1–18) to Amyloid-Specific Dyes and Enhanced Fluorescence

Fluorescence is one of the most useful techniques for observing and structurally depicting amyloid fibrils or protein aggregation in pathogenic or clinical settings. , The most common methods for quantifying and spotting amyloid-like fibrils depend on observing the fluorescence alterations in thioflavin T and 8-anilinonaphthalene-1-sulfonic acid (ANS) dye and alteration in absorbance of Congo red (CR), an azo dye. The ANS dye binds to the hydrophobic regions, enhancing fluorescence levels with a typical shift in emission spectra such as a blue shift. First, we analyzed the fibril formation of the signal peptide of HSA protein by ANS-dye-based assay (as shown in Figure A). The reading was obtained at the initial (0 h) and final (168 h) parts of the aggregate samples, demonstrating an increased fluorescence intensity of ANS of approximately 1.5-fold. At 168 h, we observed a significant gain in fluorescence accompanied by a characteristic blue shift (549 to 516 nm).

In our study, we found that the signal peptide of the HSA protein shows a significant change in fluorescence signal when compared to the monomer and ThT-only control. The aggregates of the signal peptide of HSA demonstrated an ∼8-fold increment in ThT fluorescence intensity. It indicates the generation of amyloid-like fibrils in the aggregate-inducing environment under physiological settings, as shown in Figure B. We also noticed that CR binds to signal peptides of HSA proteins and demonstrates a significant shift in spectra from 488 to 491 nm (Figure C). The signal peptide of HSA (residues 1–18) was also positive for the ANS assay, where we observed an increase in fluorescence spectra accompanied by characteristics of a blue-shift in spectra from 541 nm to 498 nm.

3.5. Signal Peptides of HSA (Residues 1–18) Display Fibril-Like Structure in Morphology Assessment of AFM and TEM

Atomic force microscopy and high-resolution TEM are versatile techniques for analyzing protein aggregation in in vitro environments. , In our study, we analyzed and quantitatively computed the heights of amyloid fibrils of our signal peptide samples (as shown in Figure A). The height of the fibrils of the signal peptide was approximately 20 nm (Figure AIII). Next, we confirmed the amyloid fibril formation of our aggregated sample by using transmission electron microscopy. A snapshot of the TEM image clearly showed a distinctive amyloid-like fibrillar structure with protruding short and long densely overlapped fibrils (as shown in Figure B).

4. Conclusions

From our initial prediction, we found the first 18 residues of HSA to have a high aggregation propensity. We utilized the MD simulation to understand the self-aggregation nature of signal peptides of HSA (residues 1–18). Our MD simulation study demonstrated the aggregation and oligomerization nature of signal peptides of HSA. Further, our morphological analysis verified the formation of typical amyloid-like fibrils. Shortly, it will help to understand the mechanisms of the entire HSA protein and its involvement in the transportation of various endogenous and exogenous substances. Our findings also indicate a possible role in the imbalance of HSA signal peptides in amyloidogenic diseases. In the future, more preclinical studies are warranted to validate the role of the self-aggregation potential of HSA peptides (regions 1–18).
