Abstract

The blood-brain barrier (BBB) greatly hinders the delivery of therapeutic agents to the brain. Vast efforts have been dedicated to exploring novel strategies and developing technologies to enhance the delivery of drugs, nucleic acids, antibodies, etc., effectively in the context of brain tumors, neurodegenerative diseases, and other brain pathologies. These strategies aim to guarantee impactful and targeted accumulation of the delivered agents within the brain or within specific areas or niches through either localized sustained delivery (convection-enhanced delivery and implantable biodegradable polymer-based systems), encapsulation of the agents within vectors that can enhance their ability to penetrate the BBB and achieve proper interstitial distribution (viral vectors, exosomes, and nanoparticles), or modulation of the BBB to facilitate the crossing of the delivered cargo (focused ultrasound and receptor/transporter-mediated delivery). Indeed, exploring these innovative strategies and how they may work synergistically with conventional treatments is crucial to advancing the management of brain pathologies.

1. Introduction

The blood-brain barrier (BBB) selectively regulates the transport of circulating substances into the brain. While this structure is crucial for maintaining brain homeostasis and protecting the brain against pathogens and toxic metabolites, simultaneously, it limits the optimal distribution of >98% of potential therapeutic agents to the brain. In particular, only highly lipophilic molecules with few hydrogen bonds and a molecular weight of less than 400 Da have the ability to cross the BBB. Hence, all large drugs and a great proportion of small-molecule drugs lack the ability to penetrate the BBB and act in the brain.

Structurally, the BBB is composed of a neurovascular unit (NVU) formed by tightly sealed endothelial cells joined together with tight junctions and surrounding supporting cells. The barrier blocks paracellular transport and limits transcytosis. Functionally, the BBB utilizes selective mechanisms, such as receptor-mediated and carrier-mediated transport, to regulate the movement of molecules entering and exiting the brain parenchyma 1,2.

To overcome the BBB for the purpose of drug delivery, many novel strategies have been investigated, which can be broadly characterized into categories. Local delivery techniques, including convection-enhanced delivery (CED) and local biodegradable polymer-based drug release, bypass the BBB by delivering therapeutics directly into the targeted site. BBB-penetrant strategies, such as nanoparticles, exosomes, and vectors, cross the BBB by utilizing the brain’s endogenous transport pathways. BBB-disrupting strategies, including focused ultrasound (FUS), transiently increase the barrier’s permeability to enhance drug biodistribution. Intranasal delivery offers a noninvasive and direct route, while receptor-mediated transport leverages existing transport mechanisms. This paper seeks to review evolving strategies for therapeutic purposes.

2. Local Delivery Techniques

2.1. Convection-Enhanced Delivery

2.1.1. Overview

Convection-enhanced delivery (CED) is a direct drug delivery method that can bypass the BBB by allowing the delivery of high volumes of therapeutics while limiting systemic toxicities. CED functions through placing small catheters into the targeted region while an infusion pump generates a pressure gradient using a bulk flow technique, leading to the direct infusion of therapeutics into the tumor and the surrounding areas. Interest in CED has grown since a wide range of compounds can be homogeneously delivered, achieving a greater area of local drug distribution as compared to conventional diffusion-based techniques. Various studies investigated the utility of CED, and the outcomes are dependent on multiple variables including (1) catheter characteristics (e.g., couple-controlled catheter movements with constant pressure infusion provides higher infusion volume versus stationary catheters), (2) infusion frequency (e.g., better outcomes with single versus serial infusions of Serclutamab talirine in glioblastoma), (3) CED flow rate (e.g., Hamilton syringes versus catheters), and (4) tumor heterogeneity (e.g., the heterogeneity in cell density and vascularization can lead to differences in drug distribution, diffusion, and clearance). Therefore, all of these variables need to be considered to achieve an optimal outcome for the particular disease treated. These challenges and obstacles still need to be tackled to optimize the use of CED, such as backflow from the catheters and changes in the tumoral landscape after procedures.

2.1.2. CED in Brain Tumors

Diffuse intrinsic pontine glioma (DIPG) is one of the most aggressive central nervous system (CNS) cancers in the pediatric population, with a median overall survival of lower than 1 year. Souweidane et al. reported the findings from a phase 1 clinical trial which investigated the safety and efficacy of CED of the antibody I-8H9, targeting the glioma-associated B7–H3 antigen, in 28 children with DIPG who have been treated with radiation therapy previously. Apart from one case of transient hemiparesis and skin infection, no other complications occurred and dose-limiting toxicities were not observed. These findings show that CED may be an efficient strategy for drug delivery to brain stem tumors, which should be further investigated in future trials. Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is the most commonly occurring primary malignant brain tumor, which is locally invasive and exhibits high recurrence rates with a poor prognosis despite surgery and chemoradiation. Several trials have been conducted to assess the safety and efficacy of CED of therapeutic agents in recurrent GBM. − In a phase 1 trial by van Putten et al., 19 patients with recurrent GBM underwent CED of the oncolytic Delta24-RGD virus (DNX-2401), inducing a local inflammatory response against the tumor. Eight patients showed survival or radiographic response to the administered treatment; however, nine cases sustained serious adverse events, mainly attributed to increased intracranial pressure. Interestingly, one case showed complete response without any sign of tumor recurrence or requirement for further treatment. In a parallel fashion, topotecan is an effective chemotherapeutic agent against glioma cells, but the BBB has limited its application in the clinical setting. To address this obstacle, Spinazzi et al. conducted a phase 1 trial, in which five patients with recurrent GBM, who had already received surgery and chemoradiation, underwent subcutaneous implantation of a catheter-pump system capable of CED of topotecan into the brain. Their trial has demonstrated that topotecan significantly decreased tumor growth in all patients, though one case developed treatment-related intraoperative supplemental motor area syndrome. Similarly, Bos et al. conducted a phase 1 trial in which 15 patients with recurrent GBM received peritumoral and intertumoral CED of human recombinant bone morphogenic protein 4 (hrBMP4). Areas that received hrBMP4 showed tumor growth inhibition. Additionally, hrBMP4 infusion was safe and well-tolerated, and no dose-limiting toxicities were detected. Despite the limited sample size, the short follow-up period, and the absence of a control group, these findings are promising and merit further investigation (clinical trials investigating CED are summarized in Table ).

2.2. Implantable Polymer-Based Drug Delivery

2.2.1. Overview

Polymer-based local delivery has emerged as a pivotal avenue for bypassing the BBB in the management of GBM and other brain tumors. In fact, chemotherapeutics, particularly carmustine (BCNU), became standard care in the 1990s. , However, their effectiveness is constrained by structural factors. Specifically, BCNU has a low molecular weight that permits only partial BBB crossing. Chemotherapeutic drugs in general are hindered by their short serum half-life and severe systemic toxicities. To maintain a high therapeutic concentration, high level doses of chemotherapeutics are needed; however, this high dosing increases the risk of systemic toxicity and adverse events.

Polymer-based local delivery offers several advantages in comparison to other delivery methods. By enabling direct interstitial delivery of a chemotherapeutic drug or drugs into the resection cavity, polymer-based delivery achieves prolonged drug action with reduced systemic side effects. This controlled and sustained release of the drugs from polymers, specifically polymeric wafers, achieves consistent and effective drug concentrations, thus optimizing treatment outcomes.

2.2.2. Nonbiodegradable and Biodegradable Polymers

The first polymer that was used for locally controlled release of drugs was ethylene vinyl acetate copolymer (EVAc), which utilized first-order diffusion kinetics, releasing over 90% of the encapsulated drug. EVAc’s diffusion percentage was determined by molecular weight, charge, and solubility of the drug loaded. However, a critical limitation of EVAc-based systems is their nonbiodegradability. Therefore, an additional surgery for polymer removal would be a necessity to remove the nondegraded remnants from the brain parenchyma. Despite the significant drawbacks, nonbiodegradable polymers have certain advantages, such as extended stability to provide a sustained release. Polyethylene glycol (PEG) (molecular weight > 20,000) is another example of a nonbiodegradable polymer utilized in clinical trials for its conjugation with nanoparticles to improve drug solubility. While there are benefits observed when it comes to drug stability and release, the long-term implantation of the polymer can provoke chronic inflammation, since the polymer is recognized as a foreign body to the host tissue.

To circumvent the need for additional surgery, biodegradable polymers were developed. Two of the most widely established biodegradable polymers are poly­(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) and polyanhydrides. PLGA is a synthetic carrier that is favored for its biocompatibility and biodegradability. The monomeric components in PLGA, lactic acid and glycolic acid, naturally metabolize in the body, achieving sustained drug delivery with minimal systemic toxicity. , Owing to these properties, PLGA gained vast popularity within the realm of localized biodegradable delivery and remains one of the most versatile polymers used in biomaterial design and development. −

The second commonly used polymer is a polyanhydride matrix poly­[1,3-bis­(carboxyphenoxy)­propane-co-sebacic acid] (PCPP:SA). PCPP polymers degrade through a spontaneous reaction with water to form carboxylic acid and nontoxic byproducts that are naturally eliminated. The hydrophobic anhydride linkage in PCPP creates a stable backbone with biodegradability, a greater half-life for the drugs, controlled delivery, and drug protection. The major advantage of biodegradable polymers lies in their ability to degrade in situ, eliminating the need for a second invasive procedure for removal. Additionally, the polymeric matrix allows for controlled drug release and enhanced drug stability, even after polymer degradation. Further, the degradation rate can be tuned based on the polymer’s monomer component constitution and concentration.

2.2.3. Biodegradable BCNU-Loaded Polymers

The development of PCPP:SA as a local platform for the delivery of chemotherapeutics in treating brain tumors altered the manner in which brain tumor therapy was approached. Gliadel is a PCPP:SA-based wafer loaded with BCNU that has demonstrated significant efficacy in clinical trials. − BCNU is embedded within a hydrophobic copolymer matrix, which facilitates localized drug release in the resection cavity. While studies have shown that the majority of BCNU is liberated from the wafer matrix within the first week of polymer implantation, the effect of local chemotherapy is significant. ,, BCNU wafers utilize surface erosion for degradation, which degrades layer by layer for controlled diffusion. For the treatment of brain tumor patients, local delivery through wafers reduces systemic toxicities, which is notable in comparison to systemic BCNU delivery.

Clinical trials (phases I and II) have confirmed the safety of Gliadel wafers. The initial BCNU loading dose was determined to be 3.85%. These studies demonstrated a 50% increase in the 6-month survival of patients receiving Gliadel compared to those receiving placebo wafers. Subsequent phase III trials further validated these findings. A 29% reduction in mortality risk and a survival advantage at 2 and 3 years was documented, further emphasizing the safety and efficacy of BCNU wafers. , Later, an increase in the BCNU loading dose within the polymer was tested. The standard tolerated dose of BCNU for Gliadel was 3.85%; however, higher doses (6.5, 10,14.5, or 20%) were tested without encountering dose-limiting toxicities. ,

The combination of BCNU-loaded polymers with TMZ, radiation, and other treatment methods may enhance the overall survival. TMZ is an orally administered alkylating agent, typically given daily over several weeks. In this context, Brem et al. have shown that loading TMZ into polyanhydride CPP:SA polymers allowed for higher localized dose concentrations in the rodent brain, in contrast to orally administered TMZ treatment. Over the past two decades, many retrospective and prospective trials have evaluated the coupling of TMZ and BCNU polymers. Wait et al. summarized the observations from 10 studies, concluding that the combination of both modalities is safe and effective. Clinically, BCNU wafers are implanted into the resection cavity postsurgery, followed by TMZ and/or radiation beginning on day 4. This combination has been associated with a 76% 1 year survival rate compared to the 59.2% survival rate of BCNU-only treatment. These results suggest that the integration of BCNU polymers with oral TMZ with/without radiotherapy may extend survival by several months. Recently, Champeaux-Depond analyzed a French national database of patients with high-grade glioma who received Gliadel implantation following surgical resection from 2008 to 2019. The median overall survival was 17.04 months, and a multivariable logistic regression model showed that the concomitant use of Gliadel wafers with radiation and oral TMZ was significantly associated with enhanced overall survival. The Japan Clinical Oncology Group Study (MACS study) is currently conducting a randomized phase III trial assessing the efficiency of combining maximal GBM resection with BCNU wafer implantation, followed by TMZ chemoradiotherapy (3 weeks post-resection). Preliminary data suggest that the expected 2-year survival rate for resection + BCNU wafers + TMZ is 12% greater than resection + TMZ alone.

2.2.4. Other Implantable Polymer Formulations

In addition to BCNU, the pCPP:SA copolymer was used to locally deliver the hypoxia-inducible factor 1 (HIF1) inhibitor acriflavine (ACF) in a 9L rat model. This delivery of ACF showed a substantial improvement in overall survival of the rats receiving this therapy as compared to untreated controls and those receiving systemic ACF. Additionally, the implantation of ACF wafers resulted in total tumor clearance in >90% of the rats. Besides pCPP:SA, other polymeric formulations have been used to synthesize implantable compressed wafers that can achieve sustained drug delivery. For instance, the copolymer of fatty acid dimer and sebacic acid (FAD:SA) that loads hydrophilic drugs has also shown promising results in rodent models, though its efficiency in human trials remains unexplored. ,

Polymeric wafers are highly favored for versatility and efficiency in localized delivery; however, an emerging technique of electrospinning polymers into nanofibers offers advantages, such as a lower impact on intracranial pressure and a high surface area to volume ratio. In vitro, these nanofibers induce high rates of cell apoptosis in GBM compared to free drug delivery. − Additionally, the delivery of BCNU using electrospun NanoMesh fibers has shown favorable release kinetics and promising efficacy in a rat model of GBM. Hydrogels, which are another formulation mechanism, consist of a three-dimensional hydrophilic polymer that can be generated in situ to conform to accommodate the shape of the resection area and achieve maximal contact along the boundaries of the cavity. For instance, the OncoGel formulation (hydrogel of PLGA and PEG loaded with paclitaxel) was shown to provide a controlled localized release of paclitaxel in 9L gliosarcoma rats, resulting in tumor shrinkage and increased survival (clinical trials investigating local implantable biodegradable polymeric systems are summarized in Table ).

3. BBB-Penetrant Strategies

3.1. Exosomes

3.1.1. Overview

Extracellular vesicles (EVs) are nanomicroscale membrane-bound vesicles secreted by almost all types of cells. EVs can carry biologically active cargo, including lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids, and have been found to mediate long-distance communication among various cell types. In the context of drug delivery, EVs are comparable to liposomes, given that both are phospholipid-based. However, EVs can be composed of a mixture of lipids and surface proteins, some of which can facilitate tissue targeting or mitigate nonspecific interactions. Two major subtype classifications of EVs exist: ectosomes and exosomes. Given that exosomes have excellent drug-carrying capacity and can accommodate hydrophilic and hydrophobic drugs, they will be the focus of this section. In fact, exosomes offer numerous advantages as a delivery strategy, owing to their biocompatibility, versatility, and effective transport. Currently, exosomes are being investigated in clinical trials for different uses, including depression (NCT04202770) and ischemic stroke (NCT06138210). However, their clinical translation as a drug delivery vehicle still faces multiple challenges related to the scalability of production, maintaining consistency in characteristics, maximizing drug loading capabilities, and ensuring their stability and safety when delivered exogenously to patients. ,

3.1.2. Neurodegenerative Diseases

Exosome-based therapeutic applications have received considerable attention, given their extended blood circulation half-life, BBB permeability, and low toxicity and immunogenicity. Exosomes have shown promise in a number of non-neoplastic and neoplastic brain pathologies (Figure ). In Alzheimer’s disease (AD), exosomes have been utilized for delivering therapeutic cargo to ameliorate cognitive deficits in murine models. Cui et al. created brain-targeting exosomes derived from mesenchymal stem cells by modifying the exosomes with the CNS-specific rabies viral glycoprotein (RVG) peptide to enhance the efficacy and delivery of exosomes in the brains of transgenic Alzheimer’s mice. Two other studies have successfully delivered exosome-encapsulated natural compounds across the BBB to alleviate neurodegenerative phenotypes in murine models. , Additionally, Perets et al. established a neuroimaging method for longitudinally and quantitatively tracking exosomes derived from mesenchymal stem cells based on gold nanoparticle labeling agents and X-ray computed tomography. These studies found that the exosomes accumulated in neurodegenerative and neurodevelopmental murine model brain regions for as long as 96 h, while they showed a diffuse distribution pattern and clearance within 24 h in healthy controls. Additionally, the exosome accumulation and homing mechanism appeared to be neuroinflammation-driven, as the neuroinflammatory signals in diseased brains were correlated with the exosome accumulation. Finally, these studies show that the exosomes primarily accumulate within neuronal cells in the diseased regions but not in glial cells.

In the context of Parkinson’s disease (PD), exosomes have been found to both facilitate and mitigate disease progression. Guo et al. observed that microglia-derived exosomes treated with human α-synuclein fibrils can induce protein aggregation in recipient neurons. They found that the combination of these exosomes with microglia-derived proinflammatory cytokines augmented protein aggregation in neurons. When injected stereotactically into the brain, these exosomes resulted in nigrostriatal degeneration in a time-dependent manner, while irradiating microglia in these animals significantly decreased the accumulation of α-synuclein. Finally, they observed that CD11b+ exosomes from cerebrospinal fluid patients with PD contained α-synuclein oligomers, which could induce α-synuclein aggregation in neurons. Additionally, Jiang et al. observed that microRNA-137 found in serum exosomes plays a role in inducing oxidative stress in neurons and that down-regulation of miR-137 derived from exosomes lessens oxidative stress injury in murine models of PD. Two studies have utilized the RVG peptide targeting approach mentioned above to target and deliver exosomes containing vector-based therapies to reduce neuropathological and behavioral deficits in mouse models of PD. , In other non-neoplastic brain pathologies, exosomes have been observed to improve pathological processes in demyelinating disorder, , stroke, perimenopausal depression, and CNS tuberculosis.

3.1.3. Brain Tumors

Exosomes have recently become a key player in neoplastic brain diseases and are being investigated as therapeutic platforms in brain tumor therapy. These therapeutic approaches center on engineering exosomes with highly decorated surfaces to enhance their targeting and functional capabilities. In two papers, Lee et al. engineered exosomes and nanovesicles with the T7 peptide on the surface, which is the transferrin receptor binding peptide. , Given that GBM tumor cells are known to overexpress the transferrin receptor, these studies found that T7 peptide-decorated exosomes were more efficient at delivering cargo into the brain and inducing the expression of tumor suppressor genes than unmodified or rabies viral glycoprotein (RVG)-modified exosomes. Liu et al. used this same T7 peptide approach to deliver exosomes containing siRNA targeting the YY1 protein to demonstrate that this therapy synergizes with chemotherapy and radiation therapy to significantly improve survival time in GBM-bearing mice. Wu and Li et al., similarly, modified exosomes with angiopep-2 on their surface with the aim of altering the protein corona such that these particles would have increased targeting and uptake into GBM cells and show less absorption of serum proteins and phagocytosis by macrophages. The angiopep-2 expressing exosomes showed enhanced BBB penetration and targeting to GBM cells and augmented the delivery of docetaxel to the tumor tissue. Additionally, the angiopep-2-modified exosomes significantly attenuated tumor progression in an orthotopic GBM model. Using a similar approach, Liang et al. conjugated angiopep-2 and CD133 aptamers onto the surface of exosomes loaded with Temozolomide. These dual-receptor-specific exosomes achieved in vitro inhibition of U87MG human glioma and glioma stem cell proliferation as well as extension of the median survival time of U87MG tumor-bearing mice. Two studies have created platforms combining magnetic nanoparticles with exosomes to improve the targeting of the exosomes as well as enable imaging of the exosomes in vivo. Li et al. developed a composite therapeutic platform, combining magnetic nanoparticle targeting and drug delivery properties, with engineered exosome BBB penetration and siRNA encapsulation abilities. They demonstrated the effectiveness of ferroptosis therapy through the breakdown of the ferroptosis defense axis with the aid of Fe3O4 nanoparticles, which facilitated Fe2+ release. Jia et al. developed neuropilin-1-targeted peptide-targeted and superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles (SPIONs) loaded exosomes. In orthotopic GBM models, these exosomes crossed the BBB and allowed for targeted imaging and therapy. Rehman et al. decorated mesenchymal stem cell-derived exosomes with heme oxygenase-1 (Hmox1) specific short peptide (HSSP) for specifically targeting TMZ-resistant glioma cells. These targeted exosomes were loaded with STAT3-targeted siRNA and were found to inhibit tumor cell proliferation, decrease the level of MGMT expression in TMZ-resistant GBM cells, and synergize with TMZ-loaded exosomes in vivo. Additionally, a few studies have utilized exosomes to augment immune responses to brain tumors. One example is that by Cui et al., who designed immune exosomes, which are serum micelles decorated with CpG oligonucleotides and agonists of TLR9. These immune exosomes were then used to coat the pure drug nanomicelles. These studies demonstrated that immune exosome-coated micelles can bind transferrin, prolong blood circulation, remain intact while crossing the BBB, and target GBM cells. These particles induced apoptosis in GBM cells, stimulated dendritic cell maturation, and polarized tumor-associated macrophages, generating anti-GBM immune responses in the GBM microenvironment (Clinical trials investigating exosome-based delivery are summarized in Table ).

3.2. Vectors

3.2.1. Overview

In its simplest form, gene therapy revolves around the utilization of viral or nonviral carriers to introduce genetic cargo into target cells with the aim of preventing or curing diseases by altering certain genes of interest.

3.2.2. Viral Vectors

In its simplest form, gene therapy revolves around the utilization of viral or nonviral carriers to introduce genetic cargo into target cells with the aim of preventing or curing diseases by altering certain genes of interest. While a number of gene editing technologies exist, some of the viral vectors that have shown the greatest promise in preclinical and clinical investigations are adeno-associated virus (AAV) vectors, recombinant AAVs, short regulatory DNA sequences, and peptides. − AAV vectors are nonpathogenic single-stranded DNA parvoviruses that can transduce cells via endocytosis and trafficking into the nucleus, at which point the AAV genome is converted to double-stranded DNA for transcription and translation. Importantly, a very small portion of the AAV genome plays an essential role in vector production and ensuring persistent cell transduction, leaving almost 96% of the AAV genome to be re-engineered for gene therapy. A limitation of AAV vectors is their ability to package a maximum of ∼5.0 kb of DNA. Additionally, while versatile in gene editing capacity, high systemic doses are generally required to achieve CNS transgene expression due to the BBB. A considerable amount of the administered vector is taken up by other tissues, resulting in varying degrees of adverse effects.

While it has been appreciated that parental serotypes have differing abilities to cross the BBB, − only recently have some of the structural features that facilitate the BBB penetration of AAVs been characterized. Albright et al. used rational design and screening to identify key clusters of residues on the AAVrh.10 capsid that augment its ability to cross the BBB and achieve successful transduction of neurons in mice. This work identified a minimal footprint from AAVrh.10 for crossing the BBB, which, when grafted onto AAV1, generated a neurotropic, liver-detargeted vector for global CNS gene transfer. More recently, Huang et al. engineered peptide-modified AAV capsids that enhanced brain transduction by introducing de novo interactions with LY6A and LY6C1, proteins expressed on the BBB of mice. This work demonstrated that directly targeting AAV capsids to defined cell surface proteins expressed on the BBB can efficiently result in robust gene delivery vectors with predictable in vivo tropisms. As was noted in the original paper, these findings cannot be translated to primates, given that these proteins do not have homologues in primates. To address this challenge of the translation of BBB-penetrating-AAVs, Chuapoco et al. describe AAV.CAP-Mac, an engineered AAV9 variant observed to have improved efficiency for brain-wide transgene expression in multiple nonhuman primate species. Using this platform, they showed successful delivery of a cocktail for three fluorescent reporters and GCaMP8s for the optical evaluation of ex vivo neuronal activity. Another group attempted to address the translatability of AAVs from murine models to humans by employing an in vitro model of the human BBB consisting of human cerebral microvasculature endothelial cells (hCMEC/D3) cocultured with primary astrocytes in a transwell system. Using this model, Song et al. isolated a catalog of capsids with enhanced BBB permeability and transduction of hiPSC-derived cortical neurons and primary astrocytes.

3.2.3. CNS Applications

In addition to understanding the mechanism of gene therapy, BBB permeability, and tropism, preclinical studies have also highlighted the efficacy of these therapeutic approaches in CNS pathologies, including AD, PD, and brain tumors. Two recent studies have utilized vector conjugate nanodelivery platforms for gene delivery to the CNS for AD. Israel et al. conjugated 6 different peptide vectors, each with specificity for different transcytosis receptors, to a 50 kDa poly­(β-l-malic acid)-trileucine polymer, forming P/LLL(40%)/vector conjugate. They found that BBB permeability was significantly increased using the vector conjugates of P/LLL(40%) due to the copolymer group binding at the endothelial membrane and subsequently making the sites needed for vector–receptor complex formation more accessible. As an extension of this work and to design a platform with cell-type specificity, Israel et al. conjugated a D-configured-peptide vector for improved targeting and delivery of microRNA and antisense RNA specifically to neurons or amyloid plaques compared to astrocytes and other glia in a mouse model of AD. Finally, Marino et al. leveraged the BBB crossing properties of AAVs to deliver a single domain antibody (VHHs), also known as nanobodies, to inhibit BACE1, a well-characterized target in AD. This AAV-mediated approach of a VHH resulted in whole-brain, persistent anti-BACE1 VHH (B9) production in an AD mouse model for up to 12 months after administration. The resulting sustained VHH-B9 expression also resulted in greater than 12 months of positive effects on cognitive function, neuroinflammation, amyloid load, and synaptic function. In brain tumor research, but also employing a nanocomplex approach, Neves et al. designed a cell-penetrating peptide, WRAP5, with a transferrin sequence to contain plasmid DNA encoding tumor suppressor p53. These complexes were internalized by GBM cells and trafficked to the nucleus. In the same work described above by Israel et al., they show that the P/LLL/A2 copolymer showed the highest penetrance into the tumor microenvironment of the GL261 syngeneic GBM mouse model (clinical trials investigating vector-based delivery are summarized in Table ).

3.3. Nanoparticles

3.3.1. Overview

Nanoparticles, microscopic entities with diameters ranging from 1 to 100 nm, have revolutionized our approach to addressing the BBB and facilitated groundbreaking innovations in drug delivery to the CNS. Various nanoparticles are explored, including Cu2–x Se particles, gold nanoparticles, solid lipid nanoparticles, PEGylated polyester nanoparticles, and albumin-bound nanoparticles.

3.3.2. Imaging and Therapeutics for BBB Exploration

Recent technological advancements have made it feasible to develop highly specialized nanoparticles such as ultrasmall Cu2–x Se particles with a 3.0 nm diameter (Figure ). These have been pivotal in advancing noninvasive imaging techniques. Photoacoustic imaging, made possible through these nanoparticles, has given researchers unprecedented visibility into the workings of the BBB. Zhang et al. combined focused ultrasound and Cu2–x Se nanoparticles in murine models to produce high-resolution images of nanoparticle distribution in deep-seated brain regions. This advancement has profound implications for targeted drug delivery and disease mapping. Additionally, a key study by Ohta et al. explored the optimal size of gold nanoparticles (AuNPs) for BBB penetration. Their work underscored the significance of balancing nanoparticle size against effective excretion, concluding that medium-sized AuNPs (15 nm) offered an optimal trade-off.

3.3.3. Strategies to Augment BBB Permeability

Li et al. applied a groundbreaking combination of AuNPs with transcranial picosecond laser pulses, inducing temporary and reversible permeability changes in the BBB. This resulted in the successful delivery of bioactive substances, such as human IgG antibodies and adeno-associated virus particles, into the CNS. Furthermore, the picosecond laser-induced nanoscale pressure waves had the added advantage of loosening tight junctions without damaging surrounding cellular structures.

Another noteworthy study by Lilius et al. demonstrated the utility of intracisterna magna administration to deliver medium-sized, 111In-radiolabeled AuNPs to deep brain tissues. Their research found that hypertonic saline coadministration dramatically elevated nanoparticle uptake, enhancing the effectiveness of the delivery mechanism and opening new doors for therapeutic intervention.

3.3.4. Design and Biochemical Optimization

While manipulating external conditions to aid in nanoparticle delivery is invaluable, the intrinsic design of nanoparticles also offers a plethora of opportunities for optimization. Wünsch et al. employed lecithin-stabilized nanoparticles coated with ApoE. These particles exhibited an unprecedented ability to traverse an in vitro BBB model of primary porcine brain capillary endothelial cells via ApoE-mediated transcytosis. This approach essentially mimics the body’s natural lipid trafficking mechanisms, thereby broadening the scope of substances that could be effectively delivered to the CNS. Rabanel et al. investigated how varying surface properties and sizes of PEGylated polyester nanoparticles influence their uptake. Interestingly, while these variations affected the endocytosis rates, they had minimal influence on the particles’ ability to cross the cell barrier. This counterintuitive finding suggested that other intrinsic factors may be involved, indicating an avenue for further exploration.

3.3.5. Non-Neoplastic Diseases

The versatility of nanoparticles makes them highly applicable for treating a wide range of neurological pathologies beyond neurodegenerative diseases. For example, traumatic brain injury (TBI) and ALS represent feasible targets. Bharadwaj et al. provided a nuanced understanding of how varying types of TBI could affect BBB permeability differently, setting the stage for more personalized therapeutic approaches. Moreover, Han et al. designed lipid-covered nanoparticles that act as radical scavengers. These nanoparticles were remarkably successful in reducing calcium influx and neuroinflammation following TBI. However, as Violatto et al. demonstrated, not all nanoparticles are equal in their effectiveness; some failed to cross the BBB or release their pharmaceutical payloads, emphasizing the critical need for more sophisticated drug-tethering techniques.

3.3.6. Neoplastic Diseases

Nanoparticles have emerged as a crucial tool in addressing the challenging treatment landscape of CNS malignancies, which have historically been resistant to conventional therapies due to the BBB. Liang et al. employed nanoparticles covalently bonded to albumin, exploiting a protein secreted protein acidic and rich in cysteine, or SPARC-dependent, uptake mechanism to effectively bypass the BBB. Similarly, Ha et al. used albumin nanoparticles in conjunction with ultrasound to transiently penetrate the BBB in a U87MG orthotopic mouse model. Rizzuto et al. took a different approach, using human apoferritin (HFn) as a nanovector for delivering monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) through an in vitro BBB model. The cellular uptake of these HFn-mAb complexes by human cerebral microvascular endothelial cells was confirmed, maintaining the biological activity of the antibodies. Wu et al. explored the optimization of nanoparticles for BBB crossing and glioma targeting, emphasizing the promise of dual ligand modifications while acknowledging inherent challenges such as stability and toxicity. He et al. utilized solid lipid nanoparticles (SLN) to encapsulate β-elemene, a traditional Chinese medicine with antitumor properties but poor solubility and bioavailability. The SLN-encapsulated form not only inhibited cell growth more effectively but also showed enhanced pharmacokinetic properties compared with its traditional form. Finally, Gkountas et al. highlighted the potential of magnetism in enhancing the number of nanoparticles, particularly larger ones around 100 nm, that could successfully bypass the BBB. Despite the promise that magnetism provides due to its safety and efficacy in driving brain permeation, there are some challenges that remain to be tackled. The most important is the current inability to form a localized magnetic field of acceptable intensity without the use of highly invasive techniques.

3.3.7. Current Challenges and Future Directions

While the promise of nanoparticle-mediated drug delivery to the CNS is clear, several challenges remain. Specificity in drug delivery is a key concern; unintended interactions could compromise treatment effectiveness or induce adverse effects. Additionally, issues related to nanoparticle stability and the scalability of production methods must be addressed. Complex pharmacokinetics further complicates the picture, requiring a deeper understanding of systemic absorption, metabolism, and excretion. Overcoming these challenges is crucial for the successful translation of lab-based findings into viable clinical treatments for neurological disorders (Clinical trials investigating nanoparticle-based delivery are summarized in Table ).

4. BBB-Disrupting Strategies

4.1. Focused Ultrasound

4.1.1. Overview

Focused ultrasound (FUS) relies on the use of a concave transducer or an array of transducers that can effectively deliver targeted ultrasound waves to a determined area. Therapeutic FUS has been investigated for many decades. In the 1950s, initial trials of therapeutic FUS for treating psychiatric diseases, movement disorders, and brain tumors were conducted. − At the present time, FUS has been approved for the treatment of many diseases, including uterine fibroids and lesions of the breast, prostate, and liver. Another major application that has attracted considerable interest is the ability of low-intensity FUS to transiently disrupt the BBB and allow the delivery of therapeutic agents or nucleic acids for gene therapy. The mechanical force carried by the FUS waves induces temporary alterations in tight junctions, decreases P-glycoprotein, and increases caveolae formation. Additionally, the coupling of FUS with intravascularly circulating microbubbles promotes the cavitation of these microbubbles, which in its turn augments the structural disruption of the BBB. Herein, we review recent FUS applications for the purpose of BBB opening (Figure ).

4.1.2. Alzheimer’s Disease

AD is the most commonly encountered neurodegenerative disorder, with an estimated prevalence of triple by 2050. Therefore, experimental and clinical research studies have investigated various disease-modifying treatments and explored potential avenues to facilitate their delivery, including the use of FUS. − In mouse models of AD, it was shown that FUS-assisted BBB opening upregulated brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), EGR1, and acetylcholinesterase in the hippocampus, which increased hippocampal neurogenesis and improved spatial memory and cognition. Interestingly, Kong et al. found that this improved cognition lasted up to 7 weeks. Continued intraneuronal inflammation-driven stress and pro-apoptosis are some of the underlying pathogeneses that have been targeted by some studies. , In a healthy situation, nerve growth factor (NGF) augments nerve survival, growth, and synaptic plasticity through the tropomyosin receptor kinase A (TrkA) pathway. However, in AD (and hence the defective TrkA pathway), endogenous NGF predominantly binds to the p75 neurotrophin receptor (p75NTR), yielding neuronal inflammation and apoptosis. By using a mouse model of AD, Xhima et al. showed that MRI-guided FUS-mediated delivery of the selective TrkA agonist D3 (a peptidomimetic agonist of TrkA) to the forebrain region augmented neurotrophin signaling and cholinergic function. Utilizing a similar model, Liu et al. investigated the FUS-assisted delivery of quercetin-modified sulfur nanoparticles (Qc@SNPs). They showed that upon Qc@SNPs accumulation in the brain parenchyma, it offsets endoplasmic reticulum stress, optimizing calcium homeostasis and oxidative stress, hence ameliorating neuronal inflammation and apoptosis. Furthermore, the Morris water maze experiment has shown improved learning and memory abilities in these mice. Using the 5XFAD model of AD in mice, Antoniou et al. demonstrated the successful FUS-mediated delivery of an antibody against amyloid β (i.e., the main component of amyloid plaque). However, the efficacy of this method needs to be further investigated. In line with preclinical investigations, clinical studies have also demonstrated the efficacy of FUS-mediated transient BBB opening in patients with AD without cognitive disturbances or other adverse events. − In addition, Park et al. and Rezai et al. have proven the ability of FUS to reduce amyloid plaque burden, , though the possible subsequent effects on AD symptoms need to be established by future studies.

4.1.3. Parkinson’s Disease

PD is the second most abundant neurodegenerative disease, involving about 3% of individuals older than 80. Medical therapy, ablative surgery, and deep brain stimulation are conventional treatments. FUS-mediated interventions such as gene delivery, BBB opening, and ablation have been under investigation. − The use of AAV.SIRT3-myc (recombinant AAV serotype overexpressing myc-tagged Sirtuin 3) for the delivery of gene therapy was investigated in a PD rat model and exhibited protective and regenerative abilities. Trinh et al. used a similar model to investigate MRI-guided FUS-assisted delivery of AAV.SIRT3-myc, following intravenous injection, to the striatum and substania nigra (SN). Post-mortem immunohistochemistry revealed SIRT3-myc expression in the striatum and not in the SN, despite MRI-proven sufficient permeabilization. Given that gene expression in both the striatum and the SN is required for optimal treatment, Aly et al. investigated FUS-assisted intranasal delivery of plasmid DNA nanoparticles into deep brain regions in a rodent model of PD. They showed that this technique yielded an 80 and 40% increase in gene expression in the SN and dopaminergic neurons of the sonicated hemisphere, respectively. Along with preclinical research, clinical studies exhibited the safety, feasibility, and reversibility of BBB opening in PD patients at the right parietal-occipital-temporal junction, putamen, and striatum. These findings were followed by a study by Meng et al., which evaluated MRI-guided FUS-mediated delivery of intravenous recombinant lysosomal enzyme glucocerebrosidase (GCase) in four patients with PD due to GBA1 mutations. Apart from two episodes of post-treatment transient dyskinesia, no other adverse events occurred. At 6 months of follow-up, blinded Movement Disorder Society-Unified Parkinson’s Disease Rating Scale motor scores showed a 12% reduction relative to the baseline value in patients off medication. Although these results seem promising, future studies need to corroborate these findings with a larger sample size and a longer follow-up.

4.1.4. Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis

Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) is a rare neurodegenerative disease with a poor prognosis, with a predicted 2–4 years of survival after diagnosis. Current medications with moderate benefits include riluzole and edaravone. To enhance edaravone delivery and using an ALS mouse model, Shen et al. demonstrated that FUS-mediated edaravone delivery to the motor cortex resulted in a more remarkable neuromuscular improvement as compared to mice treated with edaravone only. As the next step, Abrahao et al. applied MRI-guided FUS to the primary motor cortex of four patients with ALS. This study demonstrated the safety and efficacy of the FUS-mediated transient BBB opening. Future investigations should focus on the FUS-assisted delivery of disease-modifying therapeutics in this patient population.

4.1.5. Malignant Glioma

Researchers have investigated FUS’s role in enhancing drug delivery through the BBB in patients with glioma. − In this context, TMZ is an established chemotherapeutic agent for grade IV glioma. Using a rat model of GBM, Dong et al. applied FUS following Temozolomide therapy, and found that the FUS group showed higher glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP) and lower K i-67 versus the control group, indicating that FUS can augment Temozolomide delivery through the BBB. Wei et al. investigated the efficacy of the FUS-mediated delivery of Etoposide to a murine GBM model. In comparison to the control group, the FUS-assisted treatment decreased tumor growth by 45% and prolonged survival by 30%. Gambogic acid (GA) is a common chemotherapeutic agent, although its pharmacodynamic characteristics limit its efficacy in brain tumors. To increase its efficacy in brain tumors, Wang et al. formulated GA-loaded poly lactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) nanobubbles conjugated into a cationic microbubble complex (CMC) (GA-PLGA-CMC). Using a murine glioma model, they assessed FUS-triggered delivery of GA-PLGA-CMC, in which the first FUS waves opened the BBB with the help of the CMC and delivered GA-PLGA into the brain. The second FUS irradiation augmented GA delivery into the brain tumor, yielding tumor growth inhibition. In a similar context, Panobinostat is a chemotherapeutic medication that has shown promising in vitro findings against DIPG. To enhance Panobinostat efficacy, Martinez et al. evaluated FUS-assisted Panobinostat delivery in a mouse DIPG model. This strategy resulted in a 3-fold increase in intertumoral Panobinostat concentration, 71% tumor volume reduction, and an increase in survival of 10 days. In line with the preclinical studies, clinical trials have been conducted to evaluate the safety and feasibility of FUS-mediated BBB opening in patients with glioma. − In a phase 0 trial by Anastasiadis et al., four patients harboring infiltrating glioma underwent transcranial FUS for targeted BBB opening. Although this study was limited by its small sample size, it showed that this technique can be applied safely for transient targeted BBB opening in glioma patients for the purpose of drug delivery. Park et al. conducted a phase 1 trial in which six GBM patients underwent MRI-guided FUS adjacent to the resection margin on the day of adjuvant Temozolomide chemotherapy, demonstrating transient BBB openings at the targets. At the 15-month follow-up, the 1 year survival was 100%; two cases sustained recurrence, and no FUS-related complication was found. , Sonabend et al. reported a phase 1 clinical trial in which 17 patients with recurrent GBM underwent US device implantation for albumin-bound paclitaxel delivery. − Imaging proved successful BBB opening at the targets, which subsided over 1 h. The only complications associated with US-mediated BBB opening were a transient mild headache. Paclitaxel-related complications included self-resolving encephalopathy associated with the 260 mg/m2 dose, neutropenia (47%), leukopenia (29%), and hypertension (29%). These studies open a new chapter and augment the possibility of the application of previously BBB-impermeable therapeutic agents − (clinical trials investigating FUS-mediated BBB opening to assist delivery are summarized in Table ).

5. Intranasal Delivery

5.1. Overview

The nasal cavity offers one of the most direct routes to access the CNS. Considering its unique anatomy and CNS proximity, the transnasal delivery of compounds has been studied since the late 1900s. Anatomically, the cavity can be separated into three regions (Figure ): (1) a vestibular region with poor potential for drug absorption, (2) a respiratory region with the largest surface area in humans and extensive vascularization, and (3) an olfactory region with the highest capacity for direct drug delivery. Across these three sections, the trigeminal and olfactory cranial nerves facilitate CNS access. The trigeminal nerve predominates in the respiratory region, where molecular drug trafficking occurs through the innervating sensory fibers of the nerve’s ophthalmic (V1) and maxillary (V2) divisions. At the same time, the olfactory nerve, present only in its designated region, allows for CNS access along sensory neurons that project to the olfactory bulb. Of the two, the olfactory system contributes most to CNS drug delivery. ,

The transnasal approach can be viewed as a local and direct drug delivery paradigm. With its anatomical proximity, it benefits from local delivery and avoids systemic complications like non-CNS adverse effects, preabsorption metabolism, hepatic first-pass metabolism, and dilution from plasma protein binding. , Its direct access circumvents issues like poor CNS bioavailability that plague systemic drug formulations and enables significantly lower dosages, up to 0.01–1% of oral doses. Further, its ease of access makes patient self-administration a possibility. To this point, minimally invasive nasal depot, or the MIND technique, was recently described, where a common outpatient procedure already employed by ear, nose, and throat surgeons to endoscopically access the nasal cavity was repurposed to successfully deliver oligonucleotide therapy to increase neurotrophic factors and counter neurodegenerative processes.

Despite these advantages of the transnasal approach, the MIND technique also illustrates continued challenges in designing transnasal therapeutics. Though there has been extensive focus on this subject, with innumerable therapies reported successfully in vitro and in vivo, the technique has largely failed in clinical trials. Beyond the universal obstacles of drug discovery and therapeutic design, the approach’s advantage with anatomical proximity is also one of its greatest drawbacks. The olfactory epithelium, which is the major conduit for transnasal delivery, makes up only 3–5% of the human nasal cavity’s total surface area. Further, since nasopharyngeal mucus is constantly moved into the GI tract by mucociliary action, compounds that reach the olfactory epithelium only have ∼15–20 min of real contact time before being swept away, in addition to degradation by resident proteases and nucleases within the nasopharyngeal mucus. Though there are formidable challenges, efforts in recent years have continued to substantiate the transnasal approach as a promising means of CNS drug delivery.

5.2. Neurodegenerative Disease

In neurodegeneration, broad efforts have been reported on AD, PD, and Huntington’s disease (HD) therapies. For transnasal approaches, nanomedicine-based delivery systems have been gaining traction as a modular delivery system. In brief, nanoparticles help to counter physiological barriers by shielding compounds before they reach the CNS or augment drug absorption through hydrophobic formulations. A variety of acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (e.g., galantamine, donepezil, rivastigmine) and other AD-targeting compounds have been successfully trialed in nanoparticle formulations in crossing the BBB. Modifying a nanoparticle coating to better bind both olfactory epithelium and β-amyloid deposits has been recently reported as a transnasal method for AD therapy. For HD, a modified lipid-membrane nanoparticle was proposed as a theoretically ideal transnasal therapy for administering engeletin, a compound that reduces mitochondrial dysfunction and blocks deleterious NF-κB signaling. In AD, a gel-based intranasal delivery system has been evaluated in vitro and ex vivo for the model drug donepezil, confirming how increasing the delivery system’s viscosity properties can improve CNS penetration. Similarly, in PD, a thermosensitive gel was optimized in mice to change states from liquid to solid in situ to help maximize drug retention time and CNS delivery. Another paradigm shift in treating neurodegenerative disorders such as AD and PD has been targeting calcium-permeable amyloid pores rather than canonical amyloid aggregates. With this focus, an antipore peptide, AmyP53, was recently developed and shown in animal models to successfully traffic into the CNS after intranasal administration.

Nasal delivery has been used for numerous other CNS pathologies as well, where the common theme has been addressing the limitations of a drug’s CNS bioavailability by formulating it into a delivery vehicle, oftentimes utilizing nanomedicine, for transnasal delivery. Though there have been extensive efforts in this process, clinical trials are lacking for most. Most reports have focused on packaging standard medicines for transnasal delivery. For instance, organophosphate poisoning, which irreversibly binds to and disables acetylcholinesterases, can be rescued in rat models treated with intranasal quaternary ammonium antidotes contained in a modified liposomal delivery system. The antifungal ketoconazole was similarly trafficked into the CNS through an intranasal lipid carrier system and reduced disease burden in mice with fungal CNS infections. In a rat model of AIDS with neurological complications, administering the anti-HIV dolutegravir in a nanoemulsion gel intranasally could confine the medicine to the CNS with minimal systemic levels. For schizophrenia, the standard antipsychotic olanzapine was loaded in micelle nanocarriers and given intranasally in an in vivo schizophrenia rat model to reduce symptoms. Oral tizanidine, a common medication for muscular spasms, has poor CNS bioavailability and was formulated into nanosized “cubosomes” for transnasal delivery into rats. Other intranasal studies have trialed novel therapeutics. In epilepsy, the psychostimulant citicoline has been loaded into niosomes, a form of nanoparticle, for transnasal delivery and shown to diffuse successfully in vitro and ex vivo. Finally, gene editing through microRNA against neuroinflammation was shown in vivo to improve deficits in mice with intracranial hemorrhage.

5.3. Brain Tumors

Finally, neurooncology has seen an abundance of transnasal delivery applications. In recent years, both chemotherapies and novel gene editing techniques have been applied in transnasal approaches with preclinical success. In vitro work for nanoemulsions of the chemotherapy lomustine, though limited without in vivo or ex vivo data, showed increased release kinetics for intranasal delivery compared to standard lomustine. Taking a step further, nanoparticle packaging systems for combination chemotherapies of doxorubicin-erlotinib and paclitaxel-miltefosine have reduced tumor volume and prolonged survival in rodent GBM models. , Further, they have shown reduced systemic side effects compared to traditional oral therapy, likely by virtue of their local intranasal delivery. Similarly, a nanomicelle shell system demonstrated analogous outcomes in delivering antiglioma siRNA transnasally. Notably, one recent study in a mouse model of GBM found evidence that photostimulation of the lymphatic network significantly enhanced the liposomal delivery into the CNS, highlighting the importance of the lymphatic system in this route of delivery.

Across a variety of CNS pathologies, transnasal delivery continues to be an area of great pharmaceutical interest. Its ability to bypass the BBB while providing such a short direct route to the CNS remains a key element to its therapeutic advantage. Despite the bevy of preclinical data available on different medications for transnasal administration, clinical trials are urgently needed to define which options are the most clinically promising (Clinical trials investigating intranasal delivery are summarized in Table ).

6. Receptor and Transporter-Mediated Delivery

6.1. Overview

Multiple receptor and transport systems, which play critical roles in molecular trafficking, may be utilized to facilitate drug delivery to the CNS (Figure ). ,

6.2. Caveolin-Mediated Transport

Caveolin-mediated transcytosis is known to play an important role in transporting large macromolecule cargoes, such as albumin and lipoproteins, across the BBB. Caveolins are scaffolding proteins which are enriched in certain areas of the endothelial cell plasma membrane and form membrane invaginations with high localized concentrations of cholesterol, glycosphingolipids, and lipid-anchoring proteins. , Specifically, caveolin-1 is the primary caveolin protein expressed by BBB endothelial cells. During CNS pathologies in which BBB integrity is compromised, caveolin-1 expression increases, suggesting that it plays a critical role in BBB permeability. , Additionally, an increase in caveolin-mediated transcytosis is believed to be the physiological mechanism underlying BBB disruption via FUS.

6.3. Claudin-5 Modulation

Claudin-5 (Cldn5) proteins are the primary constituents of endothelial cell tight junctions (TJ), which strictly regulate paracellular movement across the BBB. TJs, comprised of Cldn5, present a significant barrier to the delivery of hydrophilic compounds and molecules over 400 Da. Thus, TJ modulators that target Cldn5 to increase BBB permeability are being investigated for their ability to enhance drug delivery to the brain. For example, enterotoxin’s (CPE) C-terminal claudin binding domain (cCPE) can enhance paracellular flow of small solutes by removing specific claudin subtypes from TJs in a tissue-specific manner. , Cldn5-binding cCPE-variants have demonstrated potential to safely increase BBB permeability for drug delivery when applied to in vitro models. In mouse models, M01 is a compound that has been found to facilitate paclitaxel delivery via binding to the extracellular domain of Cldn5. Additionally, Cldn5 modulation via anti-Cldn5 monoclonal antibody has been found to increase BBB permeability in primates, but a safe therapeutic window has yet to be established.

6.4. Transferrin Receptor-Mediated Transport

Transferrin receptors (TfR), which are expressed on BBB endothelial cells as well as brain parenchymal cells, are critical for iron uptake into the brain. TfR expression is unique to the endothelial cells of the brain microvasculature, and TfRs are not found on endothelial cells of the general vasculature. Iron ions complexed with transferrin protein in the blood are understood to enter the brain via TfR-mediated endocytosis, although detailed mechanisms of this transport mechanism have yet to be fully elucidated. Immunofluorescence-based transcellular transport studies have revealed that TfR conducts the bidirectional transport of iron, indicating that drug delivery strategies targeting this pathway must also account for flow in the brain-to-blood direction. Fusion of anti-TfR monoclonal antibodies to therapeutic peptides is a promising drug delivery system, and optimal physiologically based pharmacokinetic modeling is under current investigation.

Development of clinically effective AD treatments requires a simultaneous innovation in drug delivery mechanisms. Importantly, AD is known to cause BBB dysfunction and may impair the normal functioning of various receptors and transport mechanisms. TfR-mediated transport across endothelial cells remains intact in mouse models of amyloid beta and tau-protein pathologies, suggesting this pathway is a promising target for AD drug delivery. Specifically, transferrin-Pep63-liposomes have been demonstrated to reduce amyloid beta plaque burden in AD mouse models. Synthesis of transporter proteins that may be coadministered with therapeutic compounds is another avenue of exploration. Previously, Sarkar et al. synthesized a transporter protein modeled after the LDLR-binding domain of carrier protein apolipoprotein E (ApoE) to increase antibody delivery to the brain in a mouse model. Zou et al. similarly synthesized an analogous transporter protein, K16APoE, which ameliorated AD in a mouse model when coadministered with the therapeutic HAYED peptide.

Treatment of neoplastic disease is another area in which enhanced drug delivery strategies are urgently needed. TfR-targeting strategies are currently being explored for their potential to enhance the effects of drugs against brain neoplasms. Recently, Kang et al. employed docetaxel liposomes, which were comodified with RI7217 monoclonal antibody against TfR and muscone, a BBB permeabilizing compound, to treat glioma in a mouse model. A separate study successfully utilized 2D-HFn, a compound that binds TfR to traverse the BBB, to enhance doxorubicin treatment of glioma in a mouse model. Caveolar transport mechanisms have also been explored in the context of neoplastic disease, and methamphetamine has been shown to temporarily permeabilize the BBB and enhance doxorubicin delivery. Enhanced drug delivery strategies via receptor and transporter-targeted mechanisms are needed to increase the number of clinically effective treatments.

7. Conclusions and Future Directions

In this review, strategies to bypass or overcome the BBB were summarized and analyzed. As it was noted, to maximize CED outcomes, numerous catheter-related variables need to be optimized, and heterogeneous tumor profiles need to be taken into consideration. As for implantable biodegradable polymers, the advent of BCNU wafers constituted a great milestone in this field; however, these wafers have their own set of limitations. Hence, an investigation into more advanced formulations that can carry multiple drugs, sustain a longer and more consistent release, and achieve maximal contact with the resection cavity is still needed. Exosomes, with their cargo-carrying capacity, can be made highly tissue-specific via tissue engineering. In addition, exosomes can be combined with other biomaterials or inorganic components such as magnetic nanoparticles for improved targeting and tracking. Their clinical translation will rely on evaluation in clinically relevant systems and additional insights into the biology of exosomes that mediate cell infiltration and immune evasion. Recent mechanistic insights into the transport of AAVs have led to gene delivery systems that feature more efficient crossing of the BBB and cell-type-specific targeting within the CNS. Continued efforts toward understanding AAV biology and the associated therapeutic challenges and successes will serve as the foundation for future clinical translation of these CNS delivery platforms. In parallel, translational research has demonstrated the efficacy of FUS in drug delivery in different animal models and disease contexts. Clinical studies have further supported the safety of FUS-mediated BBB opening and amyloid plaque reduction in AD, and the efficacy of FUS-assisted drug delivery in other neurodegenerative diseases and brain tumors. However, limited sample sizes, the absence of control groups, and short-term follow-up periods have limited the ability of these findings to drive FDA approval. Future randomized clinical trials are needed to build on and corroborate these promising findings. Further, transnasal delivery continues to be an area of pharmaceutical interest in many brain pathologies. The key element in its therapeutic advantage is the proximity of the nasal cavity to the brain. Additionally, coupling methods, such as FUS-assisted intranasal delivery, may enhance outcomes. However, randomized controlled trials are needed to optimize these strategies and establish their safety, feasibility, and efficacy. Another field that has attracted significant interest is nanomedicine and its various applications in drug delivery. Importantly, the nanoparticle size is a crucial factor that dictates effective BBB penetration and excretion. Additionally, various alterations and combinations applied to nanoparticles have been investigated to augment their capability. Indeed, there is a need to enhance the specificity of nanoparticles for targeted drug delivery. Moreover, there is also a lack of deep understanding regarding the complex pharmacokinetics involved in the systemic absorption, metabolism, and excretion of nanoparticles. Finally, the proper functioning of multiple receptors and transporter pathways is essential to the BBB's integrity. Strategies targeting these pathways are being investigated for their ability to enhance drug delivery across the BBB. Effective methods that augment the delivery of drugs through these pathways are critical to the clinical application and success of treatments for neurodegenerative diseases and brain tumors.
