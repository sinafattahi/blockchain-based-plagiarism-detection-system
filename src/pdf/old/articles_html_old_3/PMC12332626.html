Abstract

Class B G-protein coupled receptors (GPCRs) are significant therapeutic recipients in cardiovascular, neurological, and metabolic diseases. The human gut microbiome is a complex microbial ecology recently identified as a possible source of bioactive peptides that control host physiological functions. Candidate peptides were found using advanced bioinformatics tools including sequence homology analysis, structure modeling, and molecular docking. These peptides were then evaluated for their binding affinity and potential functional regulation of the GPCR activity. Molecular dynamics simulations offered additional insights regarding the stability and interaction diversity of peptide-receptor complexes, highlighting receptor conformational state of G-protein interaction. The findings identify unique microbial peptides capable of influencing class B GPCR function, providing important insights into microbiome–host interactions and therapeutic potential. This study emphasizes the gut microbiome’s previously untapped potential as a source of GPCR modulators, opening up new avenues for microbiome-driven therapy approaches for metabolic and endocrine disorders.

1. Introduction

The human microflora can be interpreted as the dynamic population of microbes colonizing with their diverse set of genes and variable products. Various physiological and multiomics analyses supported by in-cell studies and animal experiments establish the interlink of gut microbiome communities in maintaining body homeostasis. The dynamics of microbiome composition in the gut were studied extensively to correlate with dysbiosis like nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), cardiovascular disorders, obesity-linked disorders like diabetes mellitus, and neurodegenerative disarrays with immune modulation and inflammatory responses. − Microbial diversity varies throughout the gastrointestinal tract depending on pH, oxygen levels, and the presence of bile and mucus. The lower gut microbiome community represented predominantly by anaerobic bacteria belongs to species of Actinobacteria, Bacteroidetes, Firmicutes ( and ), Actinobacteria, Proteobacteria, and Verrucomicrobia (). Noninvasive and invasive experimental techniques like magnetically controlled sampling capsule endoscopy (MSCE) and surgical ileostomy of intestinal effluents map the gut microbiome composition. , Over the period, integrating the advanced techniques of metagenomics, metatranscriptomics, and metabolomics has identified the highly dynamic microbiome composition across the different populations of the world. , Further, this integrative approach also focused on identifying the interplay between different species that might result in viable biofilm formation. The microbiome’s contribution in generating short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) like butyrate, propionate and acetate from complex carbohydrates in the colon has been studied for metabolite contribution. These microbial metabolites were found to have a supplemental role directly or indirectly in maintaining homeostasis for energy metabolism, glucose-lipid metabolism, fortifying immunity, and even in the prevention of cancer. −

GPCRs constitute the most targeted class of receptors for commercially available drugs. The seven transmembrane helices are the signature of these membrane proteins, responsible for relaying the signals through the membrane. GPCRs usually transduce signaling through G-protein and arrestin pathways that induce a subsequent intracellular signaling cascade toward the effector molecule. Signaling by GPCRs is mediated by four distinct types of G-proteins, and their signaling is modulated mainly by two arrestins for the vast majority of GPCRs, with two other arrestins exclusively modulating signaling in the visual system. , Heterotrimer G-proteins consist of α, β, and γ domains and are mainly classified into Gαs, Gαq, Gαi, and Gα1/2 based upon the type of signaling induced by the α-domain while functioning by switching between two nucleotide (GDP/GTP) binding states. The expression patterns of the Gα protein vary among different tissues. Gαs and Gαq show ubiquitous expression, while Gαt (transducing) is expressed specifically in rod cells. While both GPCRs and G-proteins diffuse freely through the membrane, receptors activated by natural ligand or drug molecules promote G-protein binding on the cytoplasmic side, where receptors catalyze the exchange of GTP for GDP of the Gα domain. The GTP-bound Gα domain will undergo dissociation of the heterotrimer, making the Gα-GTP domain free to bind and initiate different downstream pathways. Subsequently, the free receptor will be specifically phosphorylated at serine and threonine residues of ICL3 and the cytoplasmic tail by G-protein receptor kinases (GRKs). The phosphorylation of the receptor is critical to initiate the arrestin signaling pathways, which either leads to receptor internalization or other pathways like MAPK. The GPCRs were classified into five subfamilies based on sequence homology and molecular architecture.

The class B group of the GPCR family is represented by 15 membrane-bound peptide hormone receptors important for various physiological functions. It constitutes a subclass of therapeutically important receptors that communicate for glucose and amino-acid metabolism, cardiovascular and bone development and regeneration, along with immune responses. A detailed information about class B GPCR members regulating different metabolic pathways and diseases is highlighted in Figure . Several class B -GPCR extracellular domain (ECD) structures in complex with peptide hormones were reported earlier. These structures provide important insight into the α-β-β-α conformation of ECD and also characterize their role in ligand specificity. , The structures of the antagonist-bound state of several full-length receptors highlight the insights of the inactive conformation. In contrast, these structures explain the structural plasticity of transmembrane helices associated with different states of the receptor but also enlighten the role of biased agonism for specific metabolic modulations. The ligand-bound ternary complexes of various class-B GPCRs with G-proteins such as secretin-bound secretin receptor–Gs complex (PDB: 7D3S), glucagon-bound glucagon receptor in the absence and presence of Gs/i (PDB: 5YQZ; 6WPW), , Glp-bound GLP1-R-Gs (PDB: 7LCJ), PTH analogue and PTHrP-bound PTH1r-Gs complex (PDB: 6NBI; 8HAF), , CRF-bound CRFR1/2 in complex with Gs (PDB: 6PB1; 6PB0), and PACAP27-bound PAC1R-Gs (PDB: 8E3X) were able to resolve the signaling state of these receptors upon activation.

There are 21 class B GPCR peptide hormones that function as cognate ligands for 15 receptors. Structures of GPCRs in complex with these peptides revealed the novel binding pockets, which can be utilized to develop peptide-based drugs targeting these sites. These peptides consist of 40–52 amino acids with the designated role of C-termini in ECD binding and N-termini in receptor activation. The biochemical studies with GLP-1R, GIPR, PTH-R1, and CRF-R2, where receptor-tethered N-termini of peptides show activation signals in Luciferase/cAMP assays, validate the presence of activation-specific critical residues in the N-terminal region of peptides. − Based on this hypothesis, we want to investigate microbiome-derived bioactive peptides’ regulatory role with similar N-terminal amino acids in targeting class B GPCR family members. The role of microbiome-derived peptides as antimicrobials is well studied, with characterized targets and molecular pathways. The microbiome data mining even identified the newer peptides mimicking the activity of antibiotics in rodent models. However, these stable peptides’ role in activating GPCRs has not been explored yet. Identifying their possible role in regulating these receptors might pave the way for designing naturally stable peptide-based drugs as agonists or antagonists. The microbiome-derived peptides with their smaller size exhibit better structural stability and may keep them intact while reaching targeted sites

Overall, the importance of microbiome-derived metabolites and their assimilation in human physiology has been extensively investigated. However, GPCR activity modulation by microbial peptides having sequence similarity to small peptide hormones is still unknown. Here, in this in silico work, we investigated the binding properties of microbiome peptides to respective class B GPCRs using docking and simulation studies. Further, we were able to identify the molecular state of the microbiome peptide-induced receptor’s closeness to G-protein signaling.

2. Methods

2.1. Search for Microbiome-Derived Peptides Having Receptor-Binding Residues

In order to identify the microbiome peptide having sequence similarity to the amino acid sequences of native peptides, an NCBI BLAST search was performed against a gut microbial peptide sequence database. The PepFunk database (https://shiny.imetalab.ca/pepFunk/) was utilized for this purpose. Based on the literature review, N-terminal amino acid residues of ligand peptides critical for binding and receptor activation were noted. These residues were then used as a reference for sequence matching during the search for relevant peptide sequences.

From the matched peptide sequences, residues located near the N-terminus were selected for further analysis. , In line with previous literature on GIP-bound GIPR (PDB ID: 7RA3), Tyr1 and Ala2 at the N-terminus of Predencin (derived from the species name “”) were identified as critical for binding with the GIP receptor. Similarly, Asp1 and Asn2 at the N-terminus of Paraexcin (derived from the species name “”) were found to be essential for urocortin (UCN) binding with the CRF receptor (PDB ID: 6BP0). The first five N-terminal amino acids of the native peptides were subsequently used for sequence BLAST searches against the peptide database.

2.2. Retrieval of Target Receptor Structure Coordinates

The PDB structures of the active receptors with ligand peptide and G-protein occupied ternary complexes for gastric inhibitory polypeptide (GIP)-bound glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide receptor (GIPR) (PDB ID: 7RA3) and the Urocortin1-bound corticotropin-releasing factor 1 receptor (PDB ID: 6BP0) were retrieved from the protein data bank (pdb). The visualization software Chimera was used to examine the respective ligand binding position. The coordinates of these binding sites were subsequently used for docking studies. The principal residues critical for peptide binding and receptor stimulation were identified from the literature and documented.

2.3. Modeling of Peptides

The peptide conformation was modeled using the PEP-FOLD server , available at PEP-FOLD3. Peptide structures were modelled using PEP-FOLD3 . Each peptide sequence was submitted with default settings, generating up to 200 structural models. The top-ranked model based on sOPEP energy scoring was selected for downstream analyses. The server algorithm conducts a set of 100 simulations for a 15-residue peptide, with each simulation exploring a distinct folds of the conformational space. The output includes a compilation of all generated models along with detailed clustering information and the top conformation from the five best clusters. PEP-FOLD 3 allows users to bias 3D modeling by specifying regions to preserve during simulations. This can be achieved either by using a prior 3D structure and selecting regions to retain, or by accepting the server’s internal optimal predictions for specific residues. This functionality enables iterative modeling for refining existing structures or generating alternative conformations that explore different topological spaces.

2.4. Molecular Docking

Protein-peptide docking is a computer-aided process used to predict the interactions between a protein and a peptide, playing a key role in elucidating protein function, drug design, and various molecular biology applications. The 3D structures of the target proteins, including the GIPR (PDB ID: 7RA3) and the CRFR1 (PDB ID: 6PB0), were retrieved, while the peptide sequence was designed and its 3D structure generated using PEP-FOLD. Energy minimization was applied to both protein and peptide structures to resolve steric clashes and eliminate unfavorable interactions. Proper protonation states were assigned based on the environmental pH using tools such as PROPKA or PDB 2PQR. Multiple peptide conformations were generated through conformational sampling to explore different potential binding modes. Docking simulations were conducted using MdockPep, an FFT-based flexible docking tool optimized for peptide–protein complexes. The receptor was prepared by removing nonessential water molecules and heteroatoms. Peptide docking was performed with the default scoring function (ITScorePeP), and 100 docking poses were generated for each peptide. The top-ranked pose was selected based on binding score and visual inspection for biological relevance. , The peptide was then docked to the protein’s active or binding site to predict the interaction.

The docked complexes were assessed using the scoring functions provided by the docking software, which generally indicate the binding affinity between the protein and the peptide. Key interactions, including hydrophobic contacts, hydrogen bonds and salt bridges, were analyzed to understand the protein–peptide interaction. Docked poses were clustered to determine the most likely binding mode. Visualization tools such as PyMOL and Chimera were employed to examine the binding conformations and interactions in greater detail. For consistency, docking was performed using multiple software platforms to compare the results. In our controlled study, the docking of many microbial peptides with two amino acid identities at the N-termini did not dock to the target receptor.

2.5. Molecular Simulation

The receptor-peptide complex having most favorable binding energy was subjected to a 300 ns molecular dynamics (MD) simulation using Gromacs 2024, with input files prepared via Charmm-Gui. Initially, a 100 ns simulation was carried out for both the complexes to gain an in-depth understanding of the stability of the interaction and of the conformational shifts; a further 300 ns simulation was carried out. , The ligand topologies were generated using CGENFF in Charmm-Gui, and parameters were obtained through the Charmm-Gui Force field Generation module. − The CHARMM36 force field was used for the simulation, and the system was first reduced in vacuum for 5000 steps using the steepest descent technique. The protein–ligand combination was then solvated in a cubic periodic box with a 0.5 nm buffer using the Simple Point Charge (SPC) water model. To neutralize the system, sodium (Na+) and chloride (Cl–) ions were introduced at 0.15 M concentrations. , Energy minimization was conducted using the steepest descent algorithm for up to 50,000 steps, with a convergence threshold set to a maximum force of 1000 kJ/mol/nm. The simulation, consisting of 125,000 steps, was conducted under NPT conditions (constant number of pressure, temperature and particles) with the system maintained at 1.0 bar and 303.15 K using the NPT ensemble in the final run, applying positional restraints to the heavy atoms of the complex. The production MD simulations were run for 300 ns with a 2 fs integration time step, employing the V-rescale thermostat to maintain a temperature of 300 K and the Parrinello–Rahman barostat to regulate the pressure at 1 bar. Particle Mesh Ewald (PME) computes long-distance electrostatic interactions, and covalent bonds involving hydrogen atoms were constrained following the LINCS algorithm. The lipid bilayer was made up of cholesterol, POPC (1-palmitoyl-2-oleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine), and DOPC (1,2-dioleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine) molecules in a 1:1:2 ratio, symmetrically built to mirror the receptor’s native cellular environment. The lipid bilayer consisted of cholesterol, POPC (1-palmitoyl-2-oleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine), and DOPC (1,2-dioleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine) molecules in a ratio of 1:1:2, constructed symmetrically to match the receptor’s native cellular environment.

Periodic boundary conditions were applied based on the shape and size of the system, and approximately 3000 frames were generated per simulation. Trajectory analysis was performed using GROMACS, narrowing down on key structural metrics, such as root-mean-square deviation (RMSD), root-mean-square fluctuation (RMSF), radius of gyration (R g), and hydrogen bonding patterns. The RMSD of the protein backbone atoms was monitored to assess the system’s overall stability throughout the simulation, while RMSF provided details about the dynamicity of residues and domain. The radius of gyration was calculated to evaluate the compactness of the protein structure. Additionally, specific interactions between the peptide and GPCR were examined by calculating hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, and binding free energy using the MM-PBSA method. Further to investigate the downstream signaling induced by microbial peptide, the intermittent simulation frames of microbial peptide-bound receptors were aligned with the G-protein-occupied conformation to mark the distance deviations between key residues important for interaction with G-protein and were analyzed over time.

2.6. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and Free Energy Landscape (FEL) of Receptor-Peptide Complex

(PCA), a multivariate statistical technique, was employed to investigate the dominant molecular motions within molecular dynamics (MD) trajectories. To ensure that only internal conformational dynamics were analyzed, overall translational and rotational motions were first removed via a least-squares fitting procedure relative to a reference structure. Subsequently, a covariance matrix of atomic positional fluctuations was constructed to capture the correlated atomic displacements. Diagonalization of this matrix yielded a set of eigenvectors and corresponding eigenvalues, where the eigenvectors define the principal directions of motion and the eigenvalues quantify the magnitude or energetic contribution of these motions. By projection of the trajectory onto selected eigenvectors, the temporal evolution of the principal motions was analyzed. The GROMACS tools g_covar and g_anaeig were utilized for the computation and visualization of the covariance matrix, eigenvectors, and eigenvalues.

2.7. MMPBSA Analysis

MMPBSA (Molecular Mechanics Poisson–Boltzmann Surface Area) analysis is a widely used computational approach for estimating the binding free energy between a receptor and a ligand. This method combines molecular mechanics with continuum solvent models to provide a detailed understanding of the molecular interactions that contribute to the stability of the receptor–ligand complex. Here, MMPBSA was exercised to quantify the binding affinity of microbial peptides to GPCR, providing insights into the key interactions that govern binding and show similarity with natural ligand interactions. To estimate the binding affinity of the protein–peptide complex, we performed MMPBSA calculations using the FastDRH server.

This study implemented a comprehensive in silico pipeline that integrates peptide sequence screening through BLASTp and PepFunk database queries, validation of microbial origin via taxonomic annotation, molecular docking using MdockPep, and structural evaluation through molecular dynamics simulations with GROMACS. This workflow facilitates the systematic identification and characterization of gut microbiota-derived peptides with the potential to interact with host receptors. By combining sequence homology assessment, physicochemical property profiling, and dynamic structural analysis, the approach offers a robust framework for prioritizing candidate bioactive peptides for functional investigation.

3. Results

3.1. Screening of Microbiome-Derived Peptides for Peptidomimetics

Through the application of our integrative computational workflow, we identified a subset of microbiota-derived peptides exhibiting high sequence similarity to human endogenous peptides along with favorable physicochemical characteristics. Several of these candidates demonstrated strong binding affinities to the target receptor in molecular docking studies and sustained stable interactions throughout the 300 ns molecular dynamics simulations. These results support the hypothesis that gut microbial peptides may modulate host signaling pathways, highlighting their potential to function as molecular mimics of endogenous ligands.

Among 2,34,852 peptides derived from Pepfunk, two peptides were identified for class B GPCRs using Homology sequence alignment with matching key residues at the N-terminal region and occupying the same binding pocket as the native GPCR peptide.

3.4. Principal Component Analysis

To elucidate the predominant collective motions of the system throughout the simulation, principal component analysis (PCA) was conducted on the 300 ns molecular dynamics trajectory. Prior to the analysis, the trajectory frames were structurally aligned to a reference conformation using least-squares fitting, effectively removing global translational and rotational movements and thereby isolating intrinsic conformational dynamics. A covariance matrix was then constructed from the Cartesian positional fluctuations of the backbone atoms, and its subsequent diagonalization yielded a set of eigenvectors (principal components, PCs) along with their associated eigenvalues, which represent the directions and relative amplitudes of atomic motions, respectively. The first ten principal components captured over 85% of the total variance in atomic displacement, with PC1 and PC2 alone accounting for approximately 56.2% of the variance (PC1:34.5%, PC2:21.7%). This concentration of variance within the first few PCs indicates that the majority of significant conformational transitions can be effectively described within a low-dimensional subspace. Projection of the MD trajectory onto the PC1–PC2 plane (Figures a and a) revealed distinct clustering of conformational states, indicative of transitions between discrete metastable states sampled during the simulation. These observations suggest the presence of conformational substates relevant to the functional dynamics of the biomolecule. Furthermore, temporal analysis of the trajectory projected onto PC1 (Figures b and b) demonstrated a gradual shift in conformational space over time, consistent with large-scale structural transitions occurring during the simulation. Structural analysis of conformers located at the extrema of PC1 and PC2 revealed that the principal motions are predominantly associated with outward displacement of transmembrane helices and enhanced loop flexibility. Such motions are mechanistically significant and may correspond to key transitions involved in receptor activation, ligand modulation, or allosteric regulation.

3.5. Free Energy Landscape

To investigate the thermodynamic stability and conformational heterogeneity of the states identified through PCA, a free energy landscape (FEL) analysis was conducted by projecting the molecular dynamics trajectory onto the first two principal components (PC1 and PC2). The resulting two-dimensional FEL plot (Figures c and c) delineates the conformational space sampled by the system during the simulation, with local minima corresponding to energetically favorable, thermodynamically stable states. The landscape revealed two dominant energy basins, referred to as State A and State B, which are separated by a relatively shallow free energy barrier of approximately 2.5 kcal/mol. These basins represent distinct conformational ensembles accessed throughout the trajectory. State A, characterized by the lowest free energy, corresponds to the predominant structural population observed during the latter stages of the simulation, suggesting the stabilization of a functionally relevant conformation. Conversely, State B represents a higher-energy, metastable intermediate potentially linked to transient structural transitions, such as those involved in ligand ingress/egress or partial receptor activation. The 3D FEL Volcano plot visualization (Figures d and d) emphasizes the ruggedness of the conformational space sampled during the trajectory.

In addition to the primary basins, several minor energy wells were identified along the PC2 axis. These local minima likely reflect conformational substates within the broader dominant ensemble, possibly arising from flexible regions such as intracellular loops, transmembrane segments, or ligand-binding domains. Such microstates may play critical roles in dynamic processes, such as signal transduction, allosteric regulation, or effector recruitment.

3.7. Validation of Peptide Origin from the Human Gut Microbiome

To confirm the microbial origin of the peptides, a multistep bioinformatics pipeline was implemented. First, the peptide sequence was converted into the corresponding nucleotide sequence, and then genome blast was performed against the Integrated Microbial Genome database to get respective protein and nucleotide matches. Hits were considered microbial in origin only if no significant homology was detected in human or other eukaryotic proteomes, verified by additional BLASTp searches restricted to Homo sapiens. The obtained protein match was then validated using UNIPROT by querying in the NCBI nonredundant (nr) protein database using BLASTp with an E-value cutoff of 1 × 10–5, retaining hits with ≥90% sequence identity and ≥80% query coverage. To ensure specificity to microbial taxa, top hits were cross-referenced with taxonomic identifiers using the NCBI Taxonomy Browser. Then, the full-length amino acid sequence of the acquired microbial peptide-associated protein was analyzed using ExPasy’s Peptide Cutter to predict the cleavage patterns and assess whether the desired microbial peptide is released from the parent protein.

As represented in the infographic (Figure ), the nucleotide sequence of the microbial peptides Predencin and Paraexcin yielded two matches when blasted against the microbial genome database. Predencin showed a 100% match with dITP/XTP pyrophosphatase: CRIS 18C-A, and Paraexcin showed a match with Chaperone protein HtpG [] BIOML-A13. The protein and genome match obtained were validated in UNIPROT (ID: A0A379E6F1). Cleavage patterns of both the proteins showed that the candidate peptides Predencin and Paraexcin were being extracted from both the proteins. These results provide us with verification that these peptides indeed originate from the human gut microbiome.

4. Discussion

The N-terminal residues of a peptide ligand play an important role in the activation of G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs), particularly peptide-bound GPCRs, for a variety of reasons, including conformational change induction, contribution to ligand binding affinity, allosteric regulation, and stability. The peptide database Pepfunk consists of 234,852 peptides of variable length, and physicochemical criteria were screened for molecular docking with receptors. The N-terminal region of microbiome-derived peptides Predencin and Paraexcin demonstrated the conservation of critical residues and interactions within the binding pocket of GIPR and CRF1R during molecular docking. The role of N-terminal residues in initiating and stabilizing receptor–ligand interactions has been established previously; our analysis focused on the specific contacts made by this region of the peptide within the binding pocket and their contribution to the overall binding affinity and conformational stability of the complex. The highest-ranked binding poses of Predencin and Paraexcin demonstrated a favorable binding free energy, suggesting a strong interaction with the target receptor. N-terminal residue Tyr1 interacts with residue His361TM6 via a hydrogen bond in the TM6 region, which likely leads to additional interactions of the peptide with the receptor. The N-terminal hydrophobic residues Ala2 and Gly4 of the peptide engage with hydrophobic residues L386TM7 in the binding pocket, especially near TM7, which may reduce solvent exposure, contributing to the binding stability and the overall binding energy.

The molecular dynamics (MD) simulations provided critical insights into the stability and behavior of the protein–peptide complex over a 300 ns time. The simulation study shows the dynamic nature of interaction between Paraexcin–CRF1R; however, the complex remains stable through the duration of the simulation, as evidenced by the relatively low root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) values for both the protein and peptide, particularly in the N-terminal binding pocket.

For Predencin-bound GIPR, the RMSD plot shows various fluctuations throughout the simulation but appears stable toward the end of the simulation. A stable RMSD over time may indicate that the complex has attained structural stability.

In this study, we applied distance-based structural alignment to compare two protein conformations after MD simulations. We calculated the distances of key residues involved in the interactions between the peptide and the GPCR, between the post-MD conformation and the receptor conformation that activates the G-protein signaling pathway (Figures a and a). This approach allowed us to capture both global and local conformational changes between the structures, providing novel insights into the dynamic aspects of the formation of the GPCR–peptide complex. The distance-based method was particularly useful for identifying localized structural differences. For instance, we observed conserved residue–residue distances in the protein’s core (transmembrane domain), indicating that these regions remain stable even in the face of global conformational shifts. The movement of transmembrane region 6 observed during simulation is noteworthy, given the role it plays in receptor activation. Residues like His 361, Tyr 239, and Arg 199 in post-MD GIPR have less deviation at 220–225 ns, and similarly, His 198, Lys 351, Leu 260, and Tyr 195 in CRF1R showed structural similarity compared to that of corresponding residues in standard conformation at 100–125 ns.

Figures d and d clearly present that the TM6 helix is moving outward in both GPCRs and microbial peptide-bound receptors, whereas in the inactive conformation (Figures c and c), the TM6 helix is bent inward. The outward transition of TM6 in G protein-coupled receptors is one of the hallmark conformational shift coupled with receptor activation. This movement enables the receptor transition to an active state capable of interacting with molecular transducers. The subsiding deviation of residual distances may point to the possibility of the receptors undergoing G-protein signaling in the presence of the microbial peptides (Figures b and b). The binding free energy of the receptor–peptide complex, calculated using the MMPBSA method, provided quantitative insights into the interaction strength. The binding free energy was −25.3 kcal/mol, indicating a strong interaction between the peptide and the receptor. The MMPBSA analysis underscores the importance of hydrogen bonds and hydrophobic interactions in stabilizing the receptor–peptide complex. The strong binding affinity of Predencin and Paraexcin to GIPR and CRF1R suggests their potential role as agonists in the activation of the receptor. The key residues contributing to the binding energy were identified, including Trp154 and Lys152 in GIPR and Trp84, Arg151, and Arg293 in CRF1R. Modifications to these residues could lead to enhanced or reduced binding affinity, suggesting avenues for future peptide optimization studies.

PCA and FEL analyses served as complementary approaches to uncover the key dynamic and thermodynamic features of the system throughout the molecular dynamics (MD) simulation. By reducing the complexity of atomic motions into a set of orthogonal vectors, PCA effectively highlighted the dominant collective movements that often drive critical conformational changes related to protein function. The initial few principal components, particularly PC1 and PC2, captured a significant fraction of the overall motion, indicating that the essential dynamics are largely confined to a low-dimensional subspace. This dimensionality reduction not only simplifies interpretation but also enhances our ability to detect biologically meaningful motions. The trajectory projected along PC1 and PC2 revealed discrete conformational clusters, suggesting transitions between multiple metastable states over the simulation time scale. These findings point to the inherent flexibility of the protein and the presence of conformational substates that may underlie different functional roles.

Further structural mapping of the motions along PC1 and PC2 showed that these transitions are concentrated in specific regions of the protein such as transmembrane domains 6 and 7 and flexible loops (ICL) that are commonly associated with processes like ligand binding, allosteric control, or conformational switching. These patterns are consistent with known structural mechanisms, reinforcing the functional importance of the observed dynamics. To complement these observations, the FEL analysis provided a thermodynamic view of how the system explores conformational space. The two-dimensional FEL, projected along the primary principal components, identified distinct energy basins separated by modest barriers. The presence of two dominant minima-designated states A and B suggests that the protein alternates between stable and metastable conformations during the simulation. State A, representing the global free energy minimum, became increasingly populated in the latter stages, implying stabilization of a potentially active or functionally relevant conformation. State B, being higher in energy, may correspond to intermediate or transition-state conformations, possibly linked to functional motions such as ligand ingress or preactivation steps. Additional shallow wells along the PC2 axis point to the existence of microstates within the broader conformational ensemble, possibly arising from local flexibility in structural motifs like intracellular loops or helical kinks. These substates could be functionally relevant, offering the structural plasticity required for dynamic processes such as effector coupling or allosteric signal transmission. The three-dimensional FEL volcano plot further highlighted the rugged topography of the energy landscape, illustrating both the depth of the conformational wells and the energy barriers between them. The relatively low barrier (∼2.5 kcal/mol) separating states A and B suggests that such transitions are energetically accessible under physiological conditions, potentially facilitating the dynamic behavior necessary for biological activity.

In summary, the combined use of PCA and FEL analysis provides a robust framework for interpreting the conformational and energetic landscape of biomolecular systems. These approaches not only identify dominant structural transitions but also offer insights into the thermodynamic stability and accessibility of functional states. Such information is particularly valuable in the study of complex and dynamic proteins, including GPCRs, membrane transporters, and multidomain enzymes, where function is intricately linked to conformational flexibility. Native peptides that bind to G-protein-coupled receptors have significant therapeutic potential but with limitations like low stability and enzymatic degradation, bioavailability, short half-life, specificity, and off-targets. Microbial peptides, those derived from bacteria, fungi, and other microorganisms that reside in the human gut, have been reported to show proteolytic stability, chemical stability, improved bioavailability, and longer half-life, which suggests that microbial peptides are interesting therapeutic candidates for peptide-based drug developments with reduced side effects.

5. Conclusion

In this study, we explored the potential of human gut microbial peptides as mimicking modulators of secretin family receptors. The current work highlighted the intricate interplay between the gut microflora and host signaling pathways, demonstrating that microbial peptides could interact with and modulate the signaling of Class B GPCRs. These findings open new area into the structural and functional diversity of microbial-derived peptides with their ability to influence physiological processes, such as glucose homeostasis, appetite regulation, and hormonal responses. Furthermore, the computational approaches employed reveal key peptide sequences and receptor residues critical for binding and activation, paving the way for the rational design of therapeutic peptides. Future studies should delve deeper into in vivo functional assays and clinical investigations to validate these interactions and to explore their therapeutic applications. Harnessing the gut microbiome’s molecular repertoire may open new avenues for targeted treatments of metabolic and gastrointestinal disorders by leveraging the specificity and tunability of GPCR signaling.
