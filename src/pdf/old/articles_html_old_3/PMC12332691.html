Abstract

This study aims to synthesize semicarbazides and thiosemicarbazides and evaluate them for their biological potential. In the current study, the hydrazide was used as a precursor for the synthesis of semicarbazides (4a–c) and thiosemicarbazides (5a–f). The synthesized compounds were evaluated for their anticancer activity against neuroblastoma cells (IMR-32), malignant glioma cells (U87), and fibroblasts (NIH3T3) and antioxidant potential. Nitro-substituted semicarbazides 4c and 4d showed remarkable anticancer effects against the U87 cell line, with IC50 values of 12.6 and 13.7 μg/mL, respectively. Nevertheless, among thiosemicarbazides, 5d and 5b showed notable IC50 values of 13.0 and 14.6 μg/mL, respectively, against U87 cells. In the antioxidant assay, 4c and 4d showed significant % scavenging with IC50 values of 4.5 and 7.2 μg/mL, respectively, while 5d and 5b showed IC50 values of 5.6 and 7.0 μg/mL, respectively. Docking studies revealed that 4c, 4d, and 5d showed significant binding with the ATP-dependent enzyme topoisomerase II (IZXN) target, with binding energies of −8.1, −7.9, and −7.3 kcal/mol, respectively. ADME analysis indicated that these compounds possess substantial GIT absorption and moderate BBB penetration. These findings showed that synthesized compounds can be used as drug leads based on their anticancer, antioxidant, and in silico properties.

Introduction

Cancer is an incurable disease and the leading cause of death worldwide. Cancer is one of the most spreadable diseases due to its complex pathophysiology. Epidemiological data from the last 10 years project that there will be a 10-fold increase in the number of cancer-related fatalities in East and South Asian countries. A 62% rise in fatalities between 1990 and 2030 is reported in Pakistan. Many important genetic and molecular pathways are altered in cancer, making it a heterogeneous class of genetic diseases. The discovery of molecular targets was made through research on oncogenes and tumor suppressors, which are known to contribute to cancer progression and are currently being targeted more frequently in the treatment of metastatic cancer. Treatment plans are frequently very strict with standard chemotherapies, with many side effects and imparting drug resistance. Drug resistance, either inherent or acquired, can be associated with a variety of mechanisms, including tumor cell heterogeneity, drug efflux, metabolism, and alteration in stress-induced genes. These mechanisms alter the biological response of the drug when exposed to the tumor environment. One of these mechanisms is the cancer cell’s adaptation to the stress caused by anticancer drugs in the tumor microenvironment, which is a crucial factor in chemotherapy resistance. Some of these agents inhibit the growth of cells through inhibition of kinases in cancer-promoting pathways. Apoptosis and inhibition of angiogenesis are the two main mechanisms for inhibiting cancer growth through receptor inhibition and DNA intercalation. , Most of the synthetic derivatives reported against cancer cell lines work out through inhibition of angiogenesis by vascular endothelial growth factor receptor 2 (VEGFR 2) and apoptotic protein Bcl-2. Among the synthetic compounds that are used as chemotherapeutic agents belong to one of the most versatile classes of heterocyclic compounds, i.e., hydrazides and their derivatives. The presence of protons in hydrazides further strengthens their pharmaceutical importance after the discovery of isoniazid (hydrazide of isonicotinic acid) and hydralazine. Due to the exceptional clinical significance of isoniazid and hydralazine, researchers’ interest has developed in other hydrazide derivatives, including semicarbazides and thiosemicarbazides. The biological potential of these compounds is based on chemical structure due to the thione group, and they are typically synthesized through the nucleophilic addition reaction of hydrazides to substituted isocyanates and isothiocyanates. These derivatives have demonstrated a broad range of biological effects, including antifungal, antibacterial, antitubercular, anticonvulsant, antitumor, antioxidant, and antimalarial properties. These compounds specifically showed their anticancer effect through different processes, such as inhibiting the process of angiogenesis, inhibiting the key enzymes that promote cancer, and chelation of metal ion complexes of these compounds with specific proteins, ultimately leading to cancer cell death. These compounds are also involved in the apoptosis stimulation process in cancer cells and exhibit anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects. − Having potential pharmacological importance, nalidixic acid, an FDA-approved antimicrobial drug, has been derivatized into a thiosemicarbazide and has a significant anticancer effect, but it has not yet been approved. In Figure , some of the potential reported semicarbazides and thiosemicarbazides are mentioned. −

Therefore, in the present study, semicarbazides and thiosemicarbazides were synthesized and investigated for their anticancer and antioxidant potential. These compounds were also investigated for docking studies against alkaline phosphatase (PDB ID 1EW2), epidermal growth factor receptor (PDB ID: 1M17), ATP-dependent enzyme topoisomerase II (PDB ID: 1ZXN), and carbonic anhydrase (PDB ID: 3IAI). These compounds were also evaluated for their pharmacokinetic profile by using the SwissADMET Web server.

Results and Discussion

The synthesized compounds were evaluated for their anticancer and antioxidant potential for the first time, and they were synthesized by using two steps. In the first step, the ester was synthesized using the Fischer esterification method. Ester was used for the synthesis of hydrazide by using hydrazine monohydrate. Hydrazide was further reacted with different isocyanates and isothiocyanates in a 1:1 ratio, yielding semicarbazides and thiosemicarbazides, respectively. In this study, biologically active semicarbazides and thiosemicarbazides were synthesized with substitution at the proton of the primary amine group (–NH2) of hydrazide with different aromatic isocyanates and isothiocyanates. The synthesized compounds were confirmed by using a thin-layer chromatography (TLC) procedure by measuring the R f value differences and the melting point differences. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) was used for confirmation of functional groups, while 1H and 13C NMR spectroscopy was used further for complete structure elucidation of the final purified compounds. In FTIR spectral analysis, absorbance bands appeared at 3350–3200, 3150–3170 (–NH), 2860–2990 (–CH stretching), 1600–1680 (CO), 1040–1090 (CS), and 850–515 cm–1 (C-X, X = halogen). In 1H NMR, three characteristic singlets were observed in the range of 10.49–8.92 ppm for CONH, NH, and NH, which confirms the synthesis of the product. In 13C NMR, the carbon of the methyl group appears in the region of 21.45–21.60 ppm, while the carbonyl carbon attached to the –NH gives a peak at 181.63–166.63 ppm, and that of the carbonyl carbon or thionyl carbon of cynate attached to –NH present a peak at 156.01–166.35 ppm. The distinctive peaks verified the formation of semicarbazides and thiosemicarbazides.

Anticancer Assay

Nitro-substituted semicarbazides 4c and 4d at positions 2 and 3, respectively, showed less % cell viability against the U87 cell line but were not active against IMR-32 cells. Both compounds 4c and 4d showed % cell viability of 27 and 31% against the U87 cell line, respectively (Figure ). Semicarbazide 4b, chloro-substituted at position 3, showed comparatively the same results in both cell lines. Among thiosemicarbazides, 4-nitro-substituted compound 5d and 3-chloro-substituted compound 5b showed 35 and 32% cell viability against the U87 cell line, respectively. All of these compounds were used for the determination of IC50 values. Compounds 4c, 4d, 5b, and 5d showed less than 30% cell viability and were further proceeded to calculate IC50. Compounds 4c and 4d showed IC50 of 12.66 and 13.71 μg/mL, while 5b and 5d showed IC50 of 14.63 and 13.09 μg/mL against U87 cells, respectively (Figure ). It was also observed that compounds 4c and 5d showed 75–80% cell viability against NIH3T3 (Figure ).

Previously reported semicarbazide- and thiosemicarbazide-based compounds have an effect on the angiogenesis process of tumor cells by inhibiting key enzymes involved in the process of angiogenesis, such as topoisomerases, ATPases, and ribonucleotide reductase enzyme. Another study reported the metal complexes of semicarbazides and thiosemicarbazides involved in chelation with the proteins involved in tumorogenesis. , These compounds also have anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects, which are responsible for the stimulation of apoptosis in cancer cells, − ultimately damaging the DNA of cancer cells, changing the gene expression, and leading to its death. In the current study, nitro-substituted semicarbazides 4c and 4d showed anticancer effects with IC50 of 12.6 and 13.7 μg/mL, respectively, against the U87 cell line. Comparatively, among thiosemicarbazides, 5d and 5b also showed comparable results with IC50 values of 13.0 and 14.6 μg/mL, respectively. However, compounds did not exhibit significant results against the IMR-32 cell line. These compounds also showed moderate % cell viability and are less toxic to the normal cell line. All semicarbazides showed more than 60% cell viability; however, thiosemicarbazides also showed more than 55% cell viability against NIH3T3. However, compounds 4c and 5d showed more than 75% cell viability. It was reported that the anticancer effect of semicarbazides and thiosemicarbazides is due to oxygen, nitrogen, and sulfur atoms. In the diaryl urea moiety, the binding of the aromatic ring attached to the amide nitrogen with enzymatic pockets results in inhibition of different tumors. Indole-based semicarbazide showed the anticancer effect against human brain carcinoma (U251) and MCF-7 cell lines. It is reported that thiosemicarbazides can act as iron and copper ion chelators in cancer cells due to nitrogen and sulfur atoms, which results in a promising anticancer effect. Asif and Algamdi reported a series of nitro-substituted thiosemicarbazides with significant anticancer activity against the human neuroblastoma (SK-N-MC) cell line. In another study, Walczak-Nowicka and his co-workers reported the cytotoxic activity of cyclohexyl and naphthyl thiosemicarbazides against the glioblastoma cell line. The naphthyl-based compound showed a potent reduction in cell viability with an IC50 value of 0.27 μmol. The cyclohexyl-based compound showed a significant reduction in proliferation inhibition with an IC50 value of 43.31 μM.

Antioxidant Assay

Semicarbazides (4a–e) and thiosemicarbazides (5a–f) were analyzed for their antioxidant potential. Semicarbazides and thiosemicarbazides showed significant antioxidant activity when compared with the standard (Figure ). Nitro-substituted compounds (4c, 4d, and 5d) showed excellent % scavenging activity with IC50 values of 4.5, 7.2, and 5.6 μg/mL, respectively, while 3-chloro-substituted thiosemicarbazide (5b) showed % scavenging with an IC50 value of 7 μg/mL (Figure ). Compounds 4c and 4d showed significant % scavenging with IC50 values of 4.5 and 7.2 μg/mL, respectively, while 5d and 5b showed IC50 values of 5.6 and 7.0 μg/mL, respectively. The results of current studies correlated with previous reports of Sazeli and his colleagues reported the dichloro-substituted semicarbazides for their significant % scavenging activity with an IC50 value of 33.46 μM. However, naphthyl-substituted thiosemicarbazide showed an IC50 value of 25.47 μM. In another study, it was stated that sulfide-linked 3,4,5-trimethoxyphenyl-substituted thiosemicarbazides showed % scavenging with an IC50 of 21.63 μM. Krátký and his co-workers also reported that 4-NO2-substituted semicarbazides showed moderate antioxidant activity. 3-Nitro-substituted thiosemicarbazide displayed excellent iron chelating activity and hydroxyl radical scavenging potential, with IC50 values of 27.51 ± 1.16 and 28.38 ± 1.36 μM, respectively.

Molecular Docking Studies

Molecular docking studies were performed against four targets, and their binding energies were calculated (Table ). Compounds 4c and 4d showed strong binding energies (−8.1 and −7.5 kcal/mol) with the amino acid of the IZXN target. The hydrogen of hydrazine in compounds 4c (Figure ) and 4d (Figure ) formed hydrogen bonding with glutamine, and the nitro group of the compound showed van der Waals interaction with lysine and phenylalanine amino acid. Among thiosemicarbazides, 5b and 5d showed binding energies of −6.9 and −7.3 kcal/mol, respectively, against the IZXN target (Table ). Compound 4c has excellent binding affinity with a binding energy of −8.1 kcal/mol; the oxygen atom of the carbonyl group forms a robust hydrogen bond with the amino acid residues GLY161 and the magnesium ion. Additionally, the nitrogen atom of hydrazine and the phenyl ring exhibited π–anion interactions with amino acid GLU87. However, compound 4d also showed good binding affinity with the IZXN enzyme target, as shown in Figure . The nitro substitution on the phenyl ring showed π–π interactions with amino acids ILS141 and PHE 142. The oxygen atom of the carbonyl and the hydrogen atom of hydrazine formed a hydrogen bond and π–anion interactions with GLU87. These findings correlated with the work of Asif and Alghamdi, who reported the in silico interaction of 4-benzoyl-1-(4-methyl-imidazole-5-yl)-carbonylthio-semicarbazide and 4-benzoyl-1-(indol-2-yl)-carbonyl-thiosemicarbazide against the IZXN target. They observed that preferential binding in the ATP binding pocket is due to the π–π interactions of the phenyl ring. The overall results demonstrated that semicarbazides and thiosemicarbazides showed significant anticancer and antioxidant potential, which can be used as drug leads and for further drug development.

ADMET Pharmacokinetic Evaluation

ADMET pharmacokinetic studies play an important role in drug discovery and drug development. Ideal lead candidates are those that are well absorbed in biological systems and produce the pharmacological action, with minimal side effects. The SwissADME database Web server was used for the evaluation of the pharmacokinetic profile of the synthesized compounds (Tables and ). It was predicted that all of the compounds would show high GIT absorption and skin permeability. All of the synthesized compounds also permeate moderately in the brain.

All of the synthesized compounds showed good gastrointestinal absorption, and all of them had blood–brain barrier crossing potential. Experimental Section

Chemicals and Equipment

All chemicals (p-toluic acid and hydrazine monohydrate), isocyanates and isothiocyanates (purity ≥ 98%), and solvents (methanol, toluene, n-hexane, and ethyl acetate, purity ≥ 99.5%) used in the study were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich and Alfa Aesar by Thermo Fisher Scientific (MA). The melting point was measured by using the Gallenkamp melting point equipment, Gallenkamp, Stuart digital MP (SMP-10) apparatus, Cambridge, U.K., using capillary tubes. Aluminum plates were used to perform TLC analysis. For 1H NMR and 13C NMR spectroscopic analysis, an AvanceTM III HD NanoBay 400 MHz NMR spectrometer (MA), at COMSATS University Islamabad, Abbottabad Campus, Pakistan, was used, and dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO-d6) was used as the solvent. FTIR analysis was conducted using a Shimadzu FTIR-8400S spectrometer. GraphPad Prism software version 5 was utilized for the determination of statistical analysis. All readings were measured in triplicate. One-way ANOVA was used for the level of significance, followed by Dunnett’s t-test.

General Procedure for the Synthesis of Hydrazide

Acyl hydrazide of p-toluic acid was synthesized by the Fisher esterification method. − Methanol (30 mL) was taken to dissolve p-toluic acid (1), and conc. H2SO4 (2 mL) was added slowly and refluxed for 6–7 h at 75 °C in a round-bottom flask. The progress of the reaction was monitored by using TLC. After completion of the reaction, the mixture was allowed to stand for 10 min, and the solvent was evaporated by using a rotary evaporator. A sufficient amount of dichloromethane was used to dissolve the powder obtained, and this organic layer was washed three times with a saturated solution of sodium bicarbonate. Moisture was removed from the organic layer by passing anhydrous magnesium carbonate and further dried using a rotary evaporator. An ester was obtained (2) and used for the synthesis of hydrazide. Methanol was used to dissolve the ester and reacted with 2–3 equiv of hydrazine monohydrate through 5 h of reflux at 70 °C. The solid residue obtained was further washed with 50% methanol to get pure hydrazide.

Anticancer Assay

Cell Lines and Culture Medium

The neuroblastoma cell line (IMR-32), fibroblast normal cell line (NIH3T3), and malignant glioma cell line (U87) were procured from IRCBM, CUI Lahore campus, and NUST Islamabad, Pakistan, respectively. The cells were cultured in Roswell Park Memorial Institute medium (RPMI) supplemented with 10% heat-inactivated fetal calf serum and 0.1% antibiotic solution (penicillin and streptomycin) at 37 °C in humidified air containing 5% CO2 in an incubator.

In Vitro Anticancer Activity

The anticancer activity of the samples was performed against IMR-32, U87, and NIH3T3 cells by using the 3-(4,5-dimethylthiazolyl-2)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT) assay, and doxorubicin was used as a positive control. The cytotoxic effect of these compounds was also evaluated by using a normal cell line, i.e., fibroblast. In a 96-well plate, the cells were seeded and incubated for 24 h to get a uniform layer of cells. Methanol was used to dissolve the samples and then diluted with medium to obtain the required concentrations. The assay was performed in triplicate and incubated at 37 °C in a CO2 incubator for 48 h. After completion of treatment with samples, in each well, 30 μL of MTT solution was added and incubated for 4 h at 37 °C. After incubation, 100 μL of DMSO was added. By using a 96-well plate reader, the absorbance of formazan crystals was measured at 570 nm, and % viability was calculated. All of the compounds were first screened at 50 μg/mL concentration with all cell lines, and compounds with less than 30% cell viability were proceeded to further concentrations for calculating their IC50.

Antioxidant Assay

The free radical scavenging ability of the synthesized compounds was tested by the 2,2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH) radical scavenging assay. Ascorbic acid was used as a standard. In methanolic solution, the DPPH produces a violet color and fades to a yellow color in the presence of powerful antioxidants. Solution of DPPH (0.1 mM) and different concentrations of the synthesized compounds were prepared in methanol. In a 96-well plate, the synthesized compounds and DPPH solution were added, and this mixture was incubated for 30 min and analyzed at 517 nm by using a plate reader. The following formula was used to calculate % FRSA and was plotted against concentration to determine IC50 values. At each concentration, the experiment was repeated three times.

%FRSA=Abs of control−Abs of test compoundAbs of control×100

Here, % FRSA is the percent free radical scavenging activity, and Abs is the absorption.

Molecular Docking

A molecular docking study was carried out for the synthesized compounds by Molecular Operating Environment software (MOE) 2019.0102. Different enzymes including alkaline phosphatase (PDB ID 1EW2), epidermal growth factor receptor (PDB ID: 1M17), ATP-dependent enzyme topoisomerase II (PDB ID: 1ZXN), and carbonic anhydrase (PDB ID: 3IAI) were used as targets for docking. ChemDraw Ultra 12.0 software was used to draw chemical structures, and the MMFF-94 force field was applied for energy minimization in MOE. By using the refinement features, docking was performed, and the best triangular induced receptor pose was selected. The optimal pose showed a minimum binding energy.

Conclusions

In the current study, the newly synthesized semicarbazides and thiosemicarbazides showed significant antioxidant and anticancer effects against neuroblastoma cell lines. 4c, 4d, 5b, and 5d showed good anticancer activity in both brain cell lines in comparison with their docking results. However, most of the compounds are less cytotoxic to the fibroblast cell line. ADMET predicted scores showed the drug-likeness properties of these compounds, as these compounds crossed the BBB, supporting the anticancer effect of these compounds. These synthesized compounds can be investigated for their advanced studies and may be used as lead candidates in the future.
