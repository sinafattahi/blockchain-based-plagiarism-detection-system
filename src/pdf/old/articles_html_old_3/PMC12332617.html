Abstract

Natural rubber (NR) composites reinforced with fabrics were prepared through compression molding by sandwiching a layer of fabric between two layers of NR. A range of composite properties were assessed, including adhesion and mechanical-thermal behavior. Cross-linked NR reinforced with silk fabric had better mechanical properties compared to the same NR reinforced with nylon fabric. Furthermore, silk fabric reinforced NR had a lower dissipation energy. This study combined material characterization with pilot-scale manufacturing of racing bicycle tires. It compared commercial nylon-reinforced tires with tires reinforced using silk fabric. The silk-reinforced tire performed better than the conventional nylon-reinforced tire in durability (129%), puncture resistance (157%), and cord tensile strength (243%). In addition, life cycle assessment (LCA) revealed significant environmental advantages of silk-reinforced tires, with reductions of over 80% in global warming potential (GWP), abiotic depletion of fossil resources, ozone depletion potential (ODP), and photochemical oxidation potential (POP) compared to nylon-reinforced tires. These results highlight silk fabric as a sustainable, high-performance alternative for commercial-scale, energy-saving racing bicycle tire applications.

1. Introduction

Natural rubber (NR) has several outstanding characteristics including its high resilience, low heat hysteresis, and good adhesion, as well as high mechanical and tear resistance properties. − NR is a biobased rubber used to make products such as tires, gloves, and foams. − During production, various additives (vulcanizing agents, fillers, etc.) are used to meet specified requirements. − Even though a variety of curing agents are available, sulfur curing methods are universally employed to vulcanize NR. − Mountain biking represents a famous exercise and leisure action. It requires a bicycle made of sturdy parts that can handle off-road riding in mud, gravel, unpaved surfaces, and other

terrain. − Understanding factors, such as tire pressure and force, is crucial to increase bicycle handling, comfort, and safety. For example, knowledge of road friction enables improved trajectory formulation and consequently better vehicle control management. − In the case of product quality, NR latex contains nonrubber components such as proteins and lipids. These components affect the color of rubber products. Then, color is a crucial factor when evaluating the quality of NR. Therefore, a variety of techniques (i.e., physical and chemical processes) were used to eliminate the nonrubber components, which led to a reduction in color. The light color of NR provides higher quality and economic value.

Natural fibers are gaining popularity in the production of green materials due to their favorable mechanical qualities. − The most common reinforcements for rubber composite products, such as tires, are synthetic polyester and nylon, and sometimes natural cotton. Surajarusarn et al. determined the effects of aramid and pineapple leaf fiber (PALF) in reinforcing rubber composites. Their results showed that (PALF) at 10 phr exhibited distinctly better reinforcement than aramid fibers at both 25 and 60 °C. Gon et al. used jute fiber in rubber composites because of its high mechanical strength. Coating jute fibers with resorcinol-formaldehyde resin was successful in improving the reinforcing characteristics of these fibers. The modulus of a rubber composite was improved by 40–60%. Ude et al. studied the filament size effects on the characteristics of silk. Silk fibers had higher tensile and breaking strengths than polyester fibers. Adding a lignin filler in NR compound composites greatly impacted their chemical and morphological structures. Intapun et al. reported that Klason lignin at 10 phr increased both the mechanical and aging-resistance of a Klason lignin/rubber composite. This type of lignin from rubber wood was also a highly effective antioxidant in the composite.

Smitthipong et al. studied natural fabric (silk) as reinforcement in non-cross-linked NR to prepare a biobased material. Their results showed that silk/NR composites had better mechanical strength than nylon/NR composites. Furthermore, silk fabric in whole NR composites had a much higher mechanical strength than purified NR composites because of interactions between proteins from the whole NR and those of silk. The reinforcement mechanism of silk fabric within the NR matrix is primarily attributed to protein-based interactions. Silk fibroin contains abundant polar functional groups, such as amine (−NH2), carboxyl (−COOH), and hydroxyl (−OH) groups, which can form hydrogen bonds with polar sites or oxygen-containing groups present in natural rubber chains. In addition, van der Waals interactions between the nonpolar backbone of silk proteins and the hydrocarbon backbone of NR can further contribute to the interfacial compatibility. These interactions synergistically enhance stress transfer efficiency across the silk/NR interface, supporting the improved mechanical performance observed in silk-reinforced rubber composites.

Mayank et al. investigated using a silk composite material in aircraft applications. Spider silk had exceptional mechanical capabilities, making it an attractive fiber for composites. Duan et al. studied silk fabric in silk fibroin composites, their results demonstrated that the composite had a 70% lower weight and was resistant to degradation after 168 h. Sogawa et al. studied silk-reinforcing in NR composites, obtained by preparing a mixture of degummed silk with a natural latex solution. Silk reinforced rubber composites showed increased mechanical strength and toughness. Yang et al. studied natural silk reinforcement to increase the toughness and stiffness of epoxy–resin composites over those of a pure epoxy resin. Although these studies highlight the potential of silk fibers to improve the static mechanical properties of polymer composites, they have rarely addressed the dynamic performance required for tire applications, such as rolling resistance, wet grip, and energy dissipation under repeated cyclic loading. Mathew et al. revealed relationship between material’s dynamic properties and actual tire usage performance, for example, tan δ@60 °C (rolling resistance of tire) and tan δ@0 °C (wet traction of tire).

The current research aimed to develop an optimal rubber compound formulation by varying the amount of carbon black in three different vulcanization systems. The sulfur level and the ratio of accelerator to sulfur (A/S) were varied. This work focused on using natural materials (silk fabric) to reinforce NR composites and replace synthetic materials such as nylon fabric. Our study also aimed to develop and test silk/NR composites in bicycle tires and report these results for the first time in a scientific journal. Cross-linked rubber composites containing silk and nylon fabrics were used to manufacture bicycle tires on a pilot scale, enabling production of the first technical data for this application. Subsequently, the performance of the developed bicycle tire with silk fabric reinforcement was compared to commercial bicycle tires. The current work can be applied to develop a new type of racing bicycle tire or a high-performance bicycle tire.

2. Experimental Section

2.1. Materials

The materials and chemicals in the current study are presented in Table S1. Silk fabric and nylon fabrics were plain-woven and had similar yarn counts (Table S2). Additionally, the degummed and nondyed silk fabric represented a slightly higher denier than the nylon fabric (900 versus 840, respectively). Commercial bicycle tires (660.40 mm diameter × 44.45 mm width) were purchased from six suppliers in Bangkok, Thailand for comparison with tires developed in the present study.

2.2. Preparation of Composites on a Laboratory Scale

2.2.1. NR Compound Preparation

A two-roll mill (S­(X)­K-160A; Shanghai, China) was used to prepare the NR composites based on the formulations in Table . Briefly, the NR was masticated for 1 min. After that, all the chemicals were added, until a homogeneous dispersion was attained to produce vulcanized substances. Mixing required approximately 10 min to produce the NR compound. The rubber compounds were allowed to cool at room temperature before they were used in composite preparation.

2.2.2. Preparation of a Resorcinol Formaldehyde Latex (RFL) Solution

The RFL formulation consisted of an emulsion of rubber latex with resorcinol and formaldehyde solutions, as shown in Table S3. This solution can be coated on fabric surfaces to facilitate adhesion to rubber compounds. The preparation process consists of three steps. First, resorcinol was mixed with water. Next, sodium hydroxide was added, followed by formaldehyde (as a condensation catalyst). A continuous stirring process was performed for 6 h at ambient temperature. During the process, a partial condensation reaction took place. Next, the resin solution was mixed into latex. Finally, this RFL solution was allowed to age for at least 24 h before use.

2.2.3. Preparation of Cross-Linked Rubber Composites

Two rubber sheets sandwiched a layer of fabric-coated RFL under compression molding at 150 °C for 5 min. A 1 mm thick sandwiched sample was obtained, two types of composite samples were prepared, silk/NR and nylon/NR (Figure ).

2.3. Preparation of High-Performance Bicycle Tires at a Pilot Scale

2.3.1. Coating Fabric with Resorcinol Formaldehyde Latex (RFL)

Degummed and undyed raw silk (Nang noi variety) was utilized. RFL was applied to the fabric using a glue laminating machine, as shown in Figure . The fabric was immersed and moved through an RFL adhesive bath at 5 m/min. This setup was used to coat silk and nylon fabrics with RFL at 80 °C for 5 min. It included a rolling set for the fabric, a bath for coating RFL, an oven with an infrared heater, a set for inserting the RFL-coated fabric, a set for inserting the fabric rolls to keep the glue-coated fabric from sticking, and a motor to power the entire apparatus. The silk or nylon roll set was covered with RFL adhesive. Before the fabric was wrapped with a cloth to protect it from sticking, it was completely dried prior to placing it on a calender machine for at least 24 h before subsequent storage.

2.3.2. Preparation of Rubber Compounds

A larger sample formulation, with optimal characteristics from Table , was prepared using an 80 L Banbury mixer (X­(S)­M-80/40; Dalian, China). First, the STR20 ingredients were masticated for 60 s. Then, a ram was raised for 6 s to add all the chemicals followed by a further 130 s of mastication. Next, the ram was raised to allow the rubber compound to become more uniformly distributed. Then, the ram was used to mix the rubber compound for another 15 s, ensuring product uniformity.

2.3.3. Process for Coating Fabric with Rubber Compounds in a Calender Machine

First, the rubber compound was placed in an extruder (XJD-120; Guilin, China) at 80 °C at a screw speed of 27.3 rpm, to obtain rubber sheets. Then, two rubber sheets sandwiched the RFL-coated fabric in a calender machine (XY-3I-1730; Dalian, China) at 90 °C, with a drive speed of 28 rpm, to obtain a sandwiched composite (0.6–0.7 mm thick).

2.3.4. Molded High-Performance Bicycle Tires

The product consisted of the tire surface, wire, and rubber-coated fabric (the carcass). The tire was assembled using a tire construction machine (FT-B/C; Changhua, Taiwan), with a drum front width of 105 mm. Tire manufacturing commenced with a layer of rubber-coated fabric. Orientation of the fabric along the circumference of the tire was 50°. Then, the wire was placed along the left and right wire grooves and the wings were folded. The final step was to place the top layer of the tread, flattened the joints of the rubber sheets, and tightly attach the lot number showing the production date.

2.3.5. Bicycle Tire Curing Process

The rubber was cured in a BOM A-condition rubber dryer (40–34 MI; Changhua, Taiwan). Internal tire curing was done using steam at 10 kg/cm2 for 40 s. After that, nitrogen gas at 11 kg/cm2 was used for 8 min until the process was completed. An external heat source (hot oil) was used to warm the mold.

2.4. Characterization of Rubber Composites

Mooney viscometer was used to determine the Mooney viscosity of the rubber compound (Gotech; MV-3000; Guangdong, China) according to ISO 289–1. The test was done at 100 °C with a rotor, a heat-aging time of 1 min and a 4 min trying out time (based on the formula ML (1 + 4) at 100 °C) and then samples were prepared (approximately 34 ± 3 g). Sample properties were investigated with a moving die rheometer (Gotech; M-3000A; Guangdong, China) following ISO 6502:1991.

The surface characteristics of samples were evaluated through the contact angle of a known liquid droplet on the sample surface using an Ossila L2004A1 (Sheffield, England). A sample was put onto a clean substrate and a 30 μL aliquot of deionized water droplets was dispensed using a micropipette. A contact angle goniometer (Ossila; L2004A1; Sheffield, England) was used to film the droplet as it landed on the substrate.

Scanning electron microscopy (SEM; FEI; Quanta 450; Eindhoven, The Netherlands), thermogravimetric analysis (TGA/DSC3+; Mettler Toledo; Schwerzenbach, Switzerland), and attenuated total reflection-Fourier transform infrared (ATR-FTIR; VERTEX 70; Bruker, Germany) were performed as details in a previous work.

Uniaxial tensile testing (Gotech; AI-7000 S; Taichung, Taiwan) was performed on dumbbell-shaped samples (according to ASTM D412) with a 500 mm/min crosshead speed using a load cell of 1 kN.

H-test uniaxial tensile testing (Gotech; AI-7000 S; Taichung, Taiwan) was done to study adhesion between the fabric and the cross-linked rubber following ISO 4647. The samples were prepared by compressing 1 yarn of fabric with a rubber compound in a mold.

The 180° peeling test, following ASTM 413 and employing uniaxial tensile testing (Gotech; AI-7000 S; Taichung, Taiwan), was used to evaluate the separation force of fabrics adhering to the NR composite. The samples were prepared by compressing the fabric with a rubber compound in a mold.

Dynamic mechanical analysis (DMA; Mettler Toledo; Columbus, OH) in tension mode was done from −80 to 80 °C. Moreover, DMA was also conducted in shear mode with a strain sweep of 1 to 200 μm at 30 °C. Each rubber composite sample was cut into slices that were approximately 1 mm thick and 2 mm × 2 mm in the lateral dimensions.

2.5. Characterization of Bicycle Tires

Endurance testing was conducted to evaluate durability according to TIS 571–2019. Each sample was assembled by mounting the outer tire and inner tube onto a wheel. The inner tube was then inflated to 36 psi to support a maximum test load of 85 kg. Afterward, the sample was subjected to the full test load and run at a constant speed of 40 km/h until visible damage appeared on the tire.

Resistance to puncture (via plunger testing) was done according to TIS 571–2528. Each tire was mounted on a wheel, inflated to 50 psi at ambient temperature for 24 h. Then, the outer tire was subjected to indentation using a cylindrical steel rod (8.0 ± 0.1 mm diameter). Pressure was applied on the tread as close to the center line as possible at a 50 mm/min loading rate with a load of not less than 6.86 N.m.

Adhesion between the tire tread and the cord was tested according to TIS 571–2528. Each test sample was cut from a bicycle tire along the warp line, measuring 15 mm in width and 100 mm in length. Three pieces were obtained from each strip, which were then sanded smooth. After sanding, the samples were carefully separated between the canvas and rubber layers into two pieces, each approximately 10 mm in length.

Cord tensile strength testing was done according to TIS 571–2528. Each test piece was cut along the line of the tire cord (width 10 mm, length 140 mm) from 1 layer of canvas. Then, the cord was separated into strips.

The environmental effects of generating 1 kg of tire material were assessed using a gate-to-gate life cycle assessment (LCA), employing a methodological approach akin to that described by Yangthong et al. though that study focused on tires blended with ZnO and fly ash, whereas the present study examines silk- and nylon-reinforced composites. For all computations in the LCA model, the functional unit of 1 kg of tire was chosen as the reference foundation. The scope of the LCA was expanded to include a typical local tire-producing facility in Thailand. The general in-factory processing of the raw materials, as indicated by the quantity of each ingredient required for mixing to compounding as indicated in Table , was included in the system boundaries. The system boundary began at the point where raw materials entered the plant (“from the gate”) and ended with the production of the tire (“to the next gate”). The gate-to-gate LCA predicted the effects within the factory limits; upstream extraction and downstream use or disposal were not taken into account in this research. The LCA was conducted using openLCA (version 2.3, GreenDelta) with data from the ELCD 3.2 and ecoinvent 3.10 databases, in accordance with the principles of ISO 14040. Table S1 lists the physical components used in tire manufacturing, namely natural rubber, carbon black, and silk fabric; these were included as material flow inputs in the LCA. This gate-to-gate assessment focused exclusively on material inputs, excluding energy flows such as thermal and electrical energy.

3. Results and Discussion

The researchers used different rubber chemical formulations to prepare composite samples, as shown in Table . Additionally, the performances of the developed high-performance bicycle tire were compared with those of commercial bicycle tires.

3.1. Characterization of Reinforced Rubber Composites

3.1.1. Viscosity

Mooney viscosity indicates the flowability or deformation of a rubber compound. The CB50/CV sample had the highest Mooney viscosity, while other samples had similar Mooney viscosities (Table S5). This was because the carbon black in the rubber compound displaced the molecular chains of the rubber, making them resistant to motion, resulting in rubber compounds with increased Mooney viscosities (for CB50/CV). However, changing the vulcanization system of the samples did not affect the Mooney viscosity of the rubber compounds.

3.1.2. Curing Behavior and Vulcanization Characteristics of Rubber Composites

The curing properties of the samples are given in Table S5. Scorch time (t s2) is related to the start of vulcanization, while curing time (t c90) indicates the curing time at 90% vulcanization. In the current study, the semiefficient vulcanized system provided a safer scorch time and reduced the length of the production process because the samples were more resistant to heat deterioration than with the conventional vulcanization system (Figure S1). Additionally, the CB50/CV formulation with higher carbon black concentration resulted in the shortest scorch time. This is because sulfur on carbon black surfaces produced faster scorch and vulcanization times.

3.1.3. Hydrophobicity and Surface Interaction between Fabrics and Rubber

A liquid will have little contact with the surface and will form a dew-drop shape. Typically, fabrics are relatively more hydrophilic while rubber compounds are hydrophobic. In the cases where the liquid is watery, a well-dispersed surface is hydrophilic and a poorly dispersed surface is hydrophobic. Contact angle measurements of the rubber compounds, RFL-coated silk fabric, and RFL-coated nylon fabric, were evaluated. We found that all the contact angles were similar. RFL improved the hydrophobic surfaces of both types of fabric, making them more like (and compatible with) the rubber compound (Table S6 and Figure ). The contact angle of the silk fabric without RFL-coating was around 74°, so silk fabrics were relatively more hydrophilic.

3.1.4. SEM Analysis of Fiber Fracture and Interfacial Morphology in Rubber Composites

SEM image of cryo-fractured composites is shown in Figure . A cross-linked composite was prepared by compression of two rubber sheets sandwiching fabric. There were almost no voids between the rubber-RFL-coated silk and the rubber-RFL-coated nylon composites, indicating good fabric wetting within the rubber matrix. Thus, the adhesion between the silk and nylon fabrics with rubber could be improved by RFL. This agrees with the previous work for non-cross-linked NR. Our earlier work showed that uncoated silk and nylon reinforcements had more interfacial voids with NR when a RFL coating was not used, even though uncoated silk offers relatively better adhesion than uncoated nylon.

In Figure (a), the fractured silk fabric appears as a cluster of fine fibers with irregular and uneven fracture surfaces. The fiber ends show varying orientations and surface textures, indicating nonuniform fracture across the bundle. This may be attributed to the natural variability in silk’s microstructure and the complex stress distribution that occurs within woven fabrics during fracture. The irregularity could reflect how the fibers in the silk weave respond differently to applied stress, although this cannot be confirmed without higher magnification or further imaging.

In Figure (b), the nylon fabric bundle also shows multiple fibers but with a notably different morphology. The nylon fibers have larger diameters than the silk fibers and exhibit clean, smooth fracture surfaces with well-defined edges and consistent geometry across the bundle. This suggests that the nylon fibers fractured in a more uniform and possibly brittle manner. The consistency may result from the synthetic origin and more homogeneous internal structure of nylon compared to silk.

These differences in fiber diameter and fracture morphology between silk and nylon woven fabrics highlight how fiber type and internal structure influence failure behavior in rubber composites. In particular, the finer silk fibers imply a higher aspect ratio than the nylon fibers, assuming similar fiber lengths. A higher aspect ratio is generally associated with greater stress transfer efficiency during both elastic and plastic deformation stages, which can lead to higher composite tensile strength. As shown in Figure , the natural rubber composite reinforced with silk fabric exhibits higher tensile strength than the nylon-reinforced counterpart, suggesting a role of fiber aspect ratio in enhancing composite strength. The tensile strength results will be discussed further in a later section.

3.1.5. EDX Analysis and Molecular Characteristics of Silk and Nylon Reinforcements

The chemical composition of the silk and nylon fabrics determined from EDX spectrometry is given in Table S4. Generally, silk consists of fibroin and sericin proteins. Fibroin mainly contains the amino acids as serine (12%), alanine (30%), and glycine (43%). Figure shows the EDX results for silk, where the chemical composition consists of amino acids, such as glycine and alanine, with carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen contents of 50.45, 19.04, and 30.51% by weight, respectively. Nylon is a polyamide that may be generated through condensation polymerization. It contains an amide group [–NH­(CH2)5(CO)–] repeating in a semicrystalline molecular chain. The peptide units (NH–CO) allow for hydrogen bonding between polymer chains, resulting in the nylon structure. Figure also shows the EDX results for nylon, with the chemical components consisting of carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen at 65.76, 14.76, and 19.48% by weight, respectively. Silk proteins can interact well with other NR proteins since silk has more nitrogen and oxygen atoms than nylon.

3.1.6. TGA and DTG Analysis of Reinforced Rubber Composites

Thermal stability investigations (TGA and DTG thermograms) were performed using the composite samples. Weight changes of the materials with varying temperatures were measured using a highly sensitive balance (Figure S2). Silk/NR and nylon/NR composites were heated at 10 °C/min. Thermogravimetry revealed a transition of the reinforced fabric composites in the NR at 30–700 °C. The thermal properties of both composite samples were similar when the reinforcing fabric’s weight ratio was 5%, influenced mainly by the rubber compound. Composite material samples with low fiber contents showed little differences in their thermal properties.

3.1.7. FTIR Spectroscopy of Silk/NR and Nylon/NR Composites

ATR-FTIR spectrometric results showing the chemical structure of both composites are given in Figures S3 and S4. The wavenumber positions at 2963, 2921, and 2850 cm–1 were corresponded to stretching vibrations of the CH3, CH2, and CH groups, respectively. The wavenumber at 1539 cm–1 was attributed to the stretching vibrations of CO groups from zinc stearate in the rubber formulation. At a 1448 cm–1 wavenumber, vibrations of CH groups from the NR appeared. Additionally, the 1371 cm–1 wavenumber indicated the asymmetric stretching vibrations of the methyl groups (−CH3) in the NR structure and a wavenumber position of 834 cm–1 indicated bending of the C–H bonds in NR. However, there were no differences in the spectra of the composite rubber reinforced with silk and nylon fabrics. This occurs since the composite was most influenced by the NR matrix.

3.1.8. Adhesion Strength and Fiber–Rubber Interaction in Reinforced Composites

The H-test was used to investigate the interaction between the fibers and NR. Higher H-test values indicate greater interactions between the fibers and NR. Figure shows that the H-test value of the silk was higher than that of nylon. This occurred because the silk structure had crystalline elements and good interactions between proteins from the silk and those of NR. These factors are related to an essential function in enhancing adhesion between the silk and the rubber composite. The CB50/CV formulation tended to improve bonding strength because it had a greater amount of carbon black on the rubber chains. This resulted in less flexible chains making them harder to move, which increased the rubber strength. However, the vulcanization system did not affect the bonding strength.

Figure also shows the results of a 180° peeling test using the silk/NR and nylon/NR composites. The silk/NR composite had better results because silk molecules have longer molecular chain proteins, increasing the toughness of the resulting composite materials. A conventional vulcanizing system provided the best adhesive properties compared to an efficient vulcanizing system. This is because the former produced a more flexible product and each linkage had several sulfur atoms, resulting in good mechanical properties. The molecular chains were shorter in the latter system with each bridging bond containing only one or two sulfur atoms, resulting in a rubber composite with inferior mechanical properties.

3.1.9. Tensile Properties

The silk/NR composite represented higher tensile strength than NR alone and the nylon/NR composite (Figure ), indicating the reinforcement and compatibility of the silk fabric with the NR in the composite. The silk fabric resisted external force, so it is better to reinforce the NR. RFL was coated on the fabric surfaces. Resorcinol reacted with the silk and nylon fabric, while the latex reacted with the NR. Thus, the silk fabric with RFL is well adhered to the NR surfaces, resulting in high mechanical properties for the silk/NR composite. The hierarchical structure and surface roughness of silk fibers promote a physical anchoring effect, enhancing mechanical interlocking with the rubber phase and resisting fiber pull-out under applied load. Additionally, amino acid side chains present in silk proteins are capable of forming hydrogen bonds with polar functional groups in the NR matrix, further improving molecular-level adhesion. The RFL treatment contributes to chemical bonding, with resorcinol reacting with the fabric and the latex phase reacting with NR, leading to a robust interfacial structure. SEM observations (Figure d) confirm a continuous and compact interface with minimal voids or debonding, indicating effective stress transfer via both mechanical interlocking and molecular interactions. These synergistic mechanisms align with prior reports on protein-based fiber-reinforced rubber composites, which emphasize the combined effects of hydrogen bonding and physical anchoring in enhancing tensile performance and durability. As a result, the silk/NR composite displayed superior mechanical properties, whereas the NR reference remained weaker but considerably more extensible prior to failure. The nylon/NR represented slightly higher elongation at break than the silk-reinforced rubber (Figure S5) because the nylon structure is more amorphous, providing flexibility. Generally, the rubber industry prefers to use fabrics to reinforce tires.

3.1.10. Dynamic Mechanical Analysis

Composites of rubber reinforced with respective nylon and silk were studied using DMA with temperature sweeping of the storage modulus and tan δ measurements (Figure ). Silk fabric reinforced rubber samples had higher storage moduli than the nylon reinforced rubber samples due to immobilization of the silk molecular chains. This yields a longer relaxation time for the silk/NR composites. Furthermore, the silk-reinforced rubber samples had a maximum tan δ that was lower than the nylon-reinforced rubber samples, resulting in less heat build-up. Consequently, the silk-reinforced rubber samples had lower energy losses compared to nylon-reinforced rubber. Normally, higher carbon black-reinforced rubber yields greater relaxation times and more hysteresis compared to rubber with a lower carbon black content. Silk-reinforced rubber exhibited high relaxation time but low hysteresis compared to nylon reinforced rubber. This may ignite interest in possible applications of silk/NR composites as energy-saving materials.

The CB40/CV formulation had the best properties because it provided a good compromise in the Mooney viscosity, mechanical properties and safer scorch time for compression molding. Moreover, the silk composite rubber had lower energy losses than the nylon fabric composite rubber. This could significantly aid in application of silk/NR composites as energy-efficient materials for tire production. The silk-composite rubber formulation that resulted in better mechanical properties than nylon fabric composite rubber was chosen for further study.

3.3. Gate-to-Gate Life Cycle Assessment of Silk- and Nylon-Reinforced Bicycle Tires

The LCA was carried out to assess the environmental performance of two tire types, tire (nylon) and tire (silk), across key effect categories: global warming potential, abiotic depletion of fossil fuels, ozone layer depletion potential, and photochemical oxidation potential, as presented in Figure (a–d). The analysis reveals significant differences in the environmental burdens associated with each product. A consistent trend was observed across all impact categories, with tire (silk) showing markedly lower environmental impacts compared to their tire (nylon) counterparts.

With regard to global warming potential (GWP100a), the tire (nylon) exhibited emissions equivalent to 5.06 kg CO2, while the tire (silk) emissions were significantly lower at 0.79 kg CO2. The global warming potential of tire (nylon) is approximately six times higher, implying that their life cycle contributes considerably more to greenhouse gas accumulation. This substantial difference emphasizes the critical need for selecting materials and production methods that minimize carbon footprints.

With regard to abiotic depletion (fossil fuels), tire (nylon) recorded a consumption of 84.58 MJ, substantially higher than tire (silk) 16.92 MJ. This 5-fold increase indicates that tire (nylon) relies far more heavily on nonrenewable energy sources. High abiotic depletion values are indicative of greater pressure on finite resources, potentially leading to resource scarcity and long-term environmental degradation. This result suggests that the production process and material composition of tire (nylon) are more energy-intensive, possibly involving a greater proportion of synthetic materials derived from petroleum-based inputs compared to tire (silk).

With regard to ozone layer depletion, tire (nylon) demonstrated an impact of 2.8 × 10–7 kg CFC-11-eq, compared to 5.9 × 10–8 kg CFC-11-eq for tire (silk). Although the absolute values are small, the relative difference is significant, with tire (nylon) causing nearly five times more ozone-depleting emissions. This finding highlights the environmental advantage of tire (silk), potentially due to reduced use of chemicals or processes that release halogenated compounds known to deplete stratospheric ozone. In this study, tire (nylon) exhibited a POP of 0.001095 kg C2H4 eq, whereas tire (silk) showed a significantly lower value of 0.000207 kg C2H4 eq This represents an approximate 81% reduction in photochemical oxidation potential when transitioning from tire (nylon) to tire (silk). The higher POP in tire (nylon) was likely due to the greater use of petroleum-based materials such as synthetic fibers (e.g., nylon), which release higher levels of precursor emissions during manufacturing. In contrast, tire (silk) lower POP suggests that their material compositionpotentially including biobased reinforcements such as silkresults in significantly fewer emissions contributing to ozone formation.

These results highlight the importance of selecting low-impact materials and adopting cleaner production technologies to reduce environmental burdens across the life cycle of the tire product. They also support broader sustainability goals, such as reducing fossil fuel dependency and greenhouse gas emissions. Future research should expand the analysis to include indicators like water footprint and human toxicity, and explore alternative materials to further improve tire sustainability. Overall, this study underscores the value of life cycle thinking in advancing sustainable tire product development and informing environmental decisions in manufacturing.

4. Conclusions

Natural silk was investigated as an alternative reinforcement to synthetic nylon fabric in rubber composite materials for high-performance bicycle tires used in competitive racing. A comparative material study was conducted examining rubber composites reinforced with natural silk and synthetic nylon fabrics. This was done to guide industrial prototyping from laboratory-scale research. Based on the results of laboratory experiments, the CB40/CV formulation was the best because the CV type was more flexible, produced good mechanical properties, and CB at 40 phr was safer for the scorch time. Our laboratory-developed high-performance bicycle tire was tested against six other commercially available bicycle tires. The developed silk-reinforced bicycle tire had higher durability than commercial tire brands reinforced with nylon. Additionally, the adhesion properties between the silk-reinforced carcass layer and the tread layer in the developed product provided the highest adhesion. Plunger testing showed that the silk-reinforced tire withstood greater puncture energy than the nylon-reinforced commercial tire. The developed bicycle tire exhibited the highest durability compared to commercially available nylon-reinforced bicycle tires with the same structure. Overall, based on the results of the present work, the mechanical and adhesion capabilities of composite tires, which are important tire qualities, were enhanced using silk reinforcement. Furthermore, the developed silk-reinforced composite tire had reduced energy losses compared to nylon-reinforced composite tires. Based on the insights gathered from this study, the developed tire has superior performance to commercially available bicycle tires. Bicycle tires reinforced with silk fiber are more durable than those reinforced with nylon. High adhesion between the rubber and the silk-reinforced cord was attained. A comparative life cycle assessment further showed that the silk-reinforced tire had significantly lower environmental impacts than the nylon-reinforced tire, including global warming potential and fossil fuel depletion, highlighting the silk-reinforced tire’s potential contribution to more sustainable manufacturing practices. Nevertheless, some limitations remain. This study relied on prototype testing, which may not fully reflect long-term outdoor cycling conditions and environmental factors. Future research should focus on extended field trials, scaling up production processes, and optimizing silk–rubber bonding for industrial applications. Exploring other natural or hybrid reinforcements could also open new opportunities to further improve the tire’s sustainability and performance.
