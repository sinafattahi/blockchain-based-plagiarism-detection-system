Abstract

According to the common types of ions in mine water, the influence of ions on water solution and the change of the wetting ability of coal dust were studied. The surface tension of different ionic solutions and the change of contact angle after acting on coal were tested, which reflects the change of the wettability of the solutions. Based on zeta potential and FTIR tests, the microwetting process of coal dust surface under ionic solution was studied. The results show that the surface tension of the solution was influenced by the charge interactions, ion concentration, and ion species. The wettability enhancement effect of ions can be ordered as CO3 2– > Cl– > SO4 2– and Mg2+ > Na+ > Ca2+ when the ionic solution initially contacts with the coal dust surface, affected by the surface tension and the electricity of the coal dust. Through FTIR analysis, it is found that after the ionic solution acts on the surface of coal dust for a long time, CO3 2–, Mg2+, and Ca2+ treatment can increase the relative content of CC in coal, while Na+ can reduce the relative content of CC in coal, and the higher the relative content of CC in coal, the less easily is coal dust wettable. The results of the zeta potential experiment also show that there is a strong correlation between coal wettability and zeta potential with the increase of ion concentration. This study proves that the presence of ions in the water is beneficial to the improvement of the wettability of the solution, and the research results provide a theoretical basis for the optimization of mine water quality.

1. Introduction

As an important fossil energy, coal has been widely valued by all countries in the world in recent years. At present, large-scale high-intensity mechanized coal mining methods lead to a large amount of dust in the process of coal mining. , Coal dust will not only endanger the safety of mine production, but also cause serious environmental pollution. , At the same time, it causes coal miners to suffer from pneumoconiosis and other respiratory diseases. In recent years, it has received extensive attention from coal mining enterprises and is one of the problems that cannot be ignored in coal mining. − In the underground, measures such as coal seam water injection and spray dust reduction are usually used to reduce the amount of dust produced by wetting the coal body from the source and capture the dust particles that escape into the air. , However, due to the strong hydrophobicity of coal dust, the wettability of general mine water to coal dust is low, and it is difficult to capture dust efficiently. Most scholars have reduced the surface tension of aqueous solution by adding different kinds of chemical reagents to water, thereby improving the wettability of aqueous solution to coal dust. , Although the use of chemical reagents has improved the efficiency of dust capture, there are also a series of problems such as aggravating environmental pollution and increasing production costs. ,

Generally speaking, the underground dust water contains a large number of inorganic salt ions, such as Na+, Mg2+, Ca2+, Cl–, CO3 2–, SO4 2– and so on. Previous studies have found that the presence of ions in aqueous solution has a certain effect on the wettability of coal dust, which may be related to the properties of the solution and the interaction between ions and coal. , It is generally believed that the wettability of the solution to coal dust is related to the surface tension of the solution itself. The lower the surface tension of the solution, the smaller the contact angle with coal dust, which means that the wettability of the solution to coal dust is better. It has been found by surface tension and contact angle tests that the presence of Ca2+ in coal dust injection aqueous solution has an effect on the wettability of coal. Some scholars characterized the wettability of coal by contact angle measurement experiments, and found that the pH of mine water has a certain influence on the wettability of coal dust. In addition, zeta potential can also reflect the wettability of coal to a certain extent, which is helpful to explore the influence of ions in solution on the wettability of coal dust. , The zeta potential of carbonatite particles with different ion contents and aging time was investigated, and it was found that the specific effect of ions on the surface potential of rock particles would affect the wettability of carbonatite. The influence of metal ions on various metal minerals has been widely studied. − Previous studies have shown that Na+, Mg2+, and Ca2+ have an effect on the zeta potential between bubbles and coal particles by compressing the electric double layer, making it easier for bubbles to adhere to coal particles. The presence of Na+, Mg2+ can cause changes in galena, pyrite, dolomite, and quartz. For anions, it has also been found that the electric double layer slip effect is the main factor affecting the migration of water and Cl–. Meanwhile, the wettability of coal is closely related to the content of oxygen-containing functional groups on its surface, and contact angle test results of coal surface also verify this view. The change of oxygen-containing functional groups, such as C–O–C, C–O, CO, Si–O–Si, etc., has different effects on the wettability of coal through FTIR testing. ,,

At present, a large number of studies have confirmed that the ions in the solution change the wettability of coal by interacting with the surface of coal. In the actual process of underground dust reduction, due to the influence of geological conditions, the types and concentrations of ions in different mine water are quite different, which may lead to some differences in the wettability of the coal body. The reasons and mechanisms behind this difference have rarely been studied. Therefore, the purpose of this study is to explore how the main ions in mine water affect the wettability of coal. First, the physical properties and basic mineral phase composition of coal were tested. Based on this, the effects of physical and chemical properties of solutions containing different ions on wettability were analyzed by surface tension and contact angle experiments. Moreover, through zeta potential and FTIR tests, the interaction between ions and coal and the effect on the content of oxygen-containing functional groups were analyzed. Furthermore, combined with the analysis results of the physical and chemical properties of solutions containing different ions and their interaction with coal, the mechanism of action of different ions on the coal wettability was clarified, which provided some scientific guidance for the optimization of reasonable mine water.

2. Materials and Experiments

2.1. Samples

In this study, coal samples were collected from the 22,308 working face of Shigetai Coal Mine in Shaanxi Province and then sealed and transported to the laboratory. After crushing and screening of coal samples in the laboratory, dust samples with particle sizes of 45 to 75 μm were obtained. Then the samples were dried at 40 °C for 48 h, and finally, the prepared dust samples were sealed and preserved.

Through the test of mine water quality in the Shigetai Coal Mine, shown in Table , it is known that the mine water mainly contains ions such as Na+, Mg2+, Ca2+, Cl–, CO3 2–, and SO4 2–. In order to explore the influence of main ions in mine water on coal wettability, inorganic salt analytical reagents, including NaCl, CaCl2, MgCl2, Na2CO3, and Na2SO4 were selected.

2.3. Wetting Characteristic

2.3.1. Surface Tension

The surface tension of deionized water and different concentrations of inorganic salt solutions were measured by a surface tension meter (KRÜSS, K100). Deionized water was used to configure 0.01, 0.02, 0.03, 0.04, and 0.05 mol/L inorganic salt solutions. The same volume of solution was taken for each test, and the solution was continuously measured 15 times at an ambient temperature of 20 °C. The average value was taken as the surface tension experimental measurement result.

2.3.2. Contact Angle

An appropriate amount of coal sample was taken and made into a flake coal sample under 20 MPa pressure. These flake coal samples had a diameter of 13 mm and a thickness of 2 mm. The contact angles of deionized water and different concentrations of inorganic salt solution dropping on the coal surface were measured by an optical contact angle measuring instrument (KRÜSS, DSA 30). Each sample was tested three times, and the average value was taken as the contact angle experimental measurement result.

2.3.3. Zeta Potential

The charge of coal particles in different inorganic salt solutions was quantitatively characterized by a microelectrophoretic instrument (Shanghai, JS94H). First, 10 mg of pulverized coal was weighed and put into 10 mL of solution sample to be tested and then fully stirred to ensure uniform distribution. After the pulverized coal and solution were mixed evenly, 0.7 mL of the mixed solution was injected into the experimental sample tank for testing. Each sample was tested three times, and the average value was taken as the zeta potential experimental measurement result.

2.3.4. Infrared Spectroscopy

The infrared spectrometer (Bruker, INVENIO S) was used to characterize the coal samples after inorganic salt solution treatment. The test wavenumber was 4000–400 cm–1, the resolution was 0.4 cm–1, and the scanning count was 15 times. First, 50 mg of pulverized coal was weighed and put into 50 mL of inorganic salt solution and then fully stirred evenly. After standing, the coal sample was filtered and dried for 24 h. Finally, the dried dust sample and potassium bromide powder (spectrally pure) were ground and mixed according to the mass ratio of 1:200 and then pressed into pieces. The infrared spectrum test was carried out to obtain the infrared spectrum of the sample.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Surface Tension Analysis

In the current study, most of the focus is on the effect of a high concentration of inorganic salt ions on the surface tension of the solution. However, it can be seen from the mine water quality report that the ion concentration in the actual mine water is relatively low, so the ion concentration in the inorganic salt solution selected in this study is low. Figure shows the surface tension test results for different concentrations and types of ion solutions. It can be clearly seen from Figure a that as the concentration of inorganic salt ions increases, the addition of Cl– and SO4 2– will increase the surface tension of the solution, while the addition of CO3 2– does the opposite, which can reduce the surface tension of the solution. Based on this, the influence of common anions in mine water on the surface tension of the solution is CO3 2– > Cl– > SO4 2–. Similarly, it can be seen from Figure b that with the increase of inorganic salt ion concentration, the addition of Na+ and Ca2+ makes the surface tension of the solution increase slightly, while the addition of Mg2+ makes the surface tension of the solution decrease. The influence of common cations in mine water on the surface tension of the solution is Mg2+ > Na+ > Ca2+.

However, fundamentally speaking, compared with the effect of conventional surfactants, the effect of low-concentration inorganic salt ions on the surface tension of the solution is small and within a certain fluctuation range. It has also been proved that when the concentration of salt solution is 0.01 mol/L, the surface tension increment is about 0.2 mN/m. This is because the water–air surface repels ions, resulting in negative ion adsorption and an increase in the surface tension of the inorganic salt solution (with a few exceptions, such as most weakly acidic inorganic salt solutions). , The force effect of the inorganic salt solution at different concentrations is different. At low concentrations, image force has a great influence on the ions, while at higher concentration, the hydration force and dispersion force have a greater influence on the ions. , For the ion–surface interaction of inorganic salt solution, when the concentration is large, the influence of electrostatic force is secondary, and it is more affected by image force and hydration force. ,

Taking the force analysis of the NaCl solution system as an example, the gas–water interface is used as a reference surface to analyze the surface tension of the solution, as shown in Figure . When the solution is deionized water, as shown in Figure a, the surface tension of the deionized water is F, the attraction of air to the solution is F 1, and the resultant force of water molecules on the air–water interface is F 2. The surface tension of deionized water can be expressed as

F=F2−F1 1

In Figure b, when NaCl is added to deionized water, Na+ and Cl– will attract water molecules to their surroundings due to their charge and aggregate to form hydrated sodium ions and hydrated chloride ions, resulting in an additional inward intermolecular force at the air–water interface, which can be equivalent to F 0. For example, as shown in Figure c, the force of the whole hydrated chloride ion can be regarded as the inner and outer layers. The inner layer is the ion–dipole interaction, and the outer layer is the dipole–dipole interaction. Suppose that the force of the inner layer is f 1, and there is an electrostatic force F C between Na+ and Cl–, then

F0=f1−FC 2

Substituting Formula into Formula , the surface tension of NaCl solution can be obtained as follows

FNaCl=F2+F0−F1=F2+f1−FC−F1 3

In Figure d, the number of hydrated sodium ions and hydrated chloride ions increases, resulting in an increase in the ion–dipole interaction force f 1, and the distance between Na+ and Cl– decreases with the increase of the NaCl concentration. According to Coulomb’s law, when the distance between two charged particles decreases, the electrostatic force (F C) between them will change accordingly

FC=kq1q2r2 4

where, k is the Coulomb constant; q 1 and q 2 are the charge quantities of Na+ and Cl–, respectively; and r is the distance between Na+ and Cl–.

As the distance (r) between Na+ and Cl– decreases, the electrostatic force (F C) increases. It can be seen that when the NaCl concentration increases, if the ion–dipole interaction force (f 1) is greater than the electrostatic force (F C), the surface tension of the solution increases, and vice versa. Therefore, when the NaCl concentration is low, the surface tension of the solution usually fluctuates up and down. When the concentration of NaCl increases to a certain extent, the influence of electrostatic force between ions becomes smaller, mainly affected by the ion–dipole interaction force (f 1) and image force; therefore, the surface tension of the solution will increase obviously.

3.2. Effect of Ion Solution on the Contact Angle of Coal

3.2.1. Effect of Low Concentration Anions and Cations on the Contact Angle

Na+ and Cl– were selected as the reference indexes of the inorganic salt solution, and different concentrations of the inorganic salt solution were configured. The initial contact angle of the solution to the coal surface was tested, as shown in Figure . Because the wettability of bituminous coal is generally poor, the contact angle of deionized water is 92.6° for the selected coal sample. It can be seen from Figure a that the contact angle of each inorganic salt solution showed a decreasing trend with the increase of the Na+ concentration in each control group with the Na+ concentration as the reference index. However, compared with deionized water, the addition of Cl– and SO4 2– in low-concentration inorganic salt solution leads to deterioration of the wettability of the solution. In contrast, the addition of CO3 2– can effectively enhance the wettability of the solution. The wettability of different anions on the solution is CO3 2– > Cl– > SO4 2–. As observed from Figure a, in the case of Cl– concentration as the reference index, with the increase of Cl– concentration in each control group, the addition of Na+ and Ca2+ in the low-concentration inorganic salt solution leads to the deterioration of the wettability of the solution. In contrast, the addition of Mg2+ can effectively enhance the wettability of the solution. Studies have shown that Mg2+ is more easily adsorbed by the coal surface. Thus, it can be seen that in the low-concentration inorganic salt solution, CO3 2– and Mg2+ will significantly enhance the wettability of the solution to the coal surface. This conclusion is also verified by the surface tension test results. The surface tension of the low-concentration inorganic salt solution is also smaller than that of deionized water, indicating that the addition of CO3 2– and Mg2+ changes the physical and chemical properties of the solution.

3.2.2. Effect of the Contact Time of Inorganic Salt Solution on the Contact Angle

The wetting of coal in solution is a dynamic process. The concentration of Na+ and Cl– was selected as 0.04 mol/L as the reference index, and the change of contact angle after solution contact with coal was studied. It can be seen from Figure that the wetting of coal by an inorganic salt solution usually includes two stages: rapid spreading and slow infiltration. The contact angle decreases rapidly within 1 s and then decreases slowly. It is speculated that the wettability of the droplet to the coal is mainly related to the nature of the solution itself at the moment when the droplet is in contact with the coal body. Then as the contact time becomes longer, different ions in the solution may have physical and chemical effects with the coal body, thus affecting the wettability of the coal. In Figure a, the contact angles of various inorganic salt solutions are not much different when contacting the coal body for 0.2 s. The contact angle of the Na2CO3 solution is obviously smaller. With the increase of contact time, the contact angle of NaCl solution shows a significant downward trend, which decreases by 12.8° in 1 s (compared with the Na2CO3 solution at the same time, it only decreases by 5.3°), indicating that Cl– can better promote the physical and chemical interaction between solution and coal surface in the process of coal body infiltration compared with other anions. As shown in Figure a, when the concentration of Cl– was used as the reference index, the contact angle of NaCl solution also showed a significant decrease with the increase of contact time, and the contact angle of the solution after adding MgCl2 and CaCl2 was even greater than that of deionized water, indicating that the interaction between Mg2+ and Ca2+ with the surface of coal inhibited the wetting of the solution to the coal. According to the XRD test results of the coal samples, it can be seen that the selected test coal samples contain calcite (CaCO3) and kaolinite (Al2Si2O5(OH)4). Ca2+ and Mg2+ can react with the mineral components in coal. Ca2+ can promote the precipitation of calcite. Mg2+ easily reacts with kaolinite to form magnesium silicate precipitate, which makes some precipitate adhere to the surface of coal, reduces the affinity of coal to solution, and inhibits the wettability of solution to coal. Therefore, under the influence of Ca2+ and Mg2+, the wettability of inorganic salt solution to coal is worse than that of the Na+-containing solution.

3.3. Relationship between Zeta Potential and Wettability of Solutions Containing Different Ions

The polar functional groups on the coal surface could be ionized in water. The zeta potential of the initial state dispersed in deionized water solution is −49.8 mV, and the negative zeta potential indicates that the surface of coal contains a negative surface charge. With the same Na+ as the reference standard, it can be seen from Figure a that the absolute value of the zeta potential of pulverized coal in different inorganic salt solutions showed a decreasing trend (zeta potential was still negative), and the zeta potential of pulverized coal surface in Na2CO3 solution reached a maximum of −7.65 mV. When the concentration of Na+ is 0.04 mol/L, the absolute value of the zeta potential of pulverized coal in the solution increases. It is speculated that when the inorganic salt solution increases to a certain extent, some anions are adsorbed by the electrode, resulting in an increase in the absolute value of the zeta potential. In addition, it was also found that the absolute value of the zeta potential of pulverized coal in Na2CO3 solution was generally larger than that of Na2SO4. This was because the pH value of the solution would significantly affect the zeta potential. The Na2CO3 solution was weakly alkaline. Compared with the Na2CO3 solution, the Na2SO4 solution contained more H+. These H+ ions were easily adsorbed by the negatively charged silica surface in the coal, resulting in a decrease in the absolute value of the zeta potential of the Na2SO4 solution.

Meanwhile, with Cl– as the reference standard, similar rules can also be seen from Figure a. The absolute value of the zeta potential of pulverized coal in different inorganic salt solutions shows a decreasing trend (zeta potential is still negative), and when the Cl– concentration is 0.04 mol/L, the zeta potential of the surface of pulverized coal in three inorganic salt solutions of NaCl, CaCl2, and MgCl2 shows approximate potential values, which are −12.50 mV, −13.62 mV, and −12.12 mV, respectively.

By comparison, it is found that the absolute value of the zeta potential in NaCl solution is generally greater than that in CaCl2 and MgCl2 solutions, which also shows that the zeta potential in divalent electrolyte solution is smaller, and the absolute value of the zeta potential of Ca2+ is smaller than that of Mg2+. Through the zeta potential of several inorganic salt solutions as a whole, regardless of Na+ or Cl– as the reference standard, each inorganic salt solution showed particularity when the reference standard ion concentration was 0.04 mol/L, and the critical point of the effect of a low-concentration inorganic salt solution on wettability may be 0.04 mol/L.

Comparing the zeta potential of different inorganic salt solutions with their corresponding surface tension and contact angle trends, as shown in Figure , it can be found that as the ion concentration in the solution increases, the zeta potential of the inorganic salt is basically consistent with the surface tension and contact angle trends. Comprehensive analysis shows that the zeta potential of different inorganic salt solutions has the following relationship with coal wettability by adding inorganic salts with the same concentration of Cl– and Na+ to deionized water. When the solution is close to weak acidity, with the increase of Cl– and Na+ concentration from 0.01 to 0.05 mol/L, the wettability of different ions to coal has a good correlation with the zeta potential. When the solution is weakly alkaline, the wettability of CO3 2– to coal has the opposite relationship with the zeta potential. When the concentration of Na+ in the solution is 0.04 mol/L, the wettability of CO3 2– to coal has the opposite relationship with the zeta potential.

3.4. Evolution of Coal Chemical Structure Affected by Inorganic Salt Solution

According to the contact angle and zeta potential test results of inorganic salt solution on coal samples, the solution with Na+ and Cl– concentrations of 0.04 mol/L was selected to soak the coal samples to explore whether the chemical structure of the coal body changed after different ions acted on the coal body, so as to find out the reason for the difference of wettability of the coal body. The infrared spectrum test results of raw coal and coal samples treated with different types of inorganic salt solutions are shown in Figure .

The infrared spectra of coal samples can usually be divided into four main regions. According to previous studies, the absorption bands in the spectra were divided into aromatic structures (700–900 cm–1), oxygen-containing structures (1000–1800 cm–1), aliphatic structures (2800–3000 cm–1), and hydroxyl structures (3000–3600 cm–1). Comparing the infrared spectra of raw coal and coal samples treated with different kinds of inorganic salt solutions, it can be found that the peak positions in the infrared spectra of different coal samples are basically the same, indicating that the basic chemical structure of coal samples has not changed after different solution treatments but there are obvious differences in the amplitude of characteristic peaks in some regions, especially in the oxygen-containing functional group structure region. Therefore, the oxygen-containing region (1000–1800 cm–1) spectrum is subjected to peak fitting. The peak separation and quantitative calculation were performed using the peak resolution and curve-fitting method (Gaussian peak type was chosen) with Peakfit software. Through the second derivative method, the centers of positions of adsorption peaks were obtained. The band regions of the infrared spectrogram were selected and baseline-linearized. Then the original peaks were divided and the fitted curves were gotten, as shown in Figure .

The spectra in this region are rather complex, containing a total of 13–14 individual peaks, and the correlation coefficients of the fits are all above 99.9%. Based on previous research findings, − we have attributed different peaks as follows: the peaks at 1020–1060 cm–1 are attributed to Si–O–Si or Si–O–C stretching vibrations; the peaks at 1100–1330 cm–1 are attributed to C–O stretching vibrations in aromatic ethers, alcohol hydroxyls, and phenolic hydroxyls; the peaks at 1380 cm–1 are attributed to symmetric deformation vibrations of methyl (–CH3); the peaks at 1380 cm–1 are attributed to asymmetric deformation vibrations of methylene (–CH2) and methyl (–CH3); the peaks at 1500–1620 cm–1 are attributed to aromatic CC vibrations; the peaks at 1630–1660 cm–1 are attributed to CO stretching vibrations in quinones; and the peaks at 1700 cm–1 are attributed to carboxylic acid (COOH) stretching vibrations. Compared with that of deionized water, further analysis can determine the influence of various ions on different functional group regions.

This is because treatment with deionized water can cause hydrogenation reactions between CC double bonds in coal and water, leading to an increase in the CO functional group content.

As seen in Figure a, when treated with various inorganic salts, the coal samples exhibited corresponding changes in the contents of various peaks assigned to oxygen-containing functional groups compared with treatment with deionized water. At a Na+ concentration of 0.04 mol/L, for the Si–O structure, the addition of Cl– increased its content, while CO3 2– and SO4 2– had the opposite effect, reducing the Si–O content. For the C–O structure, Cl– and SO4 2– both promoted an increase in its content, while CO3 2– had a minimal impact. Upon the addition of inorganic salts, the contents of methyl (–CH3) and methylene (–CH2) in each sample changed oppositely. It was observed that Cl– facilitated the loss of H from methyl groups to form methylene, while CO3 2– and SO4 2– promoted the combination of H with methylene to form methyl groups. For the changes in CC and CO contents, various ions had notable effects. CO3 2– and SO4 2– contributed to the breakage of more CO bonds and their combination with C atoms to form CC bonds. Although Cl– also contributed to the breakage of more CO bonds, it did not result in a transition to CC; rather, it formed more Si–O structures.

As seen in Figure b, when treated with inorganic salts at a Cl– concentration of 0.04 mol/L, Ca2+, Mg2+, and Na+ each contributed to corresponding changes in the contents of different peaks assigned to oxygen-containing functional groups in the coal samples. The addition of Na+ promoted an increase in the Si–O and C–O contents of the coal. Conversely, Ca2+ and Mg2+ had opposing effects, reducing the Si–O and C–O contents. For the changes in methyl (–CH3) and methylene (–CH2) contents in the coal samples, Na+ and Ca2+ exhibited opposing effects; Na+ facilitated the loss of H from methyl groups to form methylene, while Ca2+ promoted the combination of H with methylene to form methyl groups. Mg2+ contributed to an increase in both methyl (–CH3) and methylene (–CH2) contents in the coal samples. This may be attributed to Mg2+ facilitating the detachment of oxygen from C–O structures and their combination with H to form methyl (–CH3) and methylene (–CH2) groups. For the changes in CC and CO contents, all three cations had notable effects. Both Ca2+ and Mg2+ contributed to the breakage of more CO bonds and their combination with C atoms to form more CC bonds; however, Mg2+ had a more pronounced effect than did Ca2+. Na+ contributed to a decrease in the CO content in the coal samples, facilitating their combination with Si atoms to form Si–O–Si structures.

Based on comprehensive analysis, it can be inferred that the influence of various ions in water on the content of CO is closely related to wettability. This is because ions can easily react chemically with carboxyl groups (COOH), thereby leading to corresponding changes in the content of CC. When the Na+ concentration is constant at 0.04 mol/L, the CO3 2– and SO4 2– treatment results in an increase in the content of CC in coal, while Cl– can cause a decrease in the content of CC in coal. When the Cl– concentration is constant at 0.04 mol/L, Mg2+ treatment results in a more significant increase in the content of CC in coal compared to Ca2+, while Na+ can cause a decrease in the content of CC in coal. Furthermore, it has been observed that the higher the CC content, the poorer the wettability of the solution toward coal. When treated with Na2CO3, coal exhibits a higher CC content but over time exhibits a smaller contact angle. Perhaps because initially, the solution exhibited good wettability. Over time, the interaction between coal and solution, as well as the chemical reaction between CO3 2– and the coal surface, played a more important role in inhibiting coal wetting.

4. Discussion on the Influence of Ions on the Coal Wettability

Through surface tension experiments, it can be observed that the addition of various ions to deionized water leads to changes in the properties of the solution. At a Na+ and Cl– concentration of 0.04 mol/L, the effects of the anions CO3 2– and SO4 2– are more significant, while the impact on the cations is relatively small. Contact angle experiments indicate that at a Na+ concentration of 0.04 mol/L, the order of wettability of the anions on coal is CO3 2– > Cl– > SO4 2–. When the Cl– concentration is 0.04 mol/L, the order of wettability of the cations on coal is Mg2+ > Na+ > Ca2+. Overall, the lower the surface tension, the smaller the contact angle and the better the wettability of the solution to coal. The physicochemical properties of the solution can affect the wettability of coal. However, when Cl– is at 0.04 mol/L, MgCl2 has a higher surface tension than CaCl2, which is contrary to the results of the contact angle experiments. This suggests that the wettability of the solution on coal may not only be due to differences in the properties of the solution but also may be caused by interactions between Ca2+, Mg2+ and coal. Figure demonstrates the interactions between different cations and bituminous coal as an example of the impact of various ions on the wettability of the coal.

From Figure , it can be revealed that the interactions between various ions and bituminous coal encompass both physical and chemical aspects. First, due to the porous structure of coal, Ca2+ and Mg2+ are more easily adsorbed by the coal surface than Na+, resulting in differences in wettability. Second, Ca2+ and Mg2+ tend to react with certain minerals on the coal surface, promoting the formation of precipitates. The relationship between contact angle and time demonstrates that initially, the physical and chemical properties of the solution have a greater impact on the wettability of the coal. However, as the liquid droplet is further absorbed by the coal, the interaction between Ca2+ and Mg2+ and the coal surface becomes more intense, resulting in more Ca2+ and Mg2+ being adsorbed onto the surface. The presence of a large amount of quartz on the coal surface, compared with other mineral components, can influence the adsorption of ions. As Ca2+ and Mg2+ come into full contact with the minerals on the coal surface, they promote the formation of precipitates, preventing further wetting of the droplet and inhibiting the decrease in contact angle. Through zeta potential experiments, we have observed that as the concentration of ions in the solution increases, more cations move toward the surface of the coal. This results in a decrease in the potential difference between the solid and the liquid phases, leading to a reduction in the absolute value of the zeta potential. Furthermore, when the concentration of Na+ is 0.04 mol/L, the order of the absolute values of the zeta potential corresponding to the anions is CO3 2– < Cl– < SO4 2–. When the concentration of Cl– is 0.04 mol/L, the order of the absolute values of the zeta potential corresponding to the cations is Mg2+ < Na+ < Ca2+. At this point, the smaller the absolute value of the zeta potential, the better the wettability. The zeta potential experiment can once again prove that the interaction between ions and coal affects the wettability of coal. FTIR testing has demonstrated that after treatment with different sample solutions, there is a significant impact on the fitted peaks in the region of oxygen-containing functional groups. After the ions fully contact the coal particles, they chemically react with carboxylic acid (COOH). Mg2+ and Ca2+ primarily affect changes in the content of CC and CO, while Na+ promotes the cleavage of CO and its combination with Si to form Si–O–Si structures. The various ions have different effects on the mutual conversion of methyl (–CH3) and methylene (–CH2). Mg2+ also promotes more C–O structures to deoxygenate and combine with H to form methyl (–CH3) and methylene (–CH2). The content of CC in coal can reflect the wettability of the solution, further indicating that interactions between ions and coal affect the solution wettability.

5. Conclusions

This work is based on the basic parameters of coal and then combines surface tension, contact angle, zeta potential, and FTIR experimental analysis to investigate the relationship between various ions and their concentrations on the wettability of bituminous coal. The mechanism of how different ions in water affect the wettability of bituminous coal is explored. The conclusions are as follows:

  • (1)

    When the concentrations of Na+ and Cl– increase from 0.01 mol/L to 0.05 mol/L, both Cl– and SO4 2– cause the wettability of the solution to decrease. When the Cl– concentration is 0.04 mol/L, the difference in wettability between MgCl2 and CaCl2 solutions indicates that the interactions between ions and coal also affect solution wettability.

  • (2)

    When the solution is weakly acidic, as the concentrations of Cl– and Na+ increase from 0.01 mol/L to 0.05 mol/L, the wettability of coal by different ions shows a good correlation with the zeta potential. When the solution is alkaline, the wettability of coal by different ions exhibits opposite relationships at a concentration of 0.04 mol/L. At this point, the smaller the absolute value of the zeta potential, the better the wettability.

  • (3)

    The FTIR testing indicates that after the ions have fully contacted with the coal particles, they chemically react with carboxylic acid (–COOH) in the coal. The content of CC in the coal undergoes significant changes under the influence of the ions, and it can well reflect the wettability of the solution. The wettability of coal by solutions containing different cations is mainly characterized as follows: when the concentration of Cl– is 0.04 mol/L, Mg2+ < Ca2+ < Na+.

  • (4)

    The mechanism of how ions in water affect the wettability of bituminous coal mainly involves two processes: first, the ions affect the properties of the solution. They influence the surface tension of the solution in various ways, such as through charge interactions, ion concentration, and ion species. Second, there is an interaction between the ions and the coal. Some ions in water tend to react with certain mineral components on the coal surface to form precipitates. Alternatively, due to the porous structure of coal and chemical reactions with functional groups present on the coal surface, the ions in water are adsorbed onto the coal surface.
