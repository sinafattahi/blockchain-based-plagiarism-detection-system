Abstract

Coalbed methane (CBM) extraction plays a dual role in ensuring the safety of coal mines and enriching clean energy resources. The extraction radius of CBM boreholes is influenced by multiple factors, but the interactions of the factors and their relationships with the extraction radius remain unclear. Therefore, the effective extraction radius under varying factors was simulated using COMSOL, and the major factors were determined through sensitivity analysis and response surface methodology. Further, the relationships between the main factors, their interaction effects, and the extraction radius were studied, and a calculation model was constructed. The results indicate the following. (1) The factors are ranked in a descending order of their influence on the effective extraction radius as follows: initial gas pressure, coal thickness, initial permeability, extraction time, borehole radius. Specially, the initial permeability and drainage time have comparable levels of impact. However, the negative pressure and vertical stress exhibit weak impacts. Notably, the effective extraction area always presents a vertically expanding oval-like shape, and the thinner the coal seam, the shorter the contours that appear in the coal seam, and the smaller the curvature of the contours. The intuitive manifestation in the coal seam is that the pressure contour gradually changes from an arc to a straight line. (2) Significant interactions exist between the initial gas pressure and the initial permeability, as well as between the initial gas pressure and extraction time. Moreover, these interactions manifest as the increased initial gas pressure significantly inhibiting the impacts of the initial permeability and extraction time on the extraction radius. (3) The effective extraction radius of the group F 16–17 coal in the same mine was calculated as 4.98 m using the established model, which closely aligns with the measured value of 5 m.

1. Introduction

China is the world’s largest coal producer and consumer. However, with continuous exploitation, China’s coal mines have gradually entered the deep mining stage. Mining in deep coal faces challenges such as high gas content, high gas pressure, significant ground stress, and low permeability. , Effective CBM extraction is critical not only for mine safety but also for the rational utilization of gas resources in coal. Rapid and accurate determination of extraction radius parameters and optimization of extraction parameters are essential to ensure safe production and enhance resource utilization in mines.

The extraction radius is influenced by numerous factors, and they can be categorized into objective and subjective factors. Objective factors include coal permeability, coal thickness, gas pressure, ground stress, etc., and subjective factors comprise negative pressure, borehole radius, extraction time, etc. Among these factors, permeability and gas pressure are determinative factors for CBM extraction efficiency. Enhancing coal seam permeability is central to improving extraction efficiency, and gas pressure and gas content govern the required extraction duration. , Numerous scholars have researched the factors influencing the extraction radius, primarily focusing on four key aspects. (1) In studies on the influence of individual factors on the extraction radius, Wang et al. analyzed the impact of permeability, gas pressure, and borehole radius on the extraction radius, concluding that an increase in these factors leads to a larger effective extraction range. Zhang et al. found that burial depth exerts a highly significant influence on the extraction radius, followed by initial gas pressure and permeability. (2) Regarding research on the coupling effects of the factors, Gong et al. discovered that the initial permeability has the most significant impact on the extraction radius through orthogonal experiments, followed by the extraction time. Additionally, there is a notable interactive effect between the extraction time and initial permeability. Kong et al. found that a change in one factor may suppress the effects of others due to interactions. Thus, utilizing the orthogonal experiments to find the optimal combination of parameters is more effective for improving the extraction efficiency of CBM. (3) In field testing of the effective extraction radius, many scholars spent a lot of time measuring the effective extraction radius of CBM extraction boreholes in mines by the pressure drop, gas flow, or tracer gas methods. , Although the on-site test is intuitive, it takes a long time, is difficult to construct, and needs a large amount of work, which can easily cause a large error. , (4) In the establishment of the numerical calculation model, numerous regression equations between the effective extraction radius and the factors have been established. , There is a complex relationship between the effective extraction radius and multiple factors. Ignoring the secondary factors and focusing on the major factors are the key to constructing the calculation model of the effective extraction radius.

In summary, significant achievements have been made in recent studies on the influencing factors of CBM extraction. However, there are still some problems to be further explored, including the following. (1) The determination of the extraction radius needs to consider more factors due to the more complex gas storage state in deep mines. (2) Many previous prediction equations of the effective extraction radius are generally complicated in calculation, and they are not conducive to determine the effective extraction radius rapidly. Therefore, in this study, a methodology for determining the effective extraction radius of CBM boreholes under multifactor coupling conditions was developed. First, the fluid–solid coupling mathematical model is established, and the influence of many factors such as initial gas pressure, coal thickness, and initial permeability on the effective extraction radius is simulated through COMSOL. Then, the main control factors are determined by single-factor sensitivity analysis, and the interaction effect between the main factors and the influence on the extraction radius are analyzed by the response surface method. Finally, a mathematical model is constructed correlating the effective extraction radius with the main factors, and it is visualized through a developed program.

2. Development of a Coupled Fluid–Solid Model for CBM Extraction

2.1. Basic Assumptions

Based on the theoretical foundations of elastic mechanics and seepage mechanics related to CBM extraction in mines, the actual CBM extraction process is simplified, and the following basic assumptions are made. ,

  • 1.

    The coal body is an isotropic elastic medium, and its deformation conforms to Hooke’s law.

  • 2.

    Assuming CBM as an ideal gas, its seepage follows Darcy’s law, and the process is isothermal.

  • 3.

    The adsorption–desorption of gas conforms to the Langmuir isothermal adsorption theory.

  • 4.

    The gas pressure within the coal seam is uniform everywhere, and the original coal is saturated with CBM.

2.3. Physical Model

2.3.1. Geometric Model

A two-dimensional geometric model for CBM extraction is established with the group F coal in Pingdingshan No.8 mine as the study area. The coal seam has a thickness of 3.0–4.39 m, with an average thickness of 3.7 m. The gas pressure in a certain area is 1.5 MPa, and the vertical stress is 10 MPa. The structure of the coal seam in the area is simple and relatively stable. The roof and floor of the coal seam are sandy mudstone with poor permeability. The size of the model is 40 m × 4 m, and a borehole with a radius of 50 mm is set in the center of the model.

2.3.2. Boundary Conditions of the Model

The boundary conditions of the model are fixed constraints at the bottom, vertical stress p v at the top, and roller support boundaries around the model. Negative pressure is applied to the inner wall of the borehole. The sketch of the physical model is shown in Figure .

3. Effects of Factors on the Extraction Radius

Gas extraction involves multifield coupling. COMSOL is a multiphysics simulation software. It supports the coupling of multiple physical fields (including solid mechanics, fluid dynamics, heat transfer, etc.), and its interface is intuitive. Therefore, the process of CBM extraction was simulated using COMSOL in this study.

3.1. Effect of a Single Factor on the Extraction Radius

3.1.1. Determination of Factors

The drainage radius of boreholes is affected by many factors, including coal seam thickness, permeability, gas pressure, borehole radius, drainage pressure, drainage time, ground stress, ground temperature, geological structure, etc. For the study of coal seams, the structure of a coal seam is simple and relatively stable, and the temperature of coal seam changes little, so the geological structure and temperature are not considered. 3.1.2. Study on the Extraction Radius under Change Rates of Different Factors

We considered the coal thickness X 1, original permeability X 2, original gas pressure X 3, borehole radius X 4, negative pressure X 5, vertical stress X 6, as well as extraction time X 7 as the factors and the effective extraction radius of the borehole R as the response indicator to conduct the simulation study (the effective extraction radius refers to the radius of the area around the borehole where the gas pressure drops below 0.74 MPa through extraction). The scheme is as follows: keeping six factors unchanged, the effective extraction radius is simulated under the conditions of 10, 20, and 30% change rate of a single factor.

As shown in Figure , the effective extraction radius exhibits a significant increasing trend with the increase of the initial permeability X 2, borehole radius X 4, and extraction time X 7. Conversely, it shows a notable decreasing trend with the increase of the coal thickness X 1 and initial gas pressure X 3. However, it can be observed that the extraction radius curves corresponding to the negative pressure X 5 and vertical stress X 6 are always flat.

For the factor X 1, the maximum effective extraction radius is 2.46 m, the minimum is 1.19 m, and the variation is 1.27 m. For X 2, the maximum effective extraction radius is 2.14 m, the minimum is 1.22 m, and the variation is 0.92 m. For X 3, X 4, X 5, X 6, and X 7, the changes in effective extraction radius are 2.76, 0.46, 0.02, 0.09, and 0.93 m, respectively. The data indicates that, except for the negative extraction pressure and vertical stress, other factors have a significant impact on the effective extraction radius.

It was found that coal thickness has a special impact on CBM extraction in simulations. Therefore, the effective extraction areas were simulated under coal thicknesses of 5.2, 4.4, 3.6, and 2.8 m. The contours of the gas pressure are shown in Figure .

Intuitively, the effective extraction areas (the area within the black contour) show different shapes under different coal seam thicknesses. It can be observed from Figure that when the coal thickness is 5.2 m, the shape of the effective extraction area is an approximate circle concentric with the borehole; when the thickness is 4.4 m, its shape is oval-like with vertical expansion; and finally, when the thickness is 3.6 m, the contour of the effective extraction area continues to extend vertically and becomes a drum shape with arcs on both sides.

Essentially, the shape of the effective extraction area did not change greatly. In coal seams of different thicknesses, their shape is always oval-like with vertical expansion. This phenomenon is related to the boundary conditions of the model. The roof and floor of the coal seam in this study area are both sandy mudstone with poor permeability. Therefore, when establishing the model, the actual roof and floor were not considered, and the impermeable boundary was used instead of the coal seam surrounding rock to constrain seepage.

Taking Figure b as an example, the pressure contours near the borehole appear oval-like, the pressure contours slightly further away show two arcs, and the pressure contours further away show two straight lines. In order to clearly understand this change, the contours that were not visible in the roof and floor of the coal seam were completed (as shown in the dashed line in Figure ). Overall, the shape of the effective extraction area is basically not affected by the thickness of the coal seam, and it always presents a continuously expanding oval-like shape. The pressure contours of ″arc shape″ and ″straight shape″ are only the two sides of the contour of the entire oval-like shape. Obviously, the thinner the coal seam thickness, the shorter the contours that appear in the coal seam, and the smaller the curvature of the contours in the coal seam. The intuitive manifestation in the coal seam is that the contour of the isobaric lines gradually changes from an arc to a straight line.

3.1.3. Screening of Main Controlling Factors

To observe the influence intensity of the factors on the extraction radius more intuitively, the sensitivity coefficient is introduced to quantitatively evaluate the influence intensity of each factor. The equation of the sensitivity coefficient is

β=ΔYΔX 9

where ΔX is the change rate of the factor; ΔY is the change rate of the effective extraction radius; and β is the sensitivity coefficient, %, and the greater its absolute value, the more significant the influence of this factor on the results.

Figure shows the variations in sensitivity coefficients of different factors. The analysis reveals that the factors are ranked in a descending order of influence on the effective extraction radius as X 3, X 1, X 2, X 7, X 4. The curve for the initial gas pressure X 3 varies most dramatically, followed by the curve for the coal thickness X 1. However, the curves for the negative pressure X 5 and the vertical stress X 6 are smooth. Their maximum sensitivity coefficients are 6 and 17.86%, respectively. It indicates that factors X 5 and X 6 have a minor impact on the extraction radius, which aligns with the conclusion in Figure .

Notably, the curves for X 2 and X 7 largely overlap, and both have a maximum sensitivity coefficient of 95.24%. It demonstrates that they have a similar impact on the effective extraction radius. This phenomenon may be attributed to the low initial permeability in the study area. In fact, the impact of permeability on CBM extraction is very significant. ,

3.2. Effects of Interactions between Factors on the Extraction Radius

Response surface methodology (RSM) employs statistical analysis to study the impact of multiple factors on system responses. As a specific implementation of RSM, the Box–Behnken design (BBD) is a widely adopted experimental design approach. It can effectively analyze the interaction of factors while reducing the number of experiments by evenly distributing test points. Therefore, the experimental design was completed by BBD.

The response in the experiment is the effective extraction radius R (R can be obtained through numerical simulation), with factors X 1, X 2, X 3, X 4, and X 7. In addition, the effects of X 5 and X 6 are weak and have been eliminated. The high level of the factor is an increase of 30% from the initial value, and the low level is a decrease of 30% from the initial value. 3.3. Calculation Model for the Effective Extraction Radius of Bedding Boreholes

To address the problems of difficult construction and long period in the field measurement of the extraction radius, a model for calculating the effective extraction radius of bedding boreholes has been proposed based on regression eq . This model offers advantages of ease of operation and high visualization. The functions of the model include the prediction of the extraction radius and the preservation of historical records. The user can input the coal seam thickness, initial permeability, initial gas pressure, borehole radius, and extraction time in turn according to the parameter prompts. After clicking the ″Calculate″ button, the predicted extraction radius will be displayed in the result column of the model immediately. At the same time, this group of parameters and the corresponding predicted result will be recorded by the system for users to consult the historical calculation data, so as to compare and analyze the extraction effect under different conditions.

This model was applied to calculate the effective extraction radius for the group F 16–17 coal in Pingdingshan No.8 coal mine. The parameters are as follows: the coal thickness is 1.9 m, the initial gas pressure is 1.2 MPa, and the permeability is taken as 1.3 × 10–16 m2 (its permeability is relatively high due to the permeability enhancement measures adopted for this coal seam). In addition, the measured effective extraction radius is 5 m. The effective extraction radius calculated through this model is 4.98 m, and it is basically consistent with the measured radius. 4. Discussion

4.1. Uncertainty in the Multiphysics Coupling Simulation

The uncertainty of numerical simulation in this study mainly comes from three aspects: ① basic assumptions when constructing the model; ② setting of the model boundary conditions; and ③ neglected secondary factors. The specific analysis is as follows.

For source ①, some assumptions were made during model establishment to facilitate the study, such as assuming that the coal seam is homogeneous and gas extraction is an isothermal process. These assumptions do not align with reality and may have a certain impact on the simulation results. For source ②, the setting of model boundary conditions also contributes to the uncertainty. For instance, the boundary conditions of the model in the paper only consider the axial compression, which differs from the triaxial stress state of real coal.

For source ③, in the model, some secondary factors are neglected, including the influence of temperature, moisture content in coal, and geological structures such as faults, folds, and collapse columns. However, under special conditions, these secondary factors may become primary factors affecting gas extraction. For instance, high moisture content can reduce the permeability of coal. Additionally, geological structures such as faults and folds may change the coal structure and gas storage. , Different types of faults can have varying impacts on the permeability of coal seams. , At this moment, these neglected factors may dominate gas seepage under complex geology.

4.2. Mechanism of Interaction Effects of Factors on CBM Extraction

According to Figure , the interaction between the initial permeability and the initial gas pressure, as well as the interaction between the initial gas pressure and the extraction time, has a strong impact on the effective extraction radius. This interaction means that changes in one factor can affect how other factors impact the effective extraction radius.

4.2.1. Interaction between Initial Permeability and Initial Gas Pressure

From Figure d, it can be observed that when the initial gas pressure is low, the effective extraction radius increases rapidly with the increase in initial permeability. When the gas pressure is high, the initial permeability increases by the same magnitude, yet the growth in the extraction radius diminishes significantly. It indicates that the increase in the initial gas pressure restrains the contribution of permeability to the effective extraction radius. Mechanistically, when gas pressure is high, the significant differential pressure between the coalbed and borehole enables smooth gas flow through narrow channels. When the gas pressure is low, the smaller differential pressure is insufficient to drive effective gas migration, and at this point, the permeability becomes the dominant factor. A higher permeability can compensate for the decrease in driving force by improving the flow path.

4.2.2. Interaction between Initial Gas Pressure and Extraction Time

According to Figure f, the high initial gas pressure weakens the effect of the extraction time. It can be observed that when the initial gas pressure is low, the effective extraction radius increases rapidly with the extension of extraction time; however, when the gas pressure is high, the growth in the effective extraction radius is significantly reduced. The reason is that when the gas pressure is high, the pore pressure in the coal drops rapidly during the early stage of extraction, leading to a sharp increase in effective stress. This causes the coal fractures to be compressed and closed and the permeability to decrease significantly. , At this time, the growth of the effective extraction radius slows down. There are similar interactions among other factors as well. Their interactions jointly impact CBM drainage.

4.3. Limitations of the Computational Model

The calculation model of the effective extraction radius developed in this study includes five major factors and their interactions. It can provide reference for the rational layout of boreholes in mines with similar geological conditions to the study area. However, the model still has some limitations, including ① poor universality of the calculation model, ② uncertainty of the calculation results, and ③ uncertainty of parameter acquisition.

For limitation ①, the calculation model constructed based on specific conditions cannot be widely applied due to the complex geological conditions of mines and the differences in the main factors controlling the effective drainage radius. For instance, under the normal geothermal gradient, the coal seam permeability is mainly dominated by in situ stress. However, in some blocks with low effective stress and abnormal high temperature, the thermal stress is greater than the effective stress, and the coal seam permeability may be dominated by temperature. In addition, when determining the parameter values, only the conventional values were considered, and some extreme values, such as extremely low permeability and abnormal high pressure, were not considered, which further weakened the applicability of the model. For limitation ②, some secondary factors were neglected in the model, yet they may considerably impact the effective extraction radius under special conditions. For instance, faults and active groundwater in an area can significantly affect CBM drainage. In summary, the absence of certain factors in the model will lead to inaccurate calculation results. For limitation ③, the basic parameters of coal seams exhibit variability and fluctuation, and the values of the corresponding parameters are different in different regions. The acquisition of relevant parameters is subjective. Due to the influence of subjective factors such as parameter measurement technology and construction proficiency, the test results frequently have errors. These factors can potentially impact the calculation results.

Although the established model is not perfect in certain aspects, in the follow-up study, we will focus on the impact of these special geological conditions on drainage and will also try to collect other independent data sets and carry out relevant validation experiments to continuously improve the accuracy of the model. The method of determining the extraction radius proposed by us has a reference value for zonal drainage and targeted gas control in mines. Usually, mines often use the test data from a certain borehole to guide the gas drainage work of the whole working face or the whole mine. There are many problems in this kind of untargeted drilling arrangement. For other mines, they can refer to the methodology proposed in this paper to determine zonally extraction radii. The main steps of this method are as follows: (1) determining the factors; (2) screening out the main factors through single-factor sensitivity analysis; (3) conducting response surface analysis of the main factors; and (4) establishing an equation between the extraction radius and multiple factors.

5. Conclusions

  • 1.

    According to the sensitivity analysis, the factors influencing the effective extraction radius are ranked in a descending order of their effect as follows: initial gas pressure, coal thickness, initial permeability, extraction time, borehole radius. Especially, the impact degrees of initial permeability and extraction time on the extraction radius are similar. Notably, negative pressure and vertical stress have little effect on the effective extraction radius.

  • 2.

    According to response surface analysis, the interaction between the initial gas pressure and initial permeability, and that between the initial gas pressure and extraction time, significantly affects the effective extraction radius. The interaction indicates that changes in one factor may inhibit the contribution of other factors. Specifically, a higher initial gas pressure weakens the impact of the initial permeability and extraction time on the effective extraction radius. When the CBM extraction efficiency is low, enhancing the permeability and extending the time can improve it.

  • 3.

    A model for calculating the effective extraction radius of bedding boreholes was established based on the regression equation between the effective extraction radius and multiple factors. The effective extraction radius of the group F 16–17 coal in the same mine as the study area is calculated to be 4.98 m through the model, basically consistent with the measured result. It can provide reference for zonal gas drainage in mines.
