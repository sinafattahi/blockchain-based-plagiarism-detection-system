Abstract

The interaction between circular RNAs (circRNAs) and RNA-binding proteins (RBPs) plays a crucial role in gene regulation; however, experimental identification is costly and inefficient. Current computational methods often overlook the structural features of circRNAs, thereby limiting prediction accuracy. To address these challenges, we propose GGCRB, a deep learning framework that integrates both sequence and structural features for predicting circRNA–RBP binding sites. Sequence features are captured through five encoding schemes (HFN, ND, NCP, DPCP, and Doc2Vec), followed by convolutional layers for local pattern extraction. Structural features are derived from base-pairing adjacency matrices generated by RNAstructure and modeled using graph convolutional networks and graph attention networks to learn topological dependencies. The fused representations are further processed by bidirectional LSTM and multihead attention modules to capture global interactions. Final predictions are made through pooling and softmax layers. Extensive experiments on 16 benchmark data sets demonstrate that GGCRB significantly outperforms existing models. Ablation studies and motif analyses further confirm its effectiveness, underscoring the importance of integrating structural and sequence information for accurate prediction of circRNA–RBP interactions.

1. Introduction

CircRNAs feature covalently sealed circular architectures. , They possess disease-modulating expression profiles and functionally impact multiple disorders, especially cancers, − cardiovascular diseases, neurological disorders, and autoimmune diseases. The unique structure of circRNAs enhances stability and protein binding, influencing gene expression and cell processes. As key regulators of RNA metabolism, RBPs orchestrate RNA maturation, cellular trafficking, subcellular positioning, and translational control. Emerging evidence highlights the regulatory supremacy of circRNA-associated proteins in balancing circRNA biogenesis and catabolism. Furthermore, such interactions exhibit pleiotropic effects, spanning from translational regulation to the modulation of pathogenic cascades in various disorders. Although circRNAs have fewer binding sites for RNA-binding proteins (RBPs) than linear mRNAs, strong evidence supports their interactions. Initially regarded as a splicing byproduct with limited importance, circRNA, a type of noncoding RNA, has gained attention due to discoveries enabled by high-throughput sequencing. These advances have promoted computational research on circRNA–protein interactions, disease links, and drug development. Consequently, precise identification of circRNA–RBP binding sites serves dual purposes: elucidating their uncharacterized biological roles while simultaneously revealing novel molecular markers for clinical diagnostics and therapeutic development. ,

The gold-standard experimental pipelines for circRNA–RBP interaction analysis typically involve antibody-based RNA enrichment (RIP), cross-linking-coupled immunoprecipitation (such as CLIP variants), and nucleic acid–protein binding detection via gel shift assays (e.g., EMSA). With their respective technical merits, these experimental strategies have become indispensable for elucidating the nature of RNA–protein associations and precisely localizing binding domains. As an immunocapture-based approach, RIP enables the systematic isolation and identification of naturally occurring RNA–protein assemblies within cells. , EMSA represents a robust biochemical technique that exploits electrophoretic separation principles to detect and quantify interactions between proteins and nucleic acids. CLIP-Seq is a widely used technique. Despite their utility, these experimental approaches frequently require substantial time investment, intensive manual operations, and significant financial resources, posing scalability limitations for high-throughput analyses. Advancements in deep learning architectures are increasingly demonstrating their viability for supplementing traditional experimental paradigms in this research domain. Zhang et al. introduced the CRIP model, employing superimposed codon-based sequence encoding coupled with a hybrid architecture integrating convolutional and recurrent neural networks to extract and represent discriminative features. Jia et al. developed the PASSION framework, employing sequential feature refinement with XGBoost-based selection to identify the most discriminative feature combinations from six distinct circRNA representation approaches, subsequently enhancing predictive accuracy via an integrated deep learning architecture. Yang et al. developed HCRNet, incorporating a novel deep temporal convolution architecture (DTCN) for precise localization of circRNA–RBP binding interfaces and interaction pattern characterization. Niu et al. proposed CRBPDL, which employs an AdaBoost-based ensemble deep network integrating five feature encoding methods alongside MSRN and BiGRU to effectively capture both local and global sequence contexts for predicting circRNA–RBP binding sites. Liu et al. introduced circ-FHN, employing quadruple feature encoding strategies for sequence representation, coupled with a CNN-BiGRU fusion architecture designed to capture both local patterns and extended sequential dependencies through bidirectional gated mechanisms. Lasantha et al. developed CRIECNN, a hybrid architecture combining convolutional layers, bidirectional LSTM, and attention-based feature optimization to predict protein binding patterns across complete circRNA transcripts and their structural subunits.

Although computational methods for identifying circRNA–protein binding sites have made notable progress, several limitations remain unresolved. Most existing approaches primarily rely on the nucleotide sequence of circRNAs, and current feature encoding techniques often neglect structural information, thereby constraining model performance. In recent years, the development of next-generation RNA three-dimensional (3D) conformation prediction methods has provided new opportunities for incorporating structural features. − However, such methods typically require substantial computational resources and manual intervention, particularly when analyzing long-chain RNAs or molecules with complex spatial folding. Due to challenges related to sequence length, resolution, and cost, these 3D-based approaches are difficult to scale for large-scale applications. Therefore, integrating more accessible secondary structure information on circRNAs may help improve the accuracy of interaction prediction.

To predict the binding sites of circular RNAs and proteins, we developed the GGCRB model, which effectively integrates structural and sequence features through graph convolution and attention mechanisms, enhancing the feature representation capability. This method combines nucleotide sequence information with RNA secondary structure features to achieve a comprehensive characterization of multiscale features of circular RNAs. In sequence feature extraction, we introduced five complementary encoding strategies: HNF, ND, NCP, DPCP, and Doc2Vec to enhance the expressiveness of sequence information. Structural features were extracted using the RNAstructure tool. To fully exploit the advanced features of structural information, we designed a hybrid learning framework combining graph convolutional networks (GCN) and graph attention networks (GAT) and used convolutional neural networks (CNN) to extract advanced features of sequences. The fused multiscale features were further modeled by BiLSTM to capture sequence patterns and context dependencies, and a multihead attention layer was used for adaptive feature weighting to capture global dependencies. The model was evaluated on 16 benchmark data sets using five-fold cross-validation, achieving an overall AUC of 0.9634. The results demonstrate that GGCRB outperforms existing methods in the task of circular RNA–protein binding site prediction, proving its effectiveness.

In this study, we propose a graph neural network framework that integrates sequence and structural features for predicting RBP binding sites on circRNA. The main contributions are summarized as follows:

  • 1.

    We design a multiscale feature fusion approach that integrates both sequence and structural features of circRNAs. This overcomes the limitations of single-modality representation in previous methods and enhances the model’s ability to capture complex biological information.

  • 2.

    We construct diverse sequence feature matrices using multiple encoding schemes, including physicochemical properties, k-mer features, and pretrained embeddings. In parallel, we extract structure feature matrices from RNA secondary structures, considering base pairing and thermodynamic properties, thus enriching the model input with both local and global structural contexts.

  • 3.

    We develop a hybrid graph neural network combining GCN and GAT to learn topological dependencies from circRNA structure graphs. This enables effective modeling of intricate node–edge relationships and improves the prediction accuracy of circRNA–RBP interaction sites.

The model first extracts sequence features using multiple encoding methods and simultaneously processes RNA secondary structure information via GCN and GAT. These multiscale features are then fused and passed through convolutional layers (Conv1D), bidirectional LSTM, and multihead attention mechanisms to accurately predict circRNA–RBP binding sites.

2.1. Data Sets

Sixteen circRNA–RBP interaction data sets are collected from the circRNA–protein interaction database to evaluate the performance of GGCRB. CD-HIT is employed to remove redundant sequences from the circRNA data set, ensuring nonrepetitive data for downstream analysis. A 101-base pair genomic segment is isolated, spanning 50 nucleotides in both directions from the CLIP-seq peak summit. Binding-positive examples are obtained from high-confidence CLIP-seq peaks, with binding-negative examples generated through random selection from nonpeak circRNA segments. A balanced 1:1 ratio between binding-positive and binding-negative instances is maintained across all data sets to prevent class imbalance. In the 16 data sets, there are 189,620 positive and negative samples, with each data set containing up to 40,000 circRNA sequence samples. All data sets are partitioned into an 80–20 split, with the majority allocated for model training and the reserved subset for performance evaluation.

2.2. Sequence Feature Extraction

HNF combines nucleotide codon spatial superposition encoding and k-mer encoding methods to comprehensively capture the local contextual sequence information. HNF uses k-mer encoding for single nucleotides and dinucleotides and codon stacking encoding for trinucleotides. k-Mer encoding (k = 1 or 2) extracts local sequence patterns by representing contiguous nucleotide groups, thereby preserving neighborhood relationships within the sequence. Consider a circRNA sequence S = (c 1, c 2, c 3, ... c l ) of fixed length l = 101, where each c i denotes a nucleotide position. The sequence is encoded as 20-dimensional one-hot vectors (combining monomer and dimer features). This procedure can be formally expressed by

Xk=[x1k,x2,k,...,xnk] 1

Specifically, single-nucleotide (one-mer) encoding generates a matrix of size N 1 × 4, dinucleotide (two-mer) encoding generates a matrix of size N 2 × 16, and trinucleotide (three-mer) encoding generates a matrix of size N 3 × 21, where N is the unified sequence length after preprocessing. Through feature matrix concatenation, we generate an N × 41 integrated representation that comprehensively encodes circRNA’s sequential properties. The entries of the matrix satisfy

Xi,j={1,ifci=jth0,else 2

The subscript i specifies the ordinal position of nucleotides along the circRNA sequence S, and j th is from the set {A, G, C, U} (mononucleotide), {AA, AC, ..., UU} (dinucleotide), and {AAA, AAC, ..., UUU} (trinucleotides). X i captures nucleotide units of size 1–3 at site c i . For the standard 101-nt circRNA, this produces a 41-dimensional feature vector per position, resulting in a 101 × 41 matrix.

Nucleotide density (ND) encoding is a feature representation method that combines nucleotide position information with frequency information to describe the distribution characteristics of nucleotides in a circRNA sequence. Given a circRNA sequence S = {s 1, s 2, s 3, ..., s l }, where s i ∈ {A, G, C, U} denotes the nucleotide at position i, with l being the sequence length, the core idea of this method is to calculate the density value of each nucleotide at a specific position to reflect its distribution weight within the entire sequence. The nucleotide density d i at position i is formally expressed by

di=1∥Si∥∑j=1pf(Sj) 3

where ∥S i ∥ represents the length of the subsequence from the beginning of the sequence (the first nucleotide) to the current position (the ith nucleotide), which is used to normalize the nucleotide density and ensure consistency in the features. p represents the range of the cumulative window, i.e., the cardinality of nucleotide positions encompassed by the density metric. f(S j ) is an indicator function used to check whether the nucleotide at position j is equal to the target nucleotide q:

f(q)={1,ifsj=q0,elseq∈{A,G,C,U}andj∈1,2,3,...,l 4

That is, if the current nucleotide s i equals the target nucleotide q, then f(q) = 1; otherwise, it is 0. Using the above formula, the density information for each nucleotide at different positions can be computed. For example, a circRNA sequence S = GAAUCUACU with length l = 9 is considered. For the target nucleotide q = A, we calculate the density values d i for each position. At position 3, the subsequence is GAA, where the target nucleotide A appears two times and the subsequence length is 3, so d 3 = 0.67. At position 6, the subsequence is GAAUCU, where the target nucleotide A appears two times and the subsequence length is 6, so d 6 = 0.33. By performing similar calculations for each position, we can obtain the density matrix for the entire sequence. The same method can be applied to calculate densities for other nucleotides (G, C, U), ultimately generating a 9 × 4 density feature matrix.

The dinucleotide physicochemical property (DPCP) is a method for characterizing RNA sequences by extracting the physical and chemical properties of dinucleotides. DPCP provides an efficient way to gain insight into the structure and stability of nucleic acid molecules by quantifying the spatial and energetic properties of dinucleotides. It contains 11 key physicochemical properties, which specifically include rise, translation, slide, tilt, stacking energy, roll, torsion, enthalpy change, entropy change, hydrophilicity, and free energy. The values of DPCP i (j) are provided in Supplementary Table S1. The formula for DPCP is given below:

DPCP(i)=freq(i)×DPCPi(j) 5

where freq­(i) represents the frequency of the ith dinucleotide pair, i.e., the frequency of the ith dinucleotide pair appearing in the given sequence. DPCP i (j) represents the value of the physicochemical property (e.g., stacking energy, rise, shift, etc.) associated with the ith dinucleotide pair. This value corresponds to a specific physicochemical property of the dinucleotide pair. DPCP­(i) represents the weighted physicochemical property value, which is obtained by multiplying the frequency of the ith dinucleotide with its corresponding physicochemical property value. DPCP results in a 101 × 11 feature matrix.

Structural variations among circRNA nucleotides confer distinct biochemical functionalities, influencing their biological roles. Based on the chemical properties of these nucleotides, we have classified them into three major groups, each with unique physical and chemical properties. These three groups are categorized as follows:

ringstructure={purine,A,Gpyrimidine,C,Ufunctionalgroup={amino,A,Cketo,G,Uhydrogenbond={weak,A,Ustrong,G,C 6

Based on the above classification, we define a three-dimensional coordinate system for each nucleotide to describe the chemical properties in sequence analysis. Specifically, each nucleotide is encoded as three binary attributes (x i , y i , z i ), where the values depend on the category to which the nucleotide belongs. The encoding rules are as follows:

xi={1,ifNi∈{A,G}0,ifNi∈{C,U}yi={1,ifNi∈{A,C}0,ifNi∈{G,U}zi={1,ifNi∈{A,U}0,ifNi∈{C,G} 7

In this three-dimensional coordinate system, nucleotides {A, G, C, U} are uniquely encoded as three-bit vectors: (1,1,1), (1,0,0), (0,1,0), and (0,0,1) respectively. Using this encoding scheme, we assign a three-dimensional vector to each nucleotide, allowing us to quantify its differences in various physicochemical properties. By applying these encodings to each nucleotide in the circRNA sequence, we construct a feature matrix of size (101 × 3), where each row corresponds to a nucleotide in the sequence, and each column represents its binary values across the three different categories.

Doc2Vec is a natural language processing technique for converting text, sequences, or documents into fixed-length dense vector representations. Doc2Vec is widely used for long sequence feature extraction and representation learning. Compared with Word2Vec’s distributed representation for words only, Doc2Vec is able to learn semantic features of complete sequences, which is suitable for higher-level feature analysis. In Doc2Vec, each sequence is treated as a separate “document”. The algorithm captures local and global semantic features by encoding the contextual information in the sequence as numeric vectors and generates a fixed-dimensional representation. Input sequences of variable lengths are transformed into output vectors with uniform dimensionality, standardizing feature space for ML processing. Doc2Vec adopts the process of partitioning a circRNA sequence into fixed-length fragments (e.g., 101-nucleotide sequences). Each sequence fragment is treated as a document, and the context of the nucleotide fragment is used as the “word”. The distribution of nucleotides in the sequence is characterized by Doc2Vec, which embeds the sequence fragments as fixed-dimensional feature vectors. After training, the model is used to transform all circRNA sequences into vector representations that serve as inputs to downstream classification or regression models. Compared with traditional sequence representation methods, Doc2Vec generates length-agnostic sequence embeddings by modeling global contextual relationships via distributed representations, enabling robust feature extraction.

2.3. Structure Feature Extraction

In this work, we employ RNAstructure to determine circRNA secondary structures, generating a 101 × 101 feature matrix that captures local and global RNA structural characteristics. The 101-nt circRNA fragments contain 50-nt sequences on both sides of the target binding region. This length setting ensures that the contextual information surrounding the binding site is sufficiently captured, while avoiding redundancy or computational overhead caused by longer sequences. We preprocess the circRNA sequences and utilize RNAstructure to predict their two-dimensional folding configurations. The algorithm employs MFE as the optimization criterion to compute nucleotide pairing probabilities and energy variations. The predicted results are typically output as a CT (connectivity table) file.

The CT file describes the positions of nucleotides in the sequence and the index of each nucleotide’s paired base (if there is no pairing, the index is 0). For example, given a 101-nucleotide circRNA sequence, the RNAstructure tool will output a data table with 101 rows, where each row records the specific pairing information on a nucleotide. Given a circRNA sequence S = {N 1, N 2, ..., N n } of length n, where nucleotides N i take values from {A, U, C, G}, RNAstructure produces the pairing set P = {(i, j)|where N i pairs with N j }. Following this, the adjacency matrix A can be formally defined:

A[i,j]={1,if(i,j)∈Por(j,i)∈P0,else 8

The constructed adjacency matrix A is an n × n sparse matrix. When a ij = 1, this denotes a base-pairing interaction between nucleotides at positions i and j.

2.4. Feature Fusion and Prediction

2.4.1. Convolutional Neural Networks

CNN are a powerful tool widely used in deep learning, particularly well-suited for autonomously extracting feature representations from data. Via convolutional operations, CNN hierarchically extracts features from input data, enabling robust identification of localized patterns and intricate nonlinear correlations. The CNN in this study is mainly used for the extraction of sequence features, where the sequence coding matrix of cyclic RNA (circRNA) is processed by a one-dimensional convolution operation to extract potential feature representations. The matrix input to the CNN is H, which has a size of m × l, where m denotes the total dimension of the features, which are generated by multiple coding methods (e.g., HNF, DPCP, ND, and NCP), and the dimensions of the features for each coding method are 4141, 1111, 101, and 303, respectively, for a total of m = 56. The circRNA fragment has a length (L) of 101 nucleotides. As a key element in CNNs, the 1D convolution operation facilitates the capture of local sequence patterns. If the convolution kernel has a length of h r , then it slides from H’s first l columns, yielding an output feature per position. The convolution formula is as follows:

yij=ReLU(Xi×W[:,:,j]+bj) 9

where X i is the submatrix consisting of columns i through i + h r – 1 of the original input matrix H. The weight matrix W and bias term b are associated with each convolutional filter. Here, j indicates the filter index, and yij corresponds to the output generated by the jth filter at location i.

2.4.2. Graph Convolutional Networks

This research employs GCN to derive circRNA’s topological characteristics by analyzing the RNA adjacency matrix and identity matrix, enabling the capture of nucleotide interactions and overall structural patterns through graph-based feature aggregation. Matrix A captures base-pairing interactions within the RNA secondary structure, configured as a 101 × 101 sparse matrix. The unit matrix I serves as the initial feature of each node and is used to ensure that the node maintains self-information (i.e., the node’s own features) during computation at each level, rather than relying exclusively on the features of its neighbors. The matrix size is also 101 × 101. By adding self-loops, nodes can integrate both neighborhood information and their intrinsic features during message passing. The basic operation of the GCN is graph convolution, which is the updating of each node’s features using the node’s local neighborhood information. The formula for each layer is as follows:

H(l+1)=ReLU(D̃−1/2ÃD̃−1/2H(l)W(l)) 10

where H (l) denotes the layer l node feature matrix, initially set as the identity matrix I. Ã = A + I joins the adjacency matrix of the self-loop, indicating that each node can also use its own information. D̃ represents the degree matrix of nodes, while W (l) corresponds to the trainable weight matrix at layer l.

2.4.3. Graph Attention Networks

GAT employs multihead attention mechanisms to dynamically compute varying importance scores for neighboring nodes in graph-structured data. In this study, GAT is used to further extract RNA structural features, addressing the limitation of uniform weights in GCN and emphasizing the importance of specific structures. Unlike GCN, which uses a fixed normalized adjacency matrix, GAT utilizes attention-based computation to determine the significance of adjacent nodes, allowing for a more flexible focus on relationships between key bases. The GAT layer performs secondary processing on the initial node features extracted by GCN, further enriching the node representations, especially by assigning higher weights to critical regions of the RNA (such as important pairing sites). GAT’s attention mechanism computes varying attention coefficients for each node’s local neighborhood, reflecting the relative significance of neighboring features. The calculation formula for the weight of each edge is as follows:

aij=exp(leakyReLU(aT[Whi∥Whj]))∑k∈N(i)exp(leakyReLU(aT[Whi∥Whk])) 11

h i and h j correspond to the feature embeddings of node i and node j, respectively. W serves as the learnable transformation matrix for node features, while a functions as the attention vector that computes edge importance scores. ∥ denotes the vector concatenation operation.

By applying attention weights, through feature aggregation, the central node’s embedding is updated based on its neighborhood information, as expressed by the following formula:

hi(l+1)=ReLU(∑j∈N(i)aijWhj) 12

The weight matrix W is a learnable component that undergoes optimization during GAT training.

Enhancing model stability, GAT employs multihead attention that calculates several distinct attention patterns and combines their outputs to produce more comprehensive features, as formulated below:

hi(l+1)=∥k=1Kσ(∑j∈N(i)aij(k)W(K)hj(l)) 13

where K represents the total count of attention heads, with the symbol ∥ indicating feature vector concatenation.

The GCN and GAT hybrid architecture effectively captures fundamental RNA graph characteristics while dynamically weighting feature significance via attention mechanisms, enabling comprehensive RNA structural representation.

2.4.4. BiLSTM

BiLSTM is an important module for further mining temporal dependencies and contextual information from multiscale features. By capturing information from both the forward and backward directions of the input features, BiLSTM can effectively extract bidirectional dependencies within the features, providing a more comprehensive contextual representation for downstream tasks. BiLSTM considers information from both the preceding and succeeding directions in a single time step to effectively learn the latent contextual dependencies within circRNA sequences or feature matrices. Unlike static feature extraction methods such as convolution, BiLSTM dynamically captures the long-range dependencies within feature sequences, making it suitable for biological sequences like RNA, which exhibit complex dependency patterns. BiLSTM receives fused inputs from sequence features (extracted by Conv1D) and structural features (extracted by GCN and GAT), enabling further dynamic combination of these features.

The BiLSTM takes as input the multimodally fused sequence features of dimensionality RT×d , with T corresponding to the temporal steps and d denoting the feature embedding size. Information is extracted through both the forward and backward LSTMs.

In the forward direction, the LSTM unit iteratively processes temporal data starting from the initial to the terminal time step. The formula is as follows:

ht→=LSTM(xt,ht−1→) 14

Here, ht→ represents the current time step’s hidden state, with ht−1→ being its immediate predecessor in the sequence.

Operating in reverse chronological order, the backward LSTM analyzes the input sequence starting from the final time step back to the initial one. The mathematical formulation is presented below:

ht←=LSTM(xt,ht+1←) 15

Here, ht← captures the system state at time step t, while ht+1← represents its immediate successor in the temporal sequence.

BiLSTM concatenates the forward and backward hidden states to generate a comprehensive bidirectional feature representation.

ht=[ht→;ht←],ht∈R2dh 16

Here, h t represents the consolidated feature embedding at time step t, encapsulating both local and global contextual information.

The final output is a full sequence of hidden state matrices:

H=[h1,h2,...,hT,],H∈RT×2dh 17

LSTM serves as the foundational component of BiLSTM. Given input sequence X = [x 1, x 2, ..., x n ], the computation at time step t is defined by

ft=σ(Wf·[ht−1,xt]+bf) 18
it=σ(Wi·[ht−1,xt]+bi) 19
C̃t=tanh(WC·[ht−1,xt]+bC) 20
Ct=ft⊙Ct−1+it⊙C̃t 21
ot=σ(Wo·[ht−1,xt]+bo) 22
ht=ot⊙tanh(Ct) 23

Here, f t represents the forget gate’s activation value, quantifying how much information from the prior time step is preserved. W f and b f correspond to the learnable parameters of the forget gate, specifically the transformation weights and offset terms. h t–1 represents the LSTM’s hidden state from the preceding time step. x t represents the input feature vector at time step t. σ denotes the activation function. i t represents the input gate’s activation value, quantifying how much new information is stored at time step t. C̃t represents the candidate memory content proposed for time step t, while C t denotes the finalized memory state at this time step. ⊙ denotes element-by-element multiplication. o t represents the output gate’s activation value, regulating the exposure degree of the cell state at time step t. h t represents the LSTM’s hidden state at time step t, demonstrating the network’s capability to model distant temporal relationships.

2.4.5. Multihead Attention

This work employs a multihead self-attention architecture to capture long-range interactions in circRNA sequences. This approach enhances the model’s representational power through parallel computation of attention distributions across multiple projected feature subspaces. Specifically, the multiattention mechanism receives the time step sequence feature output from BiLSTM as input, computes the correlation between each time step and other time steps, and thus generates a global sequence representation containing contextual information. For a circRNA sequence of length 101, the output of each attention head is spliced and linearly transformed to generate new sequence features, providing richer feature representations for the subsequent fully connected layer. Empirical evidence demonstrates that the MHA architecture substantially strengthens the model’s capacity for capturing distal sequence relationships, leading to superior predictive outcomes.

The MHA architecture extends single-head attention by first projecting input features H∈RT×d into triplets of query (Q), key (K), and value (V) matrices. Attention weights are derived through computing similarity scores via Q and K dot products, applying softmax normalization, and parallel computation across h independent heads, each operating in distinct feature subspaces. The mathematical formulation is

Q=HWQ,K=HWK,V=HWV 24
attention(Q,K,V)=softmax(QKTdk)V 25
multihead(Q,K,V)=concat(head1,head2,...,headh)WO 26
headi=attention(QWiQ,KWiK,VWiV) 27

where T is the sequence length and d is the feature dimension. WQ,WK,andWV∈Rd×dk are the learnable projection matrices, and d k is the feature dimension of each attention head. Q,K,andV∈RT×d are the query, key, and value matrices obtained by linearly transforming the input sequence. The attention weights are calculated through a scaled dot-product operation QKTdk , where d k represents the key vector dimension. This scaling prevents gradient vanishing issues caused by large values. The architecture employs h parallel attention heads, each with dedicated projection matrices WiQ,WiK,andWiV∈Rd×dk and final output transformation W O .

2.4.6. The Fully Connected Layers

Positioned after the multihead attention module, the FC layer transforms high-dimensional sequence features into lower-dimensional classification representations. The FC layer projects these high-dimensional representations to a reduced feature space while integrating global sequence information. The FC layer processes global features Z∈RT×dout generated by the attention module, with T denoting sequence length and d out representing feature dimensionality. These representations are subsequently merged to form a unified global feature vector Zglobal∈Rdout . The fully connected layer consists of two layers of neurons. The initial layer processes Z global through an affine mapping succeeded by a nonlinearity, producing O fc1.The output of the first layer is further mapped to the classifier’s dimension space, resulting in O fc2. Following processing by both FC layers, the softmax layer ultimately transforms the output into class probabilities.

Ofc1=g(Wfc1·Zglobal+bfc1) 28
Ofc2=g(Wfc2·Ofc1+bfc2) 29
P=softmax(Ofc2) 30

The architecture comprises two fully connected layers: the first layer applies a linear transformation parametrized by weight matrix Wfc1∈Rdfc1×dout and bias vector bfc1∈Rdfc1 , followed by ReLU activation g. The subsequent layer processes features using parameters Wfc2∈Rdfc2×dfc1 and bfc2∈Rdfc2 , ultimately producing class probability distribution P∈Rnclass through the output layer.

2.4.7. Loss Function

The model is optimized using a cross-entropy minimization criterion as the objective. The loss function aims to reduce the discrepancy between model predictions and ground truth labels, thereby enhancing circRNA–RBP interaction classification performance. Its mathematical expression is

L=−1N∑i=1N[yilog(y^i)+(1−yi)log(1−y^i)] 31

where ŷ i represents the predicted value of the ith instance and y i corresponds to the ground truth annotation, with N indicating the batch size.

3. Results

3.1. Performance Evaluation

To holistically assess the model’s forecasting capability, this research implements quintuple cross-validation testing on 16 circular RNA collections and uses five commonly employed evaluation metrics: AUC-ROC, predictive precision, true positive rate, F1-index, and overall classification accuracy. Among these, AUC is the primary evaluation metric to measure the model’s discriminative ability, with a value range from 0.5 (random guessing) to 1 (perfect prediction). The mathematical definitions of these metrics are as follows:

precision=TPTP+FP 32
recall=TPTP+FN 33
F1=2×precision×recallprecision+recall 34
ACC=TP+TNTP+TN+FP+FN 35

where TP represents correctly identified interacting circRNA–RBP pairs, while TN denotes properly recognized noninteracting pairs. FN indicates missed true interactions, and FP corresponds to erroneously predicted nonexistent interactions.

GGCRB’s forecasting efficacy was systematically examined across 16 circRNA–protein interaction collections, and the results are shown in Figure and Table , demonstrating the model performance on the five evaluated metrics. In all data sets, the AUC of GGCRB is more than 0.94 on 15 data sets; especially, on the AUF1, HNRNPC, PUM2, and TAF15 data sets, the AUC is more than 0.99.

The results, detailed in Figure and Table , demonstrate that GGCRB consistently outperforms competing methods. Specifically, GGCRB achieves the highest AUC scores on 14 out of the 16 data sets, with particularly notable improvements on MOV10, IGF2BP1, IGF2BP3, LIN28A, and PTB data sets. 3.3. Ablation Experiments

To validate the effectiveness of individual components in the GGCRB model, we designed eight ablation experiments to systematically assess the impact of different feature encoding strategies and structural modules on model performance. GGCRB integrates five types of feature encoding methods and incorporates GCN, GAT, and multihead attention mechanisms. The specific combinations of modules are summarized in Table , and their corresponding results are illustrated in the bar chart shown in Figure . The configurations and motivations of each variant are as follows:

  • M1 removes NCP and DPCP encodings to evaluate model performance under the absence of nucleotide physicochemical properties, which represent low-level sequence features.

  • M2 eliminates HNF and ND encodings to assess the contribution of higher-order k-mer frequency and nucleotide density features.

  • M3 removes the Doc2Vec encoding to investigate the role of deep semantic information derived from a global sequence context.

  • M4 removes all structural features and disables the GCN and GAT modules, aiming to determine whether structural information is critical to performance improvement.

  • M5 retains all feature encodings but replaces the graph-based modules (GCN and GAT) with conventional CNNs, to compare the effectiveness of graph neural networks versus convolutional approaches in structural modeling.

  • M6 removes only the HNF encoding to further isolate and examine its individual contribution to sequence representation.

  • M7 preserves the full architecture except for the removal of the multihead attention mechanism, in order to evaluate its impact on modeling global sequence dependencies.

  • M8 simplifies the original three-layer structure module (“GCN → GAT → GCN”) to a two-layer configuration (“GCN → GAT”) to assess the effect of structural modeling depth.

4. Discussion

The proposed GGCRB model effectively integrates multiscale features and shows promising performance in circRNA–RBP binding site prediction. The combination of GCN and GAT enables effective learning of RNA secondary structure topology, while multihead attention captures long-range sequence dependencies, thereby enhancing feature representation. However, GGCRB has limitations. The reliance on predicted RNA secondary structures may not fully capture dynamic or tertiary conformations, potentially limiting structural feature fidelity. Additionally, the limited diversity and size of available circRNA–RBP data sets constrain the model’s generalizability, especially for rare interaction types. The model’s complexity also entails considerable computational cost, which may hinder scalability. Our future work will explore advanced architectures such as graph autoencoders and transformers to better capture complex RNA features. Expanding and diversifying data sets will improve robustness and applicability. Finally, optimizing computational efficiency will facilitate broader application in large-scale and real-time scenarios.

5. Conclusions

The superiority of GGCRB stems from its efficient fusion of multiscale features. This model combines multiple sequence encoding methods and utilizes a hybrid GCN and GAT framework to capture structural features, thereby comprehensively capturing both local and global features of circular RNAs. Overall, GGCRB offers a new and effective solution for the prediction of circular RNA–RBP interactions and provides valuable references for the optimization and improvement of future models.
