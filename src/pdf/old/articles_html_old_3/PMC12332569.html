Abstract

Phosphorus-based anode materials exhibit a high theoretical specific capacity as negative electrodes in sodium ion batteries. However, their low conductivity necessitates compounding with carbon materials. The alloying process between phosphorus and sodium in phosphorus/carbon anodes results in significant volume expansion, leading to phosphorus powdering and detachment, thereby compromising the battery cycle life. Mitigating the volume expansion of phosphorus-based materials and enhancing the interfacial bonding between phosphorus and carbon are effective strategies to address these challenges. This review discusses advancements in phosphorus/carbon anode materials focusing on size and structure control of phosphorus-based materials, composite modification of carbon materials with diverse structures, and interface reinforcement in phosphorus/carbon composites. The analysis and comparison of various methodologies shed light on research approaches and design strategies for phosphorus/carbon anode materials, offering insights for enhancing the cycle life of sodium ion batteries.

1. Introduction

Phosphorus-based materials show great promise as anodes for high-capacity sodium-ion batteries (SIBs), boasting a theoretical specific capacity of 2596 mAh g–1, surpassing commercial graphite materials at 372 mAh g–1. − While white phosphorus (WP) is highly reactive and flammable in air, rendering it unsuitable for SIB negative electrodes, red phosphorus (RP) offers stability and abundant availability. Black phosphorus (BP) exhibits a high theoretical specific capacity, rapid charging capabilities, structural stability, and swift reaction kinetics akin to metal phosphides, making it a focus of research for secondary battery applications. − Despite these advantages, the inherently low ionic and electronic conductivity of phosphorus (<10–14 S/cm–1) limits its reactivity. − Consequently, the incorporation of high-conductivity carbon materials is typically required to enhance the electrochemical performance of phosphorus-based anodes. − Conversely, the alloying reaction between phosphorus and sodium results in a significant volume expansion exceeding 300%, causing the breakdown of active materials and the cyclic fracture and restoration of solid-electrolyte interphase (SEI) films. This phenomenon poses a challenge to the widespread utilization of phosphorus-based anodes in sodium-ion batteries. − Current approaches involve diverse strategies, including structural manipulation and interface adjustments, to enhance the conductivity disparity and mitigate volume expansion in phosphorus-based anode materials, as depicted in Figure .

2. Size and Structure Modulation of Red Phosphorus in Phosphorus–Carbon Composites

Through the dimensional and structural regulation of phosphorus–carbon composites, the volume expansion and pulverization during charge/discharge cycles in sodium-ion batteries can be effectively mitigated. Techniques such as ball milling or evaporation–condensation methods enable precise adjustment of red phosphorus’s particle size and architecture, thereby increasing the specific surface area, shortening ion diffusion pathways, and enhancing electrolyte-electrode interfacial contact. , This strategic optimization of phosphorus morphology and configuration serves as a critical approach to enhance the electrochemical activity of phosphorus/carbon-based composites as advanced anode materials for sodium-ion batteries.

Gao et al. developed a three-dimensional carbon/red phosphorus/graphene aerogel (C@P/GA) composite through an advanced vapor redistribution strategy. By employing morphological engineering, red phosphorus nanoparticles (10–20 nm) were uniformly encapsulated within a 3D conductive carbon matrix (as shown in Figure a). During electrochemical processes (P + 3Na+ + 3e– ↔ Na3P), the flexible carbon framework and GA architecture effectively accommodated the volume expansion of red phosphorus. XRD analysis revealed an interlayer spacing of 0.369 nm in C@P/GA, facilitating sodium-ion intercalation. As a sodium-ion battery (SIB) anode material, it demonstrated a capacity of 1095.5 mA h g–1 after 200 cycles at 1.6 A g–1. Confining red phosphorus within this 3D conductive network maintained the structural integrity and enhanced the electrochemical performance. However, the high cost of graphene oxide (GO) presents challenges for commercial scalability. Liu et al. fabricated a red phosphorus/reduced graphene oxide (P@RGO) composite via physical vapor deposition (PVD) under vacuum conditions. In this process, RP vapor was infiltrated into the interlayer spaces of RGO, and controlled growth of RP nanodots (79, 161, and 223 nm) was achieved during condensation. The reduced particle size enhanced the specific surface area, providing abundant active sites and improving sodium-ion adsorption/desorption efficiency. The layered RGO matrix served as a uniform deposition substrate, while the homogeneous distribution of nanodots mitigated the localized stress concentration and alleviated volume expansion during sodiation/desodiation (as illustrated in Figure b). As a sodium-ion-battery (SIB) anode, the composite delivered a capacity of 914 mAh g–1 after 300 cycles at 1.6 A g–1. Despite achieving nanoscale RP particles via PVD, the relatively large particle sizes still induced significant volume expansion rates, leading to potential structural collapse of the layered architecture during repeated alloying/dealloying processes.

Liu et al. synthesized a red phosphorus@carbon nanofiber (RP@CNF) composite via an evaporation–condensation method. Flash thermal treatment was subsequently applied to remove surface-deposited RP, confining most of the RP within the CNF interior to form a 1D architecture. This design endowed the sodium-ion battery anode with an exceptional ductility. Finite element analysis revealed that the CNF shell mechanically constrained the radial expansion of RP, converting it into longitudinal elongation. This mechanism effectively reduced internal stresses (e.g., transforming hoop stress from tensile to compressive) and suppressed crack formation (as depicted in Figure c). The RP@CNF anode demonstrated a capacity retention of 1850 mA h g–1 after 500 cycles at 0.1 A g–1 and maintained a charge capacity exceeding 1000 mAh g–1 even after 5000 cycles at 1 A g–1. The self-supporting P@CNF electrode eliminated the need for binders or conductive additives, simplifying fabrication and reducing costs, thus showing promise for scalable applications. Hu et al. employed a chemical bonding strategy to prepare a P-SPAN composite anode through high-energy ball milling of red phosphorus with sulfurized polyacrylonitrile (SPAN) (as shown in Figure d). The C–S–S moieties in SPAN exhibited high reactivity, facilitating the formation of P–S chemical bonds during milling, which strengthened the interfacial adhesion between phosphorus and the matrix. The P-SPAN composite displayed a significantly higher Na+ diffusion coefficient compared to nonbonded counterparts, indicating enhanced kinetics. As a sodium-ion-battery (SIB) anode, it delivered a capacity of 1355 mAh g–1 after 100 cycles at 0.52 A g–1.

The performance of red phosphorus (RP)-based anodes has been significantly enhanced through nanosizing, conductive network construction, and interface optimization, effectively addressing the challenges of volume expansion and structural instability. The RP@CNF composite fabricated via the evaporation–condensation method demonstrates exceptional electrochemical properties through unique mechanical confinement and self-supporting design. However, the high cost of graphene/CNF materials and energy-intensive processes (e.g., PVD, flash thermal treatment) hinder their scalability. Future efforts should prioritize low-cost alternatives such as waste-biomass-derived carbon (e.g., porous carbon from coconut shells or straw) to replace graphene. Concurrent optimization of process parameters (e.g., ball-milling duration and temperature) is required to balance performance and energy efficiency. Further exploration of cost-effective and ecofriendly scalable fabrication routes remains critical for practical implementation.

3.1. Composite Modification of Phosphorus-Based Materials by Carbon Nanofibers and Carbon Nanotubes

Both carbon nanofibers (CNFs) and carbon nanotubes (CNTs) are effective in constructing conductive network architectures. CNFs offer shorter ion and electron transport pathways, while CNTs exhibit high aspect ratios and uniform pore structure distribution. , Such interconnected conductive networks not only enhance electron transport and electrical conductivity but also maintain structural integrity during sodiation processes. These advantages have driven extensive research into their applications in phosphorus-based anode materials for sodium-ion batteries. −

Compared to conventional powdered carbon materials, electrospun carbon nanofibers (CNFs) offer superior intercalation kinetics, shorter ion/electron transport pathways, and increased active sites for sodium storage. The composite of red phosphorus (RP) with CNFs effectively addresses RP’s inherent low conductivity during cycling. Liu et al. synthesized a phosphorus–carbon composite (P@C) via electrospinning, embedding RP nanoparticles uniformly within porous nitrogen-doped nanofibers. The freestanding P@C membrane (illustrated in Figure a) served directly as a sodium-ion battery (SIB) anode. The interconnected CNF network significantly enhanced the conductivity, enabling the composite to deliver a capacity of 650 mAh g–1 after 1000 cycles at 2 A g–1. When paired with a Na3V2(PO4)2F3/C cathode in a pouch cell configuration (cathode mass loading: 4.3 mg cm–2), the full cell retained a capacity of 848 mAh g–1 after 100 cycles at 1 A g–1. Ding et al. fabricated hollow nanofiber composites (CSP@C) via electrospinning combined with graphene under ambient conditions. The hollow structure effectively buffers volume expansion during charge/discharge cycles, while density functional theory calculations confirm that Cu atoms dispersed within the phosphide matrix not only enhance capacity but also improve electrochemical performance. As a sodium-ion battery anode, the material maintains approximately 80% capacity retention after 200 cycles at 0.05 A g–1 (with a mass loading of ∼1.2 mg cm–2 per electrode). Although the layered tubular CSP exhibits superior melting temperature and enhanced stability - crucial characteristics for improving battery safety and facilitating large-scale applications in automotive power batteries and energy storage systems - its practical implementation remains constrained by relatively low specific capacity and energy density.

Lei et al. fabricated a composite anode material for SIBs by integrating phosphorus-rich phosphorus selenide (P9Se) with porous carbon nanotubes (pCNTs) via an evaporation–condensation method (as shown in Figure b). Selenium doping lowers the Fermi level (Ef) of sodiated products (Ef of Na3P decreases from −0.33 eV to −0.39 eV), promoting deeper alloying reactions. The formed P–Se bonds suppress electrolyte decomposition side reactions, enabling a uniform SEI layer formation and enhanced interfacial stability (Figure c). By optimizing the thermodynamic and kinetic properties of sodiated products (e.g., improved ion transport), this anode delivers initial discharge/charge capacities of 1937.5 mAh g–1 and 1665.1 mAh g–1 at 0.2 A g–1, respectively, and retains a reversible capacity of 1318 mAh g–1 after 200 cycles at 2 A g–1 (electrode mass loading: 0.5–1 mg cm–2). Liu et al. synthesized a mesoporous carbon framework (TBMC) by simultaneously growing resorcinol-formaldehyde resin/silica (SiO2) on carbon nanotubes, followed by resin carbonization and SiO2 removal. The P@TBMC composite (Figure d) was prepared via a vaporization-condensation-conversion strategy. Owing to the high sp2 carbon content facilitating electron transfer, the P@TBMC anode achieves 756.8 mAh g–1 after 150 cycles at 0.25 A g–1 (84.2% retention) and maintains 580 mAh g–1 after 800 cycles at 2.5 A g–1 with negligible capacity decay (mass loading: 0.8–1 mg cm–2). A full cell assembled with the Na3V2(PO4)2 cathode exhibits an initial discharge capacity of 90.3 mAh g–1 at 0.2 A g–1 (based on cathode mass) and retains 80.4 mAh g–1 after 100 cycles. Ruan et al. designed a three-dimensional N-doped strip-like carbon nanotube graphene (N-SGCNT) with hierarchical porous structures and loaded red phosphorus onto the scaffold via evaporation–condensation to form P@N-SGCNT (Figure d). As an SIB anode, P@N-SGCNT demonstrates an initial discharge capacity of 3201 mAh g–1 (mass loading: 1.3 mg cm–2) and retains 1936 mAh g–1 after 2000 cycles at 1 A g–1. A full cell paired with a Na­(Ni1/3Fe1/3Mn1/3)­O2 cathode delivers 1576 mAh g–1 after 140 cycles at 0.1 A g–1. P@N-SGCNT demonstrates significant potential for practical applications due to its high mass loading and robust full-cell performance.

Carbon nanofibers (CNFs) and carbon nanotubes (CNTs), with their inherent mechanical robustness and low electrical resistivity, form an interwoven conductive network when compounded with red phosphorus, effectively addressing the poor conductivity of phosphorus in sodium-ion battery (SIB) anodes. Phosphorus–carbon composites fabricated via electrospinning leverage this conductive network to shorten electron transport pathways, while the hierarchical porous structure of CNTs accelerates ion diffusion and mitigates volume expansion. Notably, N-doping in the P@N-SGCNT material enhances interfacial adsorption capabilities, contributing to its superior electrochemical performance in full-cell configurations. However, most studies report mass loadings <1.5 mg cm–2, as conventional laboratory-scale doctor-blade coating struggles to achieve uniform high-load electrodes. Consequently, the “high specific capacity” observed in low-load electrodes fails to translate into practical energy density.

3.2. Composite Modification of Red Phosphorus by Graphene Nanosheets

Graphene nanosheets, with their excellent electrical conductivity and mechanical robustness, synergistically enhance the conductivity and cycling stability of phosphorus-based materials when employed as sodium-ion battery (SIB) anodes, owing to the enlarged interfacial contact area between phosphorus and GNS. −

Sun et al. synthesized a nitrogen-doped graphene/carbon nanotube aerogel (P@NGCA) via freeze-drying and evaporation–condensation methods as a freestanding anode for high-performance sodium-ion batteries (Figure a). The NGCA exhibits exceptional electrical conductivity and mechanical stability. Its porous structure (specific surface area: 147.0 m2 g–1, pore volume: 0.365 cm3 g–1) accommodates volume expansion of red phosphorus, suppressing particle pulverization. Graphitic nitrogen embedded in the carbon skeleton enhances the overall electronic conductivity and reduces polarization, delivering an areal capacity of 3.3 mA·h·cm–2 as a SIB anode. Liu et al. developed a nanoporous red phosphorus/reduced graphene oxide composite (NPRP@RGO) for SIB anodes. The nanoporous architecture of red phosphorus minimizes sodium-ion diffusion lengths, while the RGO-based conductive network facilitates rapid electron/ion transport. Consequently, NPRP@RGO achieves a capacity of 1249.7 mAh g–1 after 150 cycles at 0.17 A g–1 and retains 775.3 mAh g–1 after 1500 cycles at 5.12 A g–1, demonstrating remarkable cycling stability. Wang et al. fabricated hollow FeP nanospheres encapsulated by carbon layers and embedded in a graphene network (H-FeP@C@GR) through hydrothermal synthesis, carbon coating, phosphidation, and carbothermal reactions (Figure b and c). The carbon layer and graphene network synergistically mitigate volume expansion, prevent FeP pulverization, enhance electronic conductivity, and reduce interfacial resistance. Notably, the solid-electrolyte interphase (SEI) preferentially forms on the carbon surface, avoiding the continuous decomposition of active materials. As an SIB anode, H-FeP@C@GR delivers an initial discharge capacity of 995 mAh g–1 (mass loading: 1.2 mg cm–2) but exhibits rapid capacity decay, retaining 400 mAh g–1 after 250 cycles at 0.1 A g–1, attributed to sluggish conversion reaction kinetics caused by the large ionic radius of Na+.

Liu et al. leveraged the high solubility of red phosphorus in ethylenediamine to control its particle size via HCl concentration regulation, achieving in situ dispersion of 5–10 nm nanoscale red phosphorus (NRP) on reduced graphene oxide (rGO) surfaces to form NRP@rGO composites (Figure d). DFT calculations revealed a Na3P/rGO binding energy of 1.272 eV after discharge, confirming rGO’s capability to stabilize NRP during cycling. The NRP@rGO anode demonstrates exceptional long-term cycling stability, retaining a specific capacity of 662 mAh g–1 after 1400 cycles at 2 A g–1 (electrode mass loading: 0.5–0.7 mg cm–2). Additionally, Mansouri et al. employed density functional theory (DFT) and ab initio molecular dynamics (AIMD) simulations to evaluate the structural stability (thermodynamic/kinetic), electronic properties, sodium adsorption capacity, diffusion kinetics, and energy storage performance of C6BN/black phosphorus (BP) nanocomposites. The C6BN and BP layers are bonded via weak van der Waals interactions with an interfacial formation energy of −31.30 meV and an interlayer spacing of 3.45 Å (Figure e). The composite reduces BP’s bandgap from 0.86 to 0.53 eV, exhibiting metallic characteristics after sodium adsorption. This configuration achieves an optimal sodium adsorption energy of −2.06 eV at the C6BN/BP interface, surpassing that of pristine BP (−1.76 eV), thereby significantly enhancing electronic conductivity and sodium storage capability

Graphene nanosheets provide a high-surface-area substrate for nanosized red phosphorus (NRP), enabling uniform dispersion of phosphorus on the carbon matrix while enhancing electron transport efficiency. The interlayer spacing of reduced graphene oxide (rGO) offers additional free volume to accommodate phosphorus volume expansion, thereby contributing to the superior electrochemical performance of phosphorus/graphene nanocomposites. Notably, the phosphoamine-assisted synthesis of phosphorus–carbon compositeswhere reaction kinetics and NRP particle size are controlled by modulating H+ concentrationdemonstrates advantages over conventional ball milling and evaporation–condensation methods, including lower energy consumption, enhanced safety, scalability, and continuous production feasibility. These features position it as a promising strategy for industrial-scale applications.

3.3. Carbon Nanocage Encapsulation Structure

Carbon nanocages, characterized by their high specific surface area, external surface defects, abundant nanochannels, and hierarchical porous architecture, have emerged as a multifunctional advanced platform for energy storage in recent years. , When employed as a host matrix for red phosphorus, the spacious internal cavities of carbon nanocages accommodate volume expansion, while their interconnected conductive network ensures rapid electron transport, synergistically enhancing the electrochemical performance of phosphorus-based composites.

Santhoshkumar et al. synthesized multichannel nanoporous carbon (MRPN) via a solvothermal method using phosphorus triiodide (PI3), iodobenzene (C6H5I), cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB), and ethylene glycol ((CH2OH)2). By controlling the reaction time and surfactant (CTAB) dosage, spherical hollow nanoparticles (50–100 nm) with multichannel pores were obtained. As a sodium-ion battery (SIB) anode, MRPN delivered a maximum capacity of 1814 mAh g–1 after 100 cycles at 0.2 A g–1, but the capacity declined to 735 mAh g–1 after 400 cycles (mass loading: 2.1 mg cm–2). While the broad pore size distribution mitigated volume expansion, large pores (>50 nm) induced RP agglomeration, compromising long-term cyclability. Liu et al. developed honeycomb-like hierarchical micro-/mesoporous carbon nanospheres (HHPCNS) with ultrahigh pore volume (3.258 cm3 g–1) through coprecipitation of poly­(acrylic acid) (PAA) and calcium hydroxide (Ca­(OH)2), followed by high-temperature carbonization. The enlarged pore volume accommodated RP loading and volume expansion, while uniformly distributed mesopores ensured homogeneous RP dispersion (Figure a). The HHPCNSs/P composite exhibited a high specific capacity of 2367.6 mAh g–1 at 0.1 A g–1, excellent rate capability (831.1 mAh g–1 at 10 A g–1), and cycling stability (1269.4 and 861.8 mAh g–1 after 1000 cycles at 2 and 5 A g–1, respectively; mass loading: 1.2 mg cm–2). In a full cell with a Na3V2(PO4)3 cathode, it retained 834.2 mAh g–1 after 200 cycles at 0.5 A g–1. Liu et al. encapsulated red phosphorus within carbon nanocages (RP@CNC) via a phosphine amine-based approach. RP-ethylenediamine precursor solution was injected into CNCs (50 mg) under ultrasonication and vacuum-assisted infiltration, resulting in RP precipitation and confinement within CNC cavities (as illustrated in Figure b). The large internal volume of CNCs enabled high RP loading (85.3 wt %) while alleviating volume expansion. The RP@CNC composite delivered 1363 mAh g–1 after 150 cycles at 100 mA g–1 and maintained over 80% capacity retention after 1300 cycles at 5 A g–1.

Carbon nanocages, featuring hollow internal cavities and abundant nanoscale channels, effectively restrict the volume expansion of red phosphorus (RP). Their high specific surface area and surface defects enhance RP loading capacity, while their tunable electronic structure facilitates heteroatom doping. These structural advantages enable phosphorus–carbon nanocage composites to deliver superior electrochemical performance. However, current synthesis approaches face challenges: solvothermal methods rely on costly precursors (e.g., PI3, CTAB), the energy-intensive high-temperature carbonization of HHPCNSs increases production costs, and the vacuum impregnation process for RP@CNC composites lacks scalability for continuous manufacturing. Future development should focus on hierarchical structural optimization: outer micropores to confine RP nanoparticles → middle mesopores to facilitate ion transport → inner macropores to accommodate expansion. Additionally, cost-effective alternatives must be explored, such as developing biomass-derived nanocages using cellulose and lignin as carbon sources combined with template-assisted methods (e.g., ZnO nanospheres) to synthesize hierarchically porous carbon, thereby replacing expensive polymer precursors.

The phosphorus (P) loading significantly impacts the electrochemical performance of sodium-ion battery (SIB) anodes, including the initial capacity, cycling stability, and rate capability. High P loading typically enhances the initial capacity but exacerbates volume expansion and interfacial side reactions, leading to accelerated capacity fading. For instance, the P x Se composite (70 wt % P) delivered an initial capacity of 1937.5 mAh g–1. However, severe volume expansion caused rapid capacity decay, retaining only 1318 mAh g–1 after 200 cycles at 2 A g–1 (decay rate: 0.34% per cycle). In contrast, the HHPCNSs/P composite (58.1 wt % P) employed hollow porous carbon spheres to buffer expansion, maintaining 1269.4 mAh g–1 after 1000 cycles at 2 A g–1 (decay rate: 0.12% per cycle). The moderately loaded P@TBMC (40 wt % P) leveraged a mesoporous carbon framework to provide rapid ion transport channels, achieving 580 mAh g–1 after 800 cycles at 2.5 A g–1 (decay rate: 0.05% per cycle). These results demonstrate that moderate P loading (30–50 wt %) combined with structurally stable carbon matrices achieves an optimal balance between capacity retention and cycling longevity.

In summary, one-dimensional (1D) carbon nanofibers (CNFs), carbon nanotubes (CNTs), two-dimensional (2D) graphene nanosheets, and three-dimensional (3D) carbon nanocages improve the electrochemical performance of phosphorus–carbon composites. Their common advantage lies in enhancing electrical conductivity while confining the volume expansion of red phosphorus. As shown in Figure , under identical current densities, 2D carbon architectures (e.g., NPRP@RGO) demonstrate superior performance. The layered graphene nanosheet structure provides abundant active sites, while the interlayer spacing effectively confines red phosphorus volume expansion. In contrast, 1D carbon materials exhibit limited surface active sites, leading to uneven phosphorus distribution or aggregation, which degrades electrochemical performance. Meanwhile, 3D carbon nanocages suffer from low mechanical strength in hollow structures, causing structural collapse during prolonged cycling. Current studies face critical limitations: laboratory-scale electrodes typically exhibit mass loadings <2 mg cm–2 and area capacities of 1–3 mA h cm–2, falling short of industrial requirements. Existing full-cell systems also show low energy density (e.g., P@TBMC full-cell capacity: 90.3 mAh g–1), primarily due to cathode capacity limitations (e.g., Na3V2(PO4)3: ∼120 mAh g–1). Future advancements should prioritize dry electrode processing to fabricate binder-free electrodes via direct calendaring, achieving mass loadings of 3–5 mg cm–2. Concurrently, presodiation techniques (e.g., Na2S additives or metallic Na foil) should compensate for initial Coulombic efficiency loss. Pairing these anodes with high-capacity Mn/Fe-rich layered oxides (e.g., Na2/3[Fe1/2Mn1/2]­O2) will further enhance the full-cell energy density.

4. Interfacial Strengthening of Phosphorus-Based Materials/Carbon Composites

Phosphorus–carbon composites with tailored carbon architectures can effectively mitigate volume expansion and address conductivity limitations in sodium-ion battery (SIB) anodes. However, weak interfacial adhesion between phosphorus (red/black) and carbon matrices often leads to phosphorus detachment during long-term cycling, resulting in capacity degradation. Strengthening the phosphorus–carbon interface is therefore critical for enhancing cycling stability. Current strategies to improve interfacial bonding include 1. Protective coatings (e.g., conductive polymers, carbon layers). − 2. P–C bond formation via chemical interactions. 3. P–O–C bond construction through oxygen-containing functional groups. − 4. Synergistic P–C/P–O–C bonding for dual interfacial reinforcement. 5. Interfacial binder reinforcement with functional adhesives. 6. Electrolyte interface stabilization using optimized additives.

4.1. Coating Protects the Structurally Reinforced Phosphorus/Carbon Interface

The repeated volume changes in phosphorus–carbon composite anodes during prolonged alloying/dealloying processes in sodium-ion batteries (SIBs) not only induce active material pulverization and interfacial delamination but also trigger continuous solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) growth, leading to rapid capacity decay. To address these challenges, researchers have developed protective surface coatings such as polyaniline (PANI), polypyrrole (PPy), , sulfur (S), polydopamine (PDA), , and carbon layers. These coatings mitigate volume changes in phosphorus-based materials and prevent direct contact with electrolytes.

Jin et al. fabricated a ternary composite (BP-G/PANI) by uniformly coating polyaniline (PANI) on black phosphorus-graphite (BP-G) particles. The PANI coating adapts to the substantial volume changes of BP during cycling through its swelling properties, effectively suppressing electrode structural fracture and minimizing repeated solid-electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer rupture/reformation (Figure a). The PANI-coated BP-G/PANI electrode exhibits only 19% thickness increase after cycling, compared to 60% expansion and cracking in uncoated BP-G. This structural stability preserves SEI integrity and prevents active material detachment from the current collector. PANI establishes optimized ion transport pathways (electrolyte → PANI → BP-G → BP), significantly reducing charge transfer resistance. As an SIB anode, it delivers 520 mAh g–1 after 1000 cycles at 4 A g–1 (mass loading: 0.8–1.2 mg cm–2). However, long-term swelling-shrinkage of PANI may induce coating fatigue cracks, while BP remains costly and air-sensitive. He et al. developed a facile method to synthesize carbon-coated Ni x P nanorods using phosphoric acid resin as dual carbon/phosphorus sources (Figure b). The carbon layer mechanically constrains volume variations in nickel phosphides during cycling. Phosphorus-doped carbon enhances composite conductivity, reduces charge transfer resistance, and introduces lattice defects that expand interlayer spacing to strengthen Na+ adsorption. This anode maintains 130 mAh g–1 after 5000 cycles at 2 A g–1. While the carbon coating extends the cycle life for metal phosphide anodes, the process suffers from high energy consumption and limited capacity. Fang et al. prepared P@AC@PPy composites via evaporation–condensation followed by interfacial oxidative polymerization of pyrrole (PPy) on P@AC. The conductive PPy coating isolates active materials from electrolyte while improving air oxidation resistance (Figure c). With a high phosphorus content (52 wt %), the composite achieves an initial capacity of 800 mAh g–1 at 0.05 A g–1 and retains 484 mAh g–1 after 60 days at 0.2 A g–1 (mass loading: 1.0–1.2 mg cm–2).

Song et al. proposed a surface reconstruction strategy to fabricate P/C@S composites by high-energy ball milling P@C (prepared via evaporation–condensation) with sulfur (S8) powder. The surface red phosphorus (RP) layer transforms into a passivated P4Sn layer with an enhanced oxidation resistance. This P4S n layer generates an artificial Na3PS4-rich solid-electrolyte interphase (SEI) during initial sodiation via in situ conversion reactions (Figure d), ensuring rapid Na+ storage kinetics and stable electrode/electrolyte interfaces. As an SIB anode, P/C@S delivers 510 mAh g–1 after 1000 cycles at 5 A g–1 (mass loading: 1 mg cm–2). A full cell paired with Na3V2(PO4)3 (NVP) cathode achieves 103 mAh g–1 after 200 cycles at 0.55 A g–1, with areal capacity scaling linearly with cathode mass loading (9.9–19.6 mg cm–2) while maintaining specific capacity. However, precise control of reaction conditions is required, and sulfur dissolution in electrolytes during long-term cycling may alter the SEI composition. Liu et al. synthesized P-CNT@Pd composites by coating polydopamine (Pd) on P-CNT (prepared via high-energy ball milling) through self-polymerization of dopamine under weakly alkaline conditions. The in situ-formed Pd coating provides elastic buffering to accommodate volume changes of red phosphorus and prevents direct electrolyte contact. As an SIB anode, P-CNT@Pd exhibits 730 mAh g–1 after 2000 cycles at 2.6 A g–1 and 470 mAh g–1 after 5000 cycles at 5.2 A g–1, demonstrating exceptional cycling stability. Wang et al. synthesized carbon-coated nickel phosphide (Ni2P@C) submicron spheres via solvothermal synthesis of Ni-glycerate spheres, hydrothermal carbon coating with glucose, high-temperature calcination to form Ni@C, and subsequent phosphidation using NaH2PO2 (Figure e). The Ni2P@C consists of 30–40 nm Ni2P nanoparticles assembled into 500 nm spheres with a 2–3 nm carbon shell. This structure elevates Na+ diffusion coefficient to 2.7 × 10–13 cm2 s–1 (vs 1.6 × 10–13 cm2 s–1 for uncoated Ni2P), delivering 296 mAh g–1 after 100 cycles at 0.05 A g–1, significantly outperforming bare Ni2P (122 mAh g–1)

Coating protection strategiesincluding chemical reconstruction (sulfur-based passivation), physical encapsulation (carbon layers), and dynamic interfaces (polymer coatings)significantly enhance interfacial stability and sodium-ion transport efficiency in phosphorus-based anodes through surface conversion mechanisms. The “sandwich” architecture formed by the coating, phosphorus matrix, and carbon framework effectively encapsulates red phosphorus, mitigating its detachment. The inherent conductivity of the coating further improves the composite’s overall electrical conductivity. As summarized in Table , BP-G/PANI demonstrates superior electrochemical performance, yet its practical application is hindered by the high cost and air sensitivity of black phosphorus. In contrast, the P-CNT@Pd composite exhibits optimal comprehensive performance under high current density (5.2 A g–1) and ultralong cycling (5000 cycles), which is attributed to its elastic-conductive synergistic interface design. While the P/C@S composite achieves interfacial stability via artificial SEI layers, the long-term chemical durability of sulfur-derived SEI remains questionable due to potential sulfur dissolution-induced capacity decay. Although coatings mitigate volume expansion, challenges persist in suppressing nanoparticle agglomeration and coating fracture during prolonged cycling. Future efforts should focus on computation-experiment synergy: leveraging machine learning-guided screening of optimal coating compositions/thicknesses and employing in situ characterization to unravel dynamic SEI evolution mechanisms.

4.2. Constructing Strong P–C Bonds to Strengthen the Phosphorus/Carbon Interface

Constructing robust P–C chemical bonds between red phosphorus (RP) and carbon matrices enables tight contact between RP and the carbon matrix, thereby mitigating interfacial degradation between phosphorus and carbon during electrochemical cycling while ensuring sufficient electrical conductivity. −

Li et al. combined computational simulations and spectroscopic characterization to reveal the critical role of P–C bond stability in governing electrochemical performance. During discharge, P–P bonds are prone to rupture, while the bonding states of P–C bonds remain nearly unchanged, with electrons predominantly localized on the P atoms to form lone pairs (Figure a). The retained P–C clusters act as nucleation centers for P–P bond regeneration during recharging, thereby enhancing the structural stability of red phosphorus. Song et al. synthesized NTO/C-BP composites by integrating black phosphorus (BP) nanoparticles with carbon-coated sodium titanate (NTO/C) via high-energy ball milling. The composite features a stabilized architecture with P–O–Ti and P–C bonds (Figure b). BP chemically anchors to the carbon layer on NTO through robust P–C bonds, effectively suppressing volume expansion during Na+ insertion/extraction. These bonds enhance the composite’s electronic conductivity, reduce electrode polarization (interface impedance: 326.7 Ω vs 675.1 Ω for pristine BP), and establish a 3D conductive network that shortens Na+ diffusion paths. As an SIB anode, the material delivers 143.3 mAh g–1 after 400 cycles at 200 mA g–1 (mass loading: 1.5–2 mg cm–2).

Ye et al. synthesized vanadium diphosphide/phosphorus/carbon composites (VP2/P/C) via high-energy ball milling. XPS analysis confirmed the existence of P–C bonds, while VP2 served as a buffering matrix synergizing with the carbon framework to suppress volume expansion during Na+ insertion/extraction. The P–C bonds anchored phosphorus to the carbon matrix, minimizing pulverization and agglomeration, while the conductive carbon network reduced the interfacial resistance. As an SIB anode, the composite retained 387 mAh g–1 after 200 cycles at 500 mA g–1 and exhibited a rate capability of 280 mAh g–1 at 2 A g–1 (Figure c). Wu et al. developed a high-performance P/C hybrid anode by encapsulating red phosphorus into nitrogen-doped porous carbon nanotubes (P@N-PCTs) via evaporation–condensation. The formation of P–C bonds significantly enhanced the electronic conductivity and alleviated electrode pulverization. The P@N-PCTs anode delivered 1157 mA h g–1 after 100 cycles at 2 A g–1 and 572 mAh g–1 at 10 A g–1. Yuan et al. constructed ordered porous Co x P nanosheet/nanoneedle arrays on carbon cloth (Co x P@CFC) as a freestanding flexible electrode (Figure d). This hierarchical micronano architecture provided efficient pathways for electron/ion transport and accommodated volume changes. The introduction of Co3+/Co2+ mixed valence states and P vacancies significantly reduced the charge transfer resistance and accelerated Na+ diffusion. Covalent Co x P–C bonding via P–C bonds ensured strong interfacial interactions, preventing active material disintegration. The anode achieved 814 mAh g–1 after 100 cycles at 100 mA g–1 and maintained undiminished capacity over 9000 cycles at 5 A g–1 (mass loading: 1.3–1.6 mg cm–2). Zhang et al. fabricated red phosphorus/nitrogen-doped graphene composites (P/GN) by confining amorphous red phosphorus within a flexible nitrogen-doped graphene (GN) framework via evaporation–condensation. Robust P–C bonds between RP and GN enhanced structural stability (Figure e), while nitrogen doping (pyridinic N and pyrrolic N) introduced defect sites to accelerate Na+ kinetics. The P/GN anode retained 809 mAh g–1 after 300 cycles at 1500 mA g–1.

In phosphorus/carbon composite materials, the formation of P–C bonds significantly enhances the interfacial interaction between red phosphorus and carbon matrices, effectively mitigating the pulverization of red phosphorus caused by volumetric expansion during cycling. Furthermore, the P–C bonds substantially improve the electrical conductivity and structural stability of red phosphorus, contributing to the exceptional long-term cycling stability of the phosphorus/carbon composite anodes. As shown in Table , the Co x P@CFC composite demonstrates optimal performance through synergistic effects of hierarchical structural design, chemical bonding, and defect engineering. The P–C bonds coupled with defect engineering in Co x P effectively modulate the charge distribution and reduce the diffusion barrier for Na+ ions. The porous architecture (pore size: 50–200 nm) facilitates rapid ion transport pathways while accommodating volume variations. The self-supporting carbon cloth substrate integrates mechanical flexibility with high conductivity, enabling binder-free electrode configuration. However, current characterization of P–C bonds predominantly relies on indirect evidence from X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), lacking comprehensive analysis of bond configuration (sp2/sp3 hybridization and single/double bonds), spatial distribution (surface/interior), and dynamic evolution during cycling. Moreover, existing synthesis methods such as evaporation–condensation and high-energy ball milling present challenges in process complexity and production cost, hindering industrial scalability. Future research should focus on developing green, low-cost, and scalable synthesis strategies to accelerate practical implementation of these materials.

4.3. Constructing Strong P–O–C Chemical Bonds to Strengthen the Phosphorus/Carbon Interface

The P–O–C bond exhibits a higher bond energy (585 kJ mol–1) compared to the P–C bond (264 kJ mol–1), which enables phosphorus/carbon composite anode materials to achieve enhanced cycling stability and reversible capacity while maintaining structural integrity of the electrode.

Yao et al. synthesized hollow porous carbon nanosphere/amorphous red phosphorus (HPCNSs/P) composites via an evaporation–condensation method, achieving optimal design through theoretical calculations and experimental validation. For the first time, molecular dynamics (MD) simulations and density functional theory (DFT) calculations systematically elucidated the detailed mechanism of P4 molecule adsorption and deposition on carbon substrates during synthesis. These simulations revealed that the morphology of P4 aggregates and P/C composites is predominantly governed by the pore size and surface states of the carbon matrix. Oxygen-containing functional groups on the carbon surface chemically bond with phosphorus, stabilizing the composite structure (Figure a). The HPCNSs/P anode delivered a capacity of 1700 mAh g–1 after 300 cycles at 0.1 A g–1 (based on the phosphorus mass) and retained over 76% capacity after 1000 cycles at 1.0 A g–1. Sun et al. developed a red phosphorus/carbon composite (P/C) with ultrafine red phosphorus particles (10 nm) embedded in a three-dimensional carbon skeleton via a carbothermal reduction strategy. Carbonization of polyethylene glycol generated an interconnected nanoporous carbon framework, where ultrafine phosphorus nanoparticles formed P–O–C bonds with the carbon matrix (Figure b). As a sodium-ion battery (SIB) anode, this material exhibited a capacity of 920 mAh g–1 after 160 cycles at 240 mA g–1 with 88% capacity retention (electrode loading: 2.5 mg cm–2).

Yu et al. fabricated black phosphorus/reduced graphene oxide (BP/rGO) composites through high-energy ball milling. By increasing oxygen functional groups in the precursors, the P–O–C bond content was enhanced by 164%. The covalent bonding strength surpassed that of physical adsorption or van der Waals interactions, ensuring stable BP-rGO integration during cycling and mitigating active material loss. Comparative analysis (Figure c) demonstrated that high P–O–C-bonded BP/rGO developed only minor cracks, while low-bonding samples suffered severe pulverization. The P–O–C bonds reduced direct BP-electrolyte contact, suppressing excessive solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) growth and electrolyte decomposition, thereby improving the initial Coulombic efficiency (ICE) to 83.55%. The BP/rGO anode exhibited enhanced cycling retention from 6.3% to 60.9% after 100 cycles at 1 A g–1 (electrode loading: 0.7–0.8 mg cm–2). Li et al. confined nanoscale red phosphorus within hierarchical porous carbon (HPC) via an evaporation–condensation strategy to create P/HPC composites. P–O–C bonds formed during condensation strengthened the interfacial adhesion between phosphorus and carbon, enhancing the structural stability during repeated sodiation/desodiation (Figure d). The P/HPC anode delivered an initial capacity of 2202 mAh g–1 (based on phosphorus mass) and retained 929 mAh g–1 after 100 cycles at 5.2 A g–1 (electrode loading: 2 mg cm–2). Zhang et al. introduced oxygen functional groups via TiO2 addition during high-energy ball milling to synthesize P/C composites. This strategy promoted P–O–C bond formation (Figure e), enhancing the interfacial bonding strength between phosphorus and carbon, which improved cycling stability and rate capability. The resulting P/C anode maintained a capacity of 640 mAh g–1 after 100 cycles at 0.5 A g–1 (electrode loading: 2 mg cm–2).

As summarized in Table , phosphorus/carbon composite anodes fabricated with diverse interfacial enhancement strategies consistently demonstrate that P–O–C bonding effectively stabilizes red phosphorus, thereby enabling a superior electrochemical performance. Modulating oxygen-containing functional groups on carbon matrices to promote P–O–C bond formation significantly enhances sodium-ion battery anode properties. Among these, the HPCNSs/P composite exhibits optimal capacity, cycling longevity, and process controllability due to its hierarchical structural design and robust interfacial bonding. However, its practical application is constrained by template-dependent carbon matrix synthesis and low volumetric energy density. For scalable production, achieving uniform spatial distribution of P–O–C bonds remains challenging. Furthermore, oxygen functionalities (e.g., −OH, −COOH)typically introduced via precursor treatment or postoxidationare prone to desorption or structural reorganization under high-temperature conditions or prolonged cycling, compromising P–O–C bond stability. Future efforts could focus on encapsulating phosphorus/carbon composites with polymer layers rich in −OH/–COOH groups. Such coatings would serve dual roles: (1) acting as precursors for in situ P–O–C bond formation and (2) functioning as buffer layers to mitigate volume fluctuations during cycling, thereby synergistically addressing interfacial instability and mechanical degradation.

4.4. P–C Bonds and P–O–C Chemical Bonds Together Strengthen the Phosphorus/Carbon Interface

The synergistic interfacial engineering of P–C and P–O–C bonds has emerged as a research frontier due to their unique electronic and ionic regulation capabilities. The P–C bonds establish rapid electron transport pathways through strong covalent coupling, significantly reducing interfacial resistance while suppressing active material delamination. Concurrently, the P–O–C bonds optimize the sodium-ion adsorption kinetics via polar interactions. The dual-functional mechanism integrating both bond types substantially enhances the electrochemical performance of the phosphorus/carbon anodes.

For instance, Shuai et al. developed a sandwich-structured phosphorene-graphene hybrid anode (P/G) for SIBs through chemical activation of electrochemically exfoliated phosphorene and graphene. Oxygen functionalities (−OH, −COOH) on the phosphorene and graphene surfaces reacted with phosphorus under thermal treatment to form P–C and P–O–C bonds (Figure a). In situ XPS analysis confirmed the stability of P–O–C bonds during cycling, which mitigated electrolyte decomposition (P–C bonds remained intact after 200 cycles). These stable chemical bonds served as electron transport bridges, maintaining phosphorene structural integrity during sodiation/desodiation. The P/G anode delivered a retained capacity of 1011 mAh g–1 after 200 cycles at 5 A g–1 (electrode loading: 0.5 mg cm–2). Similarly, Ai et al. synthesized black phosphorus/hard carbon composites (BP-HC) via high-energy ball milling. As illustrated in Figure b, strong interfacial interactions between BP and HC were achieved through concurrent P–C and P–O–C bond formation. Covalent bonding redistributed electron cloud density, reducing charge transfer resistance (31.1 Ω for BP-HC vs 758.5 Ω for pristine BP). The hierarchical pore structure (meso-/macropores) in hard carbon facilitated rapid ion transport, achieving a Na+ diffusion coefficient of 9.96 × 10–13 cm2 s–1. The bonded interface minimized direct BP-electrolyte contact, suppressing excessive SEI growth (initial Coulombic efficiency: 75.75% for BP-HC vs 25.33% for BP). The BP-HC anode maintained 346.3 mAh g–1 after 300 cycles at 6 A g–1.

The synergistic interplay between P–C and P–O–C bonds significantly enhances the performance of sodium-ion battery anodes by concurrently optimizing electron/ion transport and mechanical stability. While P–C bonds establish robust covalent networks for efficient electron conduction, P–O–C bonds facilitate favorable ion adsorption–desorption kinetics through polar interactions. This dual-bond architecture effectively addresses the intrinsic limitations of single-bond systems, particularly in balancing structural integrity with electrochemical activity during prolonged cycling.

4.5. Interfacial Binder Strengthening Phosphorus/Carbon Interface

Binders play a critical role in maintaining the electrode structural integrity by cohesively integrating active materials, conductive additives, and current collectors. However, current research on binder systems remains relatively limited. , In sodium-ion batteries (SIBs), polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) is widely adopted as the binder with N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) as the processing solvent. While PVDF offers moderate adhesion, its limited fracture strain (<10%) fails to accommodate the substantial volume fluctuations (>300%) inherent to alloy-type anode materials like red phosphorus during sodiation/desodiation. For such systems, binders must exhibit high elasticity to preserve electrical connectivity between active materials and current collectors while mitigating mechanical degradation. −

Zhou et al. systematically investigated ball-milling duration, binder selection, conductive additives, and electrolyte formulations to optimize slurry processing for phosphorus/carbon (P/C) anodes. Sodium alginate (SA), with its hydroxyl-rich structure, outperformed conventional PVDF binders by facilitating hydrogen-bond formation with phosphorus, enhancing interfacial adhesion. As shown in Figure a, the SA-bound P/C composite achieved a higher phosphorus content (35.5 wt %) compared to the PVDF-based counterpart (32.1 wt %), along with an improved initial Coulombic efficiency (ICE) of 84.3%. The strengthened H-bond network effectively mitigated phosphorus detachment during cycling. Xiao et al. conducted a comprehensive evaluation of seven binderssodium alginate (SA), poly­(acrylic acid) (PAA), polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP), polyethylene glycol (PEG), chitosan (CHI), carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC), and PVDFon the electrochemical performance of P/C anodes. Figure b illustrates the cyclic structural evolution of electrodes with different binders. SA-, PAA-, and CMC-bound electrodes exhibited minimal oxidation, while CHI- and PEG-based systems showed partial surface oxidation. In contrast, PVP- and PVDF-containing electrodes suffered severe oxidation, forming isolated large particles postcycling due to inadequate volume accommodation. The oxidized layers in CHI/PEG electrodes partially alleviated volumetric strain, whereas aqueous binders (SA, PAA, and CMC) maintained structural integrity through robust polymer-phosphorus interactions. The SA-bound P/C anode demonstrated exceptional performance, delivering a reversible capacity of 1835 mAh g–1 at 0.8 A g–1 (electrode loading: 1.5 mg cm–2) and retaining 1082 mAh g–1 after 200 cycles at 8 A g–1 (Figure b).

SA-, CMC-, and PAA-based electrodes exhibit superior reversible capacity, electrochemical stability, and rate capability, attributed to the minimal oxidation of active phosphorus and their high adhesive strength/flexibility to accommodate significant volume fluctuations during cycling. Recent studies have further enhanced interfacial stability by integrating in situ-formed P–O–C bonds during composite fabrication. Song et al. engineered a three-dimensional network (c-NaCMC-CA) using sodium carboxymethyl cellulose (NaCMC) as a matrix and citric acid (CA) as a cross-linker via esterification. Red phosphorus was ball-milled with carboxylic-acid-functionalized carbon nanotubes (CNTs) to form P/CNT composites with P–O–C bonds. The dual stabilization mechanismcombining covalent P–O–C bonding and cross-linked polymer bindersensured structural integrity (Figure c). The resulting anode delivered a retained capacity of 1586.2 mAh g–1 after 100 cycles at 0.52 A g–1. Yan et al. pioneered a novel approach by simultaneously introducing electronic (acetylene black) and ionic (β″-Al2O3) conductive phases during black phosphorus (BP) ball-milling. This strategy created a BP/β″-Al2O3/C composite with optimized 3D mass transport channels, paired with an ecofriendly aqueous binder. In situ P–O–C bond formation during cycling was observed, as illustrated in Figure d. Comparative analysis of dynamic structural evolution revealed that the CMC/SMT binder addresses electrode failure via three synergistic mechanisms: (1) mechanical reinforcement (3D network), (2) chemical bonding (P–O–C), and (3) interfacial optimization (stable SEI). The BP/β″-Al2O3/C anode achieved 967.4 mAh g–1 after 400 cycles at 1 A g–1 (electrode loading: 1.1–1.3 mg cm–2). In full-cell configuration with Na4Fe3(PO4)2P2O7 (NFPP) cathodes, the BP/β″-Al2O3/C||NFPP system retained 507.5 mAh g–1 after 200 cycles at 0.2 A g–1, demonstrating exceptional electrochemical performance.

The critical role of binders in enhancing the electrochemical performance of sustainable high-capacity electrode materials has been widely recognized. Proper binder selection significantly improves the performance of phosphorus–carbon anodes in sodium-ion batteries (SIBs). As shown in Table , the P/C (sodium alginate, SA) composite demonstrated exceptional performance. The hydrogen-bonding network in the SA binder reduces phosphorus oxidation to <5% (compared to >40% in PVDF-based systems) and minimizes postcycling electrode crack density. While aqueous binders offer environmental and cost advantages, their standalone use often suffers from insufficient adhesion, necessitating synergistic combinations with components like styrene–butadiene rubber (SBR) or citric acid (CA).

4.6. Electrolyte Interface Stabilization

Electrolyte composition profoundly influences the interfacial stability and ion transport kinetics of phosphorus/carbon anodes by modulating the chemical composition and structure of the solid electrolyte interphase (SEI). The fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) additive, with its low lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy level, undergoes preferential decomposition to form an NaF-rich SEI layer, thereby enhancing interfacial stability.

Song et al. optimized a chemically presodiated red phosphorus/carbon composite (P/C@S) using an electrolyte of ethylene carbonate (EC)/propylene carbonate (PC) (1:1 v/v) with 5% fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) in 1 M NaClO4. The low LUMO energy level of FEC (−0.87 eV vs −0.38 eV for EC and −0.33 eV for PC) enabled its preferential decomposition at low potentials (∼1.0 V vs Na+/Na), forming a NaF-rich SEI layer. Key mechanisms include 1. Chemical presodiation: Reaction of Na-Bp with P/C@S generated Na x P alloys, compensating sodium loss. 2. FEC-driven SEI formation: The presodiated anode’s low potential triggered FEC decomposition, yielding a NaF-rich initial SEI. 3. SEI stabilization: The initial SEI evolved into a robust structure, suppressing side reactions (Figure a). The pNa-P/C@S anode retained 853.6 mAh g–1 after 100 cycles at 0.2 A g–1 (mass loading: 1.0–1.2 mg cm–2). Full cells paired with Na3V2(PO4)2F3 or Na3V2(PO4)3 cathodes achieved initial Coulombic efficiencies (ICE) > 98% and energy densities of 233.7–285.1 Wh kg–1. Carbonate-based electrolytes containing FEC are currently the preferred choice for large-scale applications of phosphorus–carbon anodes due to their interfacial stability and broad compatibility. Future efforts should focus on optimizing their cost and wettability while exploring hybrid electrolyte systems (e.g., carbonate-ether blends) to balance high energy density with fast-charging requirements. Although FEC facilitates NaF-rich SEI formation, excessive addition may result in overly thick SEI layers and increased impedance. Notably, different phosphorus-based materials (red phosphorus, black phosphorus, metal phosphides) exhibit distinct responses to electrolytes, and there is currently no unified adaptation standard. Additionally, most studies rely on half-cell tests with low electrode mass loadings (<2 mg cm–2), failing to reflect practical conditions. Future work should employ first-principles calculations to simulate the adsorption and decomposition behaviors of electrolyte components on phosphorus surfaces and conduct multifactor studies integrating binder-electrolyte-cathode interactions to advance full-cell performance optimization.

The sodium alginate (SA) binder forms a hydroxyl-derived hydrogen-bonding network that dynamically reconfigures during charge/discharge cycles, accommodating the volume expansion of red phosphorus. The porous structure of aqueous binders (porosity >50%) enhances electrolyte infiltration and shortens sodium-ion diffusion pathways. The P–C bond provides covalent electronic coupling to establish a continuous conductive network, significantly reducing interfacial resistance (e.g., from ∼100 Ω to <10 Ω). Concurrently, the P–O–C bond optimizes sodium-ion adsorption energy via polar interactions, shortening diffusion pathways and elevating the diffusion coefficient to ∼10–12 cm2 s–1 (vs 10–14 cm2 s–1 in conventional materials). This approach not only enhances the performance of phosphorus–carbon composite anodes but also facilitates scalable production for industrial applications.

Coating protection strategies are widely employed for interfacial stabilization, such as carbon coatings or polymer coatings, which construct physical encapsulation on phosphorus surfaces to reduce oxidation, suppress pulverization, and buffer volume changes, thereby extending material lifespan. Synergistic P–C/P–O–C bond interfacial engineering enhances the bonding between phosphorus and the carbon matrix: P–C bonds establish electron transport channels, reducing interfacial resistance; P–O–C bonds optimize Na+ adsorption and diffusion through polar interactions while inhibiting excessive SEI layer growth, achieving simultaneous structural stability and kinetic enhancement. Multifunctional aqueous binders (e.g., sodium alginate, CMC, PAA), with abundant −OH or −COO functional groups, not only strengthen interfacial adhesion via hydrogen bonding or in situ P–O–C bond formation but also exhibit excellent flexibility and self-healing capabilities. These properties effectively alleviate volumetric stress, maintain the electrode structural integrity, and significantly improve cycling stability. Electrolyte additives (e.g., FEC) decompose preferentially due to their low LUMO energy levels, forming a stable NaF-rich SEI layer that suppresses side reactions and enhances the initial Coulombic efficiency (ICE). Carbonate-based electrolytes are suitable for materials such as red phosphorus, while ether-based electrolytes (e.g., DME) demonstrate superior kinetic performance in metal phosphides. Through the synergistic design of multidimensional interfacial regulation strategiesintegrating chemical bonding, binder optimization, electrolyte engineering, and coating protectionit is possible to overcome the performance limitations of phosphorus/carbon anodes in practical applications. Future efforts should focus on precise control of bonding mechanisms, full-cell system validation under industrial conditions, and the development of low-cost, ecofriendly fabrication processes to advance their large-scale application in high-performance sodium-ion batteries.

5. Summary and Outlook

In summary, this study demonstrates that the synergistic design of nanosizing, carbon material compositing, and interfacial reinforcement strategies significantly enhances the electrochemical performance of phosphorus–carbon anode materials in sodium-ion batteries. Nanosizing red or black phosphorus via ball milling or evaporation–condensation methods effectively shortens sodium-ion diffusion paths and mitigates volume expansion. For instance, hollow porous carbon nanospheres encapsulating red phosphorus systems exhibit over 76% capacity retention after 1000 cycles at 1.0 A g–1. By integrating 1D, 2D, or 3D carbon frameworks (e.g., carbon nanotubes, graphene, and carbon nanocages) to construct conductive networks and confinement spaces, both electron transport capability and mechanical support are enhanced, further improving the cycling stability. Building on this, the introduction of P–C and P–O–C bonds achieves strong covalent coupling, significantly reducing interfacial resistance and suppressing active material detachment. Concurrently, multifunctional aqueous binders (e.g., sodium alginate, CMC) form hydrogen-bonding networks to accommodate volume changes and prevent electrode cracking. When combined with FEC-containing electrolyte systems, a stable NaF-rich SEI layer is formed in situ, reducing side reactions and boosting initial Coulombic efficiency. The most promising strategy involves integrating nanoconfinement architectures, 3D conductive carbon matrices, and chemical bonding-dynamic interfacial engineering, realizing a “structure-composition-interface” trinity design. This approach lays the foundation for scalable fabrication and practical applications of high-performance, long-cycle-life phosphorus/carbon anodes. However, despite their exceptional laboratory-scale electrochemical performance (e.g., high capacity and long cycling stability), large-scale production and real-world sodium-ion battery (SIB) implementation of phosphorus/carbon composites face critical challenges:

5.1. Development of Low-Cost Scalable Manufacturing Processes

Advance laboratory-scale techniques such as evaporation–condensation and high-energy ball milling have been used toward continuous production lines. Explore biomass-derived carbon as a substitute for expensive graphene/carbon nanotubes. Optimize parameters (e.g., ball-milling time, temperature, and solvent ratios) via machine learning to balance performance and energy consumption.

5.2. Full-Cell Performance Validation and Cathode Compatibility Optimization

Accelerate compatibility studies with high-capacity cathodes (e.g., Mn/Fe-rich layered oxides) to improve the overall energy density. Increase electrode mass loading to meet practical requirements (e.g., 3–5 mg cm–2).

5.3. Advanced Exploration of Multifunctional Interface Engineering

Utilize in situ XPS, AFM, and DFT simulations to uncover interfacial bonding evolution mechanisms. Guide synergistic design of novel binders, electrolytes, and additives.

5.4. Exploration of Green and Sustainable Synthesis Pathways

Adopt nontoxic solvents and low-energy processes (e.g., microwave-assisted carbonization) to enhance environmental friendliness. Promote green development in phosphorus/carbon anode industries.
