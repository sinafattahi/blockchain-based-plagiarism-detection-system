Abstract

Co­(II)-ion-polluted water has become a significant global environmental issue recently. This study synthesized a CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite through a hydrothermal method. The nanocomposite was analyzed using various techniques, including X-ray diffraction (XRD), Fourier transform infrared spectrometry (FTIR), scanning electron microscopy (SEM), and the Brunauer–Emmet–Teller (BET) method. RSM-CCD was utilized to model and optimize the adsorption of Co­(II) in aqueous solutions, subsequently examining the isotherm and kinetic models. The maximum adsorption (905.22) was achieved at an initial Co­(II) concentration of 65.144 mg/L, a solution pH of 6.02, an adsorbent dosage of 1.058 g/L, and a contact time of 47.958 min. The removal efficiency was obtained to be 96.356%. The kinetic adsorption results were accurately fitted using the pseudo-second-order model, and the equilibrium data were well described using the Freundlich isotherm behavior. The adsorbent exhibited excellent reusability, maintaining high recovery efficiencies of 97.86% in KOH and 89.7% in HNO3 during the first cycle. The adsorption of Co­(II) is governed by electrostatic attraction, hydrogen bonding, ion exchange, and surface complexation with a functional group on the nanocomposite surface. This study highlights the potential of the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite in addressing water pollution challenges.

1. Introduction

Many parts of the world continue to be concerned about heavy metal pollution and, in particular, combined heavy metal environmental contamination of freshwater supplies. Cadmium-related aquatic contamination, for instance, has been documented at several U.S. EPA National Priority List (NPL) sites. Several companies and landfills leak cadmium into the environment, which can lead to several health problems, including kidney damage and cancer. Furthermore, anthropogenic activities such as mining, burning fossil fuels, and some industrial setups release lead, another hazardous metal, into the environment. Another hazardous heavy metal species that can harm the respiratory system is chromium. Therefore, proper treatment of such contaminated water bodies is necessary due to the toxicity issues of lead, chromium, and cadmium.

Numerous scientific domains, including photocatalysis, catalysis, energy, electronics, medicine, and more, have found use for nanomaterials. Vanadate compounds combine with transition metal particles to generate various crystal formations with intriguing morphologies and excellent photocatalytic activity. Nanostructured transitional metal vanadates serve a variety of purposes, including electronic, coloring, semiconductor, magnetic, and therapeutic. Additionally, batteries, implanted cardiac defibrillators (ICDs), and catalysis all require transition metal vanadates. But the dangerous component found in industrial effluent and other sources of drinking water, such as groundwater, is heavy metals. − For this reason, environmental chemists find it difficult to remove heavy metals and degrade dyes found in wastewater.

Copper vanadate (CuV2O6) is a mixed metal oxide composed of copper and vanadium, known for its unique physicochemical properties, such as high thermal stability, variable oxidation states, and strong redox activity. Copper metavanadate nanostructures can be synthesized using a variety of techniques, including hydrothermal, co-precipitation, and solution processes. Because of its low cost and short response time, the hydrothermal method produces high-quality crystalline materials, making it one of the most useful techniques. In recent years, CuV2O6 has gained attention in environmental remediation, particularly in the adsorption and removal of heavy metal ions and dyes from wastewater, due to its ability to form strong coordination interactions with pollutant species. Moreover, its facile synthesis, low cost, and chemical versatility further enhance its potential as a functional material in sustainable water treatment technologies. ,

Carbon–nitrogen materials have garnered increasing interest recently because of their super hardness, electrical and mechanical properties, and low friction coefficient. Because of their greater stability, graphite structures of carbon nitride (g-C3N4) have garnered more interest. Fuel cells, CO2 capture, adsorption, and catalysts have all made use of it. The coordination interaction between heavy metal ions and functional groups is the adsorption mechanism of g-C3N4 for heavy metal ions. Compared to the carbonated functional groups, the metal ions in the nitrogen-containing functional group have a greater coordination capacity. Despite having a strong ability to adsorb heavy metal ions, g-C3N4 faces challenges with recovery and separation in industrial settings.

In this study, a novel CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite was synthesized and systematically investigated for its potential in Co­(II) adsorption from aqueous solutions. The structural, morphological, and surface properties of the synthesized composite were characterized by using X-ray diffraction (XRD), Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR), Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area analysis, and scanning electron microscopy (SEM). The adsorption performance was thoroughly evaluated through batch experiments, and the experimental data were fitted to various kinetic and isotherm models to elucidate the adsorption behavior. Furthermore, a plausible adsorption mechanism was proposed based on the combined characterization and adsorption results. The findings highlight the efficiency of the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite as a promising adsorbent for the removal of Co­(II) from aqueous solutions.

2. Materials and Methods

Copper nitrate (Cu­(NO3)2·5H2O), potassium hydroxide (KOH), NH4OH ammonium metavanadate (NH4VO3), cobalt­(II) chloride (CoCl2), and urea (CO­(NH2)2) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. During this work, DI H2O was used. All chemicals for this study was analytical research grade.

2.1. Synthesis of Copper Metavanadate (CuV2O6)

The synthesis of copper metavanadate (CuV2O6) was carried out as in previous work with modification. A typical procedure was adding 1.0 mmol of aqueous NH4VO3 dropwise to 4.5 mmol of aqueous Cu­(NO)2·5H2O while stirring for an hour. A 1 M NH4OH solution was continually dropped into the solution until pH ≈ 9. Following the full addition of NH4OH, the mixture was stirred for 6 h. The final precipitate, which had first been cleaned with water to remove any unreacted component, was separated by centrifugation at 4000 rpm and then repeatedly cleaned with ethanol. For 4 h, the final precipitate was dried in a hot air oven at 90 °C. The resulting precipitate was calcined for 4 h at 400 °C to get CuV2O6. Finally, the brown powder was obtained.

2.2. Synthesis of Graphitic Carbon Nitride (g-C3N4)

Typically, 5 g of CO­(NH2)2 was put in a crucible and subjected to heat in a furnace for 2:30 h at 500 °C in order to prepare g-C3N4 as in prior work with modification. Ultimately, g-C3N4 was reduced to a pale-yellow powder and kept for subsequent characterization and composite synthesis.

2.3. Synthesis of CuV2O6/g-C3N4 Nanocomposite

The CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite was obtained hydrothermally by combining 1.0 g of both CuV2O6 and g-C3N4 in 60 mL of DI water and stirring for 40 min. After that, the mixture was placed inside an autoclave lined with Teflon and heated to 200 °C for 20 h. After the autoclave was cooled, the composite was washed using ethanol and DI water to remove unreacted species. The obtained precipitates were again placed in an oven at 70 °C overnight. The synthesis process for the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite is depicted in Figure .

2.4. Material Characterization

FTIR spectroscopy resolution at 4 cm–1 in the 4000–500 cm–1 wavenumber range (PerkinElmer, Spectrum Two, USA) was used for CuV2O6, g-C3N4, CuV2O6/g-C3N4, and CuV2O6/g-C3N4 after Co­(II) adsorption caused functional groups to be attached to them. The crystalline structure of each material was analyzed by using an X-ray diffraction (XRD) technique. The XRD instrument used was a DRAWELL XRD 7000 X-ray machine, which emitted CuK radiation with a wavelength of 1.541 Å. The diffraction pattern was then generated. The morphology of CuV2O6, g-C3N4, CuV2O6/g-C3N4, and CuV2O6/g-C3N4 after Co­(II) adsorption was also studied using SEM. For pore size and surface area analysis, the BELSORP MAX II instrument was used.

2.6. Adsorption Experiments

A batch mode system was used for the adsorption tests to find the best pH and starting concentration of Co­(II). A predetermined quantity of the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite was introduced into a 100 mL Erlenmeyer flask that already contained a solution of CoCl2. The mixture was subsequently agitated at 200 rpm. For the adsorption investigations, a solution containing Co­(II) with a concentration of 50 mg/L was utilized. It was an experiment in which 1.1 g of CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite was mixed with 100 mL of different concentrations of Co­(II) solution in a 250 mL conical flask. The concentration was evaluated using a Thermo X Series II prior to and subsequent to adsorption studies. The study also examined the effects of other factors, including the concentration of Co­(II), pH level, dosage of CuV2O6/g-C3N4, and contact time. The pH values ranged from 4 to 8, and the highest removal percentage was chosen for further analysis.

3. Result and Discussion

3.1. XRD Studies

Each and every diffraction peak in the CuV2O6 XRD spectrum shown in Figure (a) was determined to be the distinctive peak of the CuV2O6 nanoparticle peaks (JCPDS card no: 01-073-0189), further demonstrating the formation of the CuV2O6 phase. The diffraction peak values that have been obtained are 24.6°, 27.4°, 28.9°, 30.67°, 32.1°, 35.4°, 38.7°, 45.3°, 48.6°, 52.4°, 58.9°, 61.4°, 65.1°, and 68.08°, which correspond to the planes (110), (112), (020), (021), (200), (112), (113), (004), (313), (132), (423), (316), (335), and (243), respectively, Figure (a). Using the Debye–Scherrer equation. D = 0.9λ/β cos θ, where D is the average crystalline size of the nanoparticle, β is the fwhm of significant peaks, θ is the Bragg angle, and λ is the wavelength of Cu Kα, the average crystalline size was determined to be 32.8 nm. The g-C3N4 material’s XRD analysis showed two distinct peaks at about 12.1° and 27.2°, which correspond to the (100) and (002) crystallographic planes, respectively, as shown in Figure (a). The (002) plane describes the stacking of conjugated aromatic systems, while the (100) plane is known as the tris-triazine unit. The nanocomposite shows the diffraction peaks at 2θ = 11.8°, 24.2°, 26.9°, 30.5°, 35.1°, 38.4°, 42.8°, 48.7°, 52.6°, 56.3°, 61.1°, and 67.7°, which correspond to (100), (110), (002), (021), (112), (113), (004), (313), (132), (423), (316), (335), and (243), respectively. The diffraction peaks at 11.8° and 26.9° appeared due to the addition of g-C3N4 as shown Figure (b). After the adsorption, the XRD graph clearly shows the peaks matched with Co­(II) in which it is derived from CoCl2, which is evidence that the adsorption takes place.

3.2. FTIR Studies

The CuV2O6 FTIR spectrum is displayed in Figure . The absorption peak at 3735 cm–1 was attributed to the vibrations of free OH–. The asymmetric vibration of V–O–Cu and V–O–V bonds was the cause of the absorption bands in the 815–972 cm–1 IR region. The absorption peak at 972 cm–1 was assigned to V–O symmetric stretching, while the adsorption band at 685 cm–1 was assigned to symmetric stretching of the VO5 unit. The IR band at 601 cm–1 was observed due to vibration of VO4 3–. These results are found to be in agreement with prior results. − The FTIR of g-C3N4 Figure shows broad absorption peaks in the range of 3000–3500 cm–1, corresponding to the N–H stretching vibration of the uncondensed amino group and O–H vibration due to adsorption of water onto the surface. The peak at 1607 cm–1 could be assigned to C–N stretching, and the peaks at 1455 cm–1, 1455 cm–1, 1322 cm–1, and 1240 cm–1 can be attributed to aromatic C–N stretching vibrations. − Due to the partial condensation of the amino groups, the absorption peak at 797 cm–1 belonged to the N–H deformation mode. The two absorption peaks in the FTIR spectrum in Figure confirm the presence of CuV2O6 and g-C3N4, providing evidence for the successful formation of the nanocomposite. In the nanocomposite spectrum, the peaks observed at 1615 cm–1 and 1470 cm–1 correspond to the C–N stretching and C–N stretching vibrations of g-C3N4, respectively, both showing a shift compared to the pristine g-C3N4. Additionally, the peak at 1328 cm–1 is attributed to the C–N stretching vibration of g-C3N4, shifted from 1322 cm–1. A noticeable shift is also observed for the peak at 972 cm–1 in pristine CuV2O6, which appears at 1088 cm–1 after the composite formation. This FTIR result is consistent with XRD and other characterization results.

3.3. BET Studies

Since it directly affects the availability of active sites for adsorption, the BET surface area is essential for evaluating the adsorption effectiveness of materials. The BET graph of both the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite and after adsorption is depicted in Figure . With a remarkably high BET surface area of 27.9 m2/g, the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite offers a higher number of accessible active sites for adsorption. However, as shown in Table S(1), adsorption of Co­(II) led to a significant decrease in the BET surface area. The significant decrease in specific surface area observed after Co­(II) adsorption can be attributed to the adsorption of Co­(II) and may lead to pore blockage, where metal ions occupy or obstruct the internal pores of the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite, reducing nitrogen access during BET measurement. Additionally, particle agglomeration and surface coverage caused by adsorbed metal species may contribute to structural densification, further limiting surface exposure.

3.4. Morphology Studies

Figure (a–d) shows the morphologies of CuV2O6, g-C3N4, and the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite, and after Co­(II) had been obtained by SEM. As shown in Figure (b), the SEM morphology of g-C3N4 demonstrates a porous structure that maximizes the surface area and accessibility of active sites, which is essential for its function as an adsorbent. Since the addition of g-C3N4 significantly altered the surface morphology of the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite, the surface of the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 is rougher than that of CuV2O6 (Figure (a,c)). Co­(II) adsorption on the nanocomposite surface alters the material’s morphology prior to adsorption, as seen in Figure (d).

The SEM morphology of CuV2O6 shows a porous and low crystallinity. Short stick-like particles of nanosize can be seen in Figure (b).

3.5. Central Composite Design (CCD) Model and Data Analysis

The Design Expert Software13 was used to adjust input variables such as initial Co­(II) concentrations, solution pH, adsorbent dosage, and contact time. A response surface method using a central composite design (CCD) was utilized to ascertain the examined independent components and ideal levels to increase the elimination percentages, as well as to make the relationships between them apparent. The study found that the highest removal percentage of Co­(II) was achieved at 98.7% with a pH of 6.5, adsorbent dosage of 1.1 g/L, initial concentration of 65 mg/L, and contact time of 55 min, while the minimum removal percentage was achieved at 72.78% with a pH level of 8, initial concentration of 80 mg/L, dosage of 1.55 g/L, and contact time of 70 min (Table S(2)), respectively. Table presents ANOVA results for the response surface quadratic model, evaluating its statistical suitability for explaining Co­(II) adsorption on the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite in the investigated range. A higher absolute F-value and lower p-value indicate stronger relevance of related variables. The model F-value of 100.63 indicates significance, with a 0.01% chance of noise. p-Values below 0.0500 indicate significant model terms, including A, B, C, D, AB, AC, BD, CD, A2, B2, C2, and D2, while values above 0.1000 indicate nonsignificant model terms. The F-value of 1.07 indicates that the lack of fit is not significant compared to pure error, with a 50.23% chance that this large F-value could be due to noise. The adjusted R 2 (0.9796) and predicted R 2 (0.9538) showed good agreement, as shown in Table S(3), model-fit statistical results. The model’s validity was confirmed by a strong correlation between predicted and experimental responses, with a coefficient of determination (R 2) of 0.9895. The factors under study account for 98.95% of the variation in the percentage removal of Co­(II), while the specified portion was only 1.83%. The experimental design, with a 0.8158% measured error, was highly reliable and precise, as the coefficient of variation (CV) was less than 10%, indicating the model’s validity and the reliability of the experimental design. The model was adaptable throughout the design space, and a signal-to-noise ratio of greater than 4 was ideal for a process design.

3.6. Interaction Effects on Co­(II) Removal Efficiency

Three-dimensional (3D) response surface plots were created against two independent process variables, keeping other variables at their central (0) level, to examine the interactions between process parameters and their effects on the response. Figure (a–d) presents 3D surface plots that depict the interactions between process variables and their corresponding output responses. Figure (a,b) demonstrates that increasing the initial centrifugation decreases the removal of Co­(II) due to the formation of an aggregation and overlap on the nanocomposite’s surface, resulting in reduced surface area and decreased adsorption efficiency, which aligns with previous research findings. The maximum removal of Co­(II) was 97.37% at a concentration of 50 mg/L, while the lowest removal was 93.03% at 80 mg/L. The removal efficiency of Co­(II) is significantly influenced by pH, with a maximum efficiency at pH 6.01 (Figure (a,c)). This is due to competition between Co­(II) and H+ for active sites on the adsorbent surface at low pH values, resulting in decreased efficiency. However, at high pH values, the adsorbent surface becomes negatively charged, converting Co­(II) to Co­(OH)2. Adsorbent dosage value is a crucial factor in the removal of heavy metal ions. As can be seen from Figure (b,d), increasing the adsorbent dosage enhances the removal efficiency of the adsorbate by increasing the number of active sites capable of adsorbing more Co­(II). The removal efficiency of the adsorbent was enhanced from 94.87% to 97.43% when the adsorbent dosage was increased from 0.65 to 1.55 g/L. The contact time for the effective removal of Co­(II) by the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 adsorbent was important in determining the equilibrium time. The percentage of Co­(II) removed was quickly at low contact times, as shown in Figure (c,d). This is caused by the presence of sufficient time for the adsorption process and the availability of additional active adsorption sites for the Co­(II) to be trapped. However, after 64 min, it no longer improves the percentage removal. This is because the Co­(II) eventually saturated the active sites on the adsorbent and separated from the surface, returning to the solution.

3.7. Optimization Process and Validation of the Model

The CCD-RSM approach was used to optimize operational variables, prioritizing removal efficiency and maximizing response. Input variables were determined within the axial point range for optimal optimization. The optimal parameters for the removal of Co­(II) were set at an initial concentration of 65.144 mg/L, a solution pH of 6.062, an adsorbent dosage of 1.058 g/L, and a contact time of 47.938 min and achieved 96.356%, as shown in Figure . The calculated function accurately describes the experimental model and intended conditions when the desirability value is 1.00.

Adsorption isotherms were formed from different initial Co­(II) concentrations at room temperature (298 K). Figure (a–c) shows linear representations of three isotherm models (Freundlich, Langmuir, and Temkin) for Co­(II) adsorption onto CuV2O6/g-C3N4, with the corresponding calculated coefficients and correlation coefficients detailed in Table . The Freundlich isotherm model provides a better description of the adsorption due to the correlation coefficient R 2 (0.992) values, indicating a heterogeneous surface on which Co­(II) adsorbed with the multilayer, as shown in Figure (a) and Table . Moreover, the study indicates moderate adsorption intensity and surface heterogeneity, as indicated by KF, n, and adsorbent capacity, with 1 > n > 0. Besides, the Langmuir isotherm was fitted with an R 2 of 0.984, revealing the maximum adsorption capacity for Co­(II) on CuV2O6/g-C3N4 at 905.22 mg/g, as per the Langmuir model as displayed in Table . The Langmuir Q max represents a theoretical maximum under ideal monolayer adsorption assumptions. Alternative models such as Temkin (R 2 = 0.964) pass with higher R 2 values, allowing them to adequately fit our data.

3.9. Adsorption Kinetics

The adsorption process mechanism requires considering the kinetics of Co­(II) on CuV2O6/g-C3N4. The adsorption process was examined using pseudo-first-order, pseudo-second-order, and intraparticle diffusion models. The models’ linear graphs were depicted in Figure (a,c), while their parameters were listed in Table . The pseudo-second-order Co­(II) plot on the adsorbent showed a close-to-1.0 R 2 value, higher than the pseudo-first-order kinetics, regardless of Co­(II), as shown in Figure (a) and Table . Therefore, the pseudo-second-order kinetic model provides a better fit for the adsorption of Co­(II) onto the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 surface, suggesting that the adsorbate adsorbed on the adsorbent was a type of chemisorption involving electrostatic interactions or hydrogen bonding. The intraparticle diffusion model provided a comprehensive understanding of the adsorption process, with an R 2 value of 0.859, as shown in Table .

3.10. Comparison Studies

The data in Table show the highest amount of Co­(II) that the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite adsorbent can hold compared to other adsorbents. The capacity for adsorption of every adsorbent is determined by various parameters, including the type of pollutant, pH level, adsorption process, time, etc. The CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite in this study exhibits a high and effective adsorption capability for Co­(II). The exceptionally high Q max observed for CuV2O6/g-C3N4 can be attributed to several synergistic factors. First, the composite possesses a high specific surface area, which provides abundant accessible adsorption sites. Second, the incorporation of Cu and V in the oxide framework introduces a variety of active centers capable of strong interactions with Co­(II) through mechanisms such as surface complexation, electrostatic attraction, and possibly ion exchange. Additionally, the g-C3N4 component contributes nitrogen-rich functional groups (−NH, −CN−), which are known to chelate metal ions effectively. The porous and heterogeneous morphologies, as evidenced by SEM and BET analyses, further facilitate rapid ion diffusion and surface binding. These combined structural and chemical features rationalize the high adsorption capacity observed in the Langmuir model.

3.11. Reusability Study

The reusability of the adsorbent is crucial for ensuring a cost-effective and practical adsorption process. We conducted sequential batch adsorption–desorption tests to determine the recycling potential of the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite. The experiments involved mixing 15 mg of the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite with a 10 mg L–1 solution of Co­(II). After that, we stirred the mixture for 10 h. The first cycle showed the highest desorption efficiency (97.86%) in basic medium (KOH) and 89.7% in acidic medium (HNO3). After six successive cycles, the desorption efficiency decreased to 71.65% and 53.88% in KOH and HNO3, respectively, as shown Figure S(2). This suggests that they have a remarkable ability to adsorb and attract Co­(II), and they may find use in industrial processes to remove it from industrial waste.

3.12. Mechanism

The study of adsorption mechanisms is crucial for comprehending the process of metal removal from aqueous solutions. Adsorption extent varies with pH and reaction conditions, influenced by analyte and adsorbent material structure, charge, and functional groups. Understanding the chemistry and mechanism involved is crucial for effective adsorption. The sorption process effectively manages electrostatic interactions between oppositely charged particles through various binding forms such as hydrogen bonding, van der Waals force, dipole–dipole induction, ion exchange, and surface complexation. Surface chemistry and pore density/volume significantly influence solute sorption onto an adsorbent surface. The adsorption of Co­(II) is governed by electrostatic attraction, hydrogen bonding, ion exchange, and surface complexation with a functional group on the CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite. Figure illustrates a plausible mechanism for Co­(II) adsorption from an aqueous solution using a CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite.

4. Conclusions

In this study, a CuV2O6/g-C3N4 nanocomposite was successfully synthesized through a hydrothermal method and was used as a novel adsorbent for Co­(II) removal from aqueous solutions. The synthesized nanocomposite was analyzed using advanced techniques like XRD, FTIR, SEM, and BET. The RSM-CCD approach was utilized to optimize the effects of process parameters. The maximum removal efficiency of 96.356% was achieved with an initial Co­(II) concentration of 65.144 mg/L, a solution pH of 6.02, an adsorbent dosage of 1.058 g/L, and a contact time of 47.058 min. The kinetic regression results indicated that a pseudo-second-order model accurately represented the adsorption process. The Freundlich isotherm model offers superior desorption due to its heterogeneous surface, allowing for multilayer adsorption of the adsorbate. Although temperature-dependent adsorption experiments were not conducted in this study, such analyses are essential for understanding the thermodynamics and mechanism of adsorption and will be considered in future work. The adsorbent exhibited excellent reusability, maintaining high recovery efficiencies of 97.86% in KOH and 89.7% in HNO3 during the first cycle. Even after six successive cycles, it retained 71.65% and 53.88% recovery in basic and acidic media, respectively, indicating a good regeneration potential. In conclusion, CuV2O6/g-C3N4 was a highly efficient adsorbent with potential applications in water treatment. In this study, linear regression was employed for comparative simplicity and consistency with the related literature. However, for improved reliability, future studies should apply nonlinear regression techniques to obtain more accurate model parameters and better reflect the true adsorption behavior.
