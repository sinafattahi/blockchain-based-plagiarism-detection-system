Synthesis of New Fluoroboronated Materials by Chemical Modification of Fluorinated Functional Polymers

Abstract

New fluoroboronated materials were obtained by chemical modification of poly­(VDF-co-MAF) copolymers synthesized by the radical copolymerization of vinylidene fluoride (VDF) with 2-(trifluoromethyl) acrylic acid (MAF). Two copolymers were first synthesized in dimethylcarbonate (DMC) or 1,1-difluoro-1-chloroethane as the solvents in 60% yields with molar masses of 22,000 and 12,000 g·mol–1, respectively. Although MAF does not homopolymerize under radical initiation, this is a suitable comonomer for VDF. The transfer rates were 0.40 and 0.21 for reactions carried out in DMC and in halogenated solvent, respectively. The resulting poly­(VDF-co-MAF) random copolymers were condensed with aminophenyl boronic acid pinacol ester (APBAPE) in the presence or absence of a HCl trap to obtain new fluoroboronated copolymer materials. Their 1H, 11B, 13C, and 19F NMR and ATR IR characterizations confirmed the successful addition of APBAPE onto the fluorinated copolymer. The thermal properties of all these original copolymers were determined. As expected, the thermal stability of poly­(VDF-co-MAF.APBAPE) copolymers displayed better behavior due to the decarboxylation of acid functions in MAF units as noted in their precursors. Such novel fluoropolymers bearing boron-containing groups may have potential applications in various areas as electronics and coatings.

Keywords: 2-(trifluoromethyl) acrylic acid, aminophenyl boronic acid pinacol ester, chemical modification, fluoroboronated polymers, NMR spectroscopy, radical copolymerization, vinylidene fluoride

Introduction

Fluoropolymers are quite important specialty polymers endowed with remarkable properties for high-tech applications: optical fibers for telecommunications and Internet of things, coatings, gaskets and O-rings in aerospace and automotive industries, materials for energy (fuel cell membranes, binders for cathodes in lithium ion batteries, backsheets for photovoltaic panels, piezoelectrical devices), membranes for water purification, etc. − Among them, poly­(vinylidene fluoride) (PVDF) is the second largest produced fluoropolymer − after poly­(tetrafluoroethylene) (PTFE) while VDF copolymers are numerous, undergoing challenging ongoing research. − ,−

As a matter of fact, boronated polymers are less numerous but have shown interesting features. − They have been involved in a wide range of applications: in medicine (reversible wettability response to nucleotide solutions, controlled release of insulin, hydrogels, − biosensors, ,,− glycopolymer nanoparticles , ), for self-healing materials, , fuel cell membranes, , flame retardants, − electronics, − and so on.

Fluorinated copolymers bearing boron-containing groups are even quite scare. On the one hand, Chung et al. − reported the radical polymerization of fluorinated monomers involving an innovating triethyl borane/oxygen system, where an ethoxy radical initiated the reaction while diethylborane trapped (or controlled) the growing macroradicals. These authors obtained relevant VDF copolymers for their electroactive (piezoelectric and ferroelectric) properties. Fluorinated dendrimers bearing boron-containing groups could find valuable properties as catalysts. , On the other hand, Li et al. reported original poly­(urethane)­s (PUs) based on telechelic poly­(VDF-co-HFP) copolymer containing carborane (C2B10H12), the carborane enhancing the chemical, mechanical, and thermal properties of such resulting fluorinated PUs for potential applications in automotive and aircraft industries. Yang et al. described the preparation of the poly­(VDF-co-trifluoromethyl acrylate lithium borate) copolymer used as original electrolyte for lithium-ion battery.

Among various strategies to design unique fluorinated functional materials, radical copolymerization is relevant. Designing materials bearing both fluorine and boron atoms sounds challenging, and the goal of this present manuscript deals with the synthesis of VDF-containing copolymers followed by a chemical modification to insert the boron-containing groups.

Experimental Section

Materials

The initiator bis­(tert-butylcyclohexyl) peroxydicarbonate was purchased from Akzo (Perkadox 16), the solvent 1,1-difluoro-1-chloroethane (Forane 142b) the vinylidene fluoride (VDF) monomer were given by Arkema (Pierre Bénite, France), monomer 2-(trifluoromethyl) acrylic acid (MAF) was kindly supplied from Tosoh Finechemicals (Shunan, Japan) and aminophenyl boronic acid pinacol ester (APBAPE) ordered from Apollo Scientific (United Kingdom).

Characterization

Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) Spectroscopy

1H, 11B, 13C, and 19F NMR spectra of the polymers were recorded on a Bruker AV III HD 400 MHz spectrometer (400, 128, 100, and 376 MHz for 1H, 11B, 13C, and 19F NMR, respectively) using a quartz tube and DMSO-d 6 as the solvent. Coupling constants and chemical shifts are provided in Hertz (Hz) and parts per million (ppm), respectively. The number of scans for 1H, 11B, 13C, and 19F NMR spectra were 64, 1024, 2048, and 16, respectively.

Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR)

FTIR spectra were recorded in attenuated total reflection (ATR) mode at 20 °C using a PerkinElmer Spectrum 1000, from 650 to 4500 cm–1 with an accuracy of ±2 cm–1.

Thermogravimetric Analysis (TGA)

The TGA of the purified polymers was carried out under air (to reach the harshest conditions) using a TA Instruments TGA 51 apparatus. The polymer samples (ca. 10–15 mg) were heated from room temperature to 550 °C at a heating rate of 20 °C·min–1, and the resulting thermograms display how the sample degrades under temperature (i.e., the remaining weight of the sample versus temperature).

Synthesis of the Poly­(VDF-co-MAF) Copolymer with DMC (P1)

An HC276 Hastelloy pressure reactor of 100 mL, fitted with a pressure gauge, a rupture disk, and two valves, was placed under vacuum (0.001 bar) for 30 min. After two vacuum-nitrogen cycles, a mixture consisting of 0.802 g (2.02 mmol) of bis­(tert-butylcyclohexyl) peroxydicarbonate as the initiator, 4.10 g (29.28 mmol) of 2-(trifluoromethyl) acrylic acid (MAF), and 50 mL of dimethyl carbonate as the solvent was introduced in it. After cooling the reactor to ca. −100 °C, 12 g (0.18 mol) of VDF was transferred into the autoclave by double weighing (i.e., difference of weight after and before transfer). The reactor was then gradually heated to 60 °C with an external metallic mantle that surrounded the autoclave, and this temperature was maintained for 10 h. A sight exotherm up to 22 bar was observed followed by a smooth decrease of pressure as evidence of the VDF consumption. After cooling to room temperature, the vessel was immersed in an ice bath for 30 min before releasing the unreacted VDF. Then, it was opened, the DMC was partially evaporated, and the copolymer (P1) was precipitated from a large excess of cold pentane. After filtration and drying, 9.7 g of a yellow powder as the poly­(VDF-co-MAF) copolymer was recovered in 60% yield and characterized by IR, 1H, and 19F NMR spectroscopy.

Synthesis of the Poly­(VDF-co-MAF) Copolymer in 1,1-Difluoro-1-chloroethane (Freon or Forane142b) (P2)

The same procedure as above was carried out: a mixture of 1.17 g (2.98 mmol) of bis­(tert-butylcyclohexyl) peroxydicarbonate, 8.2 g (58.57 mmol) of MAF and 30 mL of 1,1-difluoro-1-chloroethane (Forane142b) was transferred into a similar 100 mL autoclave as above. After cooling, 15 g (0.23 mol) of VDF was transferred, and the reactor was heated at 60 °C for 10 h. After the mixture was cooled, the unreacted VDF was released. After opening, the fluorinated solvent was partially evaporated, and then the copolymer was precipitated from cold pentane. After filtration and drying, 14.1 g of the poly­(VDF-co-MAF) copolymer (P2) was recovered as a yellow powder in 61% yield. The IR, 1H, and 19F NMR spectra are similar to those of the P1 copolymer.

Synthesis of the Poly­(VDF-co-MAF.APBAPE) Copolymer

In a 100 mL three-necked flask fitted with a reflux assembly equipped by a bubbler, placed under an argon atmosphere, 1.50 g of poly­(VDF-co-MAF) copolymer was dissolved in 4 mL of previously distilled DMF. The medium was cooled to 0 °C and 0.48 mL of oxalyl chloride was added dropwise for 1 h, and the mixture was heated to 65 °C with an oil bath until no release occurred. Then, the medium was cooled to 0 °C and a solution containing 0.95 g (4 mmol) of APBAPE (with 0.32 mL (4 mmol) of pyridine or 0.55 mL (4 mmol) of Et3N to form a HCl trap to favor the condensation) in 25 mL of DMF was added dropwise. The reaction was left at 20 °C for 16 h. The copolymer was precipitated from a large excess of water. After filtration and drying, 1.1 g of an orange powder as the poly­(VDF-co-MAF.APBAPE) copolymer was recovered in 45% yield. Similarly, prior to the condensation with aminophenyl boronic acid pinacol ester, a slight excess of thionyl chloride could also be used to react onto the poly­(VDF-co-MAF) copolymer and led to the same result.

Results and Discussion

The synthesis of poly­(VDF-co-MAF.APBAPE) copolymers was obtained in two steps: (1) the conventional radical copolymerization of VDF with 2-trifluoromethacrylic acid (MAF) (Scheme ) and (2) a chemical modification of the resulting poly­(VDF-co-MAF) copolymer (MAF units being changed into acid chloride functions) with aminophenyl boronic acid pinacol ester (APBAPE). The choice of the APBAPE amine was justified by the fact that the MAF has a carboxylic acid function, which can be modified by condensation to form a stable amido function.

This present study aims at using other initiators and media such as bis­(tert-butylcyclohexyl) peroxydicarbonate and DMC and 1,1-difluoro-1-chloroethane (Forane142b) as the solvents. Advantageously, that fluorinated solvent (as a precursor of VDF by dehydrochlorination) is not regarded as a per- or polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) according to OECD. −

Synthesis of the Poly­(VDF-co-MAF) Copolymer

MAF was shown not to homopolymerize under radical initiation, but it is a good partner for VDF in radical copolymerization. − Usually, tert-butyl peroxypivalate, , bis­(4-tert-butylcyclohexyl)­peroxydicarbonate, persulfate, or CF3 radical successfully enabled to initiate such a reaction, whatever MAF , or tert-butyl , or fluoroalkyl acrylates or functional 2-trifluoroacrylates [(bearing oligo­(ethylene glycol), dialkyl phosphonate, cyclocarbonate, or fluoroether]. Two copolymers were synthesized from the initial VDF:MAF molar ratios of 80:20 and 85:15 (Table ).

The radical copolymerization of VDF with MAF was initiated by tert-butylcyclohexyl peroxydicarbonate, leading to the expected copolymer in 60% yield. After purification by precipitation, both poly­(VDF-co-MAF) copolymers P1 were characterized by 1H and 19F NMR spectroscopy (Figures S1 and S2).

From the 19F NMR spectrum (eq ), the VDF:MAF molar ratio of 80:20 in the copolymer was calculated using the integrals of the peak centered at −68 ppm (assigned to CF3 from MAF units ,,− ) and from −90 to −118 ppm (corresponding to CF2 from VDF units − ,, ) (eqs and ).

The 1H NMR spectrum (Figure S1) enables to assess the molar mass of the polymer (considering the presence of a singlet at 0.8 ppm assigned to the nine protons in the tert-butyl end group and the triplet of triplets centered at 6.3 ppm characteristic of the CF2H end group) and the transfer rate (eqs and ). Using DMC induced some transfer as already reported in previous studies. , Actually, transfer reaction to DMC, i.e., the macroradical abstracts a hydrogen atom from the methyl group of DMC releasing a CH3OC­(O)­OCH2 radical that further reinitiated another poly­(VDF-co-MAF) copolymeric chain.

Synthesis of the Poly­(VDF-co-MAF.APBAPE) Copolymer

The poly­(VDF-co-MAF.APBAPE) copolymer was obtained by condensation of the poly­(VDF-co-MAF) copolymer (via the acid chloride from MAF units prepared from the addition of oxalyl chloride or thionyl chloride (Table )) with the aminophenyl boronic acid pinacol ester (Scheme and Table ).

HCl traps (e.g., pyridine or triethylamine) can be used, but a total removal of the resulting pyridinium or triethylammonium salts was difficult even after several washings with water as evidenced by the characteristic signals at 7.8, 7.4, and 1.5 ppm in the 1H NMR spectra (Figure S3). Athough Perfetti et al. could saponify the boronic ester into boronic acid in the presence of di­(ethanol) amine as a strong base, we noted that in the case where the synthesis does not use any HCl trap (P1.4), the resulting acidic medium could also enable that deprotection.

These poly­(VDF-co-MAF.APBAPE) copolymers were characterized by NMR and IR spectroscopies. First, 1H NMR spectra of APBAPE, poly­(VDF-co-MAF), and poly­(VDF-co-MAF.APBAPE) copolymers are presented in Figure S3. The signals assigned to the aromatic protons, observed between 6.7 and 7.7 ppm (for the APBAPE amine), clearly undergo a shift to the 7.3–7.5 ppm range after condensation. The successful condensation is evidenced by the low field shift of the peak centered at 5.25 ppm, characteristic of the amine function, to 9.00 ppm assigned to the amide. Between 1.1 and 1.2 ppm, two peaks are present, corresponding to the methyl groups of pinacol linked or not attached to boron (probably due to a reaction in an acid medium).

Second, the 11B NMR spectrum (Figure S4) confirms the modification of the boronic ester into boronic acid , by the presence of the broad signal at 22 ppm. It was observed that copolymer P1.2 also undergoes a partial saponification of arylpinacolyl boronic esters into boronic acid.

Compared to the 19F NMR spectrum of the poly­(VDF-co-MAF) copolymer, that of the poly­(VDF-co-MAF.APBAPE) copolymer (Figure S7) does not show any difference since both these copolymers are stable in acidic medium, while pyridine and triethyl amine are weak bases, which cannot enable any dehydrofluorination of VDF units.

Third, the 13C NMR spectrum of APBAPE amine (Figure S5) displays the signals located at 25, 84, and between 120 and 140 ppm attributed to the different carbon atoms. That of the poly­(VDF-co-MAF.APBAPE) copolymer (Figure S5) exhibits characteristic carbon atoms of CH2 of VDF and MAF at 42 ppm, while the quartet (2 J CF = 26 Hz) centered at 51.3 ppm corresponds to C ­(CF3). The triplet (1 J CF = 250 Hz) centered at 120 ppm is characteristic of the CF2 in VDF units, while the quartet (1 J CF = 267 Hz) located between 121 and 129 ppm arises from CF3 in MAF units. The singlets ranging between 129 and 133 ppm are attributed to aromatic carbon atoms.

The IR ATR spectra (Figure S6) also evidence the success of the condensation of the APBAPE by the absence of both vibration bands of the primary amine (at 3450 and 3350 cm–1) and the presence of that of the amide (at 3370 cm–1). The carbonyl vibration band of the amido function in the modified MAF unit is highlighted at 1710 cm–1, while those of B–O and N–H are overlapping at 1600 cm–1 and that of B–C centered at 1000 cm–1.

The thermal properties of the different copolymers were also studied by TGA under air to supply harsher conditions. As expected, the TGA thermogram of the poly­(VDF-co-MAF) copolymer (Figure ) exhibits a poor stability in air with a decomposition temperature that starts at 144 °C for a loss of 2% due to decarboxylation of MAF units. In contrast, the poly­(VDF-co-MAF.APBAPE) displays a better stability in air, and between 183 and 201 °C, a weight loss of 2% was noted. The first weight loss could be due to the decarboxylation of the remaining acid functions. However, at 500 °C, the copolymers lost 75% meaning that ca. 25% was char, assigned to the presence of boron-containing groups, which enhances the thermal properties of the resulting copolymers, hence confirming a recent study.

Conclusions

Poly­(VDF-co-MAF) copolymers, obtained by radical copolymerization of VDF with MAF, were modified to lead to original poly­(VDF-co-MAF.APBAPE) copolymers containing boron-containing groups. This condensation was carried out in the absence or presence of HCl traps (i.e., pyridine and triethylamine). The resulting copolymers, characterized by 1H, 11B, 13C, 19F NMR, and IR spectroscopy, composed of VDF/modified MAF 80/20 and 65/35 molar ratios, displayed a higher thermal stability (of 40 °C under air) than that of the nonboronated polymeric precursor. This better stability can be explained because of the presence of carboxylic acid functions in MAF units of the poly­(VDF-co-MAF) precursor inducing decarboxylation. Such novel boronated containing fluoropolymer may have specific properties for various applications including electronics and coatings.
