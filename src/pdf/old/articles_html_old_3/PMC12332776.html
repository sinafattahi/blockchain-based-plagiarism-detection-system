Abstract

To study the initial characteristics of gas desorption in high-rank soft and hard coals, a purpose-built constant-temperature gas desorption device was used to conduct gas desorption experiments on three groups of collected high-rank soft and hard coal samples. The results show that the gas desorption index (K 1) of drill cuttings from soft and hard coals follows a power function relationship with the adsorption equilibrium pressure. Under the same adsorption equilibrium pressure, the gas desorption index (K 1) of drill cuttings from soft coal is 1.39–2.66 times that of hard coal. When the adsorption equilibrium pressure is 1 MPa, in the first 1 min, the gas desorption rate of soft coal is 1.38–2.56 times that of hard coal, and in the first 10 min, the gas desorption rate of soft coal is 1.35–2.49 times that of hard coal. The initial law of gas desorption in soft and hard coals is suitable for classifying Sun Zhongxu’s form of the governing equation. To reveal the causes of the differences in the initial characteristics of high-rank soft and hard coal gas desorption from a microscopic perspective, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was used to observe the surface pore structure of soft and hard coals. Additionally, mercury intrusion porosimetry (MIP) and low-temperature N2 adsorption/desorption (LTN2GA) experiments were undertaken to ascertain the full pore size distribution of soft and hard coal samples. The results show that (1) the breccia pores on the surface of soft coal are more developed, and the pore connectivity is better, which provides favorable conditions for the rapid desorption of gas at the initial stage. Furthermore, the crushed grain pores and ink bottle pores on the surface of soft coal are more developed, providing a place for the adsorption of more gas; (2) the total pore capacity of soft coal is 4.12–10.57 times that of hard coal, and the proportion of large and medium pore capacity in soft coal is 4.47–10.70 times that of hard coal, which provides a favorable channel for the rapid desorption of gas in the initial stage. The ratio of medium and micropore specific surface area of soft coal is 2.26–2.77 times that of hard coal, offering more positions for gas adsorption. The results offer a reference for those seeking to understand the differences in gas desorption in the early stage of high-rank soft and hard coals and provide technical support for improving the accuracy of determining coalbed gas content and gas desorption index (K 1) of drill cuttings.

1. Introduction

Disasters such as coal and gas outbursts are very serious in China. Whether the gas content of the coal seam and the desorption index of drilling cuttings can be obtained accurately plays an important role in the prediction and inspection of coal and gas outburst disasters. − The estimation of the loss of gas content in the coal seam and the determination of the gas desorption index (K 1) of drill cuttings for predicting outburst risk in the coal seam working face are closely related to the initial characteristics of coal gas desorption. − Coal and gas outbursts often occur in soft coal seams, and the initial characteristics of gas desorption in medium- and low-rank soft and hard coals are quite different. , As the coal mining depth increases, the metamorphic degree of the coal seam also increases, necessitating an investigation into the characteristics of the initial stage of gas desorption in high-rank soft and hard coals and the causes of these differences to prevent and control coal and gas outburst disasters. ,

In recent years, many scholars at home and abroad have carried out relevant research on the characteristics of the initial stages of soft and hard coal gas desorption and the analysis of the reasons for the differences. Through constant-temperature gas desorption experiments, Yunbo Li found that under different pressures and particle sizes, the initial desorption rate and desorption amount of soft coal were significantly higher than those of hard coal, and suggested that the main reason for the difference was that the pores of soft coal were more developed. Jiang Jiayu verified that the desorption rate and cumulative desorption amount of soft coal in 0–2 min were larger than those of hard coal through constant-temperature gas desorption experiments and clarified that the phenomenon was related to the characteristics of mesopore and micropore size distribution by using MIP and LTN2GA experiments. Huijie et al., Mingkai et al., and Feng et al. revealed that the distribution of mesopores and macropores is the main reason for the differences in the desorption characteristics of soft and hard coal gas through LTN2GA experiments. Tu et al., through soft coal crushing experiments, found that the gas desorption rate of soft coal in the first 10 s was 11.47 times higher than that of hard coal, and the pore volume and specific surface area of micropores, mesopores, and macropores of soft coal were more than 10 times higher than those of hard coal. Guo et al. demonstrated the differences between soft and hard coals in terms of adsorption, initial desorption rate, and cumulative desorption based on the diffusion kinetic model. Through high-pressure adsorption experiments combined with multiscale pore characterization, Liu et al. found that the increase in the total pore volume and specific surface area of the micropores directly drove the initial desorption rate and desorption amount of soft coal. Zhang et al. used LTN2GA experiments to reveal the effects of coal phase and coal rank on pore structure. Wang et al. explained the molecular dynamics mechanism of the adsorption–desorption variability of soft and hard coals at the microscopic level by constructing molecular models of adsorption processes for different coal orders.

In summary, existing studies have mainly found the differences in the initial characteristics of gas desorption in soft and hard coals through theoretical analysis and experimental tests (primarily using MIP and LTN2GA experiments), and found that the characteristics of the distribution of mesopore (2–50 nm) and macropore (>50 nm) pore diameters in soft and hard coals are the main reasons for these initial desorption characteristics. However, the following problems still remain: (1) The differences in surface pore structure and internal pore size are not comprehensive enough: on the one hand, there is no experimental test on the surface pore structure of coal samples; on the other hand, the accuracy of the MIP for pore sizes larger than 100 nm is higher, while the accuracy of LTN2GA for pore sizes between 0.35 nm and 100 nm is higher. Therefore, it is difficult to accurately determine the internal pore structure of coal samples by a single experimental method. It is difficult to accurately determine the internal pore structure of coal samples by single experimental means; (2) At present, research on the differences between soft and hard coals and the analysis of their causes are more concentrated on medium- and low-rank coals, with less attention paid to high-rank coals. To address these knowledge gaps, our research team developed a specialized constant-temperature gas desorption apparatus to systematically investigate the characteristics of high-rank soft and hard coals, including initial desorption capacity, drill cutting gas desorption indices (such as the gas desorption index (K 1) of drill cuttings), and the desorption law. By combining the advantages of SEM, MIP, and LTN2GA experiments, we have systematically mapped the spatial heterogeneity of pore architecture and complete pore size distributions from surface to interior regions. This multimodal approach enables a comprehensive elucidation and quantitative characterization of the microstructural determinants governing differential initial gas desorption behaviors in high-rank soft and hard coals.

2. Gas Desorption Experiment of High-Rank Soft and Hard Coals

2.1. Experimental Device and Scheme

2.1.1. The Experimental Device

The experimental study was conducted using a constant-temperature gas desorption system to investigate gas release characteristics in high-rank soft and hard coals. The system comprises four principal components: (1) Degassing unit: Consisting of a vacuum pump with an ultimate vacuum level of 6.8 × 10– 2 Pa and a vacuum gauge (measurement range: 1 × 10– 5 Pa). (2) Pressurization unit: Configured with a 15 MPa high-pressure gas cylinder (gas purity: 99.99%) coupled with pressure sensors (measurement range: 0–2.0 MPa; accuracy: ±0.5% FS). (3) Temperature control unit: Incorporating a thermostatic chamber with precise temperature regulation (operating range: 0–95 °C; stability: ±0.01 °C). (4) Adsorption–desorption monitoring unit: Equipped with a data acquisition system and flowmeter (measurement range: 0–300 mL/min; accuracy: ±1% FS), as illustrated in Figure .

(1) Collection of coal samples: three groups of dry coal samples of soft and hard coals with a particle size of 1 to 3 mm from the C4 coal seam of Bailongshan Coal Mine in Yunnan Province, the 15# coal seam of Xinjing Coal Mine in Shanxi Province, and the 15# coal seam of Shijiazhuang Coal Mine in Shanxi Province were collected and prepared. 2.2. Experimental Result

2.2.1. Experimental Measurement of Gas Desorption Index (K 1) for Drill Cuttings of High-Rank Soft and Hard Coals

The gas desorption index (K 1) of drill cuttings for three groups of coal samples was fitted to the adsorption equilibrium pressure P, and the resulting relationship is shown in Figure .

As shown in Figure , there is a power function relationship between the gas desorption index (K 1) of drill cuttings, the desorption index of drill cuttings gas, and the adsorption equilibrium pressure for the three groups of high-rank soft and hard coals. Under the adsorption equilibrium pressures of 0.5, 0.7, 1.0, 1.2, and 1.5 MPa, the gas desorption index (K 1) of drill cuttings of BLS2 is 2.44–2.66 times higher than that of BLS1, the gas desorption index (K 1) of drill cuttings of XJ2 is 1.65–1.74 times higher than that of XJ1, and the gas desorption index (K 1) of drill cuttings of SJZ2 is 1.39–1.47 times higher than that of SJZ1. In summary, the gas desorption index (K 1) of drill cuttings of soft coal is 1.39–2.66 times that of hard coal under the same adsorption equilibrium pressure.

2.2.2. Initial Gas Desorption of High-Rank Soft and Hard Coals

The initial gas desorption volume and desorption rate over time for the three groups of high-rank soft and hard coals are shown in Figures and , respectively.

As shown in Figure , the gas desorption capacity of high-rank soft and hard coals presents a changing trend of “rapid increaseslow increase”. The total desorption capacity of SJZ2 is 6.86 cm3/g, and that of SJZ1 is 5.07 cm3/g. The total desorption capacity of SJZ2 is 1.35 times that of SJZ1.

As illustrated in Figure , the initial desorption rate of high-rank soft and hard coal gas shows a changing trend of “rapidly decreasingslowly decreasing”. To summarize: the initial gas desorption volume and initial gas desorption rate of high-rank soft and hard coals have more consistent trends, but at an adsorption equilibrium pressure of 1.0 MPa, in the first 10 min, the gas desorption volume of the soft coal is 1.35–2.49 times that of the hard coal; in the first 1 min, the gas desorption rate of the soft coal is 1.38–2.56 times that of the hard coal.

3. Microscopic-Scale Explanation of the Causes of the Differences in Gas Desorption

3.1. SEM Experiment

SEM was used to observe the surface pore structure among three groups of high-rank soft and hard coals, and the results are shown in Figure .

As shown in Figure a,b, BLS1 is dominated by gas pores, with a small number of microcracks mostly filled with minerals, accompanied by ink-bottle pores. BLS2 is mainly composed of breccia pores and crushed particle pores, accompanied by ink-bottle pores and microcracks. As shown in Figure c,d, XJ1 is dominated by gas pores, and the microcracks are mostly filled with minerals. The pores of XJ2 are more developed breccia pores. As illustrated in Figure e,f, SJZ1 is dominated by pores, with a small number of microcracks mostly filled with minerals. SJZ2 is mainly composed of breccia pores and crushed particle pores. Therefore, the pore structure of high-rank soft and hard coals with different characteristics at the initial stage of gas desorption is as follows: (1) The surface of hard coal is dominated by gas pores, which are mostly filled and blocked by minerals and exist in isolation with poor connectivity and weak desorption capacity; (2) The breccia pores on the surface of soft coal are more developed, with better pore connectivity, which provides favorable conditions for the rapid desorption of gas in the initial stage of coal samples; (3) Crushed particle pores and ink-bottle pores of soft coal are more developed, which provide more points for the adsorption of more gas by soft coal.

3.2. MIP Experiments

Coal samples with particle sizes identical to those used in the gas desorption experiments were employed to characterize the pore structure of three groups of high-rank soft and hard coals. The pore size distribution ranged from 3 to 95,000 nm, as determined by MIP using an AutoPore V9620 fully automated mercury porosimeter. The mercury intrusion-extrusion curves for hard coal and soft coal are presented in Figures and , respectively.

Figures and illustrate the mercury intrusion-extrusion characteristics of soft and hard coals from the same coal seam in the same mine, revealing the following key observations:(1) Mercury intrusion behavior: below 0.01 MPa, both soft and hard coals exhibit a rapid increase in cumulative mercury intrusion. Between 0.01 and 0.1 MPa, the cumulative mercury intrusion of soft coal continues to rise, whereas that of hard coal increases before stabilizing. This stage primarily reflects the pore space of micropores and mesopores. In the 1–10 MPa range, hard coal shows stable mercury intrusion, while soft coal demonstrates a gradual increase, corresponding to the transition from mesopores to macropores. Above 10 MPa, both coal types exhibit a slight increase in cumulative mercury intrusion, indicating the presence of visible pores and fractures. Notably, the nanoscale pores in soft coal are more developed. Overall, the cumulative mercury intrusion of soft coal increases steadily with pressure, whereas hard coal exhibits rapid initial intrusion followed by stabilization and then a slow rise. (2) Mercury extrusion behavior: at extrusion pressures exceeding 10 MPa, soft coal displays a pronounced hysteresis loop, suggesting superior pore connectivity, uniform pore distribution (predominantly open pores), and enhanced connectivity from micropores to macropores. In contrast, hard coal shows minimal hysteresis, indicating poor pore connectivity, uneven pore distribution (mostly closed or semiclosed pores), and limited interconnectivity. (3) Comparative analysis of intrusion-extrusion curves: the intrusion and extrusion curves of soft coal exhibit clear separation, with the hysteresis gap widening as withdrawal pressure decreases. Hard coal, however, shows negligible separation between its intrusion and extrusion curves. These results demonstrate that soft coal exhibits significantly greater hysteresis than hard coal, attributable to its well-developed brecciated pore structure and enhanced connectivity of large- and medium-sized pores.

3.3. LTN2GA Experiments

Coal samples with the same particle size as those used in the gas desorption experiment were tested to analyze the pore structure of three groups of high-rank soft and hard coals with pore sizes ranging from 0.35–500 nm, using Micromeritics’ ASAP 2460 low-temperature N2 adsorption instrument. The low-temperature N2 adsorption–desorption isothermal curves of hard coal are shown in Figure , and the low-temperature N2 adsorption–desorption isothermal curves of soft coal are illustrated in Figure .

The classification method of Jiang et al. was used to analyze the types of low-temperature liquid nitrogen adsorption–desorption curves for three groups of soft and hard coals. It can be seen from Figures a and a that the adsorption–desorption curves of BLS2 conform to the H1 type, and the adsorption–desorption curves of this type are stable, indicating that the coal sample contains fewer micropores and exhibits a small difference in specific surface area compared with BLS1. The BLS1 adsorption–desorption curve conforms to the H3 type, and the rate of change in the hard coal adsorption curve is slow when p < 0.4, while the desorption curve has an inflection point and hysteresis loop when p = 0.5. The results show that there are ink-bottle-shaped and slot-shaped pores in the structure, and the connectivity is good. According to Figures b and b, the adsorption–desorption curves of XJ1 and XJ2 conform to the H3 type, indicating that XJ1 and XJ2 have the same pore type as BLS1. The particle fragmentation pores of XJ2 are more developed, and the overall adsorption capacity is larger. According to Figures c and c, the adsorption–desorption curves of SJZ1 and SJZ2 conform to the H2 type, and the inflection point appears when p = 0.5 or so, indicating that the ink-bottle-shaped pores and slit plate-shaped pores of SJZ1 and SJZ2 are well-developed and contribute greatly to the amount of gas adsorption. The adsorption–desorption curves of XJ1, XJ2, SJZ1, and SJZ2 show some degree of separation in the region of low relative pressure (p < 0.4), and the reason for this phenomenon is intercalation, involving solid swelling. The difference between the adsorption–desorption curves of soft and hard coals indicates that soft coal contains more developed ink-bottle-shaped pores. These pores retain a significant amount of gas, intensifying the conflict between gas enrichment and transportation. Additionally, soft coal is primarily characterized by breccia and crushed particle pores. During coal mining, even minor disturbances may trigger the bursting of these “airbag”-like structures, leading to a series of chain reactions. The released gas rapidly disperses under pressure, impacting the coal seam and seeking pathways until it surges into the roadway, ultimately resulting in a coal and gas outburst accident.

As can be seen from Table , the total pore volume of soft coal is greater than that of hard coal, and the total pore volume (V t) of the three groups of soft coal is 4.12–10.57 times that of hard coal. The pore volumes of the three groups of soft coal macropores are 0.038837 cm3/g, 0.008279 cm3/g, and 0.009159 cm3/g, respectively, and the corresponding pore volumes of the three groups of hard coal macropores are 0.003243 cm3/g, 0.001327 cm3/g, and 0.000480 cm3/g, respectively. The pore capacity of soft coal is 6.24–19.08 times that of hard coal; The mesoporous pore volumes of the three groups of soft coal are 0.002 cm3/g, 0.00146 cm3/g, and 0.00301 cm3/g, respectively, and the corresponding pore volumes of the three groups of hard coal are 0.000574 cm3/g, 0.000853 cm3/g, and 0.001868 cm3/g, respectively. The differences in the pore volumes between macropores and mesopores of high-rank soft and hard coals are more obvious, and the macropores of soft coal are more developed, which provides a favorable channel for gas desorption in the early stage of soft coal.

3.5. Full Pore Specific Surface Area Analysis of High-Rank Soft and Hard Coals

Based on the results of all the pores of coal samples tested by LTN2GA experiments, and according to the IUPAC classification method, , the specific surface area of the coal sample was categorized into three pore size ranges: S1 (>50 nm), S2 (2–50 nm), and S3 (<2 nm). The total specific surface area (St) was defined as the sum of these three components (St = S1 + S2 + S3). The specific surface area was calculated using the Barrett–Joyner–Halenda (BJH) method, while the total specific surface area was determined based on the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) theory. Specific surface area is an important index to characterize the ability of coal to adsorb gases, where mainly mesopore and micropore specific surface areas provide locations for gas adsorption by coal bodies. , As shown in Table , the total BET specific surface area of the three groups of soft coals is 0.8357 m2/g to 1.59 m2/g, while the corresponding total BET specific surface area of hard coals is 0.498 m2/g to 0.823 m2/g, respectively, and the total BET specific surface area of soft coals is 1.38–1.93 times that of hard coals. The specific surface area of micro- and mesopores of the three groups of soft coals is 90.09%, 93.67%, and 88.39%, respectively. The corresponding specific surface area of micro- and mesopores of the three groups of hard coals is 93.03%, 93.28%, and 93.14%, respectively. Additionally, the specific surface area of micro- and mesopores of the three groups of soft coals is 2.26–2.77 times that of hard coals. The total specific surface area and the percentage of micro- and mesopore specific surface area of soft coals are larger than those of hard coals, which provide more points for soft coals to adsorb more gas and are one of the causes for the differences in the initial characteristics of gas desorption in high-rank soft and hard coals.

4. Discussion

  • Analysis of the mechanism behind differences in the initial characteristics of high-rank soft and hard coal gas desorption: Compared with hard coal, soft coal formed by tectonic destruction has a significantly faster initial gas desorption rate. The core mechanism behind this difference may lie in the significant differences between the two in terms of pore-fracture structure, diffusion capacity, and seepage characteristics: ① Differences in fracture network development: Soft coal has low strength and high brittleness, making it prone to fracturing under tectonic stress, resulting in a higher-density network of micrometer-scale fractures and a more fragmented coal body structure. These well-developed fractures provide direct, low-resistance migration pathways for gas, greatly reducing the equivalent diffusion path length required for gas to migrate from the interior of the matrix block to the main fracture channel. In contrast, hard coal has a lower fracture density and poorer connectivity. After being released from the matrix interior, gas must travel through a longer, more tortuous network of nano- to micrometer-scale pores to reach the limited fracture channels. , ② Differences in effective diffusion coefficient: Brittle deformation in soft coal produces many open pores (mainly meso- and macropores) and significantly enhances pore connectivity, greatly reducing the diffusion resistance of gas migration within the matrix and directly improving the effective diffusion coefficient. Hard coal has a denser matrix and higher mechanical strength, with a pore structure dominated by gas pores (micropores and small pores). It has poor connectivity, a complex and highly tortuous pore network, and long and tortuous diffusion paths, resulting in a significantly lower effective diffusion coefficient than soft coal. Compared to hard coal, soft coal has a higher percentage of mesopores and macropores and better pore connectivity, which provides a fundamental structural advantage for the rapid diffusion of gas within the soft coal matrix (toward fractures). The high effective diffusion coefficient of soft coal allows gas to diffuse more quickly from the coal matrix to the fracture network. ③ Differences in the dynamic response to permeability: During the initial stage of rapid decompression, the matrix shrinkage effect caused by gas desorption is more pronounced in soft coal (due to its lower elastic modulus). This significant matrix shrinkage effectively increases the opening of existing fractures, thereby significantly enhancing the permeability of the fracture system. The increase in permeability directly accelerates the flow rate of gas through the fracture network to low-pressure areas. In contrast, hard coal has a high matrix rigidity and a limited degree of shrinkage, making it difficult to effectively increase the opening of fractures. The fracture system maintains a relatively stable low permeability state in the early stages, resulting in high resistance to gas seepage.

  • Quantitative characterization of the influence of micropores (less than 0.35 nm) on the initial differences in gas desorption of soft and hard coals: Due to current experimental limitations, this study was unable to directly quantify the specific impact of closed pores and microporous structures with pore sizes smaller than 0.35 nm on the initial differences in gas desorption of soft and hard coals. This pore size (especially closed pores) may have a significant impact on gas adsorption–desorption kinetics, especially during the rapid release initial stage of desorption. Future research will employ carbon dioxide adsorption and small-angle X-ray scattering (SAXS) techniques to accurately quantify the pore structure characteristics within this scale range, thereby revealing the underlying mechanisms of the differences in the initial stages of the gas desorption of soft and hard coals.

  • Discussion on the consistency of initial characteristics of gas desorption of soft and hard coals: Based on the results of gas desorption experiments on three sets of soft and hard coal samples, it is not yet possible to fully reveal the common patterns of high-rank soft and hard coals in the initial stage of desorption. In order to improve the universality and reliability of the conclusions, follow-up work will systematically collect high-rank soft and hard coal samples with different gas grades, metamorphic degrees, destructive type, and particle size distributions and conduct systematic gas desorption experiments. Based on accumulated experimental data, we aim to fit a mathematical model that more accurately describes the initial characteristics of high-rank soft and hard coal desorption, with a view to significantly improving the accuracy of determining coalbed gas content and gas desorption index (K 1) of drill cuttings.

  • Analysis of the contradiction between the pore advantage and the tendency to protrude in soft coal: Although experimental observations indicate that soft coal typically has a more developed pore structure and better connectivity (which theoretically facilitate gas escape), accidents involving coal and gas outbursts occur more frequently in soft coal areas. The key to this phenomenon lies in the fact that coal and gas outbursts are essentially the result of the dynamic interaction among crustal stress, gas pressure, and the physical-mechanical properties of coal. Although soft coal has good gas storage and transportation conditions, its mechanical strength is significantly low. Under the stress caused by mining disturbance, soft coal is more prone to destruction and instability. This instability and destruction significantly weaken the integrity of the coal body structure, on the one hand, and on the other hand, it drastically weakens the confining pressure required to maintain the pore gas pressure, causing the enormous gas expansion energy and rock elastic potential energy stored in the coal seam to be released instantaneously. The rapid and uncontrolled release of this energy drives the gradual destruction and violent ejection of coal, ultimately triggering coal and gas outburst accidents. ,

5. Conclusions

  • Gas desorption experiments were conducted using a purpose-built constant-temperature gas desorption device. Both similarities and differences were observed in the initial gas desorption characteristics between high-rank soft coal and hard coal. The similarities include: ① the gas desorption index (K 1) of drill cuttings from both soft and hard coal follows a power function relationship with adsorption equilibrium pressure; ② the initial gas desorption characteristics of high-rank soft and hard coal both show high consistency with Sun Chongxu’s form. The differences include: ① under the same adsorption equilibrium pressure, the gas desorption index (K 1) of soft coal drill cuttings is significantly higher than that of hard coal; ② for the same desorption duration, both the cumulative gas desorption volume and desorption rate are greater for soft coal compared to hard coal.

  • The advantages of synthesized SEM, MIP, and LTN2GA experiments revealed from the surface to the inside the reasons for the different characteristics of the initial stage of gas desorption of high-rank soft and hard coals as ① Pore Structure Differences: Soft coal exhibits more developed breccia pores, crushed particle pores, and ink-bottle pores compared to hard coal, along with superior pore connectivity and adsorption capacity. These characteristics facilitate greater gas adsorption and rapid initial desorption. ②Pore Volume Comparison: The total pore volume of soft coal is 4.12–10.57 times that of hard coal, while the proportion of large and medium pores in soft coal is 4.47–10.70 times higher. This enhanced pore structure provides favorable pathways for rapid gas desorption in the initial stage. ③ Specific Surface Area Analysis: The total BET specific surface area of soft coal is 1.38–1.93 times that of hard coal, with the proportion of medium and micropore surface areas being 2.26–2.77 times greater. This increased surface area offers more active sites for gas adsorption. The primary reasons for the distinct initial gas desorption behaviors between high-rank soft and hard coals are ① the more developed breccia pores, crushed particle pores, and ink-bottle pores in soft coal, ② its superior pore connectivity and adsorption capacity, and its significantly larger total pore volume and higher proportion of large and medium pores.

  • This study systematically characterizes the initial gas desorption properties of high-rank soft and hard coals, employing both qualitative and quantitative approaches to analyze the differences in their desorption behaviors. Furthermore, we investigate the underlying mechanisms responsible for the greater propensity of soft coals to outburst phenomena. These findings provide: a theoretical framework for better understanding coal and gas outburst mechanisms and technical guidance for improving the accuracy of risk prediction and validation indicators for coal and gas outbursts.
