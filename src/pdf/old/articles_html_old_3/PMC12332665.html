Abstract

Plants can readily absorb Cd and distribute it to various parts of the body. In this study, various agronomic, physiological, and biochemical features of L. were analyzed under Cd stress along with the potential of chitosan-copper (CTS-Cu) nanocomposites in the mitigation of Cd stress. A two-factor (Cd stress and CTS-Cu nanocomposite) completely randomized design pot experiment with three replicates was conducted under natural conditions. The following treatments were administered: control: normal (untreated) plants, Cd stress: 5 mM CdCl2 (500 mL per pot), NC1: foliar application of CTS-Cu nanocomposite at 100 mg L–1, and NC2: foliar application of CTS-Cu nanocomposite at 200 mg L–1. Results showed that Cd stress reduced the performance index (PI; 52%) and quantum efficiency of photosystem II (10%). This also caused lipid peroxidation in the form of elevated malondialdehyde (33%), relative membrane permeability (31%), and excessive release of H2O2 (70%). Cd stress also disrupted the ionic balance through reducing the uptake of K+ (41%) and Ca2+ (44%). Stressed plants also had high levels of Cd metal ions in their root and shoot. Both levels of CTS-Cu nanocomposite (NC1 and NC2) mitigated oxidative stress caused by Cd toxicity. But the NC2 more efficiently reduced Cd toxicity in plants. It improved chlorophyll formation (30%) and chlorophyll fluorescence, as indicated by increased PI (372%). NC2 also increased the activities of antioxidants such as catalase (29%) and peroxidase (30%). It also improved net photosynthesis rate (64%), transpiration rate (17%), and stomatal conductance (54%). NC2 maintained reduced Cd uptake through the root (36%) and its accumulation in the shoot (41%). These findings suggest that CTS-Cu nanocomposite can be used to eliminate abiotic stresses in other crop plants as well.

Introduction

Heavy metal toxicity in plants can disrupt nutrient uptake and transport, normally leading to nutritional deficiencies associated with some elements such as iron and magnesium, which play highly significant roles in photosynthesis and respiration. Heavy metals, apart from the accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and methylglyoxal (MG), also cause a wide range of cellular damage in plants. For example, Cd toxicity impacts plant growth rigorously by elevating reactive oxygen species and malondialdehyde, lowering chlorophyll formation and impairing photosynthetic activities as result of leaf chlorosis. Moreover, Cd assimilation has also repressed the uptake of necessary nutrients and significantly delayed the development of plants.

In recent years, nanotechnology has been recognized as a valuable strategy for improving crop resilience and yield, with nanoscale nutrients and fertilizers attracting significant interest for their distinctive physicochemical characteristics that enhance nutrient accessibility, absorption, and utilization in plants. Copper nanoparticles have been explored with their extensive use in many microorganisms, starting from bacteria, fungi, algae, and even plants. Copper-based NPs based on polysaccharides are being used for seed priming before sowing for the stimulation of germination, increase in productivity, and prevention of seed diseases without influencing the biocenosis of fields. Copper-based NPs are also applied as plant growth regulators and immune protectors of plants. Recently, copper nanoparticles (CuNPs) came under the spotlight due to their potential applications in different industries, primarily because of the antibacterial, antioxidant, and anticancer property.

Chitosan (CTS), a naturally occurring biopolymer generated from chitin, has the potential to function as an elicitor and a biostimulant in the agricultural environment. CTS is a cationic polymer found in nature that is produced when chitin is deacetylated. The use of CTS as a plant growth regulator to reduce a variety of environmental stressors has grown in popularity. CTS’s nontoxicity and biocompatibility enhance its appeal due to its antibacterial, antioxidant, and chelating properties. Its ability to augment nitrogen absorption renders CTS advantageous for agricultural productivity and crop quality. Similarly, CTS nanoparticles (CTS-NPs) play an important role in the phytoremediation of heavy metal and improved the biomass of plant by increasing the nutrient uptake. CTS-NPs can enhance the resilience of crops against different abiotic stresses through a mechanism of transduction that makes use of secondary messengers. For instance, CTS-NPs application could enhance the photosynthetic rate through abscisic acid synthesis, stimulate stomatal closure, activate antioxidant enzymes via nitric oxide, stimulate hydrogen peroxide signal transduction pathways, and elevate productions of sugars and amino acids, which are key factors in adjusting osmotic balance, stress signal transduction, and energy metabolism under abiotic stresses. It is also observed that plants treated with CTS-NPs accumulated less cadmium (Cd) in their tissues than the control. Hence, CTS-NPs were able to lessen the metal toxicity in plants. Because CTS-NPs contain a variety of functional groups that bind to metals, they also exhibit chelating qualities.

L. widely known as chili pepper is a tropical fruit belonging to the family and cultivated globally in various countries. It has substantial economic and social importance as a crop. The most important pepper-based industrial product among many oleoresins and other fortified extracts is the powdered form called paprika, which constitutes about 70% of the total production and goes into use as a spice in meat products, soups, sauces, and snack foods. It is estimated that 60,000 tons of paprika powder is produced globally every year. species are utilized globally for various culinary uses and are also valued for medicinal properties. These species are used as antiseptics, stimulants, and tonics. species are used in managing dyspepsia, treating disorders associated with flatulence and anorexia as well as improving circulation and digestion. Key bioactive compounds in include dihydrocapsaicin, capsaicin, and other carotenoids. Such compositions have been known to bear considerable pharmacological potential that tend to be essential for cardiac as well as anticancer application.

It is found that metal nanoparticles (NPs) can be combined with other polymeric materials to form nanocomposites (NCPs), which have better qualities than individual NPs, including increased catalytic and antioxidative activity, high biodegradability, and biocompatibility. CTS nanocomposites are developed using diverse chemical, physical, and physicochemical methods. Previously, CTS and Schiff base metal complexes have shown beneficiary roles in managing drought-induced negative effects on pomegranate orchard. These complexes improved accumulation of osmoprotectants (proline), phytohormones, and antioxidative defense under drought stress. Despite extensive studies on CTS and copper-based nanoparticles for amelioration of abiotic stresses in plants, their combined nanocomposites present a novel approach to mitigate Cd toxicity in . The main objectives of this study were to synthesize and characterize chitosan-copper nanocomposites and to study the oxidative stress in due Cd toxicity and to assess the efficacy of chitosan-copper nanocomposite to mitigate Cd toxicity in .

Results

Characterization

UV–Visible Spectroscopy Analysis

It was utilized to validate the synthesis of a chitosan-copper (CTS-Cu) nanocomposite through the assessment of plasmon resonance and the estimation of collective oscillations of conduction band electrons in relation to electromagnetic waves. This provided insights into the stability and aggregation of nanocomposites within a liquid medium. The concept of “surface plasmon resonance” (SPR) denotes a collective excitation of electrons situated in the conduction band adjacent to the surfaces of nanoparticles. The absorption bands observed in the characteristic spectra of metallic nanoparticles are distinctive as the incident light resonates with the conduction band electrons located at the surface of the nanoparticles. Consequently, metallic nanoparticles display a distinctive optical absorption spectrum within the ultraviolet–visible spectrum. UV absorption of CTS-Cu nanocomposite was observed between 250 and 300 nm (Figure A).

Scanning Electron Microscopy

Through the application of manual measurement and enumeration of particles, or by employing advanced analytical software, SEM facilitated the examination of particle morphology and the subsequent construction of histograms derived from the captured images. A critical prerequisite for the effective operation of SEM under vacuum conditions is the mitigation of electron interactions with gaseous molecules, which is essential for attaining elevated resolution. The principle of the SEM apparatus hinges on the premise that primary electrons emitted from the source impart energy to the atomic electrons within the sample, subsequently allowing these electrons to be emitted in the form of secondary electrons (SE). The generation of an image is accomplished by the collection of these SE from any designated location on the sample surface. SEM of CTS-Cu nanocomposites showed the spherical shape (Figure B and E).

Fourier-Transform Infrared

The Fourier-transform infrared (FTIR) technique described structural variations in terms of the way molecules interact with each other and indicated the possible presence of their interactions. The peaks observed between 500 and 4000 cm–1 describe the presence of different possible functional groups in the tested sample. The spectral features at 456.20 cm–1 and 480.28 cm–1 can most probably be assigned to the Cu–O vibrational modes, which indicated that copper interacts with the composite matrix. The spectral bands appearing around 522.71 cm–1 and 536.21 cm–1 further suggest the presence of a metal oxygen bond; therefore, the assumption about copper nanoparticles or ionic species in the composite is strengthened. Further analysis of the higher wavenumber range has shown that the spectral peak at 569 cm–1 is probably caused by bending vibrations of metallic elements or other components in the structure. The wider bands around 2046.47 cm–1 that are observed may be due to the stretching of CN, possibly related to the presence of nitrile groups or its interactions that are related to carbon. These functional groups confirm the CTS-Cu nanocomposites (Figure C).

Energy-Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy

The elementary information, obtained by using EDX, in cases of nanoparticle characterization shows the relative presence of metals and nonmetals and their importance, to be considered appropriately in evaluation of the structure and possible functionalizations. EDX spectroscopy acquires the EDX spectrum, which provides a clear and accurate fingerprint of elements in the sample, because their X-ray energies are specific to the atoms of the sample. EDX also provided the quantity of elements present in the sample. In our results, EDX showed the presence of Cu 9.70%, carbon 53.99%, oxygen 35.26%, and sulfur 1.05%. The presence of copper as well as carbon and oxygen, which are fundamental to the structural composition of CTS, indicated the formation of the CTS-Cu nanocomposite. Sulfur appeared due to the constituents (CuSO4) used in the chemical reaction (Figure D).

Effects of Chitosan-Copper Nanocomposites on Growth Attributes of under Cd Stress

In this study, Cd stress (5 mM CdCl2) markedly reduced the growth of the plants. Figures and showed that Cd stress significantly reduced shoot length (34%), root length (47%), and fresh and dry weight (48%) of plants, compared with control (without stress). It also reduced the number of leaves (20%). On the other hand, CTS-Cu nanocomposite foliar spray improved the growth of in both control (without stress) and stress conditions. Results showed that foliar spray at high concentration (200 mg L–1) was more effective, and it increased the above-mentioned attributes by 44, 39, 66, 68, and 86%, respectively, compared to control (without stress) plants. Similarly in stressed plants, these growth attributes were increased by 74, 142, 133, 140, and 60%, respectively, with foliar application of 200 mg L–1 CTS-Cu nanocomposites compared to stress only plants (Figure A–E).

Effects of Chitosan-Copper Nanocomposites on Photosynthetic Pigments of under Cd Stress

Figure A–C depicts that 5 mM Cd stress rigorously impacted photosynthetic pigmentation in plants. It reduced chlorophyll (Chl) a and b and total Chl contents by 45, 34, and 40%, respectively, compared to the control plants (without stress). Meanwhile, the foliar spray of CTS-Cu nanocomposites resulted in improving these photosynthetic pigments at both concentrations and in all conditions, i.e., control (without stress) and Cd stress. However, the effect of nanocomposites was more prominent at a high level (200 mg L–1). For instance, at low concentration, nanocomposites increased Chl a and b and total Chl contents by 36, 12, and 21%, respectively, while at high concentration, it increased these pigments by 40, 16, and 30%, respectively, compared with stress only plants. Figure D shows that Cd stress elevated the level of carotenoids content (48%) in plants compared to control plants (without stress), whereas the foliar spray of CTS-Cu nanocomposites reduced the carotenoids content at both concentrations, i.e., 100 (10%) and 200 mg L–1 (31%).

Effects of Chitosan-Copper Nanocomposites on Gas Exchange Attributes, Water Use Efficiency, and Relative Water Content of under Cd Stress

In this study, results showed that Cd stress markedly changed the gas exchange attributes of plants including net photosynthesis rate (P n), transpiration rate (T r), stomatal conductance (g s), and intercellular CO2 (C i), compared to control plants (without stress). As depicted in Figure A–C, it reduced P n, T r, and g s by 22, 14, and 7% while increased the C i by 39%, compared to control (without stress) plants. Both concentrations of CTS-Cu nanocomposites (low, 100 mg L–1 and high, 200 mg L–1) improved the P n, T r, and g s in control (without stress) and stressed plants. But the high concentration had a more positive result relative to a low concentration of CTS-Cu nanocomposites. At 200 mg L–1, CTS-Cu nanocomposites improved these attributes by 48, 41, and 63% in control plants and by 64, 17, and 54% in Cd stressed plants, respectively, compared to their respective controls. In contrast, CTS-Cu nanocomposites reduced Ci in both the control and Cd stressed plants. For example, at 200 mg L–1, it reduced Ci by 10% in the control (without stress) and by 28% in Cd stressed plants, compared to their respective controls (Figure D).

Cd stress also impaired the water use efficiency (WUE; photosynthate produced per transpired molecule of water) of plants. As shown in Figure E, the WUE of was reduced by 35% at 5 mM Cd stress, compared to that without stress plants (control), whereas the foliar spray of CTS-Cu nanocomposites improved the WUE by 27 and 33% at low (100 mg L–1) and high (200 mg L–1) concentrations, respectively, when applied to Cd stressed plants, compared with stress only plants. It was also observed that Cd stressed plants had a low (29%) relative water content (RWC), equated to control (without stress) plants, as shown in Figure F. CTS-Cu nanocomposites improved the RWC of plants under Cd stress. It increased the RWC by 19 and 42% at 100 and 200 mg L–1, compared to that in stress only plants.

Effects of Chitosan-Copper Nanocomposites on Functioning of Photosynthetic Machinery of under Cd Stress

Figure A shows that Cd stress markedly reduced F V/F M (10%), F V/F O (33%), PI (52%), TRo/ABS (10%), and ETo/TRo (9%). This indicated that 5 mM Cd stress decreased the photosynthetic efficiency, energy transfer, and light absorption. The decrease in TRo/ABS and PI under Cd stress, compared with control (without stress) plants, reflects poorer trapping efficiency and photosynthetic performance. Under stress conditions, foliar application of CTS-Cu nanocomposites increased F V/F M, F V/F O, PI, ETo/RC, TRo/ABS, and ETo/TRo by 11, 48, 190, 35, 11, and 42% at low (100 mg L–1) and by 13, 63, 372, 35, 13, and 67% at high (200 mg L–1) concentrations, respectively, compared to stress only plants. This showed that CTS-Cu nanocomposites enhanced photosynthetic efficiency, energy transfer, light absorption, and overall photosynthetic performance of plants under stress conditions. Nanocomposites also resulted in efficient use of light energy in Cd stressed plants, as depicted from reduced DIo/CS by 36 and 50% at low and high concentrations, respectively, compared to stress only plants (Figure B).

Effects of Chitosan-Copper Nanocomposites on Stress Indicators (Relative Membrane Permeability, Malondialdehyde, and Hydrogen Peroxide) and Total Soluble Protein Content in under Cd Stress

Cd stress caused distortion of the cell membrane, as revealed from increased (31%) relative membrane permeability (RMP) of plants compared to control (without stress) plants. It also caused elevation in malondialdehyde (MDA) content of the stressed plants by 33% and accumulation of reactive oxygen species (H2O2), 70% higher than those in control plants. These indicators revealed severe oxidative damage in plants subjected to 5 mM Cd stress. However, CTS-Cu nanocomposite foliar spray significantly masked the effects of oxidative damage in due to Cd stress, compared with stress only plants, as it reduced RMP, MDA, and H2O2 content by 12, 14, and 28%, respectively, when applied at 100 mg L–1 and by 22, 17, and 32%, respectively, at 200 mg L–1, compared to only stressed plants (Figure A–C).

Total soluble protein (TSP) content in plants was significantly increased (87%) at 5 mM Cd stress, compared with control (without stress) plants. CTS-Cu nanocomposites also increased TSP content in both control (without stress) and stress plants. In control plants, it increased TSP content by 28 and 54%, respectively, at 100 and 200 mg L–1, while in Cd stressed plants, it increased TSP content by 15 and 37%, respectively, compared with their respective controls (Figure D).

Effects of Chitosan-Copper Nanocomposites on Antioxidants (Enzymatic and Nonenzymatic) in C. annuum under Cd Stress

Cd stressed plants showed increased activities of antioxidant enzymes including catalase (CAT; 59%), peroxidase (POD; 50%), ascorbate peroxidase (APX; 60%), and superoxide dismutase (SOD; 29%), compared with control (without stress) plants. Foliar spray of CTS-Cu nanocomposites also increased the activities of CAT and POD in both control and Cd stressed plants compared with their respective controls. But it reduced the activities of APX and SOD enzymes in Cd stressed plants compared to stress only plants. For instance, in Cd stressed plants, CTS-Cu nanocomposite spray at 200 mg L–1 increased CAT and POD activities by 29 and 30%, respectively, but it reduced the activities of APX and SOD by 26 and 14%, respectively, compared to stress only plants (Figure A–D).

Cd stress also impaired the accumulation of nonenzymatic antioxidants like total phenolics and prolines. These antioxidants were increased by 79 and 76%, respectively, under Cd stress compared to nonstressed plants (control). Foliar spray of CTS-Cu nanocomposites further increased the total phenolics and proline content of the stressed plants compared with stress only plants at both concentrations (low and high). However, high concentration (200 mg L–1) was more effective, and it increased total phenolics and proline content by 26 and 35%, respectively (Figure A,B).

Effects of Chitosan-Copper Nanocomposites on Nutritional Ions (K+ and Ca2+) and Cd Metal Uptake in the Root and Shoot of C. annuum under Cd Stress

Cd stress reduced the K+ and Ca2+ ions uptake in roots (60 and 64%, respectively) and their successive translocation in the shoot (41 and 44%, respectively) of C. annuum plants compared to control (without stress) plants. While CTS-Cu nanocomposite application in Cd stressed plants improved the uptake of K+ ions in the root by 101 and 150%, its accumulation in the shoot improved by 37 and 46%, respectively, at 100 and 200 mg L–1 concentration. Similarly, these treatments increased the Ca2+ uptake in the root by 96 and 155% while its translocation to the shoot by 33 and 70%, respectively, compared to the stress only plants (Figure A–D).

Cd stress plants had excessive amounts of Cd metal in the root (1320 μg g–1 DW) as well as shoot (745 μg g–1 DW), while the foliar spray of the CTS-Cu nanocomposite significantly reduced Cd uptake in root by 20 and 36% and its accumulation in the shoot by 20 and 41%, respectively, at low (100 mg L–1) and high (200 mg L–1) concentration, compared to stress only plants (Figure E,F).

Discussion

Cadmium (Cd) stress severely disrupts plant physiological functions, including photosynthesis, nutrient uptake, and antioxidant defense mechanisms, leading to growth inhibition and oxidative damage. Cd also caused oxidative stress that leads to DNA damage, protein denaturation, and eventual cellular apoptosis, thereby impairing normal plant growth and metabolism. For example, Cd stress has been shown to reduce photosynthetic pigments, plant biomass, and root and shoot lengths in . One of the primary causes of growth inhibition under Cd stress is the disturbance of the plant’s water balance due to Cd’s interference with soil water availability, leaving plants unable to access sufficient water. Moreover, Cd’s impact on water and nutrient uptake disrupts photosynthetic efficiency, leading to stunted growth and decreased productivity. ,

Cd stress significantly reduced chlorophyll (a and b) and carotenoid content, leading to chlorosis and a decline in photosynthetic activity. However, the application of CTS-Cu nanocomposites effectively preserved chlorophyll content by preventing its degradation, thereby enhancing photosynthetic efficiency. Furthermore, the ability of these nanocomposites to penetrate leaf structures via stomatal openings underscores their role in facilitating water transport into the plant’s vascular system. This mechanism highlights the critical interplay between stomatal conductance and plant hydration under Cd stress. Studies have reported that Cd stress reduces chlorophyll content, stunts plant growth, and disrupts nutrient homeostasis in crops such as rice, wheat, peppers, and tomato. The present study confirms these adverse effects in C. annuum, while demonstrating that foliar CTS-Cu nanocomposite application significantly alleviates growth inhibition. Compared to untreated Cd-stressed plants, CTS-Cu-treated plants exhibited higher biomass accumulation, improved chlorophyll content, and enhanced physiological functions, suggesting a protective role of these nanocomposites against Cd-induced damage. Additionally, Cd stress was associated with reduced stomatal conductance and transpiration rates, which are critical for maintaining optimal physiological processes in plants. The application of CTS-Cu nanocomposites mitigated these effects, likely by enhancing stomatal function and improving CO2 assimilation.

Our findings revealed that Cd stress resulted in an increase in total soluble protein content within plant tissues, a response commonly associated with the activation of stress-related defense mechanisms. Proteins play a crucial role in mediating plant adaptive responses to heavy metal stress, serving as key components in physiological and biochemical pathways that enhance resilience against stresses. Interestingly, treatment with CTS-Cu nanocomposites resulted in a significant improvement in soluble protein content compared with plants exposed solely to Cd. This suggests that these nanocomposites compensate for the need of excessive protein synthesis to mitigate Cd-induced damage and restore physiological balance.

Oxidative stress, characterized by an imbalance between ROS production and detoxification, is a hallmark of Cd toxicity. Key antioxidant enzymes superoxide dismutase (SOD), peroxidase (POD), and catalase (CAT) play pivotal roles in neutralizing ROS and protecting plant cells from oxidative damage. SOD acts as the first line of defense, catalyzing the dismutation of superoxide radicals into hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), which is subsequently detoxified by CAT and POD into water and oxygen. In response to Cd stress, the activities of these antioxidant enzymes were significantly elevated, indicating their critical role in mitigating oxidative damage. Furthermore, Cd stress induced the production of proline and phenolic compounds, which contribute to reducing oxidative damage by stabilizing cellular structures and scavenging free radicals.

The presence of Cd significantly elevated malondialdehyde (MDA) levels, a key marker of lipid peroxidation and oxidative stress, in the studied plants. The observed increase in the activities of SOD, POD, and CAT under Cd stress likely reflects an adaptive mechanism to counteract oxidative damage. Moreover, Cd stress may enhance the expression of genes encoding antioxidant enzymes, such as ascorbate peroxidase (APX), POD, and CAT, thereby bolstering the plant’s defense system against oxidative stress. Studies on have demonstrated that CuNPs mitigate Cd-induced stress by scavenging reactive oxygen species (ROS) and protecting cellular membranes from oxidative damage. In this study, foliar application of CTS-Cu nanocomposites further enhanced antioxidant enzyme activities, effectively scavenging ROS and mitigating oxidative damage. This highlights the potential of these nanocomposites as effective tools for combating oxidative stress in plants. Studies on copper-based nanomaterials have shown their ability to enhance plant tolerance to abiotic stress by promoting enzyme activity and reducing oxidative stress. Similarly, chitosan-based nanocomposites have been reported to modulate stress responses by activating antioxidant enzymes and enhancing nutrient uptake. The findings of the present study corroborate these reports, as CTS-Cu nanocomposite-treated plants exhibited increased activities of superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase (CAT), and peroxidase (POD), which are crucial for scavenging reactive oxygen species (ROS) generated under Cd stress.

Cd stress also had a profound effect on nutrient homeostasis in C. annuum. The concentrations of essential nutrients, such as potassium (K) and calcium (Ca), in the shoots and roots of pepper plants were significantly reduced under Cd stress, highlighting the detrimental effects of heavy metal toxicity on nutrient uptake and distribution. Furthermore, our results showed that stressed plants had elevated levels of Cd metal ions in their roots and shoot, which also augmented with nutrient uptake in plants under stress conditions. However, foliar application of CTS-Cu nanocomposites resulted in a substantial improvement in K and Ca concentrations in both shoot and roots, restoring nutrient balance and promoting plant health. Nanocomposites application also reduced Cd content in the root and shoot of plants most probably by reducing its uptake through the exclusion process or sequestering them into less harmful through chelation. Previous studies have proposed multiple mechanisms for Cd detoxification, including metal chelation, reduced Cd translocation, and enhanced antioxidant metabolism. Chitosan, as a biopolymer, is known to form complexes with metal ions, potentially reducing Cd bioavailability in plant tissues. This reduction in Cd bioavailability, combined with the ability of CTS to enhance auxin transport in root and shoot tissues, contributed to improved plant growth and development. Additionally, copper-based nanoparticles can enhance nutrient absorption and root activity, indirectly counteracting Cd-induced nutrient imbalances. The present study aligns with these findings, demonstrating that foliar CTS-Cu nanocomposite application mitigates Cd toxicity while improving mineral uptake efficiency, particularly of essential macronutrients like calcium and potassium. Our study demonstrates that CTS-Cu nanocomposites at both levels effectively mitigate Cd toxicity in C. annuum by enhancing photosynthetic attributes, bolstering antioxidant defense mechanisms, and reducing Cd uptake. However, the effect of the CTS-Cu nanocomposite was more prominent at a high level (200 mg L–1). These nanocomposites not only improve plant growth and physiological functions under heavy metal stress but also offer a sustainable and environmentally friendly solution for managing Cd toxicity in agricultural systems. As CTS-Cu nanocomposites have antimicrobial potential, their application might affect soil microbes. However, in this study, CTS-Cu nanocomposites were applied via foliar spray; its direct impact on soil must be negligible. This method also presents a practical and scalable solution for field applications. Foliar application ensures direct absorption by plant tissues, providing a more targeted and efficient delivery system compared with soil amendments. Moreover, this approach minimizes the risk of soil contamination and can be easily integrated into conventional agricultural spraying programs. Although this study was conducted under controlled conditions, the physiological benefits observed suggest that CTS-Cu nanocomposites hold promises for improving pepper production in open-field settings, particularly in HMs contaminated sites. Future field trials are needed to validate these findings under varying environmental conditions and assess long-term sustainability.

Conclusions

This study elucidated that the oxidative stress induced by Cd resulted in impaired photosynthetic efficiency, increased lipid peroxidation, and disruption of ionic balance, which collectively hindered C. annuum plant development. However, the application of chitosan-copper (CTS-Cu) nanocomposites emerged as a promising strategy to mitigate these adverse effects. The results indicated that CTS-Cu nanocomposite treatment significantly alleviated Cd toxicity through enhancing chlorophyll production, improving photosynthetic performance, and boosting antioxidant enzyme activities. Furthermore, the nanocomposite facilitated the maintenance of ionic homeostasis, reducing the uptake and accumulation of Cd in plant tissues. These findings not only underscore the potential of CTS-Cu nanocomposites in managing heavy metal stress in C. annuum but also suggest broader applications for improving resilience against abiotic stresses in various crop species. Future research could expand on these findings to explore the mechanisms underlying the protective effects of nanocomposites and their applicability in diverse agricultural practices.

Materials and Methods

Synthesis of Chitosan-Copper Nanocomposites

Acetic acid was used to prepare a 0.02 M chitosan (CTS) solution (100 mL). In a separate beaker, 0.5 M CuSO4 solution (100 mL) was prepared in deionized water. CTS was added to 0.5 M CuSO4 dropwise with continuous stirring at room temperature. The color of the solution changed from light blue to milky blue and then dark brown at the end that indicated the formation of the CTS-Cu nanocomposite. This resulted in a 1:1 (V/V) solution of CTS and CuSO4. This solution was continuously stirred for nearly 24 h at room temperature until the formation of precipitates. During this whole process, the pH of the solution was kept neutral and maintained by adding NaOH. The precipitates were collected and dried in an oven at 60 °C for 24 h. After drying, these were ground into fine powder and subjected to various characterization techniques, UV/vis spectrophotometry, scanning electron microscopy (SEM), energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX), and Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR).

Experimental Layout

This study was conducted at the Botanical Garden of the University of Education, Lahore, Pakistan. Seeds of C. annuum L. were obtained from the Punjab Seed Corporation, Lahore, Pakistan. A two-way completely randomized design experiment with three replications was conducted under natural environmental conditions. During this interval, the mean day/night temperature was recorded at 20/15 °C, and relative humidity was measured at 68%, with no precipitation and a day/night photoperiod of 11/13 h. Garden soil was collected, subjected to sieving, air-dried, and subsequently utilized to fill pots (5 kg/pot) having a 20 cm length and 15 cm diameter. The physicochemical characteristics of the soil utilized in the current investigation are detailed in Table . To ensure surface sterilization, the seeds were rinsed with a 5% sodium hypochlorite solution and subsequently washed twice with 95% ethanol. Healthy seeds of uniform sizes were sown in seedling trays. Following germination, uniformly sized seedlings at the four-leaf stage were transferred to pots. Four seedlings were maintained equidistant within each pot. After 1 week, once the seedlings had acclimatized their roots within the pots, the following treatments were administered: control, normal (untreated) plants; Cd stress, 5 mM CdCl2 solution (500 mL per pot); NC1, foliar application of the CTS-Cu nanocomposite at 100 mg L–1; and NC2, foliar application of the CTS-Cu nanocomposite at 200 mg L–1. These concentrations were selected based on prior research conducted by Gatasheh et al., Tabassum et al., and Pérez-Labrada et al. For Cd stress, the CdCl2 solution was prepared and utilized in conjunction with irrigation water. Meanwhile, CTS-Cu nanocomposite foliar spray was conducted in the morning at 10:00 am, using a manual hand sprayer until the whole plant got fully wet. These treatments were applied three times over the course of the entire experimental study at weekly intervals. Two weeks after the administration of these treatments, growth analysis and various physio–biochemical parameters were assessed as outlined below.

Biochemical and Physiological Attributes

Chlorophyll Content Determination

0.5 g of fresh leaf sample was taken and added in 10 mL of 80% acetone solution to make a paste using a pestle and mortar. This mixture was filtered, and its absorbance was recorded at 645, 663, and 480 nm. The Arnon method was used for determining chlorophyll a and b and total chlorophylls, while Lichtenthaler protocol was followed for carotenoids content.

Assessment of Gas Exchange Attributes

Infrared Gas Analyzer (IRGA) LCpro SD (ADC Bio Scientific Ltd. Hoddesdon, UK) was used to determine the gas exchange attributes. Qleaf (light source) was used from the leaf chamber, while the rest of the conditions were set to ambient. The leaf chamber had a pressure of 998 kPa and flow rate of 200.5 mL/min, with an average leaf temperature of 26 °C, and the light intensity was 914 mmol m–2 s–1. The third fully developed leaf from the apex was used for analyzing these attributes.

Determination of Relative Water Content (RWC)

Same-sized leaves from every replicate were taken and weighed (FW). These leaves were then dipped in distilled water for 3 h. The leaves became turgid and were weighed again (TW). Following that, the leaves were oven-dried at 65 °C to measure their dry weights (DW). Following the protocol of Jones and Turner, RWC was measured as

RWC(%)=[(FW−DW)/(TW−DW)]×100

Functioning of Photosynthetic Machinery and Chlorophyll Fluorescence

OS30p+ Chlorophyll Fluorometer (Opti-sciences, Inc. | Hudson, NH 03051, USA) was used for assessing the efficiency of photosystem II and chlorophyll fluorescence. The third fully matured leaf from the apex was darkly adapted for 20 min using clips. These leaves were then exposed to instant light of 3000 μmols m–2 s–1 to measure the efficiency of PSII and performance index (PI).

Determination of Relative Membrane Permeability (RMP)

Same-sized leaves were taken from all replicates and chopped into small pieces to dip in 20 mL of deionized water. These samples were shaken on a vortex for 5 s. After that, EC0 of these samples was noted. Following that, these samples were kept in the refrigerator at 4 °C overnight. On the next day, the EC1 was noted. After that, the same samples were autoclaved at 120 °C for 20 min and then the EC2 was noted.

RMP(%)=[(EC1−EC0)/(EC2−EC0)]×100

Quantification of Malondialdehyde

0.5 g of fresh leaf was blended with 5 mL of 5% trichloroacetic acid (TCA) solution at 10,000 rpm and 4 °C for 10 min. The supernatant was extracted for further use. For MDA, the solution contained 0.5 g of thiobarbituric acid (TBA) per 100 mL of distilled water. Then, equal volumes of the TBA solution and the plant extract were mixed. After incubating at 27 °C for 30 min, the mixture was placed in a hot water bath for another 30 min. After cooling in an ice water bath, absorbance values of the solution were monitored at 600 and 523 nm using a UV/vis spectrophotometer.

Quantification of Hydrogen Peroxide

Hydrogen peroxide was measured using 0.5 g of fresh plant tissue homogenized in 5 mL of 0.1% trichloroacetic acid solution. After homogenizing, the extract was mixed with phosphate buffer and a 2% potassium iodide solution to start the reaction. This mixture was incubated at 27 °C for 30 min, and its absorbance was read at 390 nm using a UV/vis spectrophotometer.

Quantification of Soluble Protein Concentration

Bradford’s method was used to measure the soluble proteins. 0.5 g of fresh leaf sample was ground in 10 mL of phosphate buffer. This mixture was centrifuged at 6000 rpm for 20 min at 4 °C. The supernatant was collected as an enzyme extract. To prepare Bradford reagent, distilled water (850 mL), phosphoric acid (100 mL), 95% ethanol (50 mL), and Coomassie brilliant blue (0.1 g) were mixed thoroughly. In 2 mL of this Bradford reagent, 0.1 mL of the enzyme extract was added and shaken on a vortex for few seconds. Its absorbance was read at 595 nm using a UV/vis spectrophotometer.

Catalase (CAT) and Peroxidase (POD) Activity Assay

0.1 mL of the enzyme extract was taken in a clean cuvette, followed by mixing with 1.9 mL of a 5.9 mM H2O2 solution. In the reaction mixture, 0.1 mL of 50 mM potassium phosphate buffer (pH 7) was added to maintain enzyme activity at the optimum level throughout the assay. After the solution was made, the cuvette was placed inside a UV/vis spectrophotometer at a 240 nm wavelength because this wavelength corresponds to the absorbance peak for H2O2. The rate of action at which catalase breaks down H2O2 was determined by measuring the rate of the decrease in absorbance at this wavelength. Absorbance readings were recorded at 30 s intervals for a total of 120 s, capturing time-dependent decreases in the concentration of H2O2. The absorbance of all collected data was then plotted against time by which the slope in the resulting curve reflected the activity level of enzyme.

For POD, a reaction mixture was prepared containing 0.1 mL of an enzyme extract, 600 μL of 20 mM guaiacol, 700 μL of 50 mM potassium phosphate buffer solution at pH 5.0, and 600 μL of 40 mM H2O2. All the ingredients were mixed intensively. The solution was then dispensed into a UV/vis spectrophotometer cuvette. The absorbance was measured at 470 nm every 30 s within the entire period of 150 s. The experiment was conducted in collaboration with king Saud University.

Ascorbate Peroxidase (APX) and Superoxide Dismutase (SOD) Assay

A reaction mixture was prepared containing ascorbic acid (7.5 mM), H2O2 (300 mM), potassium phosphate buffer (50 mM), and enzyme extract (0.1 mL). After thorough mixing, the reaction was started, and the absorbance was read at 290 nm in a UV/vis spectrophotometer to assess APX activity. The readings were taken every 30 s with a total interval of 60 s.

For the assessment of SOD activity, the reaction mixture comprised 0.3 mL of a 130 mM methionine solution, 0.3 mL of a 50 μM nitro blue tetrazolium solution, 0.3 mL of a 100 μM EDTA-Na2 solution, and 0.3 mL of a 20 μM riboflavin solution. Subsequent to the introduction of 0.05 mL of the enzyme extract into the reaction mixture, the composite was subjected to illumination at an intensity of 4000 lx for a duration of 20 min. The superoxide anions generated upon the illumination of riboflavin engage in a reaction with nitro blue tetrazolium in the presence of methionine, leading to the formation of formazan, a blue-hued complex. The SOD present within the sample solution interacts with these superoxide anions, resulting in a decreased level of formation of formazan products. Consequently, the alteration in color or optical density was measured at 560 nm to quantify the presence of SOD enzymes utilizing a UV/vis spectrophotometer.

Determination of Total Phenolics and Leaf Proline

The phenolic contents were measured as per the method of Lamuela-Raventós. Gallic acid was used as a standard for spectrophotometric determination of the phenolic content. Proline content was analyzed based on the method described by Bates et al. Leaf samples were homogenized in sulfosalicylic acid. The same volume of glacial acetic acid and ninhydrin solution was added to the mixture, which was later heated at 100 °C. After some time, 5 mL of toluene was added. The absorbance of the aspirated layer formed at the top was measured with a UV/vis spectrophotometer at 528 nm.

Determination of Inorganic Ions (K+ and Ca2+) and Cd Metal Content

For determination of inorganic ions such as potassium (K+), calcium (Ca2+), and cadmium (Cd), 0.1 g of dried root and shoot powder was added to test tubes containing 2 mL of sulfuric acid and left overnight. H2O2 was added dropwise to this mixture with continuous heating in the digestion flask until the solution became colorless. This colorless filtrate was diluted to a final volume of 50 mL with distilled water. The concentrations of K+ and Ca2+ were measured using a flame photometer (Sherwood Model 360). The atomic absorption spectrophotometer was utilized for the analysis of the Cd content. Calibration standard solutions of K+, Ca2+, and Cd were meticulously prepared and employed to construct standard curves for the quantitative analysis of these minerals.

Statistical Analysis

In this study, two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was performed on R software (Version 2024.12.0 + 467, Posit Software, PBC). The data presented in the bar graphs are the mean of three replicates ± standard error. These graphs were also made with R software. Bars sharing similar letters did not differ at the significance level (p ≤ 0.05). These letters were obtained after Duncan’s multiple range test using R software. Pearson’s correlation and principal component analysis were performed on R software at a significance level of p ≤ 0.05.
