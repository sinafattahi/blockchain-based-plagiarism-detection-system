ABSTRACT

Introduction

Clinical reasoning skills are essential to medical practice. Team‐based learning (TBL) using key‐feature questions provides students an opportunity to explicitly practice clinical reasoning skills with peers. Understanding student experiences with this learning strategy may provide insights into optimizing learning experiences that foster clinical reasoning in preparation for the care of future patients.

Methods

A descriptive qualitative design explored third‐year medical students' experiences of a clinical reasoning‐focused virtual TBL session. Six focus groups involving 26 third‐year medical students were conducted; audio recordings were transcribed verbatim. Themes were generated from analysed codes, drawing connections between common thoughts, processes and conditions experienced by participants. Data were verified, upholding principles of qualitative rigour to assure methodological credibility, transferability, confirmability and reflexivity.

Results

Data analysis revealed five themes: (a) self‐confidence, including enabling and deterring factors and peer calibration; (b) co‐learning with peers, with students identifying knowledge gaps and gaining experience establishing consensus; (c) trust, as related to sense and position of authority; (d) clinical reasoning strategies articulated by students and integrating peer feedback; and (e) clinical application, as a parallel to real‐life patients involving choosing wisely and commitment to decisions.

Conclusions

Students' perspectives on the clinical reasoning process were greatly impacted by clinical experience, including lack thereof. Their peer‐to‐peer experience highlights the power of social learning and trusting relationships on students' professional identity formation. How students translate learning from the classroom to authentic clinical encounters requires further study.

Keywords: clinical reasoning, paediatric clerkship, qualitative, student experience, team‐based learning, undergraduate medical education

1. Introduction

Clinical reasoning is critical to the practice of medicine [1], involving both thinking through problems and decision‐making. It can be broadly defined as the process that clinicians use to arrive at an initial or working diagnosis, through synthesis and interpretation of information gathered from history, physical examination and initial investigations [2], although a universal definition is not established [3, 4, 5]. In practice, clinical reasoning is nuanced [6, 7], an iterative process, with component refinement and hypothesis stratification that occur with acquisition of new information [3], and takes into account factors including context and previous experience or expertise [4]. The learning of clinical reasoning is often assumed to take place during clinical rotations, by caring for patients and observing mentors [8]. Given its complexity, teaching and assessment of clinical reasoning and associated decision‐making have been challenging areas to research.

The learning of clinical reasoning is often assumed to take place during clinical rotations, by caring for patients and observing mentors.

Key‐feature questions (KFQs) are one strategy for assessing clinical reasoning; these questions focus on key features, or significant steps in problem resolution, and may involve a difficult aspect of the problem or steps in which students are most likely to make errors [9, 10]. KFQs were included in the Medical Council of Canada's Qualifying Examination Part 1 from 1992 to 2024. Previous literature demonstrates KFQs to be reliable and valid in measuring the construct of clinical reasoning [11]. Considering the foundational importance of supporting learner growth in the development of clinical reasoning, a formative team‐based learning (TBL) session focusing on these skills has been incorporated within the paediatric clerkship at our institution since 2017; its development and outcomes have been previously described by our team [12].

TBL is an active learning strategy that is learner centred and instructor directed, shown to improve both factual and conceptual knowledge, both of which are required as students develop clinical reasoning [13]. Clinical case discussions are also an effective means of fostering clinical reasoning skills in undergraduate medical students [14]. Our previous observational study demonstrated TBL using KFQs correlated with other summative clerkship assessments and improved clinical reasoning performance after group learning [12]. Whereby think‐aloud activities are established strategies to study human thought processes [15], understanding the student experience of group discussion in TBL and the interplay with their individual clinical reasoning processes remains a gap.

Better understanding the experience of novice students co‐navigating formative clinical reasoning activities may help educators develop deliberate educational strategies. TBL group discussions among students, where students articulate their clinical reasoning and decision‐making processes to their peers to reach group consensus, provide a unique opportunity to explore their thought processes. This study builds on the findings of our previous validity evidence [12], shifting focus to the examination of group discussions from the lens of the student. Our research question was as follows: What do team‐based learning discussions between paediatric clerkship students reveal about their clinical decision‐making thought processes? The primary objective was to explore paediatric clerkship students' experiences of a KFQ‐based TBL session and its perceived impact on their development of clinical reasoning. The secondary objective was to explore what factors students describe as influencing their clinical reasoning.

Better understanding the experience of novice students co‐navigating formative clinical reasoning activities may help educators develop deliberate educational strategies.

2. Materials and Methods

2.1. Context

The MD program at our Canadian institution is structured with 2 years of preclinical learning, followed by 2 years of clerkship experiences. During clerkships, students encounter patients in various clinical settings, where learning and application of clinical reasoning are presumed to take place. The paediatric clerkship is an 8‐week mandatory clinical rotation during the third year of the MD program, among other core rotations (family medicine, internal medicine, psychiatry, surgery and obstetrics/gynaecology). During the paediatric clerkship, students' clinical experiences include inpatients, outpatients, emergency department shifts and newborn care. One day per week is dedicated to teaching sessions supplementing students' clinical learning; these involve classroom‐based clinically focused teaching and small group sessions for simulation, procedural skills, reflective practice and evidence‐based medicine. When this study was conducted, all classroom sessions, including TBL sessions, were held online because of the COVID‐19 pandemic. Procedural skills, simulation sessions and direct patient care clinical experiences remained in person.

Our modified TBL session that employs KFQs to focus on clinical reasoning aspects of cases is included within this weekly curriculum, as previously described [12]. The TBL session occurs once per rotation and involves six clinical scenarios, each with two to four associated KFQs to focus on clinical decision‐making (see Appendix 1 for scenario blueprint). Students first work through the cases independently during an Individual Readiness Assurance Test (IRAT), committing to their decision‐making and responses. This is followed by completion of a Group Readiness Assurance Test (GRAT) using the same six scenarios in small groups of four to five students, requiring group consensus. Finally, a facilitated discussion (KLF and JLF) takes place, articulating the key features of each item and discussing the rationale and reasoning for correct responses, along with discussion points regarding alternate or incorrect responses. This provides real‐time feedback to students and provides an opportunity to ask questions and seek clarification. Facilitators also highlight relevant content‐specific information for students' learning. The structure, timing and flow of our 2‐h session are depicted in Figure 1.

2.2. Study Design

A descriptive qualitative design was used to explore processes during TBL discussions among paediatric clerkship students. Given the exploratory nature of this research, our goal was to understand the core social processes and decision‐making related to the phenomena being studied [15]. Descriptive accounts of the participants and their lived experiences allowed for an in‐depth exploration of the participants' perspectives related to clinical reasoning.

A descriptive qualitative design was used to explore processes during TBL discussions among paediatric clerkship students to understand the core social processes and decision‐making.

2.3. Participants and Data Collection

A total of six focus groups were conducted using theoretical sampling. Twenty‐six students were recruited in their third‐year clerkship at a large research‐intensive university completing their paediatric rotation in the 2021–2022 academic year, beginning in September 2021. Depending on their third‐year rotation sequence, students' prior clinical experiences varied at the time of participation in this study. The TBL session is a mandatory teaching session during the paediatric clerkship; all students were required to participate in the session, but participation in this research study was voluntary. Before the session, students were informed of and invited to participate in focus groups; these took place 1–2 weeks after the TBL session. Focus groups were conducted over Zoom, involved four to five participants each and lasted approximately 60 min. The research team did not have access to TBL group lists; therefore, overlap between focus group participants and TBL group members was possible. Focus groups were led by research team members (N.M. and M.R.) who intentionally were not involved with session teaching or facilitation to encourage honesty [16]. The focus group guide (Appendix 2) was developed based on concepts related to clinical reasoning and peer teaching, as described in the literature. Focus groups were audio recorded and transcribed. This study was approved by the University's Research Ethics Board (Pro00111295).

2.4. Analysis

The transcripts of the focus groups were completed using automated transcription services and verified for accuracy (N.M.), then analysed by the four members of the research team (three clinicians and one qualitative expert). The analysis utilized an iterative process where data collection and analysis occurred simultaneously [17]. Through coding, we identified key conceptual areas and themes. Initially, open coding was used to break down the data into discrete segments to identify, describe and categorize the common themes throughout the transcriptions. These codes were then analysed to draw connections between the common thoughts, processes and conditions that were experienced by the medical students. Results were analysed and re‐analysed until no new codes were identified and saturation was achieved [17].

2.5. Trustworthiness

Our team consists of two clinician educators, a senior paediatric resident physician and a qualitative researcher. We acknowledge our lived experiences, particularly as related to our interpretation and analysis of this work. To ensure trustworthiness, we verified interpretations using the following strategies: (a) regular meetings to discuss emergent findings; (b) recording (‘memo’‐ing) our reactions to the data, noting ideas related to findings; (c) upholding principles of qualitative rigour to assure methodological credibility, transferability and confirmability; and (d) throughout this study, the authors engaged in reflexivity to critically examine their own assumptions and ensure that their interpretations remain as objective as possible, striving to authentically represent the voices and experiences of the participants. This was critical in minimizing researcher predisposed biases and ensuring that the findings were authentically represented [18].

3. Results

From our analysis, five themes emerged from the data as related to our study objectives: self‐confidence, co‐learning with peers, trust, clinical reasoning strategies and clinical application. Each theme consisted of two to four related subthemes. These are outlined in Table 1 along with exemplar quotes sampled from different participants and focus groups, capturing the essence of each theme.

Students describe self‐confidence as central to their experiences in the TBL session. They described both enablers to self‐confidence, such as making a commitment and external validation, and deterrents to self‐confidence, including second guessing, self‐doubt, feeling exposed and discomfort with uncertainty.

I think you can feel a bit exposed when you are asked to share your clinical opinions on cases that can feel kind of real, because you are being asked to make real decisions. And so, when you are in this group setting, being asked to share your thoughts … … it can just make you question your abilities as a medical student, and whether or not you are actually going to be able to make it as a physician. It's also comforting, knowing that, like, there's students around you who are kind of feeling the same, but at the same time, you still have to share your thoughts, and you still have to … justify why you feel a certain way. And that can be very intimidating (Focus Group 1, Participant 1).

Calibration with peers had the impact of being both an enabler and a deterrent to self‐confidence. Calibration refers to students seeing how confident they feel compared to their peers, the knowledge and experience they may have with respect to their peers and reflecting on how far off they feel from normative performance. Participants expressed how the IRAT requires commitment to an answer, requiring them to trust their own knowledge and clinical reasoning. Learning how their colleagues answered questions during GRAT discussions enabled participants to calibrate with their peers, which could either positively or negatively impact their self‐confidence. Students spoke of how this provided external validation that they were correct and led to an increased self‐confidence. However, when there were discrepant answers among the group members, this could create a breakdown in self‐confidence, causing self‐doubt and second guessing of their knowledge.

I would say that I did not really have the confidence until the review at the end. And then I was like, okay, that's what I thought individually. And then this is what we thought as a group, and then this is what the answer is actually supposed to be. And I was like, oh, I should have been more confident or not so confident, depending on different questions. (Focus Group 1, Participant 4).

Unique aspects of co‐learning with peers and the impact on their own personal learning and growth were described by our student participants. Co‐learning reflects the learning that takes place by working through cases alongside their peers and encompasses the social dynamics of the group learning process. In consideration of their limited clinical experiences, TBL provided students the opportunity to identify knowledge gaps and enabled them to learn from others.

I find these group discussions a great way to kind of check where everyone's at. And if there is a deficiency on my part, I find it's very motivational to study more or read more upon certain subjects. So, in that kind of [way], I can gauge where everyone else is at. I think that's one aspect I really like. (Focus Group 3, Participant 2).

These benefits were described as positive aspects of the session, as was gaining experience in establishing consensus, a skill deemed important for future clinical practice. The experience of navigating cases with peers was unique as a motivating driver for reflection, calibration and learning from the strengths of other group members.

So, it's more about learning how to, you know, work with one another and come to a conclusion like a consensus rather than figuring out who's right and who's wrong. (Focus Group 4, Participant 1).

The importance of trust in others was described by students, where both a sense of authority and someone's position of authority would increase students' trust in the knowledge, experiences and opinions of others. Participants spoke of the hierarchy in medicine, noting that hearing something from a staff or resident physician increased their trust of that clinical knowledge, as compared to a peer.

If they are a staff and they said they think it's this, well, then I am probably going to go with what they think or if it's a resident, and they have obviously been seeing a lot more of these cases than I have (Focus Group 5, Participant 2).

The sense of authority that a peer embodied when sharing their answer or reasoning, such as another student giving an opinion on the correct response, also impacted the trust. When student's felt that their peer answering the question was confident and knew what they were speaking about, they were more likely to be influenced by that person's opinion.

Tone of voice definitely helps someone who comes in guns blazing and is like, this is this, we need to order this, this and this, and this, then obviously, people are gonna be like, oh, that sounds that sounds really right. (Focus Group 1, Participant 3).

Students described their clinical reasoning strategies as they worked through the cases both alone and within the group. They were able to describe established strategies involved in clinical reasoning, even if not labelled as such; these included articulating a problem representation as they worked through the cases, highlighting the pertinent positives and negatives.

On a practical level, my approach to the cases was like highlighting the pertinent positives and negatives within the stem And then I found there was like a few key pertinent positives that really led me in one direction versus another. (Focus Group 6, Participant 1).

They describe using illness scripts, recalling key clinical information from their knowledge base that fit with the signs, symptoms and investigations provided in the cases.

When I'm just skimming through a prompt, I look for things like age, sex, and then the buzzword presentations and try to come up with differentials in the mind and kind of center how I gather my information based on that differential that's forming in my head. (Focus Group 3, Participant 2).

The TBL experience also enabled participants to integrate feedback from peers, learning how others approached a case and which strategies their peers used. This allowed them to increase their own knowledge base and add to their scripts both in consideration of alternate diagnoses and in identification of discriminating features among diagnoses.

It's nice to bounce off ideas, and then also ask for clarification as to why did you think this way rather than this way? And it's nice, just to get that immediate feedback right away (Focus Group 4, Participant 1).

Lastly, how students related their experiences from the TBL session to ‘real life’ medicine was reflected in their comments related to clinical application. Being able to relate their decisions and commitment to a limited number of responses reflected choosing wisely, where students had to prioritize and justify decisions, recognizing that resources in authentic clinical settings are not unlimited.

I think it's harder to pick investigations. I think it's influenced more by things like choosing wisely. Like, because in real life you like, Is this too invasive for the patient? Those kinds of things where it's like a diagnosis, I feel like I either know it or I do not most of the time. But especially when you are thinking of it as if this was my real patient, then it gets kind of more confusing about how far to go with investigations. (Focus Group 3, Participant 1).

Further, students discussed the use of cases as a parallel to real life where they needed to make a commitment to decisions. This was a source of discomfort for some students, although they recognized the importance and relevance to patient care.

This kind of testing format made you, forced you to choose and pick specific things as opposed to being like, oh, I would consider this. But like, in this situation, you had to make a decision and commit to very specific diagnoses, and not just like, the broad strokes, so it was uncomfortable, but I think relevant. (Focus Group 1, Participant 2).

4. Discussion

Over one academic year at our institution, paediatric clerkship students shared what happens during a dedicated clinical decision‐making TBL experience that helps, hones or hinders their clinical reasoning skills.

The experience of navigating the cases and KFQs with peers was a unique driver for reflection and calibration for our focus group participants. Our TBL session involved clinical cases developed to create authentic examples of core paediatric clinical presentations; these served as the foundation for students' rich discussions, which revealed their clinical reasoning processes. Whether equipped with the semantic qualifiers and frameworks or not, clerkship students articulated elements of the clinical reasoning diagnostic process, specifically when it comes to analytical thinking processes [2]. Students described their thought processes and approach to the cases, with hypothesis generation as they identified pertinent key information to search and select for the most likely diagnosis or script selection. Interestingly, heuristics, or rapid non‐analytic processing [ 2], were not described by our participants, which may be due to being relatively novice. Students with more perceived knowledge, experience or confidence were, however, noted to instill more sway in group decision‐making. Further, students who provided unique contextual experiences, having seen a real patient with a similar presentation, were seen by peers as having more expertise and therefore were perceived as more trustworthy, particularly if they could describe what a staff or resident taught them from the authentic patient encounter. Working with peers who had experience may have facilitated clinical sensemaking within the group [19].

Students with more perceived knowledge, experience or confidence were noted to instill more sway in group decision‐making.

Our participants identified personal benefits from the opportunity to learn from and with peers. Knopes et al. [20] suggest that peer‐to‐peer learning is critical for professional socialization, development of teamwork skills and acquiring skills in continuing professional development by learning how to address gaps in knowledge. Peer‐assisted learning, with specific student tutors, has been explored qualitatively, delving deeper into cognitive and social congruence [21]. In our study, the focus on peer‐to‐peer teaching related to clinical reasoning is a unique aspect that has, to our knowledge, not previously been described.

Given the limited literature on peer‐to‐peer learning among students at the same level of training, we look to near‐peer literature to provide further insights to our unique findings. Near‐peer teaching, defined as a learner one or more years senior teaching another learner within the same program [22, 23], has been implemented in various medical education contexts [24]. The benefits of near‐peer teaching include knowledge acquisition, participation and engagement, positive influence on professional identity formation [25] and improved team performance [20, 24]. It has been shown to be as effective as teaching provided by experienced faculty, although a potential disadvantage is the relative lack of expertise of the near‐peer teachers [22]. While recognizing that near‐peer and peer‐to‐peer learning have inherent differences, our TBL experience among peers at the same level harnesses the potential benefits of near‐peer teaching and social learning theory [26]. The added safety net of experienced facilitators ensures that the intended learning points are emphasized during the large group debrief and allows for inter‐ and intra‐group learning, particularly should the peer‐to‐peer discussions miss key concepts.

Clinical reasoning and development of expertise, as components of a physician's professional skill set, contribute to professional identity. Medical educators grapple with the construct of professional identity formation, as rife with complexity as clinical reasoning in both definition and development. A recent scoping review on professional identity formation [27] highlights the ‘inherent messiness’ when medical students, navigating personal and interpersonal tensions, are placed in dynamic systems. Our participants described the process of establishing trust in others with different experiences or perspectives, reflecting on conflicting responses and deciding on whether to concede individual perspectives or amalgamate as a group. Students discussed not only this confidence in their peers but also confidence in self as they reflected on their clinical reasoning processes. Gottlieb and colleagues [28] proposed a conceptual review of confidence, noting both the importance of self‐confidence and external relational, contextual and systemic modifiers. Reacting to different opinions allowed our participants to self‐reflect on where they were personally in the reasoning process for each clinical scenario and, more broadly, on their perceived gap to peers.

Students discussed not only … confidence in their peers but also confidence in self as they reflected on their clinical reasoning processes.

Limitations of our study include the virtual format of the learning experience, which may lend itself to a different experience; our sessions have returned to in‐person. Recent single‐centre mixed‐methods exploration of face‐to‐face small group medical student learning when compared to online noted perceived loss of social connectivity and diminished collaborative approaches virtually [29]. Voluntary, non‐probability sampling from the clerkship students may also impact responses; non‐participants may have very different perspectives and impart sampling bias. Our participants were not linked to their performance on the KFQs, and thus neither their experience nor any assessment data, whether high performing or low performing, informs these results. Focus groups may also establish groupthink, and disparate experiences may not be shared if concerned about group nonconformity or if students were concerned about sharing a negative experience about a peer who was in their TBL group. Finally, and perhaps most importantly, self‐identified descriptions of clinical reasoning processes in the small‐group setting may not necessarily reflect effective clinical reasoning with real patients.

How students translate learning from the classroom to authentic clinical encounters and how best to assess clinical reasoning skills with patients require further exploration and are future directions for study. Specifically, evaluating differences between higher and lower performing groups may help educators develop and refine teaching strategies and interventions to enhance the development of clinical reasoning among different learners.

5. Conclusion

Although not labelled as such, established clinical reasoning strategies were described by student participants when discussing their experience of a TBL session that employed KFQs. Student perspectives on the clinical reasoning process were greatly impacted by clinical experience, including lack thereof. Their peer‐to‐peer experience highlights the power of social learning and trusting relationships on students' professional identity formation. We recommend that medical educators incorporate formative active learning that focuses on clinical reasoning to allow students opportunities to deliberately practice clinical reasoning as they work through a hypothetico‐deductive approach. Expanding our TBL to other patient populations and specialties may help serve this purpose.

Appendix 2. Semi‐structured focus group questions/guide

  • What are your initial impressions of the CDM‐type questions?

  • What makes them different from other exam formats that you are familiar with? Are there aspects that are easier than other formats? Are there aspects that are more difficult? Describe.
