Abstract

Alexandrium pacificum, a globally distributed dinoflagellate, is well-known for causing harmful algal blooms and producing Paralytic Shellfish Toxins (PSTs), a threat to marine life and human health. The frequency and intensity of Alexandrium blooms have increased in recent decades, driven, in some cases, by increasing temperatures. Here, we investigated the temperature-dependent (15Â Â°C, 20Â Â°C, 25Â Â°C, and 30Â Â°C) growth rates and paralytic shellfish toxin profiles of eight A. pacificum strains while concurrently examining differences in sequences of the saxitoxin biosynthetic gene, sxtA4. These findings highlight the role of temperature, genetic diversity, and sxtA4 conserved regions in influencing toxin production and profiles in Alexandrium. Further research into the genetic mechanisms underlying saxitoxin biosynthesis will improve our understanding of Alexandriumâ€™s adaptability to changing temperatures. Such insights are essential for effective ecosystem management and safeguarding public health.

Introduction

Harmful algal blooms (HABs) occur when algae grow to high levels, often producing toxins that may harm humans and/or aquatic ecosystems. These events are a global phenomenon, impacting coastal and freshwater systems worldwide [3, 6, 7, 30, 36, 41, 59]. Among the species responsible for marine HABs, some species of Alexandrium, a genus of dinoflagellate, have attracted significant attention due to their ability to produce saxitoxins that can cause paralytic shellfish poisoning (PSP) [37, 64, 84]. The frequency and intensity of Alexandrium blooms have been rising worldwide, leading to an increase in PSP events and associated disruptions to marine ecosystems and fisheries, with significant economic impacts (Supplementary Figure 1,[9, 36, 63, 66]). In some cases, the geographic range of Alexandrium blooms are expanding due to rising temperatures, especially within higher latitude regions [7, 25, 31, 55]. However, as environmental conditions continue to shift due to climate change, predicting the long-term dynamics of Alexandrium blooms remains challenging because of the ecological complexity associated with these blooms [12, 60].

One of the key factors fostering the ecological complexity of Alexandrium blooms in marine environments may stem from its genetic diversity. Genetic diversity within a population can influence physiological traits, including growth, toxin production, and bloom dynamics. This diversity often allows a species to adapt to environmental conditions, such as changes in temperature, salinity, light, nutrient, and grazing pressure [4, 13, 21, 22, 76, 89]. Among these traits, toxin production is particularly relevant for bloom dynamics and ecological interactions. The biosynthesis of paralytic shellfish toxins is a complex, multistep process involving a suite of enzymes encoded within the sxt gene cluster [51, 61]. This modular biosynthetic pathway is initiated by the enzyme sxtA, which catalyzes the first committed step by converting arginine into a key intermediate. The sxtA gene comprises four functional domains, among which domain 4 (sxtA4) encodes an aminotransferase essential for initiating toxin synthesis [67, 70]. As the enzyme responsible for the initial transamination step, sxtA4 functions as a molecular gatekeeper of the entire PST pathwayâ€”without its activity, downstream toxin biosynthesis cannot proceed [19]. Subsequent steps involve core biosynthetic enzymes such as sxtG, sxtB, and sxtD, which construct the tricyclic saxitoxin backbone, followed by a series of tailoring enzymes including sxtSUL, sxtX, sxtH/T, and sxtU that introduce various chemical modifications to produce a structurally diverse array of PST analogs [33, 67, 68]. Notably, sxtA4 is generally detected only in toxigenic Alexandrium species and is absent or undetectable in non-toxic strains, underscoring its critical role in toxin biosynthesis [19, 67, 68, 87]. Due to its functional centrality, variation in sxtA4 gene sequences and expression levels has been proposed as a key factor explaining intraspecific differences in toxin production [19, 29, 88]. Its restricted presence in toxigenic lineages and functional indispensability also make sxtA4 a valuable molecular marker for predicting the toxin-producing potential of Alexandrium populations in natural environments [29, 68, 81].

Toxic Alexandrium species show considerable inter- and intraspecific variability in toxin production, reflected in both total toxin amount and the toxin composition [13, 34, 80, 91]. One potential source of this variability is the high genetic diversity observed within toxin biosynthesis genes, such as sxtA4. Interestingly, high genetic diversity of the saxitoxin biosynthetic initiating gene (sxtA4) was found within a single strain of A. pacificum, ACCC01 [94]. Also, the genetic diversity of this gene was observed in 11 A. minutum strains, with a unique allelic form in two non-toxic strains and varying copies (1-46) of genetically diverse sxtA4 mRNA sequences in the toxic strains [29]. This suggests that the high genetic diversity of toxin-producing genes may contribute to the ecological adaptation of Alexandrium populations to fluctuating and stressful environments. Despite these insights, the relationship between sxtA4 genetic variation and actual differences in toxin profiles remains poorly understood. Limited studies have explored the association between the genetic diversity of the sxtA4 gene in Alexandrium and how it is related to intraspecific variation in toxin production (e.g., toxin composition and/or toxin profile) among Alexandrium species. Therefore, further research is necessary to elucidate whether sxtA4 diversity alone can account for the observed physiological variation or whether interactions with downstream genes also play a significant role.

Among the toxic Alexandrium species, A. pacificum is a dinoflagellate species known for its rapid growth, causing dense blooms in estuarine and coastal waters worldwide (Supplementary Figure 2), with significant ecological and economic impacts [2, 44, 77]. Extensive ecophysiological studies have investigated toxin production and bloom formation mechanisms in A. pacificum species [1, 15, 39, 46, 57, 58, 75, 83],Van [92, 93, 95]. Despite these efforts, the underlying mechanisms of how environmental factors drive intraspecific variation in toxin production remain largely unclear, as most studiesâ€”typically based on fewer than three strainsâ€”offer only a limited view of strain-level variability [1, 57, 93]. In fact, nearly all studies cited above used a small number of strains (often one or two), which may constrain our understanding of the full extent of physiological and toxin production diversity within Alexandrium species.

To better understand the association between the genetic diversity of the sxtA4 gene and intraspecific variation in toxin production, it is essential to deepen our understanding of the speciesâ€™ genetic and ecological variability by examining a larger number of strains. Therefore, in this study, we isolated eightÂ A. pacificumÂ strains from the Korean coastal waters to investigate their physiological characteristics (Supplementary Figure 3â€“4), particularly in relation to growth responses and toxin production under varying temperatures. In addition, by cloning and sequencing the sxtA4 gene in each strain, we aim to investigate whether genetic variation in this domain may be associated with differences in toxin production. This research contributes to a broader understanding of how genetic diversity within the species may drive ecological interactions, ultimately informing future strategies for HAB management in a changing ocean environment.

Genetic Analysis of Saxitoxin Biosynthetic Gene (sxtA4)

The sxtA4 Gene Diversity

Analysis of molecular variance (AMOVA) was performed to describe the sxtA4 gene sequence variation among/within strains in Arlequin v3.5.2.2 [27]. Details regarding PCR amplification and cloning, and the phylogenetic analysis of the sxtA4 gene are provided in supplementary information. The AMOVA is based on an analysis of the variance of gene frequencies, considering the number of mutational differences between sequences. A function of the number of polymorphic sites and frequency of transition/transversion and insertion/deletion, was also calculated using the program Arlequin. Minimum spanning network using pairwise differences was constructed to determine absolute distances between sequences. Using the de novo assembly tool in the Geneious software (Version 11.1.2), 160 sequences were classified based on SNP positions. Due to the nature of de novo assembly, which combines the most similar sequences based on sequence similarity, this method generates contigs that reflect the observed variation patterns.

Sequence Similarity Network

The cloning sequences were translated into amino acid sequences using Geneious software. Open Reading Frames (ORFs) were predicted by the ORF Finder tool on the NCBI website, with a cut-off of 90 nucleotides. Search was conducted in all three reading frames (1, 2, and 3) to identify genes associated with increased diversity resulting from frame shifts. Protein function annotations were then identified using InterProScan by querying its available databases [48]. Similarity of nucleotide sequences and translated amino acid sequences were analyzed using the Biostrings and dplyr packages in the R software (Version 4.2.3) to determine the extent of variation at each position due to the SNPs (MÃ¼ller, 2020; R Core Team, 2023). A total of 166 sxtA4 gene nucleotide sequences and 54 protein sequences from dinoflagellates Alexandrium, Gymnodinium, and Pyrodinium were retrieved from the NCBI database, and their sequence similarity was analyzed. The results were subsequently visualized with ggplot2. Sequence similarity analysis was conducted by distinguishing between sequences with the identified domain associated with the function of sxtA4 and those without it.

Protein Structure Prediction

To investigate the potential functional consequences of the observed genetic diversity in the sxtA4 gene, we predicted and compared the 3D protein structures encoded by eight Alexandrium pacificum strains. Because sequence variations in the sxtA4 gene may lead to structural and functional differences in the encoded protein, we focused on predicting the structural consequences of such genetic variation. The sxtA4 gene encodes an aminotransferase domain that initiates the biosynthesis of saxitoxin; therefore, even minor sequence variations could result in structural and potentially functional divergence among strains.

Protein structures were predicted using AlphaFold2 [49, 65] based on translated amino acid sequences (Supplementary videoÂ 1). The reliability of each residue was assessed using the Predicted Local Distance Difference Test (pLDDT) score, where values above 90 indicate high confidence, scores between 70 and 90 reflect moderate confidence, and values below 70 represent low confidence. The predicted TM-score (pTM) was used to evaluate structural similarity, with a pTM value greater than 0.8 suggesting a very high likelihood that the predicted structure closely resembles experimentally determined structures.

To further assess structural variation across strains, we used the Dali web server to compare protein structures and obtain similarity Z-scores [42]. Generally, a Z-score above 20 indicates that the two structures are definitively homologous, while a score between 8 and 20 suggests they are likely homologous. In contrast, TM scores obtained from the Universal Structural Alignment (US-align) program [98] assess the structural homology between the query protein and the reference arginine aminotransferase (PDB ID: 6KFR), which is associated with the function of the sxtA4 gene. The TM-score ranges from 0 to 1, with a value of 1 indicating an identical structural match. A TM-score of 0.5 (or 0.45) or higher suggests that the structures share the same global topology for proteins. These structural comparisons allowed us to assess whether the observed nucleotide-level diversity in sxtA4 leads to potential differences in protein topology and thus may influence toxin biosynthetic capacity across strains.

Discussion

This study aimed to deepen our understanding of the physiological intraspecific variation inÂ A. pacificumÂ and to advance knowledge of the relationship between genetic diversity in the saxitoxin biosynthetic gene, environmental conditions (i.e., temperature) and toxin production within the species. A limitation in previous studies of Alexandrium species was the use of a limited number of strains, which may have underestimated the speciesâ€™ physiological and genetic diversity. Here, eight strains of A. pacificum were tested under the same temperature conditions, revealing distinct growth patterns that allowed classification into two groups. KM strains, which were isolated as vegetative cells from the water column in May, showed robust growth at 15Â Â°C, indicating adaptation to low temperatures. In contrast, LIMS strains, which were isolated as cysts from sediment in October, exhibited little growth, suggesting physiological stress. This likely reflects their lower tolerance to cold stress and may indicate that they had entered a dormant phase in response to seasonal cooling. These findings highlight the importance of using multiple strains to capture intraspecific variability, which is essential for understanding the ecological success and environmental adaptability of Alexandrium species.

Alexandrium species are generally recognized for increased toxin production at lower temperatures (8 to 17Â Â°C) [5, 23, 38, 57, 69, 93]. Additionally, cold stress has been shown to elevate toxin production and enhance the expression of the saxitoxin biosynthetic genes, sxtA and sxtG, in A. pacificum [52, 93]. However, the high toxin levels observed at 30Â Â°C in this study contrast with previous reports emphasizing cold-induced toxicity. We found increased toxin production under growth-inhibiting stress conditionsâ€”not only at the elevated temperature of 30Â Â°C across all eight strains, but also at 15Â Â°C for two LIMS strains. This discrepancy is important because Etheridge and Roesler [26] showed that toxin production can increase when dinoflagellate growth slows, implying that cells may prioritize producing secondary metabolites over cell division. This is further evidenced by recent work from Jean et al. [47] demonstrated that while A. pacificum growth decreased at 30Â Â°C, the toxin composition shifted with increased levels of some toxins and appearance of new analogs under warming conditions. Together, these findings suggest that high-temperature stress can also enhance toxicity. Consistently, our results suggest a negative relationship between growth rate and toxin production, with slower-growing strains tending to produce higher toxin levels (Figs.Â 1I, 2E, Supplementary Fig.Â 7). However, this inverse relationship appears to be relatively weak, indicating that other factors may also play significant roles in regulating toxin biosynthesis alongside growth rate. This potential trade-off might reflect complex resource allocation strategies within Alexandrium strains under varying environmental conditions [10, 11, 14]. These results highlight the complex relationship between temperature and toxin production, suggesting that toxin synthesis may be prioritized under stress, regardless of the expected temperature range for toxin production. In ecosystems, this can result in complex variations in PSP toxicity levels, with temporary increases occurring at both low and high temperature [57, 69].

Temperature changes also influenced the profiles of A. pacificum strains. As temperatures increased, the Shannon index indicated a rise in toxin diversity, while the Simpsonâ€™s diversity index decreased. This suggests that while a greater diversity of toxin analogs was produced with increasing temperature, certain toxins became more dominant among the analogs. This shift was particularly evident at 30Â Â°C, where all strains experienced growth inhibition, suggesting a potential adaptive response to thermal stress. In contrast, at 15Â Â°Câ€”where growth inhibition was limited to only LIMS groupâ€”both toxin diversity and analog dominance were less evident. Consistent with these findings, Jean et al. [47] observed that warming and polymetallic stress at 30Â Â°C led to increased production of specific toxin analogs and the appearance of previously undetected toxin analogs. This aligns with our findings of increased toxin diversity and dominance patterns under thermal stress. The increase in toxin diversity and dominance observed under thermal stress may be associated with cellular energy metabolism. Cho et al. [18] reported that the sxtA enzyme co-localizes with Rubisco II and is potentially linked to chloroplasts in Alexandrium catenella, suggesting a possible relationship between saxitoxin biosynthesis and energy-dependent cellular processes. Although the exact role of saxitoxin remains uncertain, its upregulation under stress suggests it may contribute to cellular survival mechanisms under changing environmental conditions.

Despite extensive research on the environmental factors influencing toxin production in Alexandrium [1, 15, 39, 46, 57, 58, 75, 83], Van [92, 93, 95], the underlying mechanisms associated with toxin production remain poorly understood. Previous studies have reported that physiological differences within the same algal species can potentially arise from intraspecific genetic differences [53, 71]. Given the observed variation in toxin composition among strains under the same environmental conditions, we conducted a genetic analysis to investigate whether these differences might be attributed to genetic diversity. Therefore, we analyzed the nucleotide sequences of the saxitoxin biosynthetic sxtA4 gene and found clear genetic diversity among the strains, with high intragenic variation within each strain. These findings align well with previous studies that show high intragenic diversity in this gene, containing between 100 and 378 copies [29, 68, 79, 87]. The expression levels of the proteins produced by the diverse copies of the sxtA4 gene may vary due to non-synonymous SNPs, which can alter the corresponding amino acids in the protein, potentially modifying its function or substrate affinity. These changes, in turn, can lead to differences in the composition and quantity of the final synthesized toxin analogs [17, 32, 35, 45, 78, 96]. Furthermore, our findings revealed that strains with a greater number of representative sequences produced a wider range of toxin analogsâ€”a relationship not previously reported. [29] suggested that toxin gene sequences with fewer SNPs might be associated with lower toxin diversity. This discovery highlights the crucial role of genetic diversity in shaping toxin profile ofÂ Alexandrium. Furthermore, it offers new insights into the intraspecific variation in PSP toxin production and composition, which has puzzled researchers for decades, even among strains isolated from the same location and time. These findings advance our understanding of PSP toxin variability and provide a foundation for future research aimed at mitigating the impacts of harmful algal blooms. The observed genetic variation in the sxtA4 gene could eventually support the identification of molecular markers associated with highly toxic strains. Such insights may inform the development of early-warning molecular monitoring tools to detect toxic blooms in advance, enabling more effective management strategies such as issuing timely harvesting advisories or focused monitoring efforts.

Building upon this genetic analysis, we next investigated whether the observed intragenic diversity translates into structural differences in the encoded protein, which could affect toxin production. Although there is high diversity observed in the nucleotide sequences of the sxtA4 gene, we identified a highly conserved region within those sequences and translated amino acid sequences in the corresponding region. To confirm this trend, the analysis of 166 sxtA4 gene sequences retrieved from the NCBI database in dinoflagellatesâ€”Alexandrium spp., Gymnodinium catenatum, and Pyrodinium bahamenseâ€”also revealed that this region is highly conserved in both nucleotide and amino acid sequences (Supplementary Fig.Â 10). In general, the conserved region in the sequences suggests that there is strong evolutionary pressure to maintain this sequence, likely due to its functional or structural importance [8, 43, 54, 97]. Therefore, while the sxtA4 gene is involved in the initial step of the saxitoxin biosynthetic pathway, and the conserved region identified in this study may contribute to maintaining essential enzymatic function, it is unlikely that variations in its primary sequence alone directly determine the diversity of downstream PST analogs. Rather, these variations may reflect functional tolerance within the conserved core, allowing STX biosynthesis to proceed despite sequence divergence. Hence, the observed sequence diversity likely underscores the robustness of this core function, rather than directly shaping the final toxin profile [28, 74, 86].

The predicted structural variation observed among strains may reflect functional consequences of sxtA4 gene diversity. Although the enzymatic role of the sxtA4-encoded protein in saxitoxin biosynthesis is recognized, its precise structureâ€“function relationship remains unresolved [33, 87]. By assessing the predicted 3D structures, we aimed to explore whether intragenic diversity could be associated with differences in predicted protein structure, and whether these structural differences might tentatively relate to observed toxin analog profiles. The structural differences inferred across strainsâ€”particularly in the presence or absence of key functional domainsâ€”which could in turn be loosely associated with differences in catalytic behavior. Interestingly, strains with greater diversity in predicted protein structures tended to produce a broader range of toxin analogs, suggesting a possible correlation between structural variation and toxin production (Fig.Â 6C). While causality cannot be established from these observations alone, they may reflect strain-specific adaptations in toxin biosynthesis machinery, potentially shaped by ecological or evolutionary pressures. Moreover, comparison of the known structure of arginine aminotransferase (PDB ID: 6KFR) with the predicted protein structure from this study revealed a TM-score of approximately 0.7 out of 1.0. This high similarity between the two is notable, as the observed structural resemblance to known aminotransferases supports the hypothesis that the amplified sxtA4 regions encode functionally relevant proteins. These findings underscore the potential utility of structural bioinformatics in bridging genotypic diversity and phenotypic outcomes in toxic dinoflagellates, though further functional validation will be essential.

Although this study suggests a potential link between sequence variation in the sxtA4 gene and the production of different saxitoxin analogs, this interpretation should be approached with caution. While sxtA4 plays a key role in initiating the biosynthetic pathway, the structural diversity of PST analogs is likely shaped by downstream tailoring enzymes such as sxtSUL, sxtX, sxtH, and sxtU, whose activities may vary under different environmental or physiological conditions [19, 20, 33, 67, 87] As such, the observed association between sxtA4 sequence divergence and toxin profiles remains uncertain and cannot be conclusively interpreted as causal. A major limitation of this study is that it focuses solely on sxtA4, without considering potential interactions with other genes involved in the pathway. To fully understand the mechanisms driving the production of specific saxitoxin analogs, future research must examine how sxtA4 interacts with other biosynthetic genes. A more comprehensive understanding of these geneâ€“gene interactions is essential to unravel the complexity of saxitoxin biosynthesis.

Building on the key findings of this study, several important insights have emerged regarding the ecological significance of toxin production dynamics in Alexandrium. First, the pronounced intraspecific variability in growth patterns and toxin production among different A. pacificum strains underscores the speciesâ€™ adaptive complexity, which may influence bloom dynamics and associated ecosystem effects. This highlights the ecological importance of intraspecific diversity in shaping toxin production and suggests that analyzing a broader range of isolates from diverse environments could deepen our understanding of how such diversity contributes to bloom formation and persistence. Furthermore, our results suggest that Alexandrium tends to prioritize toxin production under stressful conditions, such as growth-inhibiting temperatures. This response may represent a survival strategy in which energy is redirected from growth toward toxin synthesis, helping cells cope with environmental stress. Such a shift could, in turn, affect ecological interactions and ecosystem functioning. Notably, the genetic diversity observed in the sxtA4 gene, especially in relation to toxin composition, indicates that the ecological impacts of Alexandrium are diverse and multifaceted. This genetic variation plays a crucial role in shaping how those impacts manifest. Genomic and transcriptomic approaches could offer deeper insights into how variations across multiple genes involved in saxitoxin biosynthesis contribute to differences in toxin analog composition, thereby influencing food web dynamics and toxicity risks. The presence of conserved regions within the sxtA4 gene suggests the action of evolutionary pressures essential for maintaining saxitoxin biosynthesis. This may be important for the long-term persistence and ecological role of Alexandrium in marine ecosystems. In conclusion, while this study provides valuable insights into the interplay among genetic diversity, toxin production, and environmental stress, it also emphasizes the broader ecological implications of these dynamics. A more comprehensive understanding of these mechanisms is essential for improving the prediction, management, and mitigation of harmful algal blooms, which pose increasing threats to marine ecosystems and human health in a changing climate.
