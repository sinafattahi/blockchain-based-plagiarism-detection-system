Abstract

DNA methylation is an epigenetic modification that plays a crucial role in genome stability and cellular specialization, essential for maintaining normal cellular function and development, also a manifestation indicator of some diseases. Various tools have been proposed for methylation detection, typically leveraging a third-generation sequencing technology called nanopore sequencing, which provides more accurate DNA sequencing data. However, existing tools have their own limitations and advantages in terms of computational resources and information processing, without achieving a good balance. In this situation, we developed EDNTOM (Ensemble Deep Network Tool Of Methylation), a DNA methylation detection tool based on deep learning technology. We employed ensemble learning techniques, integrating predictions from multiple pretrained single models, and introduced an attention weight mechanism to provide accurate and reliable detection, reducing the consumption of computational resources. Results demonstrate that EDNTOM outperforms individual models. Additionally, in cross-species transfer experiments, EDNTOM exhibits strong transfer learning capabilities. We hope this work can provide a more powerful and reliable solution for methylation detection, contributing to the fields of biological science and medicine. The project code is available at https://github.com/ViceMusic/EDNTOM.

1. Introduction

DNA methylation is an epigenetic modification that primarily occurs on cytosine molecules in DNA molecules. This modification is achieved by adding a methyl group to the fifth carbon atom of cytosine. DNA methylation plays a crucial role in gene expression regulation, maintaining genome stability, and cell differentiation and development. Abnormal DNA methylation is closely associated with the occurrence and development of various diseases. , Luan et al. revealed the relationship between Netrin-1 (NTN1) gene methylation and the prognosis of Skin Cutaneous Melanoma (SKCM). By studying changes in DNA methylation, we can gain insight into the mechanisms underlying diseases. − Therefore, detecting DNA methylation behavior is of great significance in the medical field for understanding disease mechanisms, early diagnosis, treatment selection, and the formulation of personalized medical plans. −

As an epigenetic modification on DNA, DNA methylation can be detected using DNA sequencing data. The second-generation sequencing methods include Illumina sequencing, which uses PCR technology to amplify DNA samples and relies on fluorescent or chemically labeled bases for sequencing. These operations limit the ability to analyze repetitive sequences and genomic structures. Especially in low copy number samples, PCR amplification may lead to distortion. And the second-generation sequencing technology cannot achieve single-molecule sequencing. Fortunately, we now have many methods and tools that can obtain labeling information without damaging the molecules, such as the work of An et al. using Surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy (SERS) to detect molecular markers. It will not affect the molecule itself. From this perspective, we have chosen the third-generation sequencing technologies. In third-generation DNA sequencing methods, we choose to use nanopore sequencing data, which is a direct sequencing technique. Nanopore sequencing directly detects nucleotide sequences by analyzing current changes as DNA traverses the pore, bypassing chemical modification and PCR amplification to preserve native DNA integrity while generating high-quality data.

With the rise of deep learning, researchers have begun to use information methods for computational biology, such as the work of Li et al., which used deep learning techniques for N7-m7G site prediction. In the medical field, we use methylation markers to determine lymph node metastasis in gastric cancer.

In recent years, more and more methylation detection tools have combined deep learning with nanopore sequencing, providing researchers with precise sequence recognition and analysis functions. − The predominant tools for methylation detection leveraging deep learning technology principally fall into three categories: those utilizing convolutional neural networks (CNNs), those employing recurrent neural networks (RNNs), and those based on other deep learning architectures.

CNN-based deep learning tools can capture dependencies within a limited range, extracting sequence features from the data while parallel computing, reducing computational cost. Major works and tools based on CNN include DeepMP, DInoPORE. DeepMP is a DNA methylation prediction tool that utilizes CNN to determine whether the sequence is methylated. DInoPORE is a CNN-based model, distinguishing RNA editing sites by nanopore sequencing. RNN-based deep learning tools capture long-range dependencies through the learning and progression of hidden states. Major works based on RNN and its variants include DeepSignal and DeepMod. DeepSignal is a deep learning tool utilizing bidirectional long short-term memory (LSTM) models to capture sequence information. DeepMod, similar to DeepSignal, employs LSTM for methylation site detection. Additionally, there are other model architectures-based deep learning tools. MethBERT utilizes BERT for DNA methylation state prediction. DeepMethyl is another methylation prediction software based on stacked denoising autoencoders, used to predict the methylation status of DNA. In addition, NanoCon is also a methylation detection tool that utilizes transformer architecture and contrastive learning techniques.

However, CNN and RNN may encounter issues with insufficient long-term memory and may lose dependencies over long distances. Tools based on other models, such as BERT, show improvements in methylation prediction. Nevertheless, the computational cost of BERT models will increase with the input length, leading to more expensive training. In summary, each popular tool retains advantages and limitations due to underlying architectures. Given this situation, we aim to find a framework that integrates the strengths of mainstream tools.

We have observed the application of ensemble learning in this field. Ensemble learning − is a machine learning method that combines multiple learners to improve overall performance. In the field of bioinformatics, the METEORE integrates the predictions of multiple models for methylation prediction, demonstrating the universality of ensemble learning in bioinformatics. In the work by Lasantha et al., ensemble deep learning method is observed to outperform single models too. In the field of biology, the ensemble learning based model tool LightGBM has achieved the function of detecting genome assisted breeding. In emotion recognition, Wang et al.’s work also utilized ensemble learning techniques in emotion recognition tasks. In the medical field, Kazemi and Mirroshandel proposed a kidney stone detection model using ensemble learning.

Inspired by the aforementioned work, we decided to adopt an ensemble learning mechanism, integrating models. This choice aims to leverage the strengths of different models and mitigate the impact of their weaknesses.

By combining ensemble learning with attention mechanisms, we have developed an artificial intelligence tool called EDNTOM (Ensemble Deep Network Tool Of Methylation), further improving the performance of DNA methylation prediction. We have achieved promising prediction and transfer effects on both human data sets and data sets of two plant species ( and ). Compared with mainstream methylation detection tools such as DeepSignal and DeepMod, EDNTOM’s prediction results not only come from one model, but also can extract features in different dominant dimensions based on CNN, BERT and RNN, avoiding the preference of a single model. Moreover, we have designed a new attention mechanism that replaces the voting operation in traditional ensemble learning with linear projection, resulting in excellent learning ability. We fully open source the code and encourage other developers to integrate models according to their own needs in integrated learning.

EDNTOM typically achieves lower costs and more persistent convergence effects, providing great assistance for studying bioinformatics with complex and uneven data.

In the future, we expect this research to assist exploration in this field and gain more disease-related research or practical applications related to medical devices. For example, using EDNTOM to promote research and analysis of multimodal data related to methylation in the field of disease associations. We will continue to update this tool, and promote its implementation, such as with Qian et al.’s work on biocompatible materials and sensor integration.

In addition, in the integrated learning module, we have open-sourced our data processing methods. We look forward to interested researchers exploring the boundaries of algorithm tools based on their computing resources.

2. Methods

2.1. Data Set

Nanopore sequencing data of Arabidopsis (, or , GCF_000001735.4_TAIR10.1) and rice (, or , IRGSP-1.0) compiled by Ni et al. were used in this study. Because the study of the 5 mC locus is often associated with human diseases with certain specific traits in mammals, the human data set was added to these data sets as a representative 5 mC methylation data set for mammalian DNA. NA12878 is a widely used reference sample of the human genome and is part of the International Human Genome Project (HGP). NA12878 has become one of the most widely used reference samples in genomic research due to the high quality of its genomic data and the wide range of available data sets and has become an important reference point in genomic research and genomics applications. 20,000 reads from each species were randomly selected during the construction of the data set, and each Read is the electrical signal sequence information on a segment of nucleic acid sequence obtained from a DNA sample by nanopore sequencing technology.

In the data sets of the three species we used, the human data set NA12878 is a slightly imbalanced data set with a data ratio of 2:1. On the data sets of the other two species, the ratio of methylated to nonmethylated data is 1:1. In addition, NA12878 contains a total of approximately 3.5 million pieces of data. The rice and Arabidopsis data sets contain approximately 300,000 pieces of data.

Through the data processing method provided by Ni. The methylation data we obtained were processed into the same structure, including DNA sequence information, nanopore sequencing current mean, electrical signal standard deviation, and signal length. Each data represents a k-mer base sequence. In this article, we set k to 13, which is an experience-based choice. In some mainstream methylation frameworks, such as DeepSignal, a k-mer with a length of 13 is used, so we have decided to adopt this design.

For subsequent training, we divided the data set into a 9:1 ratio, with 90% being the training set and 10% being the validation set. We first define the data set as

D={(xi,yi)}i=1N⁣yi∈{0,1}

extract subsets based on methylation labels (0 or 1), calculate segmentation points τc , divide them according to the index position of the segmentation point

{Dc={xi|yi=c}⁣c∈{0,1}τc=min(⌊0.9|Dc|⌋,|Dc|−1)Dtrain←Dc[:τc],⁣Dval←Dc[τc:]

and finally randomly shuffle to ensure the rationality of the data. This division method is relatively simple, but convenient and rigorous.

2.2. Structure of Model

As illustrated in Figure , EDNTOM is a deep learning neural network model that performs nanopore methylation detection through ensemble learning and weighting mechanisms. According to the sequence of data flow, we divide the entire model into four main parts: the data preprocessing module, the symmetric encoding module, the attention weight calculation module, and the classifier module.

2.2.1. Data Preprocessing Module

In the data preprocessing module, we transform the acquired data from the data set into tensor forms suitable for input into the model. The data in our data set follows a uniform format, consisting of DNA base sequences (referred to as “seq”), nanopore data (including means, stds, and lengths), and methylation labels (“label”). As for the nanopore data, we extract the “means”, “stds” and “lens” corresponding to each base, and arrange the nanodata into a matrix of size K*3. The specific image operations are illustrated in Figure .

2.2.2. Symmetric Encoding Module

We call this module symmetric encoding because of its symmetric structure (as shown in Figure ). In this module, the data are input into three single models (CNN, RNN and BERT) to obtain their respective prediction results ο i .

οi=Modeli(x)⁣i∈{CNN,BERT,RNN}

After obtaining the original output of a single model, the data needs to be shaped to adapt to the downstream attention weight layer. Each model comes with a structure and a processing method that is completely consistent in calculation. By linear projection, all outputs of the model are projected to the same size.

fi=Classifieri(xi)=WiReLU(οi)+bi

Due to the identical prediction and classifier processing methods in each model, the data flow presents a symmetrical shape. Therefore, we refer to this module as the symmetric encoding module. This structure ensures consistency and data independence in downstream tasks after the outputs of all models are projected.

2.2.3. Attention Weight Calculation Module

In the attention weight module (as shown in Figure ), we used a neural network-based attention mechanism to extract from the output results f of the models. In all experiments in this article, we trained these three models using a human data set, which is similar to pretraining. (We also provide the weights of these basic models trained and saved on different data sets in open-source code to meet users’ pursuit of higher accuracy).

We will concatenate f i (i ∈ {RNN,BERT,CNN})­in one dimension, and then use linear layers and activation functions for linear projection. In this process, we train two weight matrices, projection matrix W 2, and concatenation matrix W 1, as well as their corresponding biases. Finally, through calculation, we obtain the integrated weight α. The mathematical expression of its operation is as follows

α=softmax(W2·tanh(W1[f1|f2|f3]+b1)+b2)

In addition, we combine the original input f i with integrated weight and fuse the calculated results. The mathematical operation expression is as follows

y=[a1f1∥a2f2∥a3f3]

Based on this attention weighting mechanism, we can dynamically assign weights to each individual input data based on the prediction results of each model. Added the problem of insufficient flexibility of trained single weights for diverse data in traditional voting.

2.2.4. Classifier Module

The final classifier module is responsible for classifying input data and mapping it to a smaller dimension to obtain the final prediction output. The architecture of this layer is shown in Figure . Through processing in this layer, the model integrates and refines information from all preceding layers to generate the final prediction output. The design of this fully connected layer aims to enable the model to capture key features in the input data optimally and produce highly accurate final predictions.

2.3. Metrics

We selected the following six model evaluation metrics: accuracy (ACC), precision, recall, F1 score, area under the precision–recall curve (AUPRC), and area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC). −

These evaluation metrics are commonly used performance metrics in deep learning to assess the accuracy and efficacy of classification models.

2.4. Setup

On the server, we configured four NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090 GPUs and utilized the PyTorch Lightning framework. During the training process, we employed the Adam optimizer to optimize model parameters and combined it with a learning rate decay strategy for better control over the learning process. To ensure fair evaluation, we adopted a strict data set partitioning scheme, splitting each data set into training, and validation sets in a 9:1 ratio. This partitioning scheme aims to achieve more stable and reliable computational results.

During the training process of the model, we set the batch to 100, the initial learning rate of the Adam optimizer to 0.001, and used binary cross loss to calculate the loss value. Unless otherwise specified, the default training epochs are 25.

In the ensemble module, our individual models (CNN, RNN, and BERT) have already been pretrained, and each has demonstrated promising performance. During the training of our overall model, the parameters of individual models are frozen and do not participate in training iterations. This separation training approach effectively reduces training pressure and difficulty, yielding better results with fewer computational rounds. Additionally, it mitigates the risk of overfitting that could arise from excessive parameters and layer depths.

3. Experiments

3.1. Comparing with Single Models

We present the actual performance results of EDNTOM and compare them with other models, including CNN, RNN, and BERT models. In this experiment, we compared the performance differences between EDNTOM and the other three models. We conducted this work on data sets of three species: humans, , and . For any of the data sets, we follow the settings in the setup section and divide the data set into two parts: training and validation. Each model was trained and tested on the same data set.

We compared the performance of models trained and validated on the same data set. Through the experiment, we compared EDNTOM with three other model tools under the same training and validation conditions on the same data set. It can be observed that compared to RNN, BERT, and CNN, EDNTOM shows improvement in accuracy, especially compared to RNN. BERT performs similarly to EDNTOM, and during the training and testing process on the Arabidopsis data set, BERT even outperforms EDNTOM in terms of recall. However, we attribute this to the selection of AUROC as the metric for comparison and saving when selecting the saved model. In conclusion, the test proves that EDNTOM integrated by a single model is not inferior to any single model in six indicators including ACC.

3.2. Comparative Experiment with Popular Tools

In order to verify the performance of our model in multiple aspects, we compared EDNTOM with two popular tools in the same field, DeepSignal and DeepMod. In this experiment, we compared multiple indicators including loss, Auroc, etc. And the same hyperparameters were tested on the Arabidopsis data set, and the hyperparameters and data set partitioning methods have been described in detail in the Data Set and Setup sections.

As shown in Figure , we conducted over 20 rounds of training on the Arabidopsis data set, and EDNTOM achieved stable convergence during the training period (final loss = 0.11497), which was 30.5% and 51.6% lower than Deepsignal (loss = 0.16528) and DeepMod (loss = 0.23709), respectively. By comparing the standard deviation of the training curve (σ = 0.0098 vs 0.0235/0.0257), the gradient fluctuation amplitude of EDNTOM is only 41.7%–38.1% of that of the comparison model. The AUROC peak of EDNTOM reached 0.99232, which is 0.9–1.8 percentage points higher than the comparison model. Moreover, the accuracy fluctuation of the validation set in the later stage of training (epoch >15) is ≤0.3%, which is significantly better than the 1.2–2.8% of the comparison model. In summary, we believe that EDNTOM not only has superior learning abilities, but can also achieve better accuracy in a shorter period of time. Furthermore, in over 20 training sessions, EDNTOM maintained a steady improvement trend, while DeepSignal failed to fully converge and exhibited oscillations in the later stages DeepMod experienced a gradient explosion in the midterm, which seriously affected its performance. From this, it can be seen that EDNTOM can adapt to more complex data and classification tasks, reducing the time consumption of users when adjusting hyperparameters. At the same time, this experiment shows that EDNTOM may still not reach its performance limit, and has considerable practical value.

Meanwhile, as shown in Table , in terms of time consumption, by implementing a strategy of freezing parameters, EDNTOM effectively reduces the pressure on training parameters while preventing gradient explosion caused by excessively deep neural networks EDNTOM’s total parameters are only 35.4% of DeepSignal’s, but its performance metrics still lead by 1.2%. In terms of inference speed, although DeepMod has achieved the fastest speed, its unit parameter inference time reaches 0.951, reflecting its complex structure and high computational density. The computational density of EDNTOM and DeepSignal is relatively low. At the same time, in terms of training efficiency, EDNTOM’s training time and unit parameter training speed far exceed DeepMod. Although slightly inferior to DeepSignal, EDNTOM only reduces the number of parameters by 64.6% by increasing training time by 87% while ensuring performance. Its overall performance can be fully comparable to DeepSignal.

3.4. Comparison with Soft Voting

In the ablation experiment above, in order to demonstrate the effectiveness of the ensemble learning part, we chose to remove the voting step and use simple concatenation for ablation comparison experiments. In this experiment, in order to demonstrate the effectiveness of the attention mechanism designed in the ensemble learning part, we used the commonly used soft voting in ensemble learning as the experimental object.

Soft voting is an algorithm widely used in ensemble learning mechanisms, which adds up the classification probabilities obtained by each learner and resets the threshold to integrate the results of all learners and make judgments. In our model, the comprehensive scoring formula for soft voting is

Score=P(y=1|x)RNN+P(y=1|x)BERT+P(y=1|x)CNN

And, since we used three models, we set the weights to 1.5 and used this to construct the decision function

mask=11+e−(S−1.5)/0.1

The final label is determined by the decision function

final_labels=[1−mask,mask]∈R2

Through this method, the experimental results we obtained on the Arabidopsis data set are shown in Figure .

Through experimental comparison, it can be concluded that the attention weight mechanism designed in EDNTOM has better convergence speed than traditional ensemble learning methods such as soft voting. The speed of loss reduction and accuracy improvement in attention weight mechanism is significantly higher than that of soft voting, and in the later stage, while maintaining performance growth, the training time of attention weight mechanism is significantly lower than that of soft voting.

By analyzing the trend of curve changes, we can find that the attention weighting mechanism can analyze features more deeply and provide reasonable weight assignments. While soft voting has already converged to its own limit, EDNTOM still maintains a good performance improvement trend. We encourage open source workers to explore the limits of this mechanism by increasing training volume, adjusting hyperparameters, and other methods within their own computing power.

3.5. Transfer Experiment

To evaluate the cross-data set transferability of EDNTOM, we conducted experiments on three species: human, , and . The model was trained on one species and tested on the other two, resulting in six cross-species transfer settings. These experiments highlight EDNTOM’s ability to generalize across diverse biological data sets, demonstrating its potential in transfer learning.

In the transfer learning experiments, we prepared two types of data sets: the pretraining data set and the transfer data set. After training the model on one data set, we fine-tuned it on another, following the same training procedure and hyperparameter settings as described in the Setup section. Each of the three species data sets served both as a pretraining and a transfer data set, resulting in a total of nine pretraining–transfer data set pairs through combinatorial permutation. During training, we monitored multiple evaluation metrics and recorded the best-performing weights. The final results were visualized using a heatmap to illustrate the model’s transferability across species.

Verify the generalization ability of the EDNTOM model through three sets of cross species migration experiments. As shown in Figure and Table , when the model was trained on human data and transferred to the rice () and Arabidopsis () test sets, its performance showed a moderate level (AUROC: 0.917–0.937). It is worth noting that reverse migration (from plants to humans) showed similar performance degradation, such as a decrease in ACC of 0.81892 for the rice training model on human data, indicating significant differences in methylation patterns between animals and plants.

3.6. Interpretability Experiment

To make the experimental results more reasonable and intuitive, we designed the following experiment: we aggregated the features of three models into one feature sequence and then reduced its dimensionality to two-dimensional space using UMAP. Subsequently, we differentiated between methylated and unmethylated samples based on the corresponding labels and plotted them on a two-dimensional space. Throughout the interpretative operation, we selected 1000 samples from the human data set. To enhance the comparison and demonstrate the superiority of our model, we performed the same feature extraction and analysis on other individual models as well.

As shown in the Figure , the data points form two distinct clusters on the graph, indicating that our model has successfully differentiated between methylated and unmethylated data features in the output. This geometric representation provides a more intuitive illustration of the effectiveness of our model. Through such dimensionality reduction plots, we achieve a more intuitive display of results than the raw data. In terms of feature classification performance, EDNTOM demonstrates better classification results compared to other models by integrating predictions from a single model. We attribute this improvement to the reasonable configuration of our attention weights, which incorporate the advantages of different models.

However, our experiments mainly focused on balanced data sets. This characteristic makes EDNTOM particularly suitable for epigenetic research scenarios where there are differences in data distribution between species.

4. Results and Discussion

In this study, we proposed EDNTOM, a nanopore-based methylation prediction model combining ensemble learning and attention mechanisms. Compared with models like DeepSignal and DeepMod, EDNTOM demonstrated faster convergence and better generalization, while using a moderate number of parameters. Additionally, compared to traditional soft-voting methods, it showed improved robustness against overfitting. Although we did not explore its performance limits due to computational constraints, the code is fully open-sourced to encourage further optimization and customization for diverse tasks.

5. Conclusion

In this study, we presented EDNTOM, a deep learning tool based on ensemble learning and attention mechanisms, designed to accurately predict methylation patterns using nanopore sequencing data. What sets this tool apart is its integration of multiple models and its clever model fusion strategy, which achieves performance compared to individual models, enabling efficient methylation prediction. This ensemble learning approach not only enhances overall performance but also, through a series of ablation experiments, we further demonstrated that the current model state has reached a considerable level of suitability. The method proposed in this study has made certain progress in this field, and we hope that these explorations can provide new research ideas for the development of genomics and bioinformatics..
