Abstract

The Ordos Basin is a typical craton basin. Underpressure exists in the tight sandstone reservoirs of the Chang 7 member of the Upper Triassic Yanchang Formation in the Yishan slope of the basin. However, overpressure was verified in these reservoirs during the Early Cretaceous period, and the evolution process from overpressure to underpressure is still unclear. In this paper, the overpressure evolution process of various lithologies in the Chang 7 member in the study area was reconstructed by using basin simulation. Then, the evolution process from overpressure to underpressure in these lithologies was quantitatively analyzed. These analyses were performed by considering the effects of temperature reduction, elastic rebound, and brittle rupture of overpressured mudstone during tectonic uplift. We also summarized the effect of the pore pressure evolution on oil migration and accumulation. The results show that the overpressure genesis in the Chang 7 member was mainly hydrocarbon generation and disequilibrium compaction, which began to form in the Early Early Cretaceous and peaked at the end of the Early Cretaceous. Since the Late Cretaceous, elastic rebound and temperature reduction due to tectonic uplift have led to current underpressure in the sandstone of the Chang 7 member. Their contributions to pressure reduction of the Chang 7 member range from 53.2% to 60.5% and 39.5% to 46.8%, respectively. In addition to these influences, the formation of nearly normal pressure in the dark mudstone of the Chang 71+2 member and two sets of black mudstone at the top and bottom of the Chang 73 member is also influenced by the brittle rupture of overpressured mudstone. This effect leads to the release of overpressure in these mudstones and simultaneously causes an increase in the pore pressure of the adjacent tight sandstones. During the entire hydrocarbon accumulation period, the excess pressure difference between the source rock and reservoir rock of the Chang 7 member was the main force driving hydrocarbon migration. This force was 1.5–3.5 MPa and 5–11 MPa during the Early Cretaceous and Late Cretaceous and Paleogene, respectively. The excess pressure difference between the dark mudstone caprock and the reservoir rock can reach 3 MPa, which strengthens the physical sealing ability of the caprocks. This study provides new concepts for the quantitative evaluation of the evolution of underpressure and its relationship with the tight oil distribution.

1. Introduction

The pressure coefficient is often used to classify abnormal pressure, and the pressure coefficient is greater than 1.1 for overpressure and less than 0.9 for underpressure. − Most of the discovered oil and gas reservoirs in the world exhibit overpressure, − while some exhibit underpressure. − However, previous studies have found that some oil and gas reservoirs are now underpressure despite exhibiting overpressure during accumulation periods in their geological histories. ,, Quantitative evaluation of the formation and evolution of abnormal pressure is crucial for studying hydrocarbon generation, migration, accumulation, and preservation in hydrocarbon-bearing basins. , To quantify the evolution of overpressure, the origin of the overpressure must be identified. At present, overpressure origins can be summarized into five main types: disequilibrium compaction, overpressure transfer, fluid expansion, lateral tectonic compression, and diagenesis. ,− Among these origins, overpressure induced by tectonic compression is primarily developed in the thrust belt of foreland basins with strong tectonic compression or in salt systems. − However, several origins of overpressure are developed in rift basins and stable cratonic basins, especially disequilibrium compaction, hydrocarbon generation, and overpressure transfer, which are the most common origins of overpressure. ,,,− The evolution process of overpressure is usually studied by inclusion analysis and numerical modeling methods. ,, At present, there is some common numerical modeling software for overpressure, such as PetroMod, BasinMod, TEMISPACK, and BASIMS. ,, However, these software approaches typically simulate the overpressure induced by disequilibrium compaction, and few of these approaches can achieve overpressure associated with hydrocarbon generation. Some efforts have derived an evaluation model for overpressure induced by hydrocarbon generation considering the various factors influencing the hydrocarbon generation process on the pore pressure. , The causes of underpressure include elastic rebound and temperature reduction. , Due to the difference in the coefficient of compression and thermal expansion between the rock skeleton particle and formation fluid, elastic rebound and temperature reduction can cause a relative increase in pore space, which in turn creates underpressure in the closure system. ,

The Yishan slope in the Ordos Basin formed a stable craton background. The reservoirs in the Chang 7 member of the Yanchang Formation (Fm) in the slope are generally developed with underpressure or near-normal pressure, as experienced due to the four stages of tectonic uplift. Across these stages, the range of uplift in the first three periods is relatively weak, with erosion thicknesses of 50–200 m, while the range of uplift in the fourth period is large, with erosion thicknesses of 1150∼1600 m. According to previous research, there are three hydrocarbon accumulation periods in the Yishan slope of the Ordos Basin: Late Middle Jurassic to Early Cretaceous, Early Cretaceous to Late Cretaceous, and Late Cretaceous to Paleogene. − During the Early Cretaceous, disequilibrium compaction and hydrocarbon generation led to overpressure in the Chang 7 member of the Yishan slope of the Ordos Basin, and tectonic uplift since the Late Cretaceous led to underpressure in this member. ,,

Regarding the quantitative evaluation of pore pressure evolution in the Chang 7 member of the Yanchang Fm in the Ordos Basin, previous studies recovered the paleo-overpressure mainly using the balancing depth method, basin modeling, and fluid inclusion analysis. , These methods can provide a favorable reconstruction of the evolutionary history of paleo-overpressure in sandstone reservoirs but poorly characterize organic-rich mudstone because hydrocarbon generation pressurization is not considered in these methods. In addition, underpressure caused by tectonic uplift was mostly quantified by elastic rebound and temperature reduction, whose research objects in this aspect were sandstone reservoirs rather than mudstones. Additionally, substantial tectonic uplift can cause rock layers to develop fractures and microfractures, resulting in pressure relief. ,− This phenomenon is common in overpressured mudstone. However, this effect of the pressure relief on the formation of underpressure in the mudstone strata that experienced a tectonic uplift in the late stage of the study area was not considered. Therefore, the differential evolution process of pore pressure in different lithologies in the Chang 7 member of the Yanchang Fm in the Yishan slope of the Ordos Basin remains unclear.

The contributions of this paper are as follows: (1) quantitative analysis of the differential evolution characteristics of the paleo-overpressure of different lithologic layers in the Chang 7 member of the study area; (2) quantitative analysis of the differential evolution characteristics of present-day underpressure or near-normal pressure in different lithologic layers in this member; and (3) revealing the relationship between pore pressure evolution and tight sandstone reservoirs in this member. This study presents new concepts for the quantitative evaluation of the formation and evolution of underpressure under similar geological conditions and guides the exploration of underpressure tight sandstone reservoirs.

2. Geological Setting

The Ordos Basin, a typical Craton Basin, is situated in the western part of the North China block and is relatively tectonically stable (Figure a). , It is adjacent to the Luliang Mountains, Taihang Mountains, Helan Mountains, Liupan Mountains, and Yin Mountains, consisting of six tectonic units (Figure b), with the study area in the southeastern part of the Yishan slope within the Ordos Basin. The overall Yishan slope is a west-dipping monocline with an angle of less than 1° and several low-amplitude nose-shaped uplifts on the slope (Figure c). The Triassic strata were influenced by three major tectonic periods: the Indosinian, Yanshan, and Himalayan orogenies. At the end of the Triassic period, the basin transformed from the North China craton depressional basin into an inland differential subsidence basin due to the Indochinese movement, and the basin underwent an erosion event. The Yanshan movement caused two erosion events during the Jurassic period, resulting in a paleotectonic pattern of a monocline with a northwest dip. In addition, the Himalayan tectonic activity caused the basin to uplift at the end of the Cretaceous period, leading to its current slope being high in the southeast and low in the northwest.

In the study area, the Mesozoic Yanchang Fm comprises ten members from top to bottom, named Chang 1–Chang 10. The Chang 7 member of the Triassic Yanchang Fm is the target layer of this study and is divided into three submembers, namely, Chang 71, Chang 72, and Chang 73. The sedimentary facies of the Upper Triassic Yanchang Formation on the Yishan slope are lake deltas, of which lakes developed from Chang 10 to Chang 8, reaching the maximum development in Chang 7. The Chang 73 submember has two sets of black mudstones, averaging a thickness of 10 and 5 m at the top and the bottom of the submember, respectively. These black mudstones are the main source rocks in the study area. The Chang 71+2 submember contains several sets of dark mudstones with a thickness less than 5 m. These dark mudstones are secondary source rocks in the study area (Figure ). , The hydrocarbon source rocks in the Chang 7 member exhibit abundant organic matter content with a TOC generally higher than 1.5%, mainly distributed between 3% and 6%, reaching up to 12%. Their chloroform bitumen “A” content ranges from 0.74% to 1.76%, and their total hydrocarbon content ranges from 66.87% to 92.02%. The black mudstone in the Chang 73 member contains more organic matter content than the dark mudstone in the Chang 71 and Chang 72 members. The majority of the organic matter types are types II1 and II2, with type II1 being the dominant variety. − The hydrocarbon source rocks in the Chang 7 member of the study area are at a thermally mature stage, with most of the Ro evolution reaching 0.7%. Additionally, the Chang 7 member in the study area typically contains thick fine sandstone reservoirs greater than 10 m in thickness, with a porosity range of 7.0–12.0%, averaging 8.4%, and a permeability range of (0.03–1.09) × 10–3 μm2, averaging 0.32 × 10–3 μm2. Moreover, the dark mudstone of the Chang 71+2 member can be considered as caprock layers.

Due to the lack of measured pore pressure data in the study area, we obtained the pore pressure distribution of the Chang 7 member reservoirs in the study area based on previous results. Zhang et al. found that the present-day pore pressure in the reservoirs as a whole shows normal pore pressure or underpressure, based on 804 formation measured pressure data from more than 700 wells in the Yanchang Fm of the Yishan slope of the Ordos Basin. The measured pressure coefficient mainly ranges from 0.5 to 0.85, and the pressure coefficient in the plane shows the characteristic of gradually decreasing from the west to the east. In addition, the pressure coefficient of the reservoir varies minimally in the vertical profile, indicating that the pressure coefficient of sandstone reservoirs in the Chang 7 member of the study area can be approximated as 0.65.

3. Data and Methods

3.1. Data Source

We collected logging data from 17 wells, which were obtained from Shaanxi Yanchang Petroleum (Group) Co., Ltd. Two wells (wells N172 and N205) provided sonic, resistivity, density, and natural gamma curves, while 15 wells provided sonic, resistivity, natural gamma, spontaneous potential, and well diameter curves. These logs have high borehole quality and are less affected by varying borehole conditions, which make them useful in identifying log responses to overpressure. − Additionally, formation temperature data were measured in several wells from Shaanxi Yanchang Petroleum (Group) Co., Ltd. and can be used as constraints for basin modeling to reconstruct the thermal history.

3.2. Method for Identifying the Origins of Paleo-Overpressure

Sonic and resistivity logs are liable to subtle textural changes in sediments, showing higher sonic and lower resistivity in overpressure mudstones than in normal pressure mudstones. , They demonstrate a favorable response to overpressure and are commonly used to explain the cause of overpressure. Moreover, density logs can reflect the porosity of mudstone and can be used to study the compaction characteristics of mudstone. Equation can be used to calculate the density porosity in the mudstones.

ϕdensity=(ρma−ρb)(ρma−ρf) 1

where ϕdensity is the density porosity (decimal fraction); ρma is the matrix density (g/cm3), taken as 2.75 g/cm3; ρb is the bulk density measured by density logging (g/cm3); and ρf is the pore fluid density (g/cm3), taken as 1.02 g/cm3.

Previous studies have shown that overpressure in the Chang 7 member of the Yishan slope of the Ordos Basin developed in the Early Cretaceous. This is mainly caused by disequilibrium compaction and hydrocarbon generation. , The combination of sonic, resistivity, density logs, density porosity data, and the velocity–density cross-plot methods can be better used to identify the two causes of overpressure. ,, Therefore, they are used here to study the cause of the overpressure in the study area. Log response characteristics to overpressure induced by disequilibrium compaction demonstrate that with increasing burial depth the sonic velocity increases, and the resistivity, density, and density porosity significantly decrease. Log response characteristics to overpressure induced by hydrocarbon generation indicate that with increasing burial depth the sonic and resistivity increase, and the density and density porosity remain constant or only slightly decrease. Data points of overpressure induced by disequilibrium compaction typically fall on the loading curve in the velocity–density cross-plot, while those by the hydrocarbon generation origin are on the unloading curve in the velocity–density cross-plot.

3.3. Basin Modeling Method for Reconstructing the Evolution of Paleo-Overpressure

Basin simulation methods are widely used for the quantitative analysis of pore pressure evolution. , Reasonable stratigraphic parameters and boundary conditions are selected to recover the burial history and thermal history of a single well in the study area. In this context, further reconstruction of the paleo-overpressure evolution process occurred in the study area before the maximum burial depth at the end of the Early Cretaceous.

3.3.1. Modeling Parameter Selection

The basin burial history was recovered using the inverse backstripping method, and the R o versus time curve was reconstructed using the Easy% R o model to infer the thermal evolution history of hydrocarbon source rocks. Basic parameters, including present-day thickness and lithology of strata, were obtained from well logging interpretation and core samples in the study area. The historical timing of the geological events was determined based on the research results from Chen et al. and Luo. The paleowater depth and paleosurface temperature parameters were modified based on the research results from Fan et al. The study area has undergone four phases of tectonic uplift since Indochina, and the amount of erosion generated by the four phases of tectonic uplift is referred to in the research results from Chen et al. The simulated organic matter type is taken as type II, with a primary hydrogen index (HI) of 550 mg/g for type II kerogen. Total organic carbon (TOC) was obtained from the existing logging data using a modified Δlog R model. The original TOC parameters were used for the simulation, with an organic carbon recovery coefficient of 1.31 in the study area. Paleo-heat flow parameters were based on Ren et al. and Jiang et al., and the adjusted heat flow parameters were kept in combination with the measured stratigraphic temperature in the study area (Figure ).

3.4. Balanced Depth Method for Calculation of Overpressure Induced by Disequilibrium Compaction

The balanced depth method is widely used to calculate the overpressure induced by disequilibrium compaction. The equation is given as follows:

PZ=γwZe+γb(Z−Ze) 12

where P Z is the pore pressure (MPa); γw is the hydrostatic pressure gradient (kg/m3), taken as 1.02 kg/m3; γb is the overlying rock pressure gradient (Pa/m), taken as 2.3 Pa/m; and Z and Z e are the actual depth and balanced depth (m), respectively.

Organic-rich mudstones contain solid-phase organic matter, which transmits a faster speed of sound than the rock skeleton. If this effect is not considered, then the predicted result of overpressure due to disequilibrium compaction using the method is too large. 3.5. Calculation Method of Pressure Reduction

Elastic rebound and temperature reduction are the primary factors influencing the pore pressure in sedimentary basins that have undergone late-stage tectonic uplift. The reduction in overlying stratigraphic thickness due to uplift erosion reduces the loading above the target layer, causing depressurization and expansion of pore space and formation fluid in the target layers. This process leads to an increase in the volume of the pore space and pore fluid, with the final pore pressure change depending on the magnitude of both increases. The compression coefficient is a physical quantity that measures changes in volume, and Russell determined the compression coefficient to be 3 × 10–4 MPa–1 for formation water and 1 × 10–3 MPa–1 for sandstone. The Young’s modulus of mudstone in the Chang 7 member is 2700 MPa, and Poisson’s ratio is 0.3 according to logging interpretation. By eq , the compression coefficient of the Chang 7 member mudstone in the study area is 4 × 10–4 MPa–1. Assuming that only formation water fills the pore space, the expansion of this water results in a volume increase of approximately 1/3 that of sandstone rebound and 3/4 that of mudstone rebound. Even a slight change in pore volume can significantly change the pressure when the formation is closed. Therefore, eq is used to calculate the abnormal pressure change resulting from elastic rebound in the sandstone.

During the uplift erosion process, both the geothermal temperature and the fluid volume decrease. This phenomenon leads to a relative increase in the formation pore space and, consequently, a decrease in the pore pressure. 4. Results

4.1. Origin of Overpressure of the Chang 7 Member in the Study Area

Our analysis of the Early Cretaceous paleo-overpressure genesis of the Chang 7 member in the study area was conducted using well N172. The sonic, resistivity, density, natural gamma, well diameter logs, and density porosity in the mudstone of well N172 and the paleopressure obtained by the balanced depth method are shown in Figure . The results indicate that the normal compaction trend in the well is above 1020 m. Below this depth of 1020 m, sonic, resistivity, and density porosity are abnormally high compared to that of the normal compaction, and density is abnormally low compared to the normal compaction. These characteristics indicate that the disequilibrium compaction was formed in the mudstone of the Chang 7 member in the study area (Figure ). Because the mudstone layer was at its maximum burial depth at the end of the Early Cretaceous period, it is speculated that this disequilibrium compaction reached its strongest level at that time.

Additionally, the cross-plot of sonic velocity–density reveals that data points in the dark mudstone of the Chang 7 member and the black mudstone of the bottom of the Chang 73 submember in well N172 deviate from the loading curve (Figure ). This phenomenon indicates that disequilibrium compaction pressurization and weak hydrocarbon generation pressurization features are present in different mudstones of the Chang 7 member. However, the black mudstone at the top of the Chang 73 submember, which has the most hydrocarbon generating capacity, does not show a tendency to deviate from the loading curve in the data points of the sonic velocity–density cross-plot as the other two mudstone sets do (Figure ). This result indicates that overpressure induced by hydrocarbon generation is not obviously developed in the black mudstone at the top of the Chang 73 submember, where the hydrocarbon generation capacity is stronger and the thickness is greater.

This observation may be attributed to the Ordos Basin undergoing intensive tectonic uplift since the Late Cretaceous period. The overpressure at the end of the Early Cretaceous period was transformed into the present underpressure by tectonic uplift. Therefore, this phenomenon resulted in a weak logging response for paleo-overpressure induced by hydrocarbon generation in well N172. However, previous studies have shown that hydrocarbon generation is one of the important origins of the paleo-overpressure in the Chang 7 member in the late Early Cretaceous in the Ordos Basin. , Our analysis concludes that the Early Cretaceous overpressure observed in the dark mudstone and black mudstone of the Chang 7 member in the study area is a result of two origins of overpressure: hydrocarbon generation and disequilibrium compaction.

4.3. Results of Overpressure Induced by Disequilibrium Compaction and Hydrocarbon Generation Using the Method of Basin Modeling

The burial history, thermal history, and pressure evolution history of 17 wells in the study area are simulated. The results of these wells exhibit similaws of these results. The measured data of R o in Figure are cited from ref , and the oil accumulation periods are cited from ref (Figure ). From 225 to 145 Ma, the burial depth varied in the Chang 7 member from 1032 to 1105 m. During this period, the vitrinite reflectance of this member ranged from 0.25% to 0.5%. Ingram et al. , quantified mudstone brittleness using the geological parameter of the over consolidation ratio (OCR) and concluded that the value of OCR exceeding a threshold will make mudstone completely brittle. Normal consolidated mudstone has an OCR of 1. However, if the OCR value in the mudstone is greater than 2.6, it transforms from ductile to brittle (Yuan et al.). Since the pressure will be released once the brittle fracture limit is reached, and this pressure release usually lasts for a certain period of time, we assume that the duration of this pressure release is 5Ma. 5.2. Discussion of the Pressure Reduction Magnitudes in Different Lithology Layers since the Late Cretaceous

Due to the relatively small pore rebound effect of mudstone during the tectonic uplift process, the changes in formation pressure caused by the pore rebound of mudstone during the late Himalayan tectonic uplift in the study area are very small and can be disregarded. Combining the calculated results of pressure reduction due to temperature reduction and brittle rupture pressure relief of mudstone, we define the OCR threshold time as the stratigraphic brittle rupture pressure relief time. Thus, the pressure reduction resulting from brittle rupture is calculated. Quantification was performed to determine the magnitude of these origins of underpressure and the contribution of these origins of pressure reduction to total pressure reduction since the Late Cretaceous period in 17 wells in the study area. In the dark mudstone of the Chang 71+2 submember, pressure reduction was due to temperature reduction, and brittle rupture pressure relief ranges from 3.10 to 3.99 MPa and 8.88 to 10.89 MPa, respectively. Their contributions to total pressure reduction since the Late Cretaceous period range from 23.33% to 30.43% and 69.57% to 76.67%, respectively (Figure ). In the black mudstone at the top of the Chang 73 submember, the pressure reduction due to temperature reduction and brittle rupture relief ranges from 3.10 to 3.99 MPa and 15.05 to 19.06 MPa, respectively. Their contributions to total pressure reduction since the Late Cretaceous period range from 16.27% to 18.88% and 81.12% to 83.73%, respectively (Figure ). Finally, pressure reduction due to temperature reduction and brittle rupture relief in the black mudstone at the bottom of the Chang 73 submember ranges from 3.13 to 4.02 MPa and 12.60 to 16.29 MPa, respectively. Their contributions to total pressure reduction since the Late Cretaceous period range from 18.61% to 22.48% and 77.52% to 81.39%, respectively (Figure ). In the sandstone of the Chang 7 member of the Yanchang Fm, pressure reduction due to elastic rebound and temperature reduction ranges from 11.75 to 15.84 MPa and 8.74 to 11.24 MPa, respectively. These contributions to the total pressure reduction occurring since the Late Cretaceous period range from 53.20% to 60.45% and 39.55% to 46.80%, respectively (Figure ).

5.3. Discussion of Overpressure Transfer-Induced Pressurization in the Sandstone along the Fractures from the Source Rocks

For the sandstone of the Chang 7 member of the study area, the current pressure coefficient is much smaller than the actual value considering only the pressure reduction caused by elastic rebound and temperature reduction since the Late Cretaceous. By analysis, overpressure transfer along the fractures from the source rocks causes the formation pressure to rise in the sandstone in the Chang 7 member of the study area during oil accumulation. The increased value of pore pressure in sandstone caused by overpressure transfer is the difference between the current measured formation pressure and the mentioned above formation pressure from numerical simulation analysis and the geological assessment. Through calculation, the total overpressure transfer makes the sandstone pressure increase by 2.54–6.42 MPa. According to the previous research, the Yanchang Fm in the southeast of the Ordos Basin experienced two large-scale oil accumulation periods: the middle and late Early Cretaceous and the late Late Cretaceous to the early Paleogene (Figure ). The excess pressure differences between the source and reservoir in the two periods are the main oil and gas migration driving force. Those values are 9–11 MPa and 18–20 MPa, respectively (Figure ). The channels of the first oil and gas migration were mainly microfractures produced during the early small-amplitude tectonic uplift. Since the Late Cretaceous, the study area has been subjected to a large-scale tectonic uplift, which caused the formation of a large number of faults and fractures. Otherwise, the brittle fracture caused by overpressure during this period also produced a large number of fractures. It can be inferred that the overpressure transfer in the late oil–gas accumulation period is stronger than that in the early oil–gas accumulation period. It is assumed that the overpressure transfer in the early accumulation period accounts for 1/4 of the total overpressure transfer in the sandstone. The overpressure transfer in the late accumulation period accounts for 3/4 of the total overpressure transfer in the sandstone. Based on this understanding, the evolution process of reservoir formation pressure is restored in this paper (Figure ).

5.4. Relationship between Abnormal Pressure Evolution and Oil Accumulation

Previous studies have concluded that buoyancy plays a limited role in oil and gas migration through tight sandstone reservoirs and that the processes governing fluid flow are primarily driven by excess pressure differences between the source rock and the reservoir rock. , At present, a large number of studies show that hydrocarbon generation and continuous oil and gas charging are the dominant factors controlling oil and gas migration and accumulation in low permeability tight oil and gas in the Chang 7 member of the Yanchang Fm in the Ordos Basin. ,,,, In this area, the evolution of the underpressure tight sandstone reservoir is divided into four phases based on the differential evolution characteristics of the abnormal pressure of different lithologic layers.

In the first phase (225–130 Ma), before the Early Cretaceous period, the reservoir had good physical properties, but the source rocks were immature. Subsequently, during the Early Cretaceous period, as the basin continued to settle substantially, the hydrocarbon source rocks of the Chang 73 submember began to mature, although some reservoir rocks of the Chang 7 member in the study area have become tight during this period. However, some reservoir rocks still retain good physical properties, ,, and the excess pressure difference between the source and reservoir has reached 2–5 MPa. So, buoyancy and excess pressure difference between the source rock and reservoir rock can act as the driving force of hydrocarbon migration in these reservoirs. Hydrocarbons migrated through prior fractures formed by minor tectonic uplift in the Late Triassic and Middle to Late Jurassic to reach the reservoir rocks under the effect of the two driving forces. There was also an excess pressure difference of 1.2–1.9 MPa between the dark mudstone caprock and the reservoir rock, and the caprock had good sealing ability, resulting in small-scale oil accumulation during this period (Figure a).

In the second phase (130–100 Ma), in the middle and late Early Cretaceous period, the physical properties of the reservoir rocks in the Chang 7 member became poor, ,, while the hydrocarbon source rocks of the Chang 7 member entered the maturity stage and produced a large amount of hydrocarbons. The two sets of black mudstones at the top and bottom of the Chang 73 submember formed an overpressure of 7.5–14.8 MPa due to disequilibrium compaction and hydrocarbon generation, resulting in an excess pressure difference of 9–11 MPa between the source rock and reservoir rock within the Chang 7 member. The oil and gas generated in large quantities collected in the tight reservoir rocks under the effect of the excess pressure difference between the source rock and reservoir rock along the residual path network , and preexisting fractures. There was also an excess pressure difference of 1.4–3.4 MPa between the dark mudstone caprock and the reservoir rock, and the caprock had favorable physical and pressure sealing conditions, resulting in large-scale oil accumulation (Figure b).

In the third phase (100–85 Ma), during the early Late Cretaceous period, the hydrocarbon generation of the Chang 7 source rocks stopped, and the strata began to uplift continuously. In this phase, the mudstone in the Chang 7 member did not change to strong brittleness and remained ductile. Pore pressure decreases occurred in both the Chang 7 member source rocks and reservoir rocks due to elastic rebound and temperature reduction caused by tectonic uplift. The over consolidation ratio (OCR) of the overpressured mudstone did not reach 2.6 during this stage; oil accumulation did not occur; and the excess pressure difference between the dark mudstone caprock and the reservoir was 2–5 MPa. The caprock remained intact and had good physical and pressure sealing conditions (Figure c).

In the fourth phase (85 Ma–present day), the black mudstone at the top of the Chang 73 submember reached the brittle fracture limit earliest during the middle and late Late Cretaceous period, allowing many microfractures to develop in the new channels for oil and gas migration. Oil began to charge into the reservoir under the effect of the excess pressure difference between the source and reservoir rock. During this period, the dark mudstone in the Chang 71+2 submember did not reach the brittle fracture limit, and an excess pressure difference of approximately 5–11 MPa could still be formed between the dark mudstone caprock and the reservoir rock; thus, the caprock had good physical and pressure sealing ability to prevent oil loss. As the formation continued to uplift, the black mudstone at the bottom of the Chang 73 submember also reached the brittle fracture limit and resulted in microfractures that allowed oil to enter the reservoir rock under the effect of the excess pressure difference between the source rock and the reservoir rock. During this period, the fractures formed in the early stage of the black mudstone at the top of the Chang 73 submember have been closed. Meanwhile, the dark mudstone in the Chang 71+2 submember still did not reach the brittle fracture limit. All of these mudstones can play a better sealing role. In the late stage of this phase, the dark mudstone in the Chang 71+2 submember began to develop microfractures. The pore pressure in the dark mudstone caprock rapidly changed to normal pressure, but the tight sandstone reservoir was still in an underpressure state. During this period, an excess pressure difference of approximately 5–11 MPa could still be formed between the dark mudstone caprock and the reservoir rock; thus, the caprock still had good physical and pressure sealing ability to prevent oil loss. During the whole oil and gas accumulation, the excess pressure difference between the different lithologic strata significantly contributes to tight sandstone oil migration and accumulation. So, abnormal pressure evolution controls oil and gas migration and accumulation and present-day hydrocarbon distribution characteristics.

6. Conclusion

The Early Cretaceous period overpressure occurring in the dark mudstones and black mudstones of the Chang 7 member in the study area results from a combination of hydrocarbon generation and disequilibrium compaction. These overpressures reached maximum values at the end of the Early Cretaceous period. The overpressure is primarily caused by hydrocarbon generation (45.72% to 67.80%) and disequilibrium compaction (32.20% to 54.08%) in the dark mudstone of the Chang 71+2 submember. Additionally, in the black mudstone at the Chang 73 submember, the overpressure is primarily caused by hydrocarbon generation (66.07% to 84.12%) and disequilibrium compaction (15.88% to 33.93%).

Since the Late Cretaceous period, elastic rebound, temperature reduction, and brittle rupture due to tectonic uplift have caused the overpressure of the Chang 7 member in the study area to become normal pressure or underpressure. This pressure decrease in the sandstone of the member is attributed to elastic rebound (53.2% to 60.5%) and temperature reduction (39.5% to 46.8%). Meanwhile, overpressure transfer makes the sandstone pressure increase to 2.54–6.42 MPa. The pressure reduction in dark mudstones in the Chang 71+2 submember is caused by temperature reduction (23.3% to 30.4%) and brittle rupture (69.6% to 76.7%). The pressure reduction in the black mudstone at the Chang 73 submember is due to temperature reduction (16.3% to 22.5%) and brittle rupture (77.5% to 83.7%).

The excess pressure difference between the hydrocarbon source rock and reservoir rock of the Chang 7 member of the study area is the primary driving force for hydrocarbon migration in the accumulation stages. During the Early Cretaceous period, buoyancy and the excess pressure difference facilitated hydrocarbon migration because the reservoir was not fully tightened. In the middle and late Early Cretaceous periods, the excess pressure difference prompted oil migration along the residual path network formed from early migration and preexisting fractures. The excess pressure difference reached 9–11 MPa. During the middle and late Late Cretaceous and the Paleogene period, a large number of fractures developed in the black mudstone and sandstone, which can be the oil and gas migration channels. Oil migrated into the reservoir along these fractures and residual path networks, under the influence of the excess pressure difference of 5–11 MPa. In addition, the excess pressure difference of approximately 3 MPa between the caprock and reservoir rock makes the caprocks have a good sealing capacity. So, during the whole oil and gas accumulation, the excess pressure difference between the different lithologic strata significantly contributes to oil migration and accumulation in the tight sandstone of the Chang 7 Member in the study area.
