Abstract

Immune cytopenias are a group of autoimmune disorders where patients develop autoantibodies against certain types of blood cells such as red blood cells (RBCs) or thrombocytes. We investigated small molecules as potential inhibitors of phagocytosis of blood cells that are prevalent in immune thrombocytopenia (ITP) and warm autoantibody immune hemolytic anemia (wAIHA). Upon screening a chemical library of over 13,000 compounds in silico, followed by evaluating 80 compounds in vitro as inhibitors of phagocytosis of opsonized RBCs by monocytes, we identified four hit molecules. These compounds contain a pyrazole moiety as a key structural feature. Here, we reveal the independent synthesis and re-evaluation of these hits, as well as revalidate the biological activities and the synthesis of their analogs to understand the structure–activity relationships. Two of the resynthesized compounds showed up to a 9-fold difference in their inhibitory activities between the commercial and synthesized batches, and the analogs exhibited either equal or weaker potency than the parent compounds targeting phagocytosis of RBCs. The role of regioisomers and the importance of an ester moiety are revealed as important structural features through these analogs. The pharmacokinetics of the promising compound 33 suggested that this compound shows significant efficacy in restoring platelet counts in the mouse model of ITP, despite the rapid hydrolysis of its methyl ester moiety.

Introduction

Immune cytopenias are allo- and autoimmune diseases defined by blood cell destruction in the spleen and liver. − A large category of immune cytopenias exists that target platelets, red blood cells (RBCs) and neutrophils, including (i) immune thrombocytopenia (ITP; phagocytosis of autoantibody-coated platelets); (ii) fetal and neonatal alloimmune thrombocytopenia (FNAIT; maternal alloantibodies crossing the placenta to platelet antigens on fetal or neonatal cells); (iii) warm autoantibody immune hemolytic anemia (wAIHA; phagocytosis of autoantibody-coated red blood cells); (iv) hemolytic transfusion reaction (HTR; phagocytosis of donor red blood cells due to preformed hemolytic alloantibodies to the donor red cell antigens); (v) delayed hemolytic transfusion reaction (DHTR; development of hemolytic alloantibodies following transfusion); (vi) hemolytic disease of the fetus and newborn (HDFN; maternal hemolytic antibodies crossing the placenta); and (vii) autoimmune neutropenia (AIN; associated with autoantibodies produced against neutrophils, mainly affecting children). −

ITP can be classified as primary or secondary and has an incidence of approximately 2–6 cases/100,000. Primary ITP, for an example, is characterized by an isolated thrombocytopenia (absolute platelet count <100 × 109/L) in the absence of any secondary causes. , An increased risk of bleeding poses the biggest clinical problem for primary ITP, however, bleeding symptoms may not always be present. , On the other hand, secondary ITP results from other manifestations, such as drugs, infections, or diseases. ,

Under normal circumstances, platelet production and clearance are highly controlled processes. Platelets are produced in the bone marrow courtesy of the hormone thrombopoietin (TPO), which is synthesized in the liver. , Mature platelets lose sialic acid from their surface and are physiologically cleared by the Ashwell–Morrell receptor on liver cells, with platelet clearance leading to production of new TPO. Additionally, apoptosis and phagocytosis are involved in the platelet clearance. Thrombocytopenia may result from reduced production or increased clearance/destruction of platelets and there are several proposed mechanisms for the increased platelet clearance portrayed in ITP. One proposed mechanism involves the presentation of platelet antigens to B cells that differentiate into antibody-secreting plasma cells and produce autoantibodies. Another mechanism for platelet clearance involves phagocytosis mediated by activating Fcγ receptors (FcγRI, FcγRIIA, FcγRIII). , Activated FcγRs activate Syk tyrosine kinase by downstream signaling pathways.

Another common immune cytopenia is autoimmune hemolytic anemias, having an incidence of approximately 1–2 cases/100,000, of which there are nine different conditions. The most important of these conditions is wAIHA. wAIHA is caused by an autoantibody to the patient’s autologous RBCs that is pan-reactive with all unrelated RBCs; thus, causing difficulties in finding compatible blood for transfusion into these individuals. It has been shown that hemolysis is due to the phagocytosis of antibody-coated RBCs by activating FcγRs. ,

The initial treatment of ITP or wAIHA may involve administration of corticosteroids (e.g., dexamethasone, prednisone) to increase platelet and RBC counts, , or immunoglobulins [e.g., intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG)] for ITP patients who are unresponsive to corticosteroids. , In Rh­(D)-positive patients, Rho­(D) immunoglobulin (anti-D) may be used as a substitute to IVIG before splenectomy, a second-line treatment. , The most agreed-upon mechanism proposes that IVIG and anti-D work by competitively inhibiting the FcγRs on the macrophages, reducing their ability to recognize the Fc segment on the autoantibody binding to the platelets or RBCs, therefore preventing phagocytosis.

Despite the above treatments, effective options for ITP or wAIHA, or the other immune cytopenia, are quite limited and costly, with poor response rates, high relapse rates, potential infusion reactions, adverse events, such as hemolysis or thrombosis, longer treatment durations, and poor tolerance. In addition to splenectomy for patients with persistent thrombocytopenia or hemolytic anemias, other second-line treatments include Rituximab (anti-CD20)a monoclonal antibody that targets B cells and may be given before and after splenectomy. , Other drugs include spleen tyrosine kinase (Syk) inhibitors, and thrombopoietin receptor agonists (TPO-RAs) which are specific for treatment of only ITP. The Syk inhibitor fostamatinib is a prodrug and is rapidly dephosphorylated to its active metabolite R-406 (Chart ) in the stomach. The mechanism of action of TPO-RAs such as avatrombopag and eltrombopag (Chart ) involves the stimulation of the TPO receptors on megakaryocytes and hematopoietic pluripotent stem cells, leading to increased platelet production.

Small molecule drugs that prevent the phagocytosis of blood cells such as platelets, RBCs will have a wider application for the treatment of multiple immune cytopenias. These molecules could be administered as the sole therapeutics or coadministered with other ITP treatment options. Small molecules that would block phagocytosis via a different mechanism than Syk inhibitors would provide a novel approach to the treatment of all immune cytopenias.

Recently we reported the in vitro evaluation of a library of 80 small molecules in an in vitro assay that mimics the phagocytosis of antibody-opsonized RBCs, that occurs in the majority of immune cytopenias. Four molecules, 9c, 19, 33, and 41 (Chart ) exhibited >69% inhibition of phagocytosis in a monocyte monolayer assay (MMA). ,

We herein report the resynthesis of commercial chemical library hits 9c, 19, 33, and 41 for validation, synthesis of novel analogs and in vitro activities in the MMA phagocytosis assays. A structure–activity relationship was derived based on targeted modifications and the pharmacokinetics of compound 33, the most promising compound are discussed in the context of efficacy in an ITP mouse model.

Results and Discussion

Chemistry

Synthesis of 9c and Analogs 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e

The retrosynthetic examination conducted on target hit compound 9c (Scheme A) indicated it could be simplified into compounds 5 and 8, which can be coupled via amidation to afford 9c. Intermediate 8 could be obtained via bromination of 7, formed from alkylation of commercially available 6. Simultaneously, 5 could be generated from the pyrazole reaction of hydrazone 4, obtained by a Japp–Klingeman reaction between aryl diazonium 2 and commercially available β-keto ester 3. However, the Japp–Klingeman reaction of 3 to produce 4 both failed using reported procedures. , Additionally, iodine-catalyzed reactions of phenylhydrazine and 3 (or ethyl-2-cyanoacetate) in DMSO to form 4 were also unsuccessful. This strategy was revised as illustrated in Scheme B, to synthesize 9c and analogs. Intermediate 4 was synthesized from 2-cyano ethyl acetate 3 and compound 2 according to a modified procedure. Compound 4 and chloroacetonitrile were refluxed in Et3N/EtOH to yield compound 5, but the reaction was incomplete. However, microwave irradiation in the presence of Et3N only led to the key intermediate 5 in moderate yields.

For the synthesis of the second key intermediate 8, N-1 alkylation of the pyrazole 6b was initially attempted. However, this reaction produced 7a in poor yields. Consequently, the methyl ester 6a was used as the starting material for alkylation and 7a was obtained in improved yields (Scheme B). Bromination of 7a with NBS under microwave irradiation gave the ester 7c in moderate yields, which was then hydrolyzed to obtain the 4-bromo derivative 8c.

In the last step to synthesize compound 9c, amidation of 5 and 8c was first performed under EDC/HOBt conditions, but the results were irreproducible with poor yields (<10%). Other unsuccessful amidations included reagents such as BCl3·(CH3)2S, BF3·O­(C2H5)2 and PPh3/COCl2. However, a modified amidation with TiCl4 improved the reproducibility and yields of 9c (Scheme B).

Following our earlier described alkylation and deprotection steps, intermediate pyrazoles 8a and 8b were synthesized from 6a or 6b, respectively, with moderate yields. Chlorination of 7a with NCS, followed by ester deprotection, gave 7d (Scheme B). On the other hand, fluorination of 7a using Selectfluor to produce 7e did not go to completion. During the reaction, it was a challenge to differentiate between 7a and 7e for monitoring the reaction progress, thus, after quenching the reaction, the mixture of 7a and 7e was carried forward to the next step without separation, and the mixture of 8a and 8e was separated.

Compound 9a was synthesized from 5 and 8a using EDC/HOBt conditions to determine if the size of bromo moiety in 8c influenced the yield of amidation. The synthesis of 9c was inconsistent through this path and was obtained in poor yields. Thus, subsequent amidations were performed with TiCl4 to afford analogs 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e (Scheme B).

Synthesis of Compounds 19 and 27

Compound 19 could be obtained by amidation of 17 using the commercially available sulfonyl chloride 18 (Scheme A). Pyrazole 16 could be formed from ethyl hydrazine 15 and dicarbonyl 14. Compound 14 can be obtained by the reduction of ester 13. Compound 13 can be produced via acylation of Boc-protected alkyl cuprate 11 and commercially available ethyl malonyl chloride 12. However, compound 13 could not be obtained from the alkyl cuprate 11. However, Compound 13 was obtained by reacting Boc-protected proline 21 with Meldrum’s acid, EDC, and DMAP, followed by refluxing in EtOH. Subsequent reduction of 13 to aldehyde 14 using DIBAL-H or LiAlH4, as well as the formation of pyrazole from 14 and 15 in an organic base or protic solvents did not yield compound 16 as conceived in Scheme A. Several other approaches were attempted to synthesize compound 16 unsuccessfully, including via pyrazole-5-pinnacol-boronic acid, Pd-catalyzed [(Pd­(PPh3)4, XPhosPdG3 or XPhosPdG4] Suzuki-Miyaura couplings, one-pot reactions with 4,4-diethoxybutan-1-amine using Lewis acids and ionic liquids, and lithiations at the 2-position of the protected pyrrolidine using LDA, n-BuLi, or sec-BuLi.

Then the synthetic strategy for compound 19 was modified and was synthesized according to Scheme B. Proline (20) was protected with t-butyloxycarbamoyl moiety (t-Boc) to yield 21, which was then subjected to EDC coupling conditions to give the Weinreb amide 22 in good yields. The reaction of compound 22 with the ethynyl trimethylsilane/ethylmagnesium bromide complex produced acetylenic ketone 23. Compound 23 represents a key intermediate as the next reaction determines the regioselectivity of 1,3- and 1,5-disubsituted pyrazoles formation (Scheme B).

Compound 23 can be reacted with hydrazine 15 to obtain compound 25 as the major product, if the 1,3-disubstituted pyrazole 25 is desired. However, if 23 is reacted with diethylamine, followed by condensation with 15, the 1,5-disubstituted pyrazole 16 would be the major product. Under acidic conditions, the regioselectivity of 1,3-disubstituted pyrazole is proposed to be governed by the increased competition for hydrazone formation through the 1,2-addition of the basic nitrogen of the hydrazine. On the other hand, the 1,5-disubstituted pyrazole 16 is preferentially formed in the absence of acid or at an elevated temperature via Michael addition followed by cyclization. Accordingly, 23 was reacted with diethylamine to form the trans-enamino ketone 24, and upon condensation with hydrazine 15, both the 1,5- and 1,3-disubstituted pyrazoles 16 and 25, respectively, were obtained.

The mixture of 16 and 25 was subjected to deprotection to obtain the regioisomeric mixture of 17 and 26. This mixture was subjected to amidation with the sulfonyl chloride 18, followed by HPLC separation, to obtain compounds 19 and 27 as pure compounds. These compounds provided the opportunity to evaluate the importance of 1,3- vs 1,5-regiochemistry for biological activity.

Synthesis of Compound 41

Compound 41 was synthesized in four steps (Scheme ). First, the Knorr-pyrazole synthesis of phenylhydrazine 28 and cyanoacetate 35 was conducted under microwave irradiation in toluene or EtOH to give 5-aminophenypyrazole 36. Then, following a Clauson-Kass reaction of 5-aminophenypyrazole 36 and the furan 37, compound 38 was obtained. Hydrolysis of the ethyl ester 38 provided the carboxylate 39. Subsequently, the final amidation step was performed with EDC/HOBt coupling of 39 and the thiazole 40 to obtain compound 41.

Structure–Activity Relationships

Within the dipyrazole compounds 9a–e, including the precursor 5, the resynthesized bromo-compound 9c displayed the best results with the lowest phagocytosis index (PI), highest percent inhibition of phagocytosis and lowest IC50 value of 17.7 ± 3.4 μM, which is at least 2-fold lower than the other compounds. On the other hand, the chloro- and fluoro- derivatives, 9d and 9e, respectively, showed poor activity in inhibiting phagocytosis of RBCs. Methyl derivative 9b gave the second-best result, leading to the suggestion that strong electron withdrawing or donating groups at this position have trivial effect on the activity. Compound 9c carrying the bromo moiety could act promiscuously to give the observed results, due to its potential chemical reactivity. Additional experiments are needed to support the potential reactivity of compound 9c. Generally, compounds with neutral or electron-donating groups in the 4-position of the pyrazole ring B (9a and 9b) gave better biological activity than compounds with electron-withdrawing groups (9d and 9e).

The pyrrolidine derivative 19, originally identified from the commercial chemical library, displayed a lower phagocytosis index, a more potent inhibition, and a lower IC50 of 25.5 ± 6.7 μM, compared to its regioisomer 27 which was inactive. This result may indicate that 1,5-disubstituted compound 19 is sterically more favorable than its 1,3-disubstituted regioisomer 27. Compound 19 carries a chiral center, but its racemic mixture was synthesized and tested at this stage. Synthesis of enantiomerically pure 19 may be a subject of future investigations. The pyrrole derivative 41 exhibited better activity than all other compounds except 9c and 33, with an IC50 value of 18.4 ± 8.5 μM. Of all the tested compounds, the thiophene derivative 33 demonstrated the highest inhibition with an IC50 of 12.4 ± 2.7 μM. This is approximately 7-fold more potent than its carboxylate analog 34. Since the only structural difference between the 33 and 34 is the presence of the methyl ester in 33, the presence of this group and its position may play a significant role in the inhibition of phagocytosis. However, further investigations are needed to corroborate this theory.

There are noteworthy differences between the in vitro results of the commercially acquired chemical library compounds and the resynthesized compounds in the laboratory. In vitro, the synthesized compounds displayed slightly less potent activities and can be partially attributed to the differences in the level of purity (or impurities). Full characterization of the commercial compounds was a challenge due to the small quantities of the compounds, and resynthesized compounds were completely characterized including purity. These differences highlight the importance of independently synthesizing and re-evaluating the compounds obtained from commercial chemical libraries. In the context of the in vitro assays, blood from ten different human donors was used in the phagocytosis assays, and it is also highly likely that the differences in the in vitro results are due to the donor–donor variability.

Despite the above differences, compounds 9c and 33 showed no toxicity, but good potency to inhibit in vitro FcγR-mediated phagocytosis. , However, 9c may act nonselectively due to the bromo-functionality and thus was considered unsuitable as a potential candidate for further investigations. Resynthesized compound 33 exhibited superiority in comparison with the other resynthesized compounds. Thus, the in vivo pharmacokinetics properties of compound 33 were determined (vide infra).

Efficacy of Compound 33

The mechanisms and potential therapies for human ITP are commonly investigated using mouse models, ,− particularly passive, antibody transfer models of ITP. ,− This model involves an antibody (clone MWReg30, rat IgG1:λ) that targets the platelet glycoprotein IIb, a self-antigen highly relevant to the human ITP. ,, Twenty four hours after the anti-CD41 injection, mice experience a significant decrease in platelet count due to immune system-mediated platelet destruction. Repeated administration of escalating doses of anti-CD41 antibody leads to prolonged thrombocytopenia that resembles chronic human ITP. , On the other hand, spontaneous platelet recovery also can occur in the absence of a dose-escalating regimen. , As a result of this antibody dose-escalation mode, a protracted platelet nadir is observed, likely by influencing the megakaryocyte compartment and compensatory platelet production as well as increasing the rate of platelet destruction. ,

In a recent report, compound 33 was investigated for its ability to ameliorate the experimental ITP in vivo in BALB/c mice by using the passive, rat antimouse CD41 antibody transfer. In comparison to untreated mice (CD41), compound 33 at a dose of 2.5 mg/kg in BALB/c mice resulted in an increased platelet count after the first day of treatment. Additionally, at 1 mg/kg of 33, a significant increase in platelet concentrations after the second day was observed.

Molecules such as compound 33 can be a potential treatment for several immune cytopenias because the in vitro phagocytosis assay uses antibody-opsonized RBCs, and in vivo testing shows the molecule is also highly effective at ameliorating ITP and in the recovery of platelet counts in an animal model.

Pharmacokinetics of 33

A single-dose pharmacokinetic analyses in healthy female and male BALB/c mice to determine the levels of compound 33 in the plasma were performed. The animals received a single dose of compound 33 (1 mg/kg) via IP injection and blood samples were collected at nine time points over 24 h (Figure ). However, the results obtained by LC–MS for both female and male mice indicated the rapid conversion of methyl ester 33 and the formation of ester hydrolysis product 34 in the blood. Compound 33 was not detected in blood/plasma and no other metabolites were identified in this experiment.

The exposure to 34 during 24 h (AUC(0–24)) was slightly higher in male mice (1.218 ± 0.1912 h*μg/mL), than that in females (0.9397 ± 0.1235 h*μg/mL). Differences in pharmacokinetics in males and females are quite interesting, and future studies need to explore this further.

Conclusions

Here, we describe the synthesis and subsequent in vitro evaluation of the four hit compounds and their analogs. The resynthesized hit molecules 9c, 19, 33, and 41 appear to be slightly less potent than the commercially acquired library molecules, buttressing the need to revalidate hit molecules and their purities when testing compounds. The analogs of these hit molecules were either equal or less potent as inhibitors of phagocytosis, compared to the resynthesized hit compounds. Generally, compounds with neutral or electron-donating groups in the 4-position of the pyrazole ring B (9a and 9b) gave better activity in vitro than compounds with electron-withdrawing groups (9d and 9e), among the dipyrazole derivatives. It is also observed that 1,3-substituion on the pyrazole moiety is not favored, but 1,5- and 1,3,5-substitutions are favored. Of all the compounds, 33 consistently demonstrated the best percent inhibition and dose response in in vitro phagocytosis assays, and good efficacy in in vivo mouse models of ITP.

Compounds 33 and 34 differ only by the presence of a methyl ester moiety, but there was approximately 7-fold difference in their in vitro activity. The presence of the methyl ester in 33 may be important, although further investigations are needed to corroborate this observation.

Experimental Section

Chemistry

General

All anhydrous reactions were performed under nitrogen atmosphere and anhydrous solvents were prepared following standard procedures. Compounds 1, 6a, 6b, 15, 18, 20, 29, 32, 35, 37, 40, reagents, and solvents were obtained from commercial sources. Reaction progress was monitored on thin layer chromatography (TLC) plates (Silica gel-60 F254). Chromatographic purifications were performed using silica gel (60 Å; 70–230 or 325–625 mesh) or Biotage flash chromatography system with Silicycle prepacked silica gel columns. Microwave irradiations were performed using a Biotage Initiator microwave (2.45 GHz, 400 W). Sonication was performed by a VWR or Fisher ultrasonic bath with 80W of ultrasonic power, 63W of heater power, and 40 kHz of operating frequency. NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker Avance spectrometer (400.27 MHz for 1H, 100.64 MHz for 13C and 376.62 MHz for 19F). Chemical shifts were reported in δ ppm using deuterated chloroform or methanol as a reference.

The purity of the compounds (two solvent systems; Supplementary data Table S1 and Figures S33–S44) was determined by tandem HPLC-PDA-MS equipped with a Waters 2545 binary gradient module, Waters PDA 2998 photodiode array detector (190–800 nm), and a Waters QDa mass spectrometer (200–1200 Da). HPLC was performed on an X-Bridge analytical C18 column (4.6 mm × 150 mm, 5 μm I.D.). Mass spectra were recorded using ESI (+ve) mode (200–800 Da). All HPLC solvents were HPLC grade. The purity methods used for analysis are described further below. All but three compounds evaluated in vitro are ≥95% pure by HPLC analysis. Compounds 9a and 9b are <95% pure in both solvent systems, while 9e is <95% pure in one solvent system (Method 2). Since these compounds did not display promising IC50 results and will not be evaluated further, no additional attempts at increasing the purity were made.

Phagocytosis Assay

The MMA to screen compounds for their ability to inhibit phagocytosis was performed as previously described. , Briefly, PBMCs were layered onto chamber slides, and monocytes were purified by adherence after 1 h at 37 °C with 5% CO2. Compounds were solubilized in 100% DMSO and then diluted in RPMI to the final concentration of 5 μM, as previously reported. Corresponding DMSO volumes in RPMI were used as a control. Final DMSO concentration used was ≤1% vol/vol. After 1 h, indicator Rh-positive red cells opsonized with anti-RhD were added to the chamber slides and then further incubated for 2 h before fixing and counting phagocytosed RBCs using phase-contrast microscopy. As a positive control for inhibition, IVIG, known to inhibit phagocytosis due to blocking of FcγRs, was used. The phagocytosis index (PI) was determined as the number of phagocytosed RBCs in 300 monocytes.

PI=#ofphagocyticRBCs300monocytes×100 1

Dose-Inhibitory Response and IC50

MMAs (as explained in the previous section) were repeated with different concentration of the compounds 5, 9a–e, 19, 27, 33, 34, and 41 including 100, 50, 25, 10, 5, 2.5, 1, and 0.5 μM with human PBMCs. The percent inhibition of phagocytosis was determined by comparing the PI in each treated sample to the PI in the untreated sample, which was determined by normalizing the PI concentration in each treated sample (phagocytosis of opsonized RBCs with vehicle only, representing 100% phagocytosis).

Pharmacokinetics of 33

All protocols involving animals were approved by the Institutional Animal Care Committee at University Health Network (UHN), Toronto, Canada (Animal Use Protocol # 7014). Seven weeks old male and female mice BALB/cJ (Stock# JAX:651) were purchased from Jackson Laboratories (Bar Harbor, Maine, USA). Animals were housed in the Animal Resource Center facility at UHN and maintained under regulated light and temperature conditions. The food and water were available ad libitum in each cage. Mice were housed for 1 week in groups of five per cage to acclimate to the facilities.

Pharmacokinetic analysis of 33 was conducted in healthy male and female mice (n = 27 per sex, 18–22 g in weight). Ten mM stock of the test compound was prepared in DMSO. Mice received 0.1 mL IP injection of 1 mg/kg 33 dissolved in 10% DMSO and PBS. The pH of the sample was adjusted to 6 with 0.1 N HCl to facilitate solubilization of 33. Blood was collected via cardiac puncture from animals anesthetized with inhaled isoflurane (induction: 5%, maintenance: 2.5%) at the following time points: 0 (predose), 5, 15, 30 min, 1, 2, 4, 6, and 24 h. Blood from three mice was collected at each time point. Samples were collected in k 2EDTA tubes (Sarstedt) to obtain plasma. Samples were spiked with internal standard, cannabidiol (CBD), at 25 μg/mL final concentration and centrifuged at room temperature for 3 min at 10,000 rpm to obtain plasma. The plasma was collected, stored in microcentrifuge tubes, and frozen at −80 °C. Frozen plasma samples (PK and standard curves) were lyophilized for 18 h at −55 °C and 0.700 mbar vacuum. Lyophilized powder was resuspended in MeOH containing 0.1% formic acid and centrifuged (5 min at 12,000 rpm) at room temperature to obtain clear supernatant that was mixed with equal volume of H2O (0.1% formic acid) and centrifuged again to remove any insoluble material (10 min at 12,000 rpm). The samples were injected into LC–MS to be analyzed for the presence of 33, 34 and other possible metabolites.

Samples were analyzed using Waters Acquity UPLC system equipped with Quaternary Solvent Manager, Waters MS 3100 mass spectrometer, Sample Manager FTN, Acquity UPLC BEH column (2.1 mm × 50 mm, C-18, 1.7 μm ID). The mobile phase for UPLC-MS analysis consisted of H2O (A) and MeOH (B), each with 0.1% formic acid. The gradient was programmed as follows: 0–4.5 min (50% A/50% B to 0% A/100% B), 4.5–5.0 min (0% A/100% B); 5.0–5.2 min (0% A/100% B to 50% A/50% B) and 5.2–6 min (50% A/50% B). The flow rate was 0.6 mL/min, and all samples were analyzed in triplicate. The mass scan (150–650 m/z), individual masses of 34 (410.5 m/z), 34 (396.5 m/z) and the internal standard (315.5 m/z) were recorded in positive and negative modes. The chromatograms were integrated using Empower 3 software. To generate standard curves, the AUC ratios (analyte/CBD) were plotted vs analyte concentration used to spike blood samples. The concentrations of the analyte were derived from the standard curve.
