Abstract

Herein, we report an investigation into the use of a scientific microwave reactor for the synthesis of chloro-boron subphthalocyanines (Cl-BsubPcs), its derivatives, and the π-extended alternative called chloro-boron subnaphthalocyanine (Cl–Cl n BsubNc). Reaction optimization screening is presented with the aid of a high-pressure liquid chromatography system equipped with a photodiode array detector (HPLC-PDA) and used for quantitative analysis to calculate the conversion of phthalonitriles and 2,3-dicyanonaphthalene to the Cl-BsubPcs and Cl–Cl n BsubNcs, respectively, by leveraging the characteristic UV–vis absorbance profiles of the Cl-BsubPcs and Cl–Cl n BsubNcs. The microwave reaction process was found to reduce the reaction time from multiple hours to 5 to 36 min, reducing the overall time needed for the synthesis of the desired compounds, and provided a streamlined process of optimizing the synthesis of peripherally and axially substituted Cl-BsubPc derivatives. With enhanced heating, difficult-to-synthesize Cl–Cl12BsubPc was found to achieve moderate conversion through multiple microwave irradiation cycles. With this streamlined process, the lack of ethereal cleavage of aryl–aryl ether moieties (i.e., p-tolyl group) attached to the periphery of a Cl-BsubPc was achieved and is also presented. Although random bay halogenation of Cl-BsubNc was not found to be suppressed under the reaction conditions in the microwave, we present the microwave synthesis of Cl–Cl n BsubNcs while tracking random bay halogenation production using HPLC-PDA and confirm their characterization via mass spectrometry.

Introduction

Chloro-boron subphthalocyanines (Cl-BsubPcs) are bowl-shaped lower homologues of phthalocyanines (Pcs), with a central tetracoordinated boron chelated to three nitrogens and one perpendicular axial group, most commonly chlorine (i.e., Cl-BsubPcs, Figure a). Cl-BsubPcs contain three isoindoline units that form a delocalized 14-π-electron system within their nonplanar conical chemical structure, resulting in an irregular aromatic carbon scaffold (Figure a). − BsubPcs have attracted increasing attention as active components in functional organic materials such as organic photovoltaics (OPVs), − organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs), , and organic field effect transistors (OFETs), due to their favorable optical and electronic properties. For applications, many other axial groups have been studied, including bromide, phenoxide, sulfide, alkoxide, , silyloxy, alkyl, alkynyl, − and phenyl − groups. Cl-BsubPcs are also routinely modified by introducing functional groups to their α- and β-periphery positions, which has allowed for tuning useful physical properties that have improved degrees of chromatographic separation of desired isomers, , and increased solubility , for ease of solution-casted fabrication of organic optoelectronic devices. Exotic BsubPc derivatives participate in advanced applications, including studies of nonlinear optical effects (NLOs), ,, organic semiconductors, organic photovoltaics (OPVs), − ,, photodynamic therapy (PDT), and novel inks and dyes. , A related compound called chloro-boron subnaphthalocyanine (i.e., Cl-BsubNc, Figure b) contains an extended aromatic system, and therefore has a red-shifted UV–vis absorption compared to Cl-BsubPcs and plays a crucial role in improved performance of organic photovoltaics (OPVs). ,−

However, to date, harsh reaction conditions are necessary to form the boron subphthalocyanine and subnaphthalocyanine structures, with longer reaction times sometimes being necessary if the Cl-BsubPc contains exotic peripheral substituents. The subnaphthalocyanines (i.e., BsubNcs, Figure b) also suffer the synthetic challenge of forming Cl–Cl n BsubNcs from random bay position halogenation to form a mixed alloyed composition, rather than pure Cl-BsubNc.

Nearly all previous syntheses of both Cl-BsubPcs and Cl-BsubNcs have been reported with standard solvent-based procedures using conductive heat transfer (i.e., a hot plate) and unreactive aromatic organic solvents with high boiling points (ca. 140–220 °C) necessary to form a BsubPc or a BsubNc. To date, the reaction pathway and mechanism of formation are not completely known, and therefore have somewhat limited the tuning of the methods of synthesis to that of harsh conductive heating. Even with harsh reaction conditions and with limited optimization, the yields are sometimes low for BsubPcs (i.e., <40%) and BsubNcs (i.e., <10%). With high temperatures and the presence of boron-based Lewis acids (i.e., BCl3, BBr3, or BF3), other processes have achieved higher/appropriate yields. Due to the outlined considerations, it is easily understood that little improvement has been made toward the method of preparation for these BsubPcs and BsubNcs, and therefore, for this study, we improved considerations toward their sustainable access.

Microwave-induced organic reaction enhancement (MORE) or microwave-assisted organic synthesis (MAOS) has been thoroughly reported in the literature for nearly all standard synthetic organic reactions, with multiple comprehensive reviews reported. − Utilizing dielectric heating, where the microwaves couple directly with dipoles and energy transfer occurs in nanoseconds (10–9 s), the microwave provides efficient energy input to reduce reaction times and facilitate chemical processes. , The microwave’s exact mechanism, whether local overheating or a specific microwave interaction with the chemical, remains disputed. − Regardless, microwave-assisted organic synthesis (MAOS) has been found to clearly expedite reactions, enhance yields, and allow for overall milder reaction conditions. With the necessity of very high reaction temperatures for the synthesis of Cl-BsubPcs and ClBsubNcs, improving the heating rates via microwave irradiation is an ideal scenario, along with the desire for improving the uniformity of heating reaction mixtures and the desired potential for local superheating of the solvent (Figure ). For this study, we have improved the rate of heating and tendency to superheat solvents within a reaction mixture, making microwave irradiation ideal for the synthesis of BsubPcs and BsubNcs, in comparison to reaction temperatures at >180 °C for prolonged reaction times (3–48 h).

To the best of our knowledge, there are only two brief reports of microwave syntheses of Cl-BsubPcs and Cl-BsubNcs, successfully demonstrating the use of similar unactivated aromatic organic solvents (i.e., 1-chloronaphthalene) in closed Teflon vessels, and using commercially available boron trichloride (BCl3) in p-xylene in traditional glassware. The former initial report demonstrated the ability to safely contain the corrosive and low-boiling BCl3 reagent in a Teflon vessel, a situation that causes concern for safety when extreme heating. Although BCl3 has a good solubility in organic solvents, its boiling point once dissolved in organic solutions is not known. The Teflon vessel ensured all low-boiling-point reagents (i.e., BCl3–, with a boiling point of 12.0–12.5 °C) were contained within the reaction vessel. The latter report showed that heating through microwave irradiation provided superior yields compared to conductive heating with the same reaction time, demonstrating the power of microwave synthesis. However, the initial reports displayed limited reaction condition screening (i.e., molar ratios, time of irradiation, power (W), repeated heating in cycles), limited characterization, no further investigation into the scale-up of the reaction, and minimal discussion of the initial results. To date, there have not been following reports of microwave syntheses of Cl-BsubPcs or Cl-BsubNcs. Thus, it was our interest to further investigate the microwave synthesis of a family of ClBsubPc and Cl-BsubNc macrocycles via microwave irradiation and create a facile protocol for our future studies and the synthesis of desired macrocycles.

For this study, we report an expanded investigation into the use of a scientific microwave reactor for the rapid synthesis of previously reported Cl-BsubPc and its derivatives using common borosilicate pressure vessels. Reaction optimization screening is presented, with standard variables chosen and investigated via a Design of Experiment (DOE) approach. With the aid of a high-pressure liquid chromatography system equipped with a photodiode array detector (HPLC-PDA), the quantitative analyses for the conversion of phthalonitrile or 2,3-dicyanonaphthalene (2,3-DCN) to Cl-BsubPc or Cl-BsubsNc, respectively, are presented, leveraging the characteristic UV–vis absorbance profiles of the Cl-BsubPcs and Cl-BsubNc. The microwave reaction process was found to reduce the reaction time to 5–36 min, increasing the throughput for the synthesis of the desired compounds compared to standard times, and provides a streamlined process of optimizing the synthesis of desired Cl-BsubPc and Cl-BsubNc derivatives. With enhanced heating and experience with a past synthesis process, the difficult-to-synthesize Cl–Cl12BsubPc was also investigated, but achieved only a moderate conversion through multiple microwave irradiation cycles. With this streamlined process, the lack of ethereal cleavage of aryl–aryl ether moieties (i.e., p-tolyl group) appended to the periphery of a Cl-BsubPc was also investigated and was achieved. We also present a detailed report on the synthesis of Cl–Cl n BsubNcs with random bay halogenation produced via microwave irradiation.

Results and Discussion

Reaction Design and Optimization

For this study, standard variables concerning the organic reaction of BsubPcs and BsubNcs were considered, along with the variables related to the new microwave reactor. First, the molar ratio between the phthalonitrile derivatives and the boron trichloride (BCl3) source was considered due to its suspected impact on conversion. Second, the time of microwave irradiation, power of the microwave (i.e., Watts, W), solvent volume (i.e., concentration), and repetitive heating in cycles (i.e., cycles) with cooling periods were also considered. The loss tangent (tanδ) of the solvent (Table ) was also considered and is defined as the ability of a material or solvent to convert microwaves into heat at a given frequency and temperature, and generally, a solvent with a high tanδ is required in MAOS for efficient heating. Therefore, 1,2-dichlorobenzene (1,2-DCB) was the only aromatic organic solvent used for this study as an ideal solvent with a high loss tangent, affording high reaction temperatures (boiling point of 180.5 °C) in a minimal amount of time for an efficient reaction investigation. Even with high-boiling-point solvents, multiple heating cycles were also included in the set of variables. The BCl3 was dissolved in p-xylene, and so p-xylene was also present as a solvent. For upcoming studies, we will consider other common organic solvents versus 1,2-DCB, such as mesitylene, p-cymene, bromobenzene, and toluene, to investigate differentiations.

With the absence of a rigorously supported multistep mechanism for the formation of ClBsubPcs, there was an unknown understanding of how each variable would impact Cl-BsubPc or Cl-BsubNc formation for each derivative. ,, Therefore, all reaction conditions were ultimately tested within the planned conditions during screening, leading to optimized conversions detected by HPLC-PDA (Figure ). HPLC-PDA analysis of reaction mixtures containing Cl-BsubPcs and Cl-BsubNcs was simple, since the starting material (i.e., phthalonitrile, 2,3-dicyanonaphthalene) and desired product (i.e., Cl-BsubPcs, Cl-BsubNcs) exhibit excellent separation on a standard C18column when using an isocratic reversed-phase solvent system of 4:1/acetonitrile:N,N-dimethylformamide (i.e., 4:1/ACN:DMF). Second, both compounds exhibit very different characteristic UV–vis spectra, making both the separation and identification of the two pertinent entities in a reaction mixture facile. Aliquots for HPLC-PDA analysis were routinely taken from the final reaction mixtures (Figure ).

Cl-BsubPc Reaction Optimization and Discussion

Cl-BsubPc was used as the model substrate to begin the investigation, with both the phthalonitrile and Cl-BsubPc (λabs = 564 nm; 4:1/acetonitrile:N,N-dimethylformamide) being well understood to have characteristic UV–vis absorbance and retention times greater than 1 min apart on our HPLC system, to allow for facile characterization within an organic reaction mixture (Figure ). We found that no conversion occurred with 7.50 equiv of BCl3 in 2.0 mL of 1,2-DCB for 2.00 min at 250 W of microwave irradiation power (Table , entry 8). Neither high BCl3 mass loading (Table , entry 7 or entry 3) or high power for microwave irradiation (Table , entry 4 or entry 5) provided an improved conversion seen by HPLC-PDA, displaying the challenges with understanding the importance of energy input for the reaction, and positive or negative effect of BCl3 loading. Although a slight plateau in conversion around 25% was initially achieved (Table , entries 3–6), an optimal conversion of 67% was found to occur with mild conditions, employing a 5.00 min reaction time at a lowered microwave power of 250 W (Table , entry 1). The concentration for the reaction proved to be most important for the reaction to occur at higher conversion, with the optimal run occurring with 1.00 mmol of phthalonitrile in only 2.0 mL of solvent.

1,2-Dichlorbenzene’s (1,2-DCB) large loss tangent aided in rapid solvent heating, while the solvent did not undergo any detectable reaction with the Cl-BsubPc product or BCl3 to form any unwanted BsubPc side products. To our delight, the chromatogram of the product mixture for the optimal conditions for Cl-BsubPc (Rt = 3.850 min) showed a reduced amount of unknown impurities eluting just before (approximately Rt = 3.700 min) and just after (approximately Rt = 3.900 min) the ClBsubPc peak (Figure ). However, when only a low conversion was achieved, multiple organic impurities were detected, possibly as unwanted polymerization products, intermediates, or foreign cyclization products; however, the impurities have minimal mass. This demonstrates the complexity of forming 8 new bonds across four components (3 phthalonitrile equiv, 1 boron equiv) to form the Cl-BsubPc.

Only after a methanol Soxhlet purification was it found that two other BsubPcs were present at 10 and 1% relative concentration, which we had never previously observed when Cl-BsubPc was synthesized following previously reported procedures using standard glassware and a hot plate (ESIFigure S2, see Supporting Information). , As both the pressure vessel and microwave irradiation are new factors in the reaction setup, the derivation of the BsubPc impurities is unknown; however, it is suspected that these changes could have caused random chlorination or underwent Friedel–Crafts-type or radical-type chemistry may be between the p-xylene solvent and the Cl-BsubPc. Attempts to detect the impurities via mass spectrometry were not successful. These unknown BsubPc-type impurities were special to Cl-BsubPc and were not detected for the other derivatives outlined in the following discussions. After low-pressure train sublimation, the impurities were removed and pure Cl-BsubPc was collected (ESI Figure S17, see Supporting Information). Second, we were glad to observe that the closed reaction flask led to the complete absence of hydrolyzed BsubPc byproduct (i.e., HO-BsubPc), which can also lead to an undesired condensation product (i.e., μ-oxo-(BsubPc)2) on HO-BsubPc reacting with Cl-BsubPc. These two impurities are common through past synthetic procedures and can be challenging to remove from pure Cl-BsubPc, often leading to a lower purity of Cl-BsubPc that drastically hinders its performance in applications, such as OPVs. The HO-BsubPc is formed if the Cl-BsubPc is exposed to atmospheric air if it has high humidity, which we have observed to occur during summer time but not winter time. With this achievement, we hope future studies can more easily and consistently achieve ultrahigh purity (>99.9%) of Cl-BsubPc free of hydrolysis and condensation impurities.

Cl-βCl6BsubPc and Cl–Cl12BsubPc Reaction Optimizations and Discussion

Both Cl-βCl6BsubPc and Cl–Cl12BsubPc are important derivatives as they exhibit deep energy levels for superior stability when pairing with pentacene in an OPV due to triplet harvesting. ClβCl6BsubPc was investigated following five reactions with varying process conditions (Table ). Overall, each reaction gradually improved in conversion to Cl-βCl6BsubPc, and the characteristic UV–vis absorbance profile and retention time of Cl-βCl6BsubPc made for a simple separation and detection (Figure ). The first reaction using standard reaction conditions with a shorter 2.00 min reaction time provided an 11% conversion (Table , entry 5), which quickly improved to 37% conversion with a 5.00 min microwave irradiation time (Table , entry 4). Increasing the reaction concentration and increasing the BCl3 equivalents both led to an increase in conversion, while maintaining one heating cycle at 400 W or less (Table , entries 3 and 2). Ultimately, a lower molar ratio of BCl3 (i.e., 3.00 equiv) was optimal, with 5.0 mL of solvent and 400 W, which provided an optimal conversion of 72% to the Cl-βCl6BsubPc (Table , entry 1).

C 1-Cl-β­(tBu)3BsubPc and C 3-Cl-β­(tBu)3BsubPc Reaction Optimizations and Discussion

As Cl-BsubPcs provide inherently challenging physical properties such as low solubility in a variety of organic solvents, the isomers C 1-Cl-β­( t Bu)3BsubPc and C 3-Cl-β­( t Bu)3BsubPc (Figure ) are well-known within the field as derivatives with improved solubility due to the tert-butyl moieties. The desire for such soluble derivatives in organic electronics results from the need for the large-scale production of flexible electronics through layer-to-layer printing. Since the C 1 and C 3 isomers of Cl-β­( t Bu)3BsubPc are challenging to separate via traditional chromatography, HPLC-PDA data were collected as a mixture of the C 1 and C 3 isomers in the reaction mixture, providing two maxima at a retention time of ca. 10.283 min on the HPLC system. Due to the separation on the HPLC system and characteristic photophysical absorbance, analysis of the relative ratios of product to starting material, as well as the crude ratio of C 1–C 3 isomer, was confirmed.

Outside of the microwave, using traditional conductive heating and glassware to form Cl-β­( t Bu)3BsubPc is known to provide a high conversion through harsh heating in the presence of a large excess of boron trichloride. , Onto the microwave, (Table and Figure ), reaction conditions only using 2.00 min of microwave irradiation (Table , entries 6–9) provided poor conversion. Even with 5.00 min of heating, conversion was poor, with a low molar ratio of BCl3 (Table , entry 5). As both time and microwave power increased (Table , entry 4), an immediate increase in the conversion was achieved. Finally, a conversion of 87% was achieved with the highest molar ratio of BCl3 in the most concentrated reaction mixture, when the highest power for the longest allotted amount of time was chosen (Table , entry 1). The result is in strong agreement with previous findings that Cl-β­( t Bu)3BsubPc is known to provide high conversion with harsher reaction conditions, although the need for multiple heating cycles was not needed. The reaction mixture was purified via silica gel chromatography.

C 1-Cl-β­(NO2)3BsubPc and C 3-Cl-β­(NO2)3BsubPc Reaction Optimizations and Discussion

The synthesis, isolation, separation, and separate characterization of the C 1-Cl-β­(NO2)3BsubPc and C 3-Cl-β­(NO2)3BsubPc isomers have been reported. , Similar to the mixture of isomers of Cl β­( t Bu)3BsubPc, the reaction mixture was characterized as a mixture of isomers using HPLC-PDA. Although separable by silica gel chromatography, the C 1-Cl-β­(NO2)3BsubPc and C 3-Cl-β­(NO2)3BsubPc isomers were detected as a symmetric single eluted signal on our reverse-phase HPLC-PDA system and required a solvent system of 100% acetonitrile (isocratic) to afford efficient separation between the 4-nitrophthalonitrile starting material and the Cl-β­(NO2)3BsubPc product. The isolated product was eventually separated via silica gel chromatography, as aligned with past reports.

Unexpectedly, the combination of the four main reaction variables (i.e., molar ratio, solvent volume, time, power) did not provide a satisfactory conversion (Table , entries 3–6), even with multiple reports for the ease of synthesis of the Cl-β­(NO2)3BsubPc isomers. , Once the reaction was cycled 3 times for a maximum amount of time (3 × 5.00 min), an immediate increase in conversion to 29% was observed with only 3.00 equiv of BCl3 being used (Table , entry 2). The optimal reaction with a relatively clean reaction mixture (Figure , entry 1), when analyzed by HPLC-PDA, and a conversion of 79% was achieved with an increased loading of BCl3 and three microwave cycles (Table , entry 1). The addition of an electron-withdrawing nitro group on 4-nitrophthalonitrile slowed down the reaction, showing the importance of the balance between the electrophilicity and nucleophilicity of the cyano functional groups in the starting material. The balance between a cyano group’s N­(sp)-nucleophilic and C­(sp)-electrophilic functionality once again provides a challenge to the understanding of the reaction for the C­(sp2)–N­(sp2) bond formation during the mechanism of BsubPc formation. In addition, the Lewis acidity and Lewis basicity balance between the phthalonitrile’s cyano group and the Lewis acid (i.e., BCl3), is another relationship that may lead to this reduction in reaction rate.

Lack of Ethereal Cleavage: C 1/C 3-Cl-β­(p-Tolyloxy)3BsubPc Reaction Optimization and Axial Substitution

With a rigid carbon scaffold, Cl-BsubPcs typically have low solubility (i.e., <0.1 mg/mL) in organic solvents, and so the addition of solubilizing groups has been a major goal in order to advance organic optoelectronic device solution-casted fabrication , and medical applications such as photodynamic therapy. ,, However, with harsh reaction conditions needed to achieve significant cyclotrimerization, the compatibility with solubilizing functional groups on the phthalonitrile tends to be scarce, ,− with mainly thioethers being reported. − Few recent reports provide access to aryl–aryl ether linkages on the periphery of BsubPcs for the purposes of improved solubility. We envisioned that the short microwave irradiation reaction times would allow for rapid investigation into the ethereal cleavage of aryl–aryl ethers on the BsubPc scaffold while providing a facile method for reaction optimization, as our HPLC-PDA method had been established.

First, a phthalonitrile functionalized with a p-tolyloxy group in the 4-position was synthesized via SNAr chemistry (Scheme ), starting with 4-nitrophthalonitrile. The 4-(p-tolyloxy)­phthalonitrile intermediate was then tested for aryl–aryl ether cleavage in the microwave during BsubPc formation. As the optimization of the microwave method was expected to be facile and afford a clean reaction mixture, axial substitution of the boron-chloride bond with a phenoxide functional group was also achieved as a synthetic model to access both a peripherally and axially substituted BsubPc derivative, with our past studies of axially substituted BsubPcs.

The surprisingly high reactivity of the 4­(p-tolyloxy)­phthalonitrile starting material was met with an initial conversion of 19%, minimal detection of side products, and no detection of ethereally cleaved-BsubPc within the 500–700 nm range via the PDA detector (Table , entry 7). The encouraging results that showed a lack of ethereal cleavage prompted us to increase the reaction time and power (Table , entry 6), and then separately increase the BCl3 loading (Table , entry 5). As the BCl3 loading, power, and time increased, it became apparent that the reactivity of the soluble phthalonitrile intermediate was a model substrate. High conversion (i.e., >90%) was realized throughout optimization (Table , entries 2–4). The compromise between a power of 500 W for a longer irradiation time of 7.00 min was optimal for a 97% conversion (Table , entry 1), ultimately providing an extremely clean product mixture for reliable HPLC-PDA analysis (Figure , entry 1).

Cl-BsubNc/Cl–Cl n BsubNc Microwave Reaction Investigation

A major challenge in the organic electronic device space includes the facile and scalable synthesis of Cl-BsubNc (i) to improve the yield and (ii) to avoid or control random bay halogenation (i.e., Cl–Cl n BsubNcs; n = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6). When synthesized in the presence of boron trihalides (BCl3 and BBr3) using standard conductive heating and traditional glassware, Cl-BsubNc or Br-BsubNc has been found to form a mixed alloy composition of Cl–Cl n BsubNcs and Br–Br n BsubNc. For Cl–Cl n BsubNcs, it was also found that commercially available Cl-BsubNc is also more accurately described as Cl–Cl n BsubNcs, with a complex mixture of randomly chlorinated BsubNcs, rather than a pure single chemical compound. Across both chemical vendors and traditional synthesis, the issue resides in the challenge of efficient separation during purification. , To the best of our knowledge, only our previously reported method has successfully eliminated the random halogenation to access pure phenoxylated BsubNc, free of random bay halogenation, via an alternative boron source and also provides a different axial substituent.

Manipulating reaction parameters can potentially alter the degree of bay position chlorination, varying the composition of each polychlorinated compound in the mixed alloy. However, we are not aware of any reports that have achieved pure Cl-BsubNc due to the outlined challenges. Therefore, we aimed to leverage the microwave procedure to rapidly screen reaction conditions that reduced bay halogenation and successfully overcome the long-standing challenge of the synthesis of pure Cl-BsubNc, free of any Cl–Cl n BsubNc side products/mixed alloys, to avoid challenging separations during purification. Using the microwave, we also expected to investigate the effect a closed vessel would have on random bay halogenation of the BsubNcs.

The synthesis of BsubNcs was performed with the microwaving of 2,3-dicyanonaphthalene (2,3-DCN) in the presence of a boron source (i.e., BCl3) within an aromatic solvent (Figure ). The reaction optimization did not follow a standard design of experiment (DOE). Given that DOE is most effective when investigating an existing process where optimization can be performed through the establishment of parameter significance, the absence of a high-yielding process for the formation of BsubNc did not deem this to be the best avenue to explore. The strategy employed for improving the synthesis of BsubNcs using the microwave process was to leverage our experience with the highest reported literature method and iteratively alter parameters according to the observed data that was rapidly acquired via the HPLC-PDA analytics.

The investigation started with screening multiple reaction conditions that used one heating cycle. However, no conversion was ever observed until two microwave irradiation cycles were employed to mimic the harsh heating conditions used to form Cl–Cl n BsubNcs (ESI Table S4, see Supporting Information). From this experience, we decided to start the second round of optimization of experiments with a minimum of two heating cycles, for an overall time of 10 min, to provide sufficient time for the formation of the macrocycle, and achieved a 24% conversion to the group of Cl–Cl n BsubNcs detected via HPLC-PDA (Table , entry 9). Adding one extra heating cycle for a total reaction time of 15.00 min did not significantly improve conversion (Table , entry 8), so three equivalents of BCl3 were added, providing a lower conversion (Table , entry 7). Small changes to the microwave irradiation power and reaction concentration did not provide major improvement (Table , entries 5 and 6) until 350 W was used for three heating cycles of 5.00 min at a 0.09 M reaction concentration to achieve a conversion of 49% (Table , entry 4). The conversion did not improve with an increase of power to 400 W (Table , entry 3), and so the variable that proved to be most important was the number of heating cycles, at a modest 350 W. Therefore, the BCl3 loading was first appropriately chosen to be one equivalent relative to the 2,3-dicyanonaphthalene, and then heated for a total of six times, for 6.00 min each time (Table , entry 2). We found that the reaction (Table , entry 2) that only used one equivalent of BCl3, but was heated six times, gave a similar conversion as the reaction (Table , entry 3) that was only heated three times, but required three equivalents of BCl3. Therefore, we coupled both the larger number of heating cycles (i.e., six), with the larger loading of BCl3 (i.e., 3.00 equiv), and accomplished a 76% conversion to the desired Cl–Cl n BsubNc macrocycles (Table , entry 1).

Conclusion

We have successfully extended the investigation of using microwave irradiation to synthesize BsubPcs and optimized the synthesis of chloro-boron subphthalocyanine (Cl-BsubPc) and its derivatives, starting from commercially available substrates such as phthalonitrile, 4,5-dichlorophthalonitrile, 3,4,5,6-tetrachlorophthalonitrile, 4-tert-butylphthalonitrile, and 4-nitrophthalonitrile; and the chloro-boron subnaphthalocyanines (Cl-BsubNcs) starting from 2,3-dicyanonaphthalene. The method employed a closed reaction pressure vessel to easily and safely contain BCl3 and reduce the reaction times from 3 to 24 h to 36 min, which is crucial to the formation of BsubPcs and BsubNcs. Microwave irradiation reduced the reaction times from 3 to 24 h to under 36 min. The successful formation of our target compounds was rapidly confirmed through high-pressure liquid chromatography (HPLC) and a photodiode array (PDA) detector to confirm the unique UV–vis absorbance spectra of the BsubPc and BsubNc compounds. A 67% conversion to Cl-BsubPc was achieved free of hydrolyzed impurity (i.e., HO-BsubPc), while Cl-βl6BsubPc was achieved in an optimized conversion of 72%. The challenging synthesis of Cl–Cl12BsubPc required three heating cycles to ultimately provide a modest conversion of 55%, while both Cl-β­( t Bu)3BsubPC and Cl-β­(NO2)3BsubPc provided 79 and 87% conversions, respectively. In particular, the microwave reactions of Cl-BsubPc, Cl-βCl6BsubPc, Cl–Cl12BsubPc, Cl-β­(NO2)3BsubPc, and Cl-β­( t Bu)3BsubPc were scaled to the 10.00 mmol scale with retention or improvement of their optimized conversion, with Cl-β­( t Bu)3BsubPc achieving nearly quantitative conversion. The facile microwave process allowed for the rapid screening and optimization of Cl-β­(p-tolyloxy)3BsubPc with near quantitative conversion; this was free of any detection of ethereal cleavage/lack of ethereal cleavage. The microwave procedure was ultimately integrated into a complete synthetic pathway from 4-nitrophthalonitrile to a fully peripherally and axially substituted BsubPc with aryl–aryl ether moieties. Finally, even though bay halogenation could not be suppressed for the synthesis of pure Cl-BsubNc, the method of iterative alteration of reaction conditions was successful in achieving a 76% conversion to Cl–Cl n BsubNcs with three major Cl–Cl n BsubNcs (i.e., Cl-BsubNc, Cl-ClBsubNc, and Cl–Cl2BsubNc) formed in the closed pressure vessel under microwave conditions. We believe these results will provide motivation for studying other common organic solvents with high loss tangents to be used for the synthesis of aromatic macrocycles. We hope the overall microwave process will act as a platform for future synthetic pathways to have peripherally substituted derivatives free of ethereal cleavage for highly organic soluble derivatives for inks, dyes, and solution-casted applications. Finally, we wish these findings to allow for the synthesis of purer aromatic macrocycles used within organic electronics for improved performance in the field of advanced functional materials.

Experimental Section

General Procedure to the Microwave Synthesis of Cl-BsubPc and Cl-BsubNc Derivatives for HPLC Analysis

1,2-Dicyanobenzene (i.e., phthalonitrile) or a derivative (1.00 mmol, 3.00 equiv) was added to the indicated amount of 1,2-dichlorobenzene in a 65 mL borosilicate round-bottom pressure vessel and purged under argon. Next, the indicated amount of boron trichloride (BCl3, 1.0 M p-xylene) was added via a dropwise addition to the reaction mixture. After complete addition, the vessel was closed under argon and sonicated at room temperature for 2 min, placed in a microwave oven, and irradiated for the indicated time and power. After irradiation, the container was removed from the oven and cooled to room temperature. If heated for multiple cycles, the reaction vessel was allowed to cool for 20 min, then was re-irradiated for the indicated time and power as specified in the Tables above. Then, once completed, an aliquot (0.1 mL) of the resulting purple crude solution was diluted in 1 mL of acetonitrile:N,N-dimethylformamide (4:1) solution, sonicated for 15 min, and analyzed via HPLC-PDA without further alteration.
