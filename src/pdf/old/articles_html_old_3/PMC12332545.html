Sustainable Synthesis of 2,4,6-Trisubstituted Pyridines Using Surface-Modified PET@UiO-66 Vials

Abstract

For the first time, we report the fabrication of a PET@UiO-66 vial as an efficient and robust catalyst for the sustainable synthesis of 2,4,6-trisubstituted pyridines using a multicomponent reaction. The structure and morphology of the fabricated PET@UiO-66 vial were characterized by using PXRD, FTIR, optical microscopy, and FESEM analysis. Nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherm analysis revealed a specific surface area (SSA) of 564.359 m2/g, a maximum pore volume of 0.06 cm3/g, and a pore diameter of 3.05 nm. Optimization studies of reaction conditions for the synthesis of 2,4,6-trisubstituted pyridines showed that TBHP as an oxidant in THF at 60 °C afforded good to excellent yields. The main advantage of this protocol is the stability of surface-modified vials, reusability, and operational simplicity.

Introduction

Pyridine derivatives constitute a pivotal class of nitrogen-containing heterocyclic compounds that hold significant importance across diverse disciplines, notably in pharmaceuticals, − food industries, agrochemicals, − homogeneous catalysis, electronics, photosensitizers, and fluorescent chemosensors. − Typically, pyridines are synthesized from acetophenones, benzaldehydes, and ammonium acetate via multicomponent reactions (MCRs), employing a wide range of catalysts, including acids, bases, metal-based catalysts, clay, organo-catalysts, heterogeneous catalysts, ionic liquids, zeolite, mesoporous organic polymers, metal–organic frameworks, photoredox catalysts, and silica-supported catalysts. − Although existing methods for preparing these compounds are valuable, they often suffer from limitations such as use of toxic solvents, nonreusable catalysts, harsh reaction conditions, low yields, poor selectivity, lengthy multistep procedures, and expensive catalysts. To address these challenges, this paper presents a new, efficient, and economical approach for synthesizing 2,4,6-triphenylpyridine derivatives, offering a viable alternative to current methods. Some of the reported heterogeneous catalysts for the synthesis of 2,4,6-triarylpyridines include CoFe2O4@Eggshell@SO3H and activated Fuller’s earth, which are recyclable and noncorrosive. Other nontoxic and green heterogeneous catalysts used are zinc ferrite, iron metal organic framework, l-proline functionalized magnetic nanoparticles, aluminum phosphate, cerium­(IV) carboxymethyl-cellulose, and zinc phosphate. Additionally, Merrifield resin-supported quinone, silica vanadic acid, and silica gel-supported polyphosphorics play a crucial role in the synthesis of 2,4,6-triphenylpyridines. It is worth mentioning that conventional methods often employ acid or base catalysts at high temperatures and involve complicated purification processes. As represented in Figure , substituted pyridines can be synthesized via diverse reaction pathways, utilizing various starting materials. − ,− However, the use of heterogeneous catalysts, particularly metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), offers significant advantages over other catalysts due to their low cost ease of separation and wide range of applications in various reactions, including multicomponent, click, addition, and free radical reactions. These advantages stem from their high surface area, tunable porosity, and ability to confine reactions within their porous structures.

The confined media provided by MOFs facilitate reactions within restricted environments, where reactants and products are spatially constrained or encapsulated within a defined space or medium. − Among the various MOFs, UiO-66, a metal–organic framework derived from zirconium and terephthalic acid, exhibits versatile properties, including mechanical, thermal, and chemical stability. Recently, functionalized and doped UiO-66 has been widely used as catalysts in organic transformation; however, its application in the multicomponent reactions has been less extensively studied. In recent years, immobilized MOFs have gained prominence due to environmental and economic concerns. In the present study, for the first time, we have modified a PET vial to create an inner surface-modified PET@UiO-66 system and utilized it for the convenient and sustainable synthesis of 2,4,6-trisubstituted pyridines, in alignment with the principles of green chemistry.

Results and Discussion

Our initial efforts began with the preparation of UiO-66-modified PET vials for chemical transformations. Treatment of PET vials with a 7 M nitric acid solution resulted in surface-hydrolyzed PET vials, which were subsequently treated with ZrCl4 and 1,4-benzenedicarboxylic acid (BDC) in the presence of glacial acetic acid and aq. HCl for about 7 days at 100 °C to furnish the surface-modified PET@UiO-66 vial. It is worth mentioning that both the inner and outer surfaces of the vial were modified to UiO-66, and the vial became brittle. In order to achieve the fabrication of the inner surface UiO-66 modified vial, the outer surface was tightly covered with Teflon, and partial hydrolysis was achieved upon the treatment with 7 M nitric acid, followed by the treatment with ZrCl4 and BDC (Figure ).

After the successful fabrication of PET@UiO-66 vials, the formation and structure of the UiO-66 MOF are were characterized by using powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) and Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy. The morphology and porosity of the PET@UiO-66 vial were examined using field emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM), polarizing optical microscopy (POM), and Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area analysis.

To confirm the formation of UiO-66 in the vial, the inner surface was scratched off, and the PXRD pattern of the resulting material was compared with the PXRD patterns of hydrolyzed PET and PET@UiO-66. The powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) pattern of the powder obtained from the scratched surface of the PET vials confirms the formation of the PET@UiO-66 MOF, as evidenced by the inclusion of the zirconium metal and the appearance of characteristic peaks at 2θ = 7.3°, 8.4°,14.7°,17.0°, 25.7°, and 30.6° corresponding to the (111), (200), (222), (400), (442), and (711) Miller indices, respectively, which is in agreement with the reported literature. − ­(Figure ).

The PXRD pattern of hydrolyzed PET chips displayed peaks at 2θ = 16.4°, 17.5°, 21.7°, 22.3°, 22.9°, 25.3°, 25.5°, and 26.3°, whereas the PET bottle exhibited a peak at 2θ = 25.5°. PXRD studies clearly revealed that hydrolysis tunes the crystallinity of the PET vial, and new peaks corresponding to acid-catalyzed hydrolysis of PET bottles were observed. It is worth mentioning that the presence of the peaks corresponding to UiO-66, along with the peaks of hydrolyzed PET at 2θ = 7.4°, 8.5°, 14.8°, 17.1°, 25.8°, and 30.8°, which are assigned to (111), (200), (222), (400), (442), and (711), respectively, clearly confirms the formation of the UiO-66 MOF on the inner surface of the PET vial (Figure ).

FTIR spectral studies provided definitive evidence for the formation of the UiO-66 MOF on the inner surface of the PET vial. The FTIR spectra of hydrolyzed PET and PET@UiO-66 are shown in Figure . Hydrolyzed PET exhibited characteristic peaks at 2973, 1712, 1406, 1339, 1236, 1091, 1012, 871, 842, 792, and 718 cm–1 (Figure ). The presence of a strong stretching frequency at 1712 cm–1 corresponding to the carboxylic acid group and a weak, broad band at 2973 cm–1 attributed to the intramolecular hydrogen-bonded hydroxyl group confirm the hydrolysis of PET. The growth of UiO-66 on the surface of the PET vial resulted in a shift in carbonyl stretching to 1696 cm–1 and the C–O stretching band to 1117 cm–1, clearly indicating the coordination of carboxylic acid with the zirconium (Zr) metal.

The nitrogen adsorption and desorption isotherms of UiO-66, isolated from the surface of the PET vial by scratching, are shown in Figure . BET surface area analysis was performed on the UiO-66-coated surface obtained by scratching the modified PET vial, with nitrogen adsorption–desorption measurements conducted at 77 K. The N2 adsorption–desorption isotherm of scratched PET@UiO-66 exhibited type IV isotherms with a hysteresis loop, indicating the formation of an interparticle mesoporous material. The Barrett–Joyner–Halenda (BJH) analysis of scratched PET@UiO-66 revealed a specific surface area (SSA) of 564.359 m2/g, a maximum pore volume of 0.06 cm3/g with a pore diameter of 3.05 nm, and an average pore radius of 1.389 nm (Figure ).

Figure shows a change in the texture of PET vials before and after surface modification into PET@UiO-66. A polarized optical microscopic image of hydrolyzed PET is shown in Figure a, indicating a homogeneous phase with transparency. It is worth mentioning that upon surface modification of the PET vial by PET@UiO-66, a morphological change was displayed, identified by both transmission and reflection mode optical microscopic images (Figure b–f). A cross-sectional image of the PET@UiO-66 modified PET vial is shown in Figure g.

The morphology and surface structure of the PET@UiO-66 modified vial were examined by using FESEM analysis. The morphological analysis revealed the existence of spherical particles that are uniform in size (Figure ). The EDS pattern of the surface-modified PET@UiO-66 vial is shown in Figure e, which displays carbon and oxygen from the PET and zirconium from the metal component.

To evaluate the synthetic utility of the PET@UiO-66 vial, we selected a multicomponent reaction for the synthesis of 2,4,6-trisubstituted pyridines. Our study began with the optimization of the reaction conditions. A series of reactions were performed using benzyl alcohol and acetophenone as model substrates and ammonium acetate serving as a nitrogen source to bring out the best reaction conditions for the synthesis of 2,4,6-triphenylpyridine using the PET@UiO-66 vial. Initially, the reaction was tested in various solvents, such as MeOH, EtOH, CH3CN, DMF, THF, and toluene, at room temperature. Unfortunately, no product formation was observed. Increasing the reaction temperature and duration also did not yield the desired product. The use of H2O2 as an oxidant in THF at 60 °C resulted in a slightly improved product formation (Table ).

However, in MeOH and CH3CN, only trace amounts of the desired product were observed. Other oxidants, such as TBPB and DTBP, also deliver trace amounts of the desired product. To our surprise, the use of TBHP in THF at 60 °C produced the desired 2,4,6-trisubstituted pyridine in a good yield. Having the optimized oxidant in our hand, next we focused on identifying a suitable solvent. Moderate to good yields were observed in polar aprotic solvents such as CH3CN, THF, and DMF in the presence of the TBHP oxidant. In contrast, significantly lower yields of 2,4,6-trisubstituted pyridine were obtained in protic solvents such as MeOH and H2O and nonpolar solvent toluene (Table ). When DMP and PCC were employed as oxidants, multiple spots were observed, indicating poor selectivity. The optimized reaction conditions for the synthesis of 2,4,6-trisubstituted pyridines involve using a vial with an inner surface modified with PET@UiO-66 as the catalyst, TBHP as an oxidant, and THF as a solvent at 60 °C for about 24 h. Using the optimized conditions, a library of 2,4,6-trisubstituted pyridines were synthesized in good to excellent yield (Scheme ). It is worth mentioning that after the completion of the reaction, the PET@UiO-66 vial was thoroughly washed with water and sonicated in DMF for 5 min to remove any stuck materials. The washed surface modified vial was reused in subsequent reactions.

The oxidation of benzyl alcohol using TBHP as an oxidizing agent and PET@UiO-66 on the vial surface as a catalyst proceeds via an oxidative dehydrogenation pathway. The process begins with the interaction of TBHP and benzyl alcohol at the zirconium center, followed by concerted hydride transfer, resulting in benzaldehyde and tert-butanol (tBuOH), respectively (Figure ). The carbonyl group of the resulting aldehyde interacts with PET@UiO-66, inducing a partial positive charge. Simultaneously, acetophenone forms an enolate, leading to aldol condensation. Subsequently, a second acetophenone molecule reacts with ammonium acetate to form an imine, which undergoes tautomerization. The resulting carbon nucleophile then participates in 1,4-conjugated addition, followed by cyclization and dehydrogenation, ultimately yielding the desired 2,4,6-triarylpyridine derivatives (Figure ).

The recyclability of PET@UiO-66 was evaluated by performing the same reaction five times to produce 2,4,6-triphenylpyridine (4a). The yields obtained in each cycle were 89%, 86%, 84%, 80%, and 75%, respectively, as given in Figure . This result demonstrates the catalyst’s recyclability, with a gradual decrease in yield over subsequent cycles. It is worth mentioning that it could be scaled-up to the vials of different volumes using the demonstrated protocol.

Conclusions

In summary, the PET@UiO-66 vial was successfully fabricated and utilized for the sustainable synthesis of 2,4,6-trisubstituted pyridines using a multicomponent reaction. The structure and morphology of the fabricated PET@UiO-66 vial were identified by PXRD, FTIR, optical microscopy, and FESEM analysis. The porous PET@UiO-66 vial acts as an efficient catalyst for the multicomponent synthesis of 2,4,6-triarylpyridines from benzyl alcohol, acetophenones, and ammonium acetate in the presence of TBHP as an oxidant in good to excellent yields. The reported method demonstrates an environmentally benign methodology for the synthesis of organic molecules by the repeated use of a catalytic vial.

Experimental Section

General Materials and Methods

All reagents and solvents used for the synthesis of PET@UiO-66 vial and 2,4,6-triphenylpyridine derivatives were purchased from commercial suppliers (Merck, Finar, SRL and Avra, TCI chemicals) and were used without further purification. Laboratory reagent (LR) grade solvents were employed for compound purification and recrystallization. AR-grade solvents were used for the synthesis of the MOF. The precoated Merck silica gel plates (TLC Silica gel 60 F254) were used to monitor the reaction progress and visualize the spots using any one or the combination of the following visualizing agents such as UV detection, KMnO4, p-anisaldehyde, H2SO4 stain, and molecular iodine.

X-ray Diffraction Studies

Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurement was performed using an XRDynamic 500 instrument to assess the structure of UiO-66 formed on the surface of the vial. PXRD patterns of PET, PET@UiO-66, and scratched UiO-66 material scratched from the vial were recorded over a diffraction angle (2θ) from 5° to 50° using Cu Kα radiation.

Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy

Infrared (IR) spectra were recorded from 400–4000 cm–1 using KBr in a PerkinElmer spectrum 100 spectrophotometer.

Field Emission Scanning Electron Microscope

The morphology of the PET@UiO-66 was identified using the field emission scanning electron microscope model-JEOL JSM7610F Plus. Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) studies were conducted alongside FESEM to investigate the elements present in PET@UiO-66.

BET Analysis

To analyze the surface area and pore size distribution of the PET@UiO-66 vial, the inner surface of the vial was scratched and analyzed using the Autosorb iQ-MP instrument, which uses N2 adsorption–desorption isotherms at 77 K.

Polarizing Optical Microscopy

To analyze the morphology of the PET@UiO-66 vial before and after modification, we have captured the surface image of hydrolyzed PET and PET@UiO-66 using an Olympus BX53, USA, instrument, applying both the transmission and reflection mode with the software micropublisher RTV 5.0, Qimaging.

NMR Analysis

1H and 13C NMR spectra were recorded on a Bruker Advance 400 MHz instrument in CDCl3 at room temperature. Chemical shifts (δ) are reported in parts per million (ppm) with reference to the internal standard TMS, and coupling constants (J) are given in Hz. Proton multiplicity is assigned using the following abbreviations: singlet (s), doublet (d), triplet (t), quartet (q), and multiplet (m).

Synthetic Procedures

Preparation of the PET@UIO-66 Vial

The PET@UIO-66 vial was prepared in two steps. First, PET vial is hydrolyzed, followed by the reaction with Zr in DMF.

Surface-modified PET vials were prepared by following the procedure reported in the literature, , Initially, the outer surface of the PET vial was wholly wrapped with Teflon. Later, the inner surface of the PET vial was hydrolyzed by treatment with a 7 M nitric acid solution for 7 h. After the hydrolysis, the PET vial was washed very well with distilled water and dried at room temperature.

For the preparation of the PET@UIO-66 vial, along with the vial, 3.46 g of ZrCl4, 2.5 g of BDC, 9.0 mL of glacial acetic acid, 1.0 mL of HCl, and 150 mL of DMF were added into a Schott bottle. The reaction mixture was sonicated for 30 min and kept in an oven for about 7 days at 100 °C. After the completion of the reaction, the PET@UIO-66 vials were taken out, washed with DMF and distilled water, and dried at 60–65 °C.

General Procedure for the Synthesis of 2,4,6-Triphenylpyridine Derivatives

In a dry PET@UiO-66 vial, benzyl alcohol (1.0 equiv), acetophenone (2.0 equiv), ammonium acetate (2.0 equiv), and TBHP (1.2 equiv) were stirred at 60 °C for 24 h. The reaction was monitored by using TLC. After the completion of the reaction, the reaction mixture was extracted from ethyl acetate and purified by recrystallization. After completion of the reaction, PET@UiO-66 vials were sonicated with water and DMF, and they were reused further for other organic reactions.
