Abstract

Nitrosoarenes are useful molecules owing to their unique electronic properties. The “NO” functional group is strongly electron-withdrawing, possessing a σpara value greater than either the nitro or trimethylammonium functional groups. Although many methods exist for the synthesis of nitroarenes, the direct incorporation of the “NO” functional group in a single step to produce nitrosoarenes has historically received less attention. In this paper, we discuss a mild and selective method for the ipso-nitrosation of arylboronic acids and their derivatives. Depending on the substrate identity, both nitro and nitrosoarenes can be formed from arylboronic acids. To overcome substrate-controlled selectivity, we developed a one-pot, two-step procedure to exclusively produce nitrosoarenes from boronic acids with no evidence of unwanted nitroarene products. A variety of arenes and heteroarenes are selectively nitrosated in good yields via reactions run open to air and without protection from moisture.

Keywords: nitrosation, nitration, boronic acid, trifluoroborate, N-nitrososulfonamide, telescoped

Nitrosoarenes are versatile building blocks used in organic synthesis, capable of undergoing a variety of transformations such as aldol reactions, cycloadditions, addition to Grignard reagents, and deoxygenation to form amines. − Nitrosoarenes can act as electrophiles at either the nitrogen or oxygen atom, depending on their mode of activation. − They have also demonstrated antiviral properties against HIV-1 infections and may serve as valuable synthetic targets due to their unique electronic properties. Interestingly, nitrosoarenes are also known to be more stable than their nitro counterparts owing to their ability to form oximes through self-tautomerization. , Baeyer first reported the synthesis of nitrosobenzenes more than a century ago. Since then, the most commonly used method to access nitrosoarenes has involved the oxidation of anilines. ,, However, this approach relies on the availability of anilines and the use of strong oxidants that are incompatible with sensitive functional groups. Recent synthetic efforts have focused on directed ipso-nitration of boronic acid derivatives to selectively form nitrosoarenes via electrophilic “NO” source (Figure ).

Compared to aromatic nitration (NO2), there is less precedence for direct C–H nitrosation of arenes. − Consequently, our primary focus was directed toward the exploration of C–H functionalization via direct nitrosation of arenes with our previously reported bench-stable reagent 3,3-dimethyl-2-nitroso-2,3-dihydrobenzo­[d]­isothiazol-1,1-dioxide (NO-1), designed for trans-nitrosation. Initial attempts showed limited reactivity for the nitrosation of toluene in hexafluoroisopropanol using modified conditions adapted from Katayev et al. for analogous nitration reactions (Figure ). , Further attempts at reaction optimization failed to yield improvements in either the reaction conversion or regioselectivity. These results encouraged us to investigate alternative routes to nitrosoarenes using NO-1.

We recognized that ipso-substitution of a suitable leaving group is the most straightforward way to access substituted products with high regioselectivity, and methods for nitration/nitrosation of arylborons have previously been established. , We initially focused on the nitrosation of arylboronic acids due to their commercial availability and lower cost compared to other arylboron sources. As shown in Table , the nitrosation of 4-methoxyphenylboronic acid was explored to determine the optimum conditions for ipso-nitrosation. We observed limited conversion to the expected nitroso product using one equivalent of NO-1 at room temperature in dichloromethane for 1 h (entry 1). Alternative solvents had a limited effect on nitrosation (entry 2), although catalytic amounts of Brönsted acids improved the yield, presumably via enhancement of the electrophilicity of NO-1 (entry 3). Heating (entry 4) and increasing the reaction time (entries 5–6) both improved the yield of the desired product, with lower temperatures being preferred to selectively favor nitrosation over nitration.

With suitable optimum conditions in hand, we sought to explore the scope of arylboronic acids that could be selectively nitrosated using NO-1. As shown in Scheme A, several arylboronic acids can be effectively nitrosated, although selectivity appears to be controlled by substrate identity. For example, while 4-methoxyphenylboronic acid is nitrosated in high yield with no nitration observed (1), less electron-rich substrates produce varying amounts of ipso-nitration products as unwanted byproducts (2–4). A trend can be established correlating the selectivity of nitrosation with the σp value of the substituent at the 4-position of arylboronic acids. A notable observation can be made between σp values of approximately 0.2–0.4 (Scheme B). , Selectivity favors nitration for substrates with sufficiently electron-withdrawing substituents, yielding products 6–8 nearly exclusively as nitroarenes. Because the observed selectivity is substrate-controlled, − we shifted our focus to exploring other arylboronic acid derivatives as precursors to nitrosoarenes. We found that, unlike arylboronic acids, aryltrifluoroborates exclusively favored nitrosation products, regardless of substrate identity (Scheme C, 2, 4, 6, 9). This is especially notable for the production of 1-nitroso-4-(trifluoromethyl)­benzene (6). When the standard reaction conditions were applied to potassium­[4-(trifluoromethyl)­phenyl]­trifluoroborate, the nitrosated product 6 was produced in good yield with no trace of nitroarene 6b. This result establishes that either the nitroso- or nitroarene products can be accessed from electron-deficient arylboron species via ipso displacement with NO-1, depending on the nature of the starting arylboron species.

Because selective nitrosation could be achieved from trifluoroborates regardless of the substrate electronics, we shifted our focus from arylboronic acids. The limited commercial availability of aryltrifluoroborates, compared with arylboronic acids, encouraged us to develop a two-step procedure that directly converts boronic acids into the analogous trifluoroborate prior to nitrosation by NO-1. Using the Lloyd-Jones method, we found that a telescoped one-pot sequence can be employed without isolating or purifying the intermediate aryltrifluoroborates. Scheme shows a brief scope of substrates that participate in the two-step nitrosation sequence starting from arylboronic acids. We found that the results for the two-step telescoped procedure are generally comparable to those for the direct nitrosation of aryltrifluoroborates. The yields of products 2, 4, and 9 in Scheme can be compared directly to the same products shown in Scheme C. These results confirm the efficiency of the two-step nitrosation process compared to that of direct nitrosation using aryltrifluoroborates as starting materials. Unfortunately, heterocyclic substrates were not effective using the developed two-step protocol, although this was shown to be the result of poor conversion from boronic acid to trifluoroborate (19).

We have observed, qualitatively, that aryltrifluoroborates nitrosate are much faster than arylboronic acids, requiring reduced reaction times to reach suitable conversion. We have found this to be true, regardless of the substrate electronics and believe that this rate difference is the source of NO/NO2 selectivity between arylboronic acids and trifluoroborates. As shown in Scheme , two fates are considered for NO-1. In the presence of a suitable nucleophilic arene, such as trifluoroborate, the reaction will occur with NO-1 to directly incorporate the nitroso functional group into the aromatic substrate (1). Under acidic conditions, NO-1 is known to decompose, liberating free “NO” into the reaction medium. Less reactive arenes, such as electron-poor boronic acids, do not successfully react with NO-1 before it decomposes under standard reaction conditions to the nitrosonium ion, which then reacts to produce the nitroarene product (2). Air oxidation to favor the nitro-product was supported via control reactions by bubbling excess oxygen into the reaction medium.

In conclusion, we have identified a straightforward strategy for selective ipso-nitrosation of arylboronic acids using NO-1 as a source of “NO.” The direct reaction of arylboronic acids produces either nitro or nitrosoarenes depending on the substrate electronics, but aryltrifluoroborates selectively yield nitrosoarenes as the exclusive product. To maximize nitrosation selectivity, we have developed a one-pot, two-step procedure by which arylboronic acids can be converted to trifluoroborates in situ and reacted directly with NO-1 without isolation or purification.

Experimental Section

General Procedure for the Preparation of Nitrobenzene via Ipso-nitrosation of Arylboronic Acid

A solution of KF (4 equiv, 0.4 mmol, 23 mg) in 40 μL H2O was added to the vial with arylboronic acids/arylboronic acid pinacol ester (1 equiv, 0.1 mmol) in 0.4 mL MeCN, stirred until dissolved completely, followed by the addition of l-(+) tartaric acid (2.5 equiv, 0.25 mmol, 37.5 mg) in MeCN/H2O (0.3 mL/40 μL) over the course of 5 min. A white precipitate was formed, which was stirred for 1h at rt. NO-1 (1.1 equiv, 0.11 mmol, 25 mg) in 0.3 mL of MeCN was added to the mixture along with 10 mol % HCl and stirred at rt for 1 h. The mixture was concentrated under reduced pressure, and the pure product was isolated in good yield by column chromatography using EtOAc/hexanes (10:90).
