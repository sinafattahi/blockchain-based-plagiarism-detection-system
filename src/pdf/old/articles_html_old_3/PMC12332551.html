Abstract

Perennial endemics of mosquito-borne dengue infection affect millions of lives across tropical and subtropical regions every year. Factors like the Earth’s rising temperature, climate change, and lack of dengue-specific drugs and vaccines have led to a significant surge in these infections, warranting urgent efforts to develop new antidengue agents. The dengue virus (DENV) envelope exposes approximately 90 E (envelope) protein dimers on its surface, making it a potent target for drug discovery and vaccine development. E protein-based virus-like particles (VLPs) may serve as an ideal system to test the binding activity of compounds in vitro. Such VLPs have not been defined for DENV serotype 2 (DENV-2). The current study demonstrates the expression of the E protein-ectodomain in and VLP generation. Previously, chebulinic acid (CA), a tannin, was demonstrated as a strong DENV-2 inhibitor in Vero cells. However, the structural complexity of CA made its synthesis a challenging task, whereas toxicity at higher doses limited its therapeutic use. To investigate binding to E protein in silico, simple analogues of CA (mono, di, tri, tetra, and pentagalloylglucose (PGG)) were selected and evaluated using molecular docking, molecular dynamics (MD) simulations, and electrostatic complementarity analysis. Herein, PGG–E-protein complex demonstrated higher docking scores, stable MD simulation patterns and electrostatics, equivalent to that of positive controls, CA, and epigallocatechin-3-gallate. Furthermore, the PGG interaction with VLP molecules was analyzed in vitro using biolayer interferometry, where nM-scale equilibrium constants were recorded. Therefore, a strong in silico and in vitro E protein-binding profile of PGG supports its potential as a DENV-2 inhibitor, which may be developed as an anti-DENV-2 therapeutic.

1. Introduction

Dengue virus (DENV) is transmitted through a mosquito vector and is responsible for the onset of dengue fever. DENV has been classified into four serotypes (DENV-1, DENV-2, DENV-3, and DENV-4), and is known to be transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes to humans. Another serotype of this virus, the fifth serotype (DENV-5), has been discovered in Sarawak state, Malaysia, with the help of isolation and genetic sequence analysis. Dengue fever has been marked as a global health concern by the World Health Organization (WHO) for being the leading cause of the arthropod-borne viral disease, which is significantly linked with a huge social and economic burden worldwide. In both tropical and subtropical regions, about 50 million people get dengue infections each year.

The infection caused by various DENV serotypes could result in asymptomatic disease in most cases, or it may show a wide spectrum of clinical symptoms, starting from mild flu-like syndrome, also known as dengue fever, to severe forms of the disease. The severe disease forms are characterized by permeability, coagulopathy, and increased vascular fragility, which later could lead to hypovolemic shock and death. Despite the known severity and clinical significance of this disease, fewer treatment regimens exist, and the therapeutic strategy is mostly dependent on supportive care. A vaccine has been developed against the dengue virus earlier and is in use in a limited number of countries; it has not been used widely due to the associated risk factors of severe dengue. Therefore, there exists an immediate need for the development of efficient vaccines and therapeutics for the efficient reduction in dengue morbidities and the burden of infection in the susceptible population.

DENV has a single-stranded RNA genome, which has a positive polarity that translates in the cytoplasm as a single polyprotein. The cellular and viral proteases process the polyprotein and release 3 structural and 7 nonstructural proteins. The structural proteins that play a key role in host entry are the capsid protein (C), membrane precursor protein (prM), and the glycosylated envelope protein (E). The envelope protein consists of 3 domains: the E protein domain-I (EDI), also known as the central domain, the dimerization domain II (EDII), which harbors the conserved fusion loop, and the putative receptor-binding domain III (EDIII). E protein has two N-linked glycosylation sites, Asn-67, which is specific to DENV serotypes, and Asn-153, which is conserved in most of the flaviviruses. The E protein is the most significant factor in DENV structure as it occupies the major area of the viral surface and is considered the primary target of the humoral immune system. Subsequently, anti-E protein antibodies have been shown to inhibit the binding of the DENV to its receptor molecules, thereby inhibiting the infection.

Mammalian cells harbor several surface molecules that act as receptors for DENV. These include lectins that recognize carbohydrates, like Dendritic cell-specific ICAM-3-grabbing nonintegrin (DC-SIGN), glycosphingolipid (GSL), laminin-binding proteins, sulfated glycosaminoglycans (GAGs), proteins with chaperone activity, and some other uncharacterized proteins found in the cells and organs of mosquitoes

An earlier study demonstrated the antiviral potential of chebulinic acid (CA), which is a plant-based complex biomolecule, as an inhibitor of DENV-2 infection. The structural complexity and large size of CA make its chemical synthesis difficult, and it has not yet been synthesized in a lab setup. Additionally, the toxic side effects of CA have also been observed at moderate to high concentrations in Vero cells, making its natural form somewhat unsafe as a potential DENV inhibitor. CA contains multiple gallic acid moieties and is generally involved in interacting with the receptor molecules through them. Therefore, natural molecules harboring similar gallic acid units were screened, and five CA analogues or gallic acid derivatives, the mono, di, tri, tetra, and pentagalloylglucose (PGG), were selected for investigating their propensity for binding the E protein of DENV-2 using molecular docking and MD simulation studies. Based on the structural and chemical similarity between CA and the gallic acid derivatives of glucose, two of the three previously defined CA binding pockets on E protein were chosen for docking experiments. The most potent binder was then used to bind the E protein in vitro. To perform this study, the viral envelope protein was cloned, expressed in , and in vitro refolded into higher-order oligomeric assemblies, virus-like particles (VLPs). Various types of VLPs of DENV serotypes have been demonstrated earlier in different host systems, mammalian cells, − insect cells, insects, , plants, Pichia pastoris, − and . , Most of these VLPs are lipid-based particles wherein a combination of DENV proteins (E, prM, and C) are embedded in the lipid bilayer, exposing the constituent protein-ectodomains on the surface. E protein-based virus-sized particles have been generated in for DENV-1, whereas, in the case of DENV-2, EDIII-HBcAg (Hepatitis B virus core antigen) fusion-protein-based VLPs have been reported. However, full-length E-ectodomain-based VLP of DENV-2 has not been demonstrated so far. Such protein-based VLPs, in addition to serving as a vaccine platform, are more desirable for anti-DENV-2 drug discovery, wherein compounds may be screened through their E protein-binding ability in vitro. Therefore, the current study also demonstrates an elaborate, optimized method to generate proteinaceous VLPs of the DENV-2 E-protein and its characterization.

2. Results

2.1. E Protein Expression, Purification, and In Vitro Refolding into Oligomers

The recombinant plasmid pET22b-E-DENV-2 was subjected to restriction digestion using XhoI and NdeI enzymes and PCR amplification using T7 forward and T7 reverse primers to confirm the presence of the recombinant gene. The products were analyzed further on an agarose gel, wherein the appearance of a fallout and an amplified DNA band, corresponding to the size of the DENV-2 envelope ectodomain gene, suggested its cloning (Supporting Figure S1). The identification of the cloned gene was thereafter confirmed by Sanger sequencing.

The culture of BL21 (DE3) cells, transformed with pET22b-E-DENV-2, was induced with IPTG and monitored for 5 h postinduction for the assessment of the E protein expression. The heterologous E protein was expressed as a fusion of a 6 × His-tag at its C-terminus. The induced and uninduced culture samples and segregated pellet and supernatant fractions of the lysed 5 h culture sample were analyzed on SDS-PAGE. As shown in Figure A, an induced protein band of ∼ 44 kDa was observed migrating with relative bands of the molecular weight marker in all the samples. The appearance of a prominent induced protein band in the pellet fraction suggested accumulation of the recombinant protein in inclusion bodies. Thereafter, the pellet fraction, solubilized by using 8 M urea, was subjected to purification using Ni-NTA-His-tag affinity chromatography under denaturing conditions. The purified recombinant protein samples were pooled and assessed on SDS-PAGE. As shown in Figure B, the recombinant protein was obtained with ∼ 95% purity.

Subsequently, the purified protein sample was subjected to refolding by employing the gradual and differential removal of the chaotropic and oxidizing reagents. This method led to a 78% recovery yield of the refolded recombinant protein (Table ). The identity of the E protein was also confirmed by Western blotting performed using an anti-DENV E protein antibody directed against domain III. As shown in Figure C, a prominent band of the refolded E protein was highlighted on the blot, suggesting it to be the recombinant E protein. The oligomerization of E protein into higher-order assemblies was investigated using SDS-PAGE, employing the presence and absence of a reducing agent. As shown in Figure D, in the absence of a reducing agent, the majority of the E protein was retained at the interface of the stacking-resolving gel, while only a small portion was observed to enter the resolving gel, indicating a higher molecular-sized assembly of the E protein.

2.2. Biophysical Analysis of E Protein Oligomers

The SDS-PAGE analysis of the E protein refolded samples suggested their higher-order assembly into oligomers. The structural change observed during refolding was further validated by the dynamic light scattering (DLS) technique, performed with the E protein (purified) samples before and after the refolding. As shown in Figure A, the average particle size of the unfolded protein sample was recorded with a diameter of 1212 nm [hydrodynamic radius (Rh) of 606 nm] with a polydispersity index (PdI) of 0.538. On the other hand, the average particle size of the refolded protein sample was recorded with a diameter of 499.4 nm (Rh of 249.7 nm) with a PdI of 0.440. The observed particle size of the E protein VLPs fell within the range of the reported sizes of virus particles (∼20–500 nm diameter). The PdI, a parameter indicative of the distribution of different-sized moieties within a sample, was anticipated to be higher, as observed, due to the expected presence of a mixed population of different-sized entities in the refolded sample.

To investigate the presence of different oligomeric entities within the refolded sample, as indicated by the DLS study, the sample was subjected to size exclusion chromatography (SEC). As shown in Figure B, a single major elution peak was observed at 46.73 mL of elution volume, in addition to 3 other comparatively low-intensity peaks, corresponding to small-sized assemblies, monomeric forms of the E protein, and impurities, respectively. The size of the first major protein peak, corresponding to the largest oligomeric assemblies, was calculated to be 876 kDa. The other peaks that were observed at 69.48 and 78.64 mL elution volumes correlated with the dimeric and monomeric forms of the refolded E protein, respectively. These fractions were not followed up on thereafter. The calculated molecular weight of the E protein is 43.8 kDa, and therefore, the stoichiometry of assembly of the E protein oligomer was estimated to be 20 (Table ). Afterward, the secondary-structure elements of the purified oligomers were studied using FTIR spectroscopy to assess the proper folding of the E protein to its natural three-dimensional state. The prominent peaks at 3341.24, 1636.00, 1217.37, and 1736.27 cm–1 (Figure C), representing the amine group, the β-sheet, the carboxylic group, and the CO stretch, respectively, demonstrated the presence of secondary-structure elements, further supporting the proper refolding of E protein to its native form. Furthermore, circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy was performed with the purified oligomers in the 180–300 nm UV range (Figure D). The data was assessed using the software Spectra Manager, which revealed the composition of the secondary-structure elements to be 14.4% α helix, 31.3% β-sheet, 23.3% β-turns, and 31% random coil. The observed structural description was found in agreement with the earlier defined crystal structure of the E protein (https://www.rcsb.org/annotations/1OAN), further supporting the proper refolding of the recombinant E-protein to its native conformation. The analysis of E protein structure in its VLP form using FTIR and CD, reflected the structural conformation of E protein molecules while organized as a VLP, validating their structural integrity and suitability for use in in vitro binding assays.

2.3. Molecular Docking and MM-GBSA dG Binding Score Analysis

Chebulinic acid (CA), a plant tannin, has shown inhibitory activity against DENV-2 in virus neutralization assays. The mechanism of its antiviral activity was proposed based on its interaction with the viral envelope protein. The information about the residues of various host receptors involved in making contact with the viral E protein and correlation of those with the CA binding residues of E protein (in silico) was used to define two binding pockets (grids 1 and 2) in the E protein dimer, which were used in the current study. As shown in Figure , for grid 1, the constituent residues were contributed by the two subunits of the homodimer, the distal end of domain II of chain A and domains I and II of chain-B. This binding pocket harbored a K1 β-hairpin loop that was shown to play a role in virus-host membrane fusion. Grid 1 residues also include 7 residues (T70, E71, G102, N103, A245, K246, K247) out of 32 residues that have been shown to interact through an interface between the E protein and host receptor DC-SIGN, and also include residues G102 and N103 of the fusion peptide. Therefore, this region was defined as a site for receptor binding and initiation of the fusion process through the K1 β-hairpin loop and the fusion peptide. On the other hand, the grid 2 constituent residues were contributed predominantly by EDIII, the associated hinge (between EDIII and EDI), and the EDI of chain A. Herein, the binding pocket harbored an essential residue, K295, which is conserved across the four serotypes of DENV and has been shown to directly bind to the GAG receptor, a common viral receptor on various host cells. Grid 2 also has an exposed sugar moiety (Fuc1399), covalently attached to the glycosylation residue N153, which is conserved in all four serotypes of DENV. This glycosyl moiety has been shown to participate in binding mannose receptors, found on the host cells, and mediate viral entry. , Based on the above-cited literature, the sites selected for docking studies represent suitable binding pockets for testing the antiviral inhibitory potential of test molecules.

Protein and ligand preparation were conducted as described in Section . Following the preparation, two grid boxes were generated for the E protein, as shown in Figure . Subsequently, separate molecular docking experiments were performed using the optimized 3D structures of five ligand molecules, namely, 1-galloylglucose, 1,6-digalloylglucose, 1,3,6-trigalloylglucose, 1,2,4,6-tetragalloylglucose, and 1,2,3,4,6-pentagalloylglucose, at grids 1 and 2, defined within the E protein dimer, adopted from the crystal structure (PDB ID: 1OAN). The results of docking analysis, thus generated for these five protein–ligand complexes, are presented in Figure and compiled in Table . In this study, CA and epigallocatechin-3-gallate (EGCG) molecules were used as positive controls for reference, as both had demonstrated anti-DENV activity in previous studies. , All ligands showed significant docking scores for binding to both pockets, equivalent to those obtained with the positive control molecules (CA and EGCG).

2.4. Analysis of ADMET (Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion, and Toxicity) Properties

ADMET study helps determine a compound’s potential and efficacy as a drug since it assesses its physicochemical characteristics, biological activities, and drug-likeness. These characteristics of a compound may be assessed through several descriptors, such as CNS activity, molecular weight, solvent accessibility, hydrophobic and polar surface areas, volume, molecular interactions, solubility, permeability, potential metabolic reactions, binding affinity to human serum albumin, oral absorption, etc. The ADMET parameters were predicted for all five test compounds using QikProp module of the Schrodinger suite, which are detailed in Supporting Table S3. The data suggest that as the number of galloyl moieties, the functional groups on the glucose molecule, increases, parameters linked to bioavailability and permeability decrease. On the other hand, the hydrophobicity and solubility scores of all the compounds were satisfactory, indicating their high solubility in an aqueous environment, which is the most desirable parameter for their function. The test compounds showed moderate to poor pharmacokinetic properties that favor conventional drug-like properties. These parameters are primarily designed for small-sized drug compounds, especially those required to cross the cellular membranes to gain access to target molecules or sites. The test compounds used in the study are neither small molecules nor required to cross the plasma membranes for their activity. Since the test compound’s activity of binding the viral particles warrants their presence in extracellular spaces, the observed ADMET data support their potential as host-entry inhibitors.

2.5. Molecular Dynamics Simulation and Electrostatic Complementarity Studies

The molecular dynamics simulations provide detailed information about the stability and structural dynamics of the protein–ligand interactions. Herein, the protein ‘Root-Mean Square Deviation’ (RMSD) values indicate the structural deviation of the protein backbone from its initial conformation; the ligand RMSD values reflect the movement and stability of the ligand within the binding pocket; the protein ‘Root-Mean Square Fluctuation’ (RMSF) data captures the per-residue flexibility of the protein; the hydrogen bonding between protein and ligand determines the overall strength of the interactions, and the radius of gyration (Rg) measures the overall compactness of the protein structure during the simulation, indicating the structure unfolding. Initial 100 ns simulations revealed that grid 2 consistently offered greater stability than grid 1, based on comparative analyses of protein–ligand RMSD, protein RMSF, hydrogen bonding, and Rg values (Table and Figures S4–S7). Protein RMSD trends mostly stayed within acceptable ranges (<5 Å), confirming the structural integrity of the protein during 100 ns simulations (Figure S4). However, the ligand RMSD shows diverse patterns from stable retention to partial or complete dissociation, with notably higher stable values at grid 2 for tetragalloylglucose, PGG, and CA (Figure S4). Protein RMSF profiles indicated moderate flexibility across most protein regions, with notably higher fluctuations at grid 1, suggesting localized instability (Figure S5). Persistent hydrogen bonding was observed at grid 2 except for trigalloylglucose, while grid 1 displayed delayed or weaker interactions (Figure S6). Additionally, Rg analysis showed grid 1 often approaching ∼ 60 Å (indicative of partial unfolding), whereas grid 2 remained compact (Rg < 35 Å) (Figure S7). Among all galloylglucose ligands, PGG displayed the most consistent and balanced stability pattern over the 100-ns time frame at grid 2.

Herein, PGG and EGCG were analyzed by using a 500 ns simulation, whereas the rest of the test molecules and CA were analyzed by using a 200 ns simulation. These extended simulations (200 and 500 ns) at grid 2 provided a better understanding of protein–ligand complex stability. As shown in the RMSD plots (Figures and S8), protein RMSD values remained within a typical range (1–6 Å), indicating reasonable conformational flexibility, especially for PGG, EGCG, and CA (Figure ) and monogalloylglucose, digalloylglucose (Figure S8).

As shown in Figure , the ligand RMSD values showed variability; PGG shows unbinding events in the initial and later stages, while EGCG and CA show late-stage unbinding events, implying possible ligand dissociation or reorientation within the binding pocket. In the case of other test compounds, di- and trigalloylglucose showed stable binding as reflected by their ligand RMSD values (Figure S8). Notably, CA and EGCG were included as positive controls in this study, as both compounds have demonstrated significant inhibitory activity against dengue virus both in silico and in vitro, , and serve as positive references to compare and assess the binding behavior of the candidate ligands (galloylglucose molecules). Among all candidates, PGG remained a more stable and promising ligand, particularly at grid 2, showing favorable long-term interaction dynamics equivalent to those of CA and EGCG.

Furthermore, the electrostatic complementarity between PGG and the binding sites (grids 1 and 2), the electrostatic potential of the ligand, and the binding pocket were analyzed using electrostatic surface potential (ESP) maps, for PGG, CA, and EGCG. These maps highlight the positional orientation of the ligand molecule within the binding pocket and also reveal regions of surface electrostatic complementarity, where opposite charges align between the ligand and protein, suggesting favorable electrostatic interactions. The ESP maps were generated using Schrodinger Maestro 14.2., illustrating the charge distribution across the ligand-binding interface, wherein regions colored in red denote electronegative potential, while blue regions indicate electropositive potential, providing visual hints for electrostatic compatibility between the ligand and protein surfaces. ESP analyses were performed for key complexes involving PGG, EGCG, and CA at both binding grids (Figure ).

The electrostatic complementarity was assessed using EC scores, calculated using the Schrodinger suite, BioLuminate. The EC scores range from +1 to 0 to −1 values, referring to perfect electrostatic complementarity, to no correlation, and to electrostatic clash, respectively. As shown in Figure , the EC scores revealed values above 0.5 at grid 1 for all three ligands, including PGG, indicating strong electrostatic surface complementarity. In contrast, grid 2 exhibited EC scores of approximately 0.4, reflecting a moderate electrostatic compatibility. These results further corroborate the docking and MD simulation findings about PGG-E-protein complex stability, underlining the contribution of both sites toward effective binding. When compared, the docking scores, MD simulation data, and EC scores of PGG were found to be equivalent to those of CA and EGCG, for some parameters, even performing better than EGCG. These results therefore support the anti-DENV-2 characteristics of PGG, identifying it as a potential antidengue therapeutic agent.

2.6. In Vitro Protein–Ligand Binding Assay

Through docking and molecular dynamics simulation studies, PGG emerged as the most stable and high-affinity binder of the E protein. The E protein-binding kinetics of PGG was investigated in vitro using a biolayer interferometry-based assay. The assay was performed at four different concentrations of ligands, ranging from 6.25 to 100 μM. Herein, PGG showed high affinity, dose-dependent binding to the E protein VLPs, which were immobilized on Ni-NTA biosensors using 6 × His-tag mediated chemistry. Subsequently, in agreement with the molecular docking studies that were performed at two different pockets, a 1:2 heterotypic ligand-binding model was adopted to plot and analyze the PGG-VLPs binding data. The binding model provided two equilibrium dissociation constants for multiple-site binding, a K D1 of 1.07 nM and a second K D2 of 25 nM (Figure A), wherein a correlation value (R 2) of 0.8982 suggested a good correlation between the concentration and the observed binding of the compound.

A similar binding assay, performed with EGCG (positive control) under the same experimental setup, produced equivalent binding affinity and kinetics data, as presented in Figure B. As per the binding kinetics, the two equilibrium dissociation constants, K D1 and 2, were observed to be 0.06 nM and 4.5 μM, respectively, with a correlation (R 2) of 0.9505. PGG showed high-affinity binding at both sites, whereas EGCG showed high affinity for only one site, signifying the limited use of available sites. The high-affinity interaction between PGG and E protein, observed during in vitro binding assay, substantiates the observations made during the docking and MD simulation studies, signifying the antiviral potential of PGG.

3. Discussion

In this study, two objectives were aligned to generate a system that serves synchronously towards the anti-dengue drug discovery and development. In this quest, a method of generating E protein-based VLPs of DENV-2 using a cost- and time-effective bacterial system was developed. The adopted method led to the successful expression, purification, and refolding of the full-length ectodomain of dengue virus serotype 2 (DENV-2) envelope E protein in BL21 cells. In the past decade, many studies focused on generating either lipid-based or protein VLPs of DENV, as summarized in Table . They explored their ability to elicit a strong immune response as vaccine candidates. These VLPs, especially lipid-based, mimic virus particles and the arrangement of proteins like E, prM, and C, depending on the combination of the proteins used in VLP formation, and generate neutralizing immune response. Often, the presence of more than one protein constituent, prM/C, etc., in addition to E protein, renders these VLPs unsuitable for screening E protein inhibitors, as nonenvelope protein molecules may serve as off-targets and produce false-positive hits.

Additionally, the higher cost of production and relatively high instability of lipid-based VLPs are factors of concern. The in vitro screening assays, being high-throughput, demand a continuous supply of VLPs, and therefore, their cost of production has a direct impact on the overall cost of the drug discovery process. Herein, an E protein VLP platform, produced using an inexpensive expression system such as , offers an easy-to-handle and scalable production process, which is more suitable and desirable. Earlier attempts to use and hosts for E protein expression reported significantly lower purification yields. − A later study demonstrated the production of dengue virus serotype 1 (DENV-1) E protein in , achieving a postrefolding yield of approximately 15–20 mg/L. However, the tendency of E proteins to form VLPs was not explored. In an attempt to generate protein-only VLPs of DENV-2, domain III of the E protein was used and expressed in fusion with HBcAg to produce chimeric VLPs. A recent study demonstrated the generation of E protein-based particles of DENV-1 in . The study, however, revealed the formation of virus-sized particulate assemblies, which reportedly lack the characteristic morphology of VLPs.

In the current study, the DENV-2 E protein expression in , and the accumulation of recombinant protein in the form of IBs turned out to be beneficial in terms of higher protein yields, resulting in high-level recovery of functional protein post-in vitro refolding (78%). Oligomerization of E protein subunits into VLPs, with a stoichiometry as high as 20, was achieved through refolding of the denatured E protein using a protocol described earlier. , The E protein dimers, formed through the association of domain II of the two subunits, further interact closely with other E protein dimers on the surface of the viral envelope. , Therefore, the use of the full-length ectodomain of E protein, which tends to be associated with other subunits within the viral envelope, and the process used for refolding may have facilitated VLP formation. To comprehensively characterize the refolded protein VLPs, various biophysical techniques were employed, including SDS-PAGE, size exclusion chromatography (SEC), Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy, circular dichroism (CD), and dynamic light scattering (DLS). SDS-PAGE (reducing/nonreducing) predicted the oligomerization of E protein within the refolded sample, which was then subjected to DLS analysis, which further validated the VLP formation by specifying the size (∼500 nm diameter) of the particles. Simultaneously, SEC analysis provided supporting data for the presence of differently sized oligomeric forms in the refolded sample, wherein the data showed one major peak corresponding to the large-sized oligomer and three distinct smaller peaks corresponding to lower assemblies, including the monomeric form and impurities. SEC also provided the purified VLPs that were further subjected to structural analyses. The FTIR and CD spectroscopic studies, conducted with purified VLPs, demonstrated the presence of anticipated secondary-structure elements within the refolded proteins, supporting the proper refolding and attainment of native structural organization by the E protein subunits. These VLPs, if used for high-throughput screening of host-entry inhibitor molecules during a preliminary analysis, may provide vital information predicting their performance in an in vitro setup and help select suitable candidates for follow-up studies. The E protein VLPs may also be developed as an effective vaccine, as the particle-like nature of antigen molecules is known to stimulate the host immune system at a broader level, leading to a strong anti-antigen humoral as well as cellular immune response. ,

Additionally, the study also explored new natural compounds as potential antidengue candidates for managing dengue infections. The focus was primarily on the analogues of CA, which have previously been shown to inhibit DENV-2 entry in Vero cells. Since the study was designed to be conducted with the CA analogues, the crystal structure (PDB ID– 1OAN), used earlier for CA docking studies, was employed for the current studies. In addition to the crystal structure, a cryo-EM structure of a mature DENV-2 virion (PDB ID – 3J27) has also been solved at 3.5-Å resolution. Later, Wen-Fan Shen et al., reconstructed the atomic model of a mature DENV-2 virus-like particle (mD2VLP) by fitting the atomic coordinates of E and M proteins of 3J27 in the cryo-EM density map of mD2VLP, representing the VLP-specific arrangement of E and M proteins (mD2VLP_3J27). In general, the docking suites perform a reliable assessment of molecular interactions with crystal structures of high resolution, as they are more complete structures, having better resolved side chains and binding sites, and may underperform with structures of low resolution. Therefore, the primary docking studies were conducted with the crystal structure (1OAN) resolved at a higher resolution (2.75 Å) than the cryo-EM structures solved at lower (3.5 Å) resolution. The dynamism of surface E and M proteins as a function of pH and temperature has been demonstrated earlier, , suggesting differential spatial arrangements of the E subunits in a crystal lattice, a protein virus-like particle, a live virion, and a lipid bilayer-based virus-like particle. Therefore, although solved at lower resolution, the cryo-EM structures of DENV-2 virion and mD2VLP offered an opportunity to test the binding activity of the test compounds with these structures in silico and compare the results with those produced by using a crystal structure. Therefore, the docking studies were repeated with the cryo-EM structures, which produced equivalent binding scores in the majority of the cases, except in the case of EGCG, which showed high docking scores with E dimers of the mD2VLP_3J27 structure. In comparison to the entire set of compounds tested in these docking experiments, PGG again demonstrated the most consistent, lowest docking and MM-GBSA binding free energy scores, validating its selection as the most stable and high-affinity binder of E protein.

To conduct the docking studies with CA analogues (gallic acid derivatives of glucose), two out of three, previously defined CA binding sites on E protein, where maximum binding scores were achieved, were chosen for docking experiments and named as grid 1 and grid 2 (Figure ). The grid 1 encompassed E protein regions carrying the K1 β-hairpin loop, involved in the fusion process; receptor DC-SIGN binding site (partial), and residues of the fusion peptide. This site played a vital role in virus-host membrane fusion, which is common to all serotypes, and therefore, this site was a prime site for drug screening. On the other hand, grid 2 harbored binding sites for host receptors like GAG and mannose receptors. , Additionally, grid 2 contains two residues, K295, which is involved in host receptor binding, and a glycosyl moiety, Fuc1399, attached to residue 153; both residues are conserved in all four serotypes of DENV. Therefore, E protein regions selected in grids 1 and 2, as discussed above, are suggested to mediate host attachment and entry activities, and therefore, if these sites are bound by drug molecules with high affinity, individually or simultaneously, they may inhibit viral infection. The in silico docking studies indicated the same, that the gallic acid derivatives may serve as DENV-2 entry inhibitors, mediating their effects through binding to the E protein at grids 1 and 2. Among the five test molecules, PGG generated the highest binding scores at grid 1, whereas moderate yet significant binding scores were observed at grid 2. Additionally, PGG demonstrated a comparatively balanced stability of the protein–ligand complex during molecular dynamics simulation studies. Herein, 100 and 500 ns MD simulation data of PGG demonstrated a pattern equivalent to CA. The other small compound used as a positive control, EGCG, which otherwise has significant structural disparity with PGG and CA, showed equivalent and rather lower docking scores. During MD simulation studies, the lower protein RMSD indicated a stable conformation; however, the ligand RMSD was consistently in an unstable range throughout. To further validate the stability and biological significance of the PGG-E protein interaction, the surface electrostatic complementarity of the docked complexes of PGG, CA, and EGCG with the E dimer was analyzed through electrostatic surface potential (ESP) maps. Herein, all three complexes showed similar scores, with PGG and CA performing better than EGCG, further validating the potential of PGG as a possible anti-DENV-2 inhibitor. Therefore, PGG was selected for the subsequent in vitro binding assay, where it exhibited high-affinity kinetics for binding E protein VLPs.

PGG, a naturally occurring, hydrolyzable plant tannin, which is associated with a wide range of biological activities such as antiviral, anticancer, and anti-inflammatory properties, makes it a promising therapeutic candidate. Recently, PGG has been demonstrated to block SARS-CoV-2′s papain-like protease (PLpro), essential for viral replication, highlighting its antiviral properties, particularly against viruses like SARS-CoV-2. Studies have shown a favorable safety profile of PGG, notably in mice, with manageable dosages. , However, PGG has also exhibited cytotoxicity in several cell lines, necessitating further investigations to optimize its therapeutic window. Additionally, PGG has been studied in animal models, including mice and prairie dogs, which demonstrate good tolerance profiles. These trials suggest that with more focused research, PGG could be repurposed as a medication for treating viral infections, particularly in combination with other drugs. To assess the drug-likeness of the test compounds, ADMET analysis was conducted, which did not predict favorable values for most of the parameters, like CNS activity, molecular weight, volume, molecular interactions, solubility, permeability, potential metabolic reactions, binding affinity to human serum albumin, oral absorption, etc. The only parameters working in favor of the molecules were hydrophobic and polar surface areas, solvent accessibility, solubility, etc., indicating mostly the extracellular confinement of the compounds. However, since the molecules are required to bind the free viral particles present in the extracellular regions, the conventional ADMET parameters may not directly correlate with the drug-likeness of the test compounds. The adverse data obtained for most of the parameters in the ADMET analysis are desired for optimal activity of the compound(s). Using in silico molecular docking, molecular dynamics simulations, and in vitro experiments, it was demonstrated that PGG strongly binds to the DENV-2 E protein and may potentially block the host entry. The in silico binding predictions were validated by biolayer interferometry (BLI) based protein–ligand-binding analysis, revealing PGG’s high affinity for the E protein VLPs, as depicted in its low KD values, calculated for two independent sites. The observed K D values may correspond to the two sites described and used in the in silico docking experiments. Nonetheless, they do confirm high-affinity, multiple-site binding of E protein by the test compounds, supporting their possible inhibitory function. While PGG demonstrated promising results, other analogues, including monogalloylglucose, digalloylglucose, trigalloylglucose, and tetragalloylglucose, also exhibited in silico binding with significant affinities. To confirm the effectiveness and safety of PGG and other analogues, additional preclinical and clinical assessments are required in the future. Research into new antiviral treatments remains essential to mitigate the impact of this debilitating illness, as dengue continues to be a serious health concern globally.

4. Conclusions

The successful expression, purification, and refolding of DENV-2 envelope protein was achieved in , yielding high-quality, well-characterized VLPs for subsequent protein–ligand binding assays. The in silico docking and MD simulation studies demonstrated strong binding affinities of galloylglucose molecules (mono, di, tri, tetra, and pentagalloylglucose) for the DENV-2 envelope protein. These findings not only provide valuable insights into identifying viable dengue antiviral candidates but also highlight the influence of the nature and the number of galloyl groups on their possible antiviral activity. Among the five tested galloylglucose molecules, pentagalloylglucose (PGG) emerged as the most promising candidate, demonstrating lower binding scores in molecular docking studies and comparatively stable interactions within the E protein-PGG complex as revealed in MD simulation studies performed at 100 and 500 ns. In addition to that, PGG showed strong and moderate electrostatic complementarity for E protein sites at grids 1 and 2, respectively. The estimated EC scores of PGG matched those of CA and were slightly better than epigallocatechin-3-gallate (EGCG). Based on these results, PGG was selected for further in vitro protein-binding studies. The in vitro binding assay, performed using biolayer interferometry (BLI), demonstrated strong binding of E protein VLPs by PGG, further supporting its potential as a host-entry inhibitor. Furthermore, PGG has reportedly demonstrated diverse pharmacological properties, including antiviral, antibacterial, anti-inflammatory, anticarcinogenic, antidiabetic, and antioxidant activities. PGG has shown good tolerance profiles in mice and prairie dogs, further supporting its use as a repurposed medication for the treatment of viral infections. In addition to PGG, trigalloylglucose also emerges as a promising alternative. Its smaller size than that of PGG and fewer galloyl groups may impact antiviral efficacy while offering advantages such as improved solubility, reduced toxicity, and enhanced pharmacokinetics. However, further experimental and clinical investigations are necessary to fully characterize and validate the antiviral potential of trigalloylglucose.

5. Materials and Methods

5.1. Protein and Gene Sequences for In Silico and In Vitro Experiments

The protein PDB ID 1OAN was used for the retrieval of the protein sequence of the Envelope protein of Dengue virus serotype 2. The selected amino acid sequence was reverse-translated into a coding gene sequence with codon bias using an online tool (https://www.bioinformatics.org/sms2/rev_trans.html). The gene DNA was commercially synthesized and cloned (Biotech Desk Pvt Ltd.) in plasmid pET22b between NdeI and XhoI restriction sites, producing a recombinant plasmid pET22b-E-DENV-2.

5.2. Plasmid and Bacterial Strains

The plasmid pET22b­(+) vector (Novagen), was used for cloning and expression of the vaccine candidate proteins in . The vector and recombinant plasmid were propagated in the DH5α strain of (Invitrogen), whereas the recombinant proteins were expressed in the BL21 (DE3) strain of (Invitrogen).

5.3. Protein Expression and Purification

The recombinant envelope protein of DENV-2 was produced in BL21 (DE3) cells. In brief, the competent BL21 (DE3) cells were transformed with recombinant plasmid pET22b-E-DENV-2. The transformed single colony was used to inoculate 5 mL of LB medium supplemented with 100 μg/mL ampicillin, and the cells were grown overnight at 37 °C with continuous shaking at 150 rpm. The next day, 400 mL terrific broth (TB) medium, supplemented with ampicillin, was inoculated with 1% overnight-grown culture of cells and allowed to grow under the same conditions as for the primary culture. At an optical density (OD) of 0.8, the culture was induced by adding 0.8 mM IPTG and incubated further for 5 h with regular shaking at 200 rpm. After 5 h, the cells were harvested by centrifugation, and the cell pellet was resuspended in lysis buffer (50 mM Tris-Cl, 5 mM EDTA, pH 8.0) supplemented with 1 mM PMSF. The cells were lysed by sonication using an ultrasonic processor (Cole-Parmer) through 2 cycles of 5 min each with a program of 20 s ON/20 s OFF pulse at 50% amplitude. The cell lysate was centrifuged at 9500 rpm for 30 min. The resulting pellet containing inclusion bodies was washed twice with washing buffer (1% sodium deoxycholate, 2% Triton X-100, 50 mM Tris-Cl, and 5 mM EDTA) and finally washed with MiliQ water. The insoluble IB pellet was resuspended in solubilizing buffer A (50 mM Tris-Cl, 8 M Urea, pH 7.4) and centrifuged at 9500 rpm for 30 min. The supernatant was collected and supplemented with 20 mM imidazole to prevent nonspecific binding, then loaded onto a Ni-NTA agarose column pre-equilibrated with the same solubilizing buffer. The unbound protein fraction was collected, and the column was washed three times with washing buffer (50 mM Tris-Cl, 8 M Urea, 40 mM Imidazole, 300 mM NaCl, pH 7.4) to remove nonspecifically bound proteins. Finally, the target protein was eluted using an elution buffer (50 mM Tris-Cl, 8 M Urea, 100 mM Imidazole, pH 7.4). The eluted protein fractions were analyzed by 12% SDS-PAGE.

5.4. In Vitro Protein Refolding

The denatured recombinant protein was in vitro refolded, following a modified version of the protocol described earlier. Briefly, all eluted protein fractions were pooled and diluted to a final concentration of 0.4 mg/mL, followed by reduction with 10 mM dithiothreitol (DTT) at room temperature for 1 h. The reduced protein was then subjected to sequential dialysis against denaturing buffer (50 mM Tris-Cl, 50 mM NaCl, 8 M urea, 1 mM EDTA, pH 8.0) under continuous stirring at 4 °C for 6–8 h. To facilitate stepwise renaturation, the urea concentration was systematically reduced (8 → 6 → 4 → 2 M) via successive dialysis steps. Subsequently, the protein solution was transferred to an oxidizing refolding buffer (50 mM Tris-Cl, 50 mM NaCl, 1 M urea, 375 μM oxidized glutathione, and 400 mM l-arginine, pH 8.0) to promote proper oxidative folding. The urea concentration was progressively decreased (1 → 0.5 → 0.25 → 0 M), keeping the other components of the buffer the same, followed by the gradual removal of oxidizing agents in four additional dialysis steps. In the final step, the refolded protein was dialyzed using a buffer (50 mM Tris-Cl and 50 mM NaCl, pH 8.0). Each dialysis step was performed under constant slow stirring for 6–8 h to ensure efficient protein refolding. The monomeric and oligomeric states of the refolded E protein were assessed via SDS-PAGE under reducing and nonreducing conditions, respectively. The refolding percentage of the protein was calculated using the following formula

5.5. Western Blotting

To confirm the identity of the purified refolded recombinant protein, Western blotting was performed by using the anti-DENV E protein antibody directed against domain III. The refolded protein sample was resolved on a 12% acrylamide gel by SDS-PAGE and transferred onto the nitrocellulose membrane. The membrane was blocked with 5% BSA prepared in Tris-Buffer saline (TBS) for 1 h at room temperature. The membrane was then washed with TBS containing 0.05% Tween-20 (TBST). Afterward, the membrane was treated with mouse anti-DENV EDIII antibody (Invitrogen MA5–30000) at a dilution of 1:5000 (Ab:TBST) and incubated overnight at 4 °C with mild rocking. The next day, the membrane was removed and washed thrice with TBST. The membrane was further incubated with a goat antimouse HRP conjugated antibody (Thermo-Scientific 31430) at a dilution of 1:10000 for 1 h, followed by three washes of TBST. The blot was developed by ECL (Amersham) HRP substrate solution on a blot scanner (LI-COR) and analyzed by Image Studio Digits Ver 4.0.

5.6. Size Exclusion Chromatography

The in vitro refolded E protein was self-assembled into oligomeric forms, which were further purified based on their molecular weights using size exclusion chromatography (SEC). The purification was performed on an KTA Pure system (GE Healthcare) utilizing a 16/600 Superdex 200 pg column with a total column volume of 120 mL and a flow rate of 0.8–1.0 mL/min. Protein fractions were continuously monitored by ultraviolet (UV) detection at 280 nm, enabling their collection in multiple fractions. Data acquisition and processing were carried out using UNICORN 7.0 software, while SEC chromatographic profiles were plotted using GraphPad Prism 8.0.1.

5.7. Characterization of In Vitro Refolded Protein Using FTIR, CD, and DLS

Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy, circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy, and dynamic light scattering (DLS) analyses were performed to characterize the refolded protein. FTIR spectroscopy was used to identify functional groups in the refolded protein sample, ensuring proper folding. The protein sample in the buffer was mixed with KBr solution, and the spectrum was recorded using an FTIR Spectrum-2 (PerkinElmer) within the infrared range of 4000–400 cm–1. CD spectroscopy was conducted to further analyze the secondary-structure elements of the refolded E protein. The measurements were taken for a sample at a protein concentration of 0.1 mg/mL in the UV range 180–300 nm, using a JASCO J-815 CD spectrometer. Spectral data was processed with JASCO spectra manager software as per Yang’s reference. Thereafter, DLS was employed to determine the particle size, polydispersity index (PDI), and size distribution of the refolded protein samples. DLS measurements, based on the diffraction characteristics of monochromatic or laser light scattered through the sample, analyzed scattered light intensities and wavelengths to calculate the molecular size and shape within the range 10–1000 nm using a Malvern Zetasizer Nano system. Data collected for all three techniques were plotted by using GraphPad Prism 8.0.1.

5.8. In Silico Studies

5.8.1. Protein Preparation, Ligand Preparation, and Grid Selection

The two of the structures of envelope glycoprotein (E) of DENV-2 (PDB ID: 1OAN and 3J27), were retrieved from the RCSB Protein Data Bank (https://www.rcsb.org/structure/1oan), whereas, the atomic model (PDB: mD2VLP_3J27) was provided by Dr. Shang-Rung Wu, Associate Professor, School of Dentistry & Institute of Oral Medicine, National Cheng Kung University, Taiwan. All three structures of E dimer (1OAN, 3J27, and mD2VLP_3J27) were prepared for molecular docking using the Protein Preparation Wizard in Maestro Schrödinger. The preparation process included the optimization of missing side chains and energy minimization to ensure structural stability. Ligand structures corresponding to seven compounds [PubChem IDs: 65238 (Pentagalloylglucose), 14464350 (Tetragalloylglucose), 452707 (Trigalloylglucose), 440221 (Digalloylglucose), 4628122 (monogalloylglucose), 72284 (Chebulinic acid) and 65064 (Epigallocatechin-3-gallate)], were obtained from the PubChem database and converted into 3D structures using the LigPrep module in Maestro Schrödinger. LigPrep was employed to refine protonation and ionization states, assign proper bond orders, and generate relevant tautomeric and ionization states for each ligand. Based on prior in silico studies on the DENV-2 E protein binding with chebulinic acid, two potential binding sites were selected as key targets for inhibition. A grid box was generated at these sites using the receptor grid generation tool in Glide (Maestro Schrödinger), with an illustration of the grid positions provided in Figure .

5.8.2. Molecular Docking

The Glide module from the Schrodinger 2019–4 suite Maestro (https://www.schrodinger.com) was used for molecular docking experiments. During the process of docking, the protein was fixed, while the ligands were flexible. The molecular mechanics-generalized born surface area (MM-GBSA) method in Prime was used for rescoring the docked pose of the ligand.

5.8.3. Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion (ADMET), and Toxicity Prediction/Qik Properties

ADMET (absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion, and toxicity) properties were predicted using QikProp. ADMET study is essential for determining a compound’s potential efficacy as a drug since it assesses a compound’s physicochemical characteristics, biological activities, and drug-likeness.

5.8.4. Molecular Dynamics (MD) Simulation Studies

Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were performed to evaluate the stability and reliability of protein–ligand interactions by observing atomic-level movements over time, providing insights into protein conformational changes, folding behavior, and ligand-binding dynamics. Based on docking scores, initially 100 ns MD simulations were carried out using Desmond for six selected complexes (1OAN-14464350, 1OAN-4628122, 1OAN-440221, 1OAN-452707, 1OAN-72284, and 1OAN-65238) at both grid positions to assess early conformational stability. Following these results, extended simulations were conducted for the same six complexes, along with epigallocatechin gallate (EGCG), exclusively at grid position 2 due to its superior stability observed in the 100 ns runs. A 200 ns simulation was performed for five complexes (1OAN-14464350, 1OAN-4628122, 1OAN-440221, 1OAN-452707, and 1OAN-72284), while 500 ns simulations were carried out for two complexes (1OAN-65238 and 1OAN-65064). All systems were solvated using the TIP3P water model within an orthorhombic simulation box with a 10 Å buffer on all sides. A physiological ionic strength of 0.15 M NaCl was maintained, and additional counterions were added to neutralize the system’s net charge. Energy minimization was conducted to relieve steric clashes, followed by equilibration steps with positional restraints. MD simulations were executed under the NPT ensemble at a constant temperature of 300 K using the Nose Hoover chain thermostat and a pressure of 1.01325 bar maintained by the Langevin barostat. A time step of 2 fs and a nonbonded interaction cutoff of 9 Å were applied, following Schrödinger Maestro’s default settings. The OPLS_2005 force field was employed throughout, with energy values recorded every 1.2 ps during the simulation run.

5.8.4.1. Electrostatic Surface Potential (ESP) Analysis

In addition to docking studies, electrostatic surface potential (ESP) maps were generated to visualize the charge distribution across the protein–ligand-binding interfaces and to evaluate electrostatic compatibility. ESP maps were prepared in Schrodinger Maestro 14.2. using the electrostatic potential color scheme. Molecular surfaces were created using the surface tool available in the style toolbox. The generated surfaces were then managed through the manage surfaces panel, and display settings were adjusted by selecting display options, where the color scheme was set to property, followed by selecting electrostatic potential. This approach utilizes the partial atomic charges to compute and visualize the electrostatic potential distribution on the molecular surface, facilitating the interpretation of electrostatic complementarity between the ligands and the binding site. For quantitative evaluation, electrostatic complementarity (EC) scores were calculated using BioLuminate (Schrodinger suite). The analysis was conducted for three protein–ligand complexes (1OAN-65238, 1OAN-72284, and 1OAN-65064) at both grid positions.

5.9. In Vitro DENV-2 E Protein–Ligand-Binding Analysis Using Biolayer Interferometry (BLI)

Biolayer Interferometry is one of the most widely used techniques to analyze biomolecular interactions for assessing kinetic parameters. It is utilized to analyze the binding affinity of molecules under in vitro conditions. The knowledge regarding molecular interactions is the primary part of the field of drug discovery, and in vitro binding studies play a crucial role in predicting the candidate drug’s pharmacological activity. This analysis detects the pattern of interference of the white light, which gets reflected from the layer of protein that has been immobilized on the biosensor’s tip. This pattern is then compared with the pattern of the internal reference layer. With this data, valuable and real-time information on the amount of bound protein on the biosensor tip could be obtained.

In this study, this technique has been utilized to study the biomolecular interaction between the DENV-2 E protein VLPs and compound Penta-O-galloyl-β-d-glucose (PGG). Herein, the PGG hydrate (Sigma-Aldrich) samples were prepared at 4 different concentrations (6.25, 12.5, 25, and 50 μM) in buffer (50 mM Tris, 50 mM NaCl, pH 7.4). After the PGG solutions were prepared, the purified DENV-2 E protein VLPs were immobilized on a Ni-NTA-coated biosensor (Octet, Sartorius). Following this, the protein-coated biosensors were washed with Tris-NaCl buffer containing 0.1% DMSO, and the baseline was set for 60 s. Afterward, the tips of the biosensors were immersed completely in the wells of the black ELISA plate containing various drug concentrations as mentioned above, allowing ligand association for 600 s. Right after the completion of this step, the dissociation step was followed for another 600 s, after which the K D values were calculated by using the Data Analysis Software, considering a 1:2 binding model with k dis as the dissociation constant (s–1) and k a as the association constant (1/Ms). K D is calculated by using the k dis/k a formula. The experiment was also performed with another flavonoid, EGCG (Sigma-Aldrich), as a control, at 4 different concentrations (12.5, 25, 50, and 100 μM), under a similar experimental setup as used in the case of the PGG binding assay. The EGCG, being a reported antidengue inhibitor, was used as a positive control in the current study.
