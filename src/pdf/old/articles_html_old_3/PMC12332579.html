Abstract

The development of heterogeneous reservoirs presents a significant challenge to effective acid stimulation, as conventional acid systems tend to preferentially flow through high-permeability channels while bypassing tighter zones. This study addresses the issue by developing a novel temperature-responsive deep-diversion acidizing system (DDA) that enables efficient redirection of acid into low-permeability formations. The system combines organic acid salts, acid-releasing precursors, buffers, and dispersants into a single formulation capable of in situ phase transformation. Under downhole temperatures ranging from 60 °C to 70 °C, the precursor generates hydrogen ions that trigger the formation of insoluble organic acid particles. These particles temporarily plug high-permeability pathways, allowing the acid to penetrate deeper into the formation. Once the diversion is complete, the generated organic acids gradually dissolve on the carbonate scale, thereby restoring flow channels and achieving a self-cleaning effect. Laboratory tests demonstrate that the DDA system maintains low viscosity under surface conditions for injectability and rapidly converts to a solid-phase plugging agent upon heating, with over 90% of the particles actively contributing to diversion. This study proposes a two-stage injection strategydiverter followed by acidto maximize the plugging effect. The system’s ability to combine deep placement, temporary plugging, and post-treatment self-dissolution offers a highly effective and operationally feasible solution for enhancing stimulation in heterogeneous water-injection wells.

1. Introduction

In recent years, as global energy demand has continuously increased, oil and gas well development has also expanded. − However, it is not feasible to discover new large fields every year. Therefore, to make the best use of limited resourcesespecially to more effectively improve reservoir recoverywellbore acidizing remains a standard stimulation technique for removing blockages and enhancing production. Wellbore acidizing aims to restore or increase oil-well performance by chemically dissolving deposits in the wellbore and adjacent formation. This process typically involves injecting acid into the well to dissolve mineral scale, drilling-mud residues, and other flow-impeding impurities in the rock, thereby improving formation permeability. Depending on formation properties and blockage types, acidizing operations are classified as acid washing, matrix acidizing, or acid fracturing and generally include preflush, acid injection, reaction (soaking), and postflush stages.

Reservoir heterogeneitya fundamental characteristic of oil and gas fieldscauses injected water to preferentially advance through high-permeability zones, leading to poor sweep efficiency, direct communication between injector and producer in high-permeability layers, low water-injection efficiency, and excessively high water cuts. During acidizing, acid overwhelmingly enters high-permeability zones, while low-permeability intervals receive little acid, exacerbating heterogeneity and resulting in suboptimal stimulation of tight zones. Moreover, conventional hydrochloric acid is not suitable for aluminosilicate reservoirs: as early as 1990, Bryant and colleagues noted that HCl treatments in such formations can precipitate hydrated silica, causing secondary formation damage.

To distribute acid uniformly and target low-permeability zones, chemical diversion acidizing is widely employed. Common diverting agents include viscoelastic surfactants, solid particles, emulsions, foams, and polymer solutions. These agents rely on their limited mobility to temporarily plug high-permeability channels and redirect acidan approach analogous to EOR conformance control. However, their poor injectivity often leads to near-wellbore plugging only, preventing deep penetration. Surfactants and polymers also adsorb onto rock surfaces during injection, further hindering fluid entry into tight formations.

In water-injector wells, scaling damage is compounded by temperature and pressure changes, which promote inorganic scale formation, reduce injectivity, and increase injection pressures. Consequently, diversion acidizing is routinely used to maintain injectivity and clear both near-wellbore and deep-formation blockages. Although fibers offer excellent plugging performance, they have low strength, degrade slowly, and cannot seal large fractures. Oil-soluble particles are limited to oil-well applications, and acid-soluble particles require an additional acid treatment step to remove filter cakeand still do not fully degrade. Water-soluble particles suffer from poor acid resistance.

To address these challenges, this paper presents a temperature-responsive deep diverting agent (DDA) specifically designed for injection-well acidization, leveraging the differential solubility of solid organic acids under varying pH conditions. Unlike conventional chemical diversion agents, DDA is a low-viscosity, solid-free solution under surface conditions that, upon exposure to reservoir temperatures (60–80 °C), precipitates insoluble organic acid particles to effect diversion. After acidizing, these particles redissolve inorganic scale into solution, achieving a self-cleaning function. This mechanism avoids the silica precipitation issues associated with HCl in aluminosilicate formations and overcomes the injectivity limitations of traditional diverting materials, enabling deep-zone acid placement and substantially improving stimulation in heterogeneous reservoirs. The DDA system’s optimal performance at 60–80 °C provides a robust theoretical and operational framework for uniform deep-layer acidizing in injection wells.

2. Experiment

2.1. Materials and Instruments

The following reagents and materials were used:

HEDTA3Na, EDTA2Na, gluconolactone, ammonium chloride, trisodium phosphate, hydrochloric acid, NH4Cl, NaHCO3, and CaCl2analytical grade. Xanthan gum, sodium alginate, quartz sand, phenolic resin, and sodium gluconateindustrial grade.

The following equipment and instruments were used:

DF-101S heating-type constant temperature magnetic stirrer, constant temperature water bath, AL204 electronic balance, oven, core displacement device, long core displacement device, Haake Mars III rheometer, hydrothermal reactor, Quanta 450 environmental scanning electron microscope (SEM), and conventional microscope.

2.2. Experiment Methods

(1) Design Concept of DDA Diversion Agent

Heterogeneity is a fundamental characteristic of oil and gas reservoirs, and the area that requires acidizing is typically the damaged zone, i.e., the low-permeability region. Therefore, enabling the acid to effectively penetrate into the low-permeability zone is the key technology to improve acidizing performance. Traditional diversion agents rely on the low mobility of materials, which leads to temporary plugging in the wellbore and near-wellbore areas, making it difficult for the acid to reach the deeper layers of the formation. For injection wells with deep-scale formations, traditional diversion agents tend to be less effective. To address this issue, the DDA designed in this study is characterized by ease of injection, deep diversion capabilities, and environmentally friendly properties.

The developed DDA system consists of four main components: 1) temporary plugging agent (D): HEDTA3Na and EDTA2Na; 2) response agent (R): gluconolactone and ammonium chloride; 3) buffering agent (B): sodium gluconate and trisodium phosphate; and 4) biodegradable dispersant (S): xanthan gum and sodium alginate.

As shown in Figure , the four steps of DDA (deep diversion acidizing) are as follows:

  • 1)

    Inject DDA into the formation;

  • 2)

    Under the response to formation temperature, solid-phase particles precipitate from DDA, creating a temporary blocking effect in the high-permeability zones;

  • 3)

    Inject acid into the formation; the acid enters the low-permeability or damaged zones and performs the deblocking function;

  • 4)

    Once the acid is consumed, the solid-phase particles from the temporary plugging agent further dissolve carbonate scales in the formation, turning into a solution.

As shown in Figure , the mechanism of action of the DDA system is as follows:

  • 1)

    At surface temperature, the response agent and buffering agent collaborate, causing the temporary plugging agent to initially form a water-soluble salt;

  • 2)

    After injection into the formation, the response agent releases H+ ions under high temperatures, reducing the pH of the system. This causes the D agent to transform into a solid state (D-H), thereby achieving the diversion function;

  • 3)

    After the acid is consumed, D-H reacts with carbonate scales and dissolves them, thus achieving a cleaning function.

(2) Preparation Method of DDA

First, a certain amount of clean water was taken, and the biodegradable dispersant (S) was added, stirring until evenly mixed. Then, the buffering agent (B), temporary plugging agent (D), and response agent (R) were added, stirring after each addition until the DDA was uniformly prepared.

(3) DDA Diversion Temperature and Time Test

First, prepare the DDA solution, ensuring that the amount of temporary plugging agent (D) added results in a 20% mass fraction of D-H. Then, take 50 mL of the DDA solution and place it in a water bath at temperatures of 30, 40, 50, 60, 70, 80 °C, and 90 °C, maintaining the temperature for 120 min (the mass fractions (wt %) mentioned in this paper all refer to the percentage relative to the aqueous phase). After the test, the liquid is filtered, and the filtered solid phase is dried at 60 °C for 24 h. Weigh the solid particles formed by the DDA, denoted as m T (g), and calculate the conversion rate Z T (%) at different temperatures using the following formula (the number 10 in the formula represents the theoretical maximum amount of D-H generated from 50 mL of DDA):

ZT=mT10×100% 1

Once the diversion temperature of DDA is obtained, the diversion time at that temperature is tested. A 50 mL portion of the prepared DDA solution was placed at 60 and 70 °C for various time intervals (10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 90, 120 min) under constant temperature conditions. After each time interval, the mass of the solid formed, m T (g), was measured, and the conversion rate Z T was measured using the same formula.

(4) Synergistic Temporary Blocking Effect of Particles and Polymers

An artificial core was prepared by consolidating quartz sand and phenolic resin, with dimensions of 6 cm in length and 2.5 cm in diameter, ensuring that the core did not react chemically with HCl. The initial permeability of the core was tested by using 3% HCl, yielding an experimental core permeability of 565 mD.

1) Short-Core Blocking Effect Experiment

The core displacement device was used for the experiment. First, one core was placed into the device. Then, 60 mL of the DDA solution was injected into the core at a flow rate of 0.5 mL/min, with the temperature maintained at 60 °C. After the DDA injection was completed, the system was kept at a constant temperature for 30 min. Finally, the permeability of the core was tested again with 3% HCl, denoted as K i. The experimental system included the following formulations: 10%D, 15%D, 20%D, 0.05%S, 0.1%S, 0.15%S, 10%D + 0.1%S, 15%D + 0.05%S, 15%D + 0.1%S, 15%D + 0.15%S, 15%D + 0.2%S, and 20%D + 0.1%S (the B and R agents were added to the DDA, but these are omitted in the formula records). The experimental core IDs were s1 to s12.

2) Long-Core Blocking Effect Experiment

A long-core displacement device was used for this experiment, where three cores were placed in the device. The DDA solution was injected into the cores at a flow rate of 1.5 mL/min, maintaining a temperature of 60 °C. After the DDA injection, the cores were removed, and the permeability at three positions (1, 2, and 3) of each core was tested with 3% HCl, yielding K 1i, K 2i, and K 3i.

The experimental system involved the following formulations: 10%D + 0.1%S, 15%D + 0.05%S, 15%D + 0.1%S, and 15%D + 0.15%S.

Injection volumes: 180 mL, 60 mL, and 120 mL for 15%D + 0.1%S.

The experimental core IDs were: g1-1, g1-2, g1-3, g2-1, g2-2, g2-3, g3-1, g3-2, g3-3, g4-1, g4-2, g4-3, g5-1, g5-2, g5-3, g6-1, g6-2, g6-3, g7-1, g7-2, and g7-3.

The core blocking rate m i is calculated using the following formula:

Ri=1−Ki565×100% 2

(5) Parallel Core Diversion Effect Experiment

Two groups of cores with different permeabilities were prepared. The high-permeability core group had a permeability of 565 mD, while the low-permeability core group had a permeability of 72 mD. The two groups of cores were then tested in parallel with three cores in each group.

The experimental procedure is as follows:

The experimental core IDs were h1, h2, and h3 for high-permeability cores and l1, l2, and l3 for low-permeability cores.

  • 1)

    Saturate the cores with 1% NH4Cl and then conduct a flow experiment at an injection rate of 3 mL/min.

  • 2)

    Inject 3% HCl and measure the flow distribution between the two cores.

  • 3)

    Inject 180 mL DDA and then shut off the system and wait for 30 min.

  • 4)

    Inject 3% HCl again to measure the flow distribution after diversion.

  • 5)

    Once the experiment is completed, remove the cores and test the permeability K i of the three sections of each core after blocking.

(6) Acidizing and Blockage Removal Effect Experiment

Core samples were prepared with scale damage. The initial permeability of the cores was 565 mD. At room temperature, they were saturated with a scaling solution (100 mL of water with 12 g of NaHCO3 and 8 g of CaCl2) and then dried in a 60 °C oven. Saturation and drying were repeated multiple times to produce cores with varying damage levels. Six pairs of high-/low-permeability cores were assembled as follows: s1 (428 mD–59 mD), s2 (413 mD–68 mD), s3 (435 mD–289 mD), s4 (431 mD–306 mD), s5 (419 mD–207 mD), and s6 (443 mD–196 mD). These cores were run in parallel for diversion-acidizing simulation at 60 °C and an injection rate of 3 mL/min.

For groups s1, s3, and s5, the protocol comprised five stages:

  • 1.

    Inject 1% NH4Cl to measure initial permeability.

  • 2.

    Inject 60 mL of DDA solution.

  • 3.

    Shut in for 30 min.

  • 4.

    Inject 120 mL of 8% HCl.

  • 5.

    Inject 1% NH4Cl to monitor postacidizing permeability recovery.

Core identifiers: s1-1, s1-2, s3-1, s3-2, s5-1, and s5-2.

For groups s2, s4, and s6, the protocol comprised three stages:

  • 1.

    Inject 1% NH4Cl to measure initial permeability.

  • 2.

    Inject 120 mL of 8% HCl.

  • 3.

    Inject 1% NH4Cl to monitor postacidizing permeability recovery.

Core identifiers: s2-1, s2-2, s4-1, s4-2, s6-1, s6-2. (7) Rheological Testing of the DDA System

To investigate the temperature resistance and shear resistance of the DDA system, rheological tests were conducted using a rheometer. The tests were performed at 60 °C with a shear rate of 0–1000 s–1 to measure the shear viscosity. Additionally, viscosity changes at different temperatures were tested by gradually increasing the temperature to 30, 40, 50, 60, 70, and 80 °C, maintaining a shear rate of 60 s–1, with each temperature point tested for 10 min.

At 60 °C, the system’s viscoelastic modulus was tested at a frequency of 1 Hz, with stress varying from 1 to 15 Pa.

(8) Cleaning Performance of the DDA System

Preparation of DDA solution: 200 mL of DDA solution was prepared and then divided into two 100 mL portions, which were placed into hydrothermal reactors labeled Hy-1 and Hy-2. The reactors were maintained at 60 °C for 120 min, during which solid particles precipitated.

Step 1 (Hy-1): The solution from reactor Hy-1 was filtered, and the solid portion was dried and weighed. The mass was recorded as Hy-m1.

Step 2 (Hy-2): After solid particles precipitated in reactor Hy-2, 10 g of calcium carbonate particles was added, and the mixture was maintained at 60 °C for 78 h. After the reaction, the fluid was filtered again, and the solid part was dried and weighed. The mass was recorded as Hy-m2.

To explore the diversion mechanism and cleaning behavior of the DDA system, the DDA solution was prepared and incubated at 60 °C for 2 h. After incubation, the solid particles were examined under a microscope to observe their morphology, and the liquid portion was observed using an environmental scanning electron microscope (ESEM). Additionally, after 72 h of reaction with calcium carbonate particles, the morphology of the residual liquid was examined using ESEM.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Diversion Temperature and Time Test of DDA

The experimental results of the diversion conversion rate of DDA at different temperatures over 120 min are shown in Figure . As the temperature increases, the conversion rate gradually increases. The conversion rates at 30, 40, 50, 60, 70, 80, and 90 °C were 2.1%, 3.9%, 16.2%, 90.6%, 95.1%, 95.2%, and 95.2%, respectively. This indicates that the diversion temperature of DDA is ≥ 60 °C, and the conversion rate tends to stabilize after 70 °C. The solidification conversion rate of DDA, i.e., the effective rate, can reach over 90%.

The particle precipitation conversion rates of DDA at 60 and 70 °C at different times are plotted in Figure . Figure shows that as time increases, the conversion rate of DDA rises rapidly. At 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 90, and 120 min, the conversion rates at 60 °C were 1.1%, 3.3%, 8.9%, 16.1%, 32.1%, 88.6%, 89.7%, and 90.6%, respectively. At 70 °C, the conversion rates at 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 90, and 120 min were 15.2%, 29.6%, 48.5%, 86.2%, 93.3%, 94.8%, 94.9%, and 95.1%, respectively.

At 60 °C, a sharp increase in the conversion rate starts at 50 min, stabilizing after 60 min. At 70 °C, the sharp increase begins at 30 min, stabilizing after 40 min. As the temperature increases, the conversion rate of the DDA accelerates.

When DDA is used in acidizing operations, high-temperature wells may experience solid-phase precipitation in the wellbore, while medium- and low-temperature wells will experience diversion deeper in the formation. Therefore, when DDA is used in high-temperature wells, a large injection volume must be used to prevent wellbore blockage. DDA can also be used for diversion fracturing. DDA can achieve deep diversion for matrix acidizing at 60 and 70 °C, with solid-phase precipitation reaching its peak after 50 min at 60 °C and 30 min at 70 °C.

3.2. The Synergistic Blocking Effect of Particles and Polymers

The temporary plugging experiment results of DDA on short rock cores are shown in Figures , . Figure presents the results of single-particle and single-polymer solution temporary plugging experiments. As the concentration of the plugging agent increases, the plugging efficiency rapidly increases. The plugging efficiency of D increases from 33.98%, 46.02%, to 50.8% at concentrations of 10%, 15%, and 20%, respectively. For S, at concentrations of 0.05%, 0.1%, and 0.15%, the plugging efficiency increases from 16.64%, 34.87%, to 59.82%. This experiment shows that single particles find it difficult to achieve a high plugging efficiency, likely due to the inability of solid particles to fully match the flow channels in shape. It is well-known that high-concentration polymers have a strong plugging ability, but their injection is difficult. To make injection easier, low-concentration S agents are chosen, but their temporary plugging effect is poorer. The S curve is steeper than the D curve, indicating that changes in polymer concentration have a greater impact on the plugging effect than changes in particle concentration.

The cooperative temporary plugging effect of particles and polymers on rock cores is shown in Figure . After cooperation, the plugging rate quickly increased to over 90%. When the polymer concentration (S) is 0.1%, the particle concentration (D) increases from 10%, 15%, to 20%, and the plugging rate rises from 91.33%, 95.75%, to 97.7%. When the particle concentration (D) is 15%, as the polymer concentration (S) increases from 0.05%, 0.1%, 0.15%, to 0.2%, the plugging rate rises from 88.85%, 95.75%, 98.05%, to 99.12%. This further demonstrates that the polymer concentration (S) has a greater impact on the plugging effect. The single-core experimental results show that the higher the concentration of any temporary plugging agent, the higher the plugging efficiency. However, during oilfield production, the temporary plugging agent will block both high-permeability and low-permeability regions. Therefore, the selection of the temporary plugging agent should be based on multiple factors. The synergistic effect of particles and polymers can rapidly improve the temporary plugging effect, with the polymer aiding particle suspension and filling the gaps between the particles and the pores, while the particles provide structural support, enhancing the system’s strength.

The temporary plugging experimental data of DDA on long rock cores are shown in Table . As shown in Figure , when S = 0.1%, the blocking rate increases with D concentration from 10% to 20%, and all three groups of experiments exhibit the same trend, with the blocking rate of the third section of the core being higher than that of the second section, which in turn is higher than that of the first section. This indicates that all three systems effectively formed deep temporary plugging. Figure shows that when D = 15%, and S concentration increases from 0.05% to 0.15%, the blocking characteristics of the three experimental groups change. When S concentration is 0.05% and 0.1%, the blocking degree of the three sections of the core gradually increases, indicating that deep temporary plugging is formed. However, when the S concentration reaches 0.15%, the blocking degree of the three sections of the core gradually decreases, indicating that deep temporary plugging is not formed. This experiment suggests that a higher polymer concentration leads to easier formation of temporary plugging near the wellbore.

3.3. Parallel Rock Core Deflection Effect Experiment

The experimental curve of the DDA parallel rock core deflection process is shown in Figure . The experiment is divided into four stages: The first stage tests the initial flow split; the second stage involves injecting DDA to test the flow split during the injection process; the third stage waits for the DDA to precipitate the solid phase; and the fourth stage tests the flow split after the DDA deflection.

In the first stage, the flow split ratio of the two groups of rock cores is 2.64 mL–0.36 mL, and the flow split ratio of the two groups is the same as the ratio of their initial permeability. This shows that during fluid injection into the reservoir, Darcy’s law is followed, indicating that in the nonhomogeneous acidizing process, it is necessary to perform deflection treatment. Otherwise, the acid will mostly enter the high-permeability region, exacerbating reservoir heterogeneity and resulting in ineffective acidizing.

In the second stage, the flow split ratio of the two groups of rock cores gradually changes from 2.64 mL to 0.36 mL to 1.83 mL to 1.17 mL. During DDA injection, the two flow curves of the heterogeneous rock cores gradually approach each other, and as the injection volume increases, the distance between the two curves becomes smaller and eventually stabilizes. The high-permeability rock core group always has a flow rate higher than that of the low-permeability rock core group. This further demonstrates that during the fluid injection process, Darcy’s law is still followed, and the injection of the deflection agent is unable to change the seepage pattern of the heterogeneous reservoir. Therefore, it is not recommended to directly add deflection agents to the acid.

In the fourth stage, the flow split ratio changes to 1.29 mL–1.71 mL, indicating that during the third stage, the solid phase precipitated by DDA blocked the high-permeability channels. This experiment proves that DDA has excellent deflection efficiency in heterogeneous reservoirs.

The permeability of the three rock cores after DDA deflection is shown in Figure . Before deflection, the permeabilities of the high-permeability group and low-permeability group rock cores were 565 and 72 mD, respectively. After deflection, the permeability of the low-permeability group rock cores was 56, 72, and 72 mD, and the permeability of the high-permeability group rock cores was 63, 46, and 42 mD. The plugging rate of the low-permeability group rock cores was 22.2%, 0%, and 0%, respectively, while the plugging rate of the high-permeability group rock cores was 88.85%, 91.86%, and 92.57%, respectively. The plugging intensity of the high-permeability group rock cores was much higher than that of the low-permeability group, and all three high-permeability rock cores formed an efficient plugging state. Only the first segment of the low-permeability group rock core was blocked. After plugging, the permeability of the high-permeability group rock cores was lower than that of the low-permeability group rock cores, indicating that DDA has excellent deep deflection effects.

3.4. Acidizing Unclogging Effect Experiment

During water injection, the injected water heats as it travels from the surface into the formation, which promotes scale deposition. To evaluate the acidizing unclogging effect on cores blocked by scale, parallel-scale-damage core acidizing tests were conducted. Figure shows the diversion acidizing sequence, and Figure shows the conventional acidizing sequence. The blockage removal performance data are listed in Table .

  • (1)

    Stages 1–3 in Figure mirror the diversion experiment results: DDA effectively temporarily plugs the high-permeability core. The permeability of the high-permeability core s1-1 drops from 428 mD to 33 mD, while the low-permeability core s1-2 drops from 59 mD to 46 mD. This demonstrates that most DDA enters the high-permeability core and only a small amount enters the low-permeability core. In Stage 4, with s1-1 still highly plugged, the acid flows predominantly into s1-2, whose permeability jumps from 46 mD to 541 mD, while s1-1 slowly recovers from 33 mD to 68 mD. This further confirms DDA’s excellent diversion acidizing effect; the muted response of s1-1 reflects DDA’s insolubility in acid. In stage 5, s1-2 permeability increases further from 541 mD to 559 mD, and s1-1 from 68 mD to 543 mD, indicating that DDA slowly dissolves in water and partially dissolves scale. This proves DDA’s strong diversion acidizing performance.

  • (2)

    In Figure , during conventional acidizing, s2-1s permeability remains steady and then rapidly increases, whereas s2-2 stays low throughoutlikely due to CO2 generation reducing acid mobility. After acidizing, s2-1 and s2-2 recover from 413 mD to 547 mD and from 68 mD to 81 mD, corresponding to improvement factors of 1.324 and 1.191. In the diversion groups (s1, s3, s5), low-permeability cores of 59 mD, 207 mD, and 289 mD recover to 559 mD, 551 mD, and 553 mD, respectivelydemonstrating that diversion acidizing effectively removes scale damage in injection wells.

3.5. The Rheological Properties of the DDA System

Viscous fluids have dispersibility, which is why the DDA system performs deep temporary plugging through the synergistic action of self-generated particles and viscous fluid. The dispersibility of the viscous fluid helps suspend, disperse, and carry the self-generated particles into the deep formation to enhance the system’s deep diversion effect. However, high-viscosity fluids are difficult to inject into the formation, while low-viscosity, slow-release particles are easier to inject and can achieve deep diversion. The rheological properties and viscoelastic modulus of the DDA system were tested by using the Haake Mars III rheometer. Figure shows that the viscosity of DDA gradually decreases with shear rate from 0 to 1000 s–1, which is a characteristic of composite polymer solutions. At a shear rate of 10 s–1, the viscosity is approximately 53 mPa·s; at 60 s–1, it is about 23 mPa·s; and at 170 s–1, it is approximately 13 mPa·s. The DDA system has a relatively low viscosity, which is beneficial for deep injection into the formation. As the shear rate increases, the viscosity fluctuates to some extent. From 10 s–1 to 230 s–1, the fluctuations gradually decrease and stabilize, indicating that the solid-phase particles in the system have some influence on the experimental results.

The temporary plugging and diversion process is similar to the enhanced oil recovery (EOR) process, where the plugging agent is injected into the formation at a low rate to ensure that the diversion material is not forced into low-permeability areas at high speeds. Therefore, the rheological properties of the DDA system at different temperatures were tested at a shear rate of 60 s–1. Figure shows that when the temperature is below 60 °C, the viscosity of DDA fluctuates between 10 and 20 mPa·s. When the temperature exceeds 60 °C, the viscosity fluctuates between 20 and 35 mPa·s. This indicates a significant change in DDA at 60 °C, where a large amount of particles precipitates. Throughout the entire experimental temperature range, the viscosity remained fluctuating, which is due to particle precipitation. The fluctuation amplitude from 25 to 60 °C is lower than that from 60 to 80 °C, suggesting that a small amount of particle precipitation occurs at lower temperatures, with 60 °C being the inflection point for DDA, where a significant amount of solid precipitates. This proves that DDA is suitable for deep diversion in formations with temperatures between 60 and 70 °C, and its low viscosity at lower temperatures aids in deep injection into the formation.

The rheological testing shows that DDA has a certain viscosity value, but its visual appearance as a fluid seems inconsistent with this. To investigate further, the viscoelastic modulus of DDA was tested, and the experimental results are shown in Figure . When the stress is below 2 Pa, both G’ and G’’ exhibit strong fluctuations, and when the stress exceeds 6 Pa, G’ and G’’ stabilize. The overall trend shows that G“ is slightly larger than G’, indicating that DDA behaves as a fluid. Throughout the entire test, both G’ and G’’ values are extremely low, around 0.3–0.4 Pa, confirming that the strength of the DDA system is low and further demonstrating that the DDA system is favorable for injection into deep formations.

3.6. Cleanliness of the DDA System

The particles in the DDA system are derived from HEDTA3Na and EDTA2Na, which are water-soluble inorganic salts. The initial state of DDA is an aqueous solution, and upon heating, the responding agent releases acidity, causing HEDTA and EDTA to precipitate and form particles. When calcium carbonate is added, HEDTA and EDTA combine with calcium to form water-soluble calcium salts, HEDTACa and EDTACa. For water injection wells, where scaling is common in the formation, DDA can ultimately achieve a self-cleaning effect by dissolving the scale. The experimental process is shown in Table . If DDA had no cleaning ability, d-2 should precipitate solid material totaling 10 + 9.1 = 19.1 g. However, the actual amount of solid precipitate, m-2, is 1.6 g, which verifies the self-cleaning ability of DDA and its capacity to further dissolve formation calcium carbonate scale.

4. Field Application Analysis

Well W-1 is an offshore water-injector well that experienced severe wellbore plugging after long-term injection. The reservoir has an average permeability of 2000 mD, ranging from 30 to 5000 mD, indicating extreme heterogeneity. In December 2024, a diversion acidizing treatment was carried out using DDA as the diverting agent and fluoboric acid as the acid. The treatment sequence was as follows: pressure test → inject DDA → inject fresh water spacer → inject acid → inject fresh water flush.

Before acidizing, the apparent injectivity index was 160–170 (m3/d)/MPa; after acidizing, it rose to 2200–2800 (m3/d)/MPa, demonstrating effective unblocking. The operational plot (Figure ) shows no leakage during the pressure test. During DDA injection, both pressure and injection rates remained nearly constant, indicating that no solids precipitated and that DDA was able to penetrate deeply into the formation. Upon acid injection, pressure and injection rates both increased, but the injectivity relative to the DDA-treated formation decreased, showing that DDA had precipitated solids in the formation and successfully diverted the acid into lower-permeability zones. In the final stage of acid injection, the injection rate stabilized while pressure dropped sharply, confirming that W-1 achieved effective unblocking.

5. Conclusions

This research presents a technically innovative and field-relevant solution to the long-standing problem of ineffective acid placement in heterogeneous formations. The developed DDA system exhibits a unique temperature-responsive behavior that facilitates deep penetration during injection and efficient channel blocking upon heating. Its low viscosity at room temperature ensures smooth injectivity, while the in situ formation of insoluble particles provides a robust and reversible plugging mechanism in high-permeability zones. Unlike traditional diverting agents that suffer from limited penetration or permanent formation damage, the DDA system offers a dynamic response that enables controlled diversion and post-treatment restoration of flow paths. Laboratory investigations confirm the system’s rapid transformation kinetics, high solid conversion efficiency, and strong diversion capability. The ability to achieve the self-dissolution of precipitated particles further enhances operational reliability and reduces the risk of residual damage. Field trial results validate the system’s effectiveness in improving acid coverage, enhancing stimulation of tight intervals, and boosting overall injection-production performance. This work provides a technically sound and practically applicable method for addressing heterogeneity-related challenges in acidizing operations, with strong potential for widespread application in mature and complex reservoirs.
