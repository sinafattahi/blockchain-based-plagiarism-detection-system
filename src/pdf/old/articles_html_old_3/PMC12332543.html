Abstract

This study presents an improved 3D printing technology that integrates a slurry circulation system (SCS) to optimize the quality of low-viscosity ceramic slurries and address the issue of slurry sedimentation during printing. The use of low-solid-content slurries, traditionally challenging due to sintering shrinkage, is a key feature of this work. The resin solvent completely burns off during sintering, while the remaining solid portion undergoes controlled shrinkage, enabling the production of high-precision ceramics with reduced demands on 3D printer accuracy. Although often avoided in ceramics, this shrinkage can help improve structural accuracy and reduce manufacturing costs. The efficacy of the SCS was validated through fluid dynamics simulations and sedimentation experiments using COMSOL Multiphysics software. The modified technology was used to fabricate bioceramics with various triply periodic minimal surface (TPMS) structures, which were characterized for porosity, pore size, and mechanical properties. In vitro cell experiments demonstrated that the fabricated ceramics had no adverse effects on the survival, proliferation, migration, or osteogenic differentiation of MC3T3-E1 cells, indicating good biocompatibility and osteogenic potential. The SCS significantly reduced slurry sedimentation, improved printing precision, and increased the yield of high-quality ceramics to 97.73%. The resulting bioceramics exhibited high porosity (∼70%) and small pore sizes (∼50 μm), with excellent mechanical properties (compressive strength of 4.00 ± 0.42 MPa and Young’s modulus of 453.00 ± 7.43 MPa for the Gyroid structure). These findings suggest that low-solid-content slurries offer a cost-effective and high-precision solution for 3D-printed bioceramics with promising applications in bone tissue engineering.

1. Introduction

Bone defects are a common medical condition, often resulting from trauma, tumors, and degenerative diseases. − Traditional autografting, however, faces clinical challenges such as limited donor availability and secondary trauma. In this context, bioceramic materials, which exhibit a calcium-to-phosphorus ratio similar to natural bone tissue, controllable biodegradation rates, and excellent mechanical properties, have become a key focus in bone repair material research. Particularly, calcium-based ceramics such as hydroxyapatite (HA) and β-tricalcium phosphate (β-TCP) have shown unique advantages in the field of personalized bone tissue engineering, as their surface microstructures significantly enhance osteoblast adhesion and induce bone apatite deposition. − According to recent studies, bioceramics demonstrate good performance not only in osteogenesis but also in angiogenesis and chondrogenesis. −

With advancements in additive manufacturing technologies, digital light processing (DLP) 3D printing has achieved submicron manufacturing precision of ceramic scaffolds through a layer-by-layer exposure technique. − Compared to traditional injection molding, this technology enables the creation of gradient porous structures and biomimetic trabecular topologies, which promote bone integration after implantation. However, to achieve the industrial-scale application of clinical-grade ceramic scaffolds, a key technical challenge must be addressed: ensuring the stability of the ceramic slurry during the printing process.

In DLP processing, while low-viscosity slurries are advantageous for achieving high-resolution printing, a fundamental contradiction exists between solid content and rheological properties. − When using submicron ceramic particles, although the sedimentation rate decreases, their high specific surface area leads to a sharp increase in slurry viscosity and poor flowability. Additionally, excessively small particle sizes rapidly increase light scattering effects, significantly reducing the penetration of curing light, which in turn compromises printing precision and layer adhesion strength. , Existing studies typically rely on mechanical stirring or the addition of dispersants to maintain slurry uniformity; ,, however, the former introduces bubble problems, while the latter may suppress the photopolymerization reaction. More critically, these methods fail to dynamically regulate the slurry’s rheological properties during continuous printing, leading to noticeable variations in the later stages of printing.

In contrast to traditional methods, the slurry circulation system (SCS) developed in this study generates a laminar shear field through a peristaltic pump (flow rate of 2.5 mL/s), effectively reducing the sedimentation rate of micrometer-sized ceramic particles while avoiding vortex formation. This design addresses three critical issues: (1) it allows real-time adjustment of flow rates to suit different equipment and printing tasks; (2) the layer-by-layer exposure characteristic of DLP printing ensures that the slurry flow rate at the curing site approaches zero, eliminating flow-induced interference with exposure precision; (3) the modular design is compatible with commercial DLP equipment, offering a cost-effective solution to the slurry sedimentation issue. This physical regulation approach avoids the side effects of dispersants, potentially providing a new paradigm for the industrial-scale production of high-performance, low-cost bone scaffolds.

This study systematically evaluates the physicochemical properties of three types of TPMS hydroxyapatite-45S5 bioglass ceramic scaffolds fabricated with the assistance of the SCS, using multiscale characterization techniques, including scanning electron microscopy, X-ray diffraction analysis, and mechanical property testing. Additionally, in vitro experiments were conducted using MC3T3-E1 cells, a preosteoblastic cell line derived from mouse calvaria. These cells are widely used in bone tissue engineering research due to their well-characterized osteogenic potential, including the expression of markers such as alkaline phosphatase (ALP) and the ability to form mineralized extracellular matrix under osteoinductive conditions. , The results demonstrated that the printed scaffolds exhibit excellent biocompatibility, with elastic moduli and compressive strengths matching those of trabecular bone. This work not only resolves the slurry stability issue in DLP printing but also provides theoretical and technical support for the development of personalized bone repair implants that combine high precision and biological activity.

2. Materials and Methods

2.1. Materials

In this investigation, the following substances were employed: hydroxyapatite (HA, needlelike, 20 nm, Macklin, China), 45S5 bioglass (BG, Chemical, China), photosensitive water-washing resin (Anycubic, China), multilayer graphene (Tanfeng, China), MEM-α medium (Gibco, USA), 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS, Pricella Biotechnology Co., Ltd., China), penicillin-streptomycin (Gibco, USA), 4% paraformaldehyde (Leagene Biotechnology Co., Ltd., China), PBS (Pricella Biotechnology Co., Ltd., China), and MC3T3-E1 cells (provided at no cost by the Stem Cell Center of Guizhou Medical University).

2.2. Methods

2.2.1. Preparation of Ceramic Slurry

HA and 45S5 bioglass were individually mixed with a photosensitive water-washing resin as the solvent to form bioceramic powders. These powders were then combined with resin to create a ceramic slurry. A small amount of multilayer graphene (G) powder was added to the slurry as a dispersant. The mass ratio of HA, BG, G, and resin was 40:10:0.2:100. The mixed slurry was placed in an agate jar and subjected to planetary ball milling for 24 h to thoroughly grind the solid particles within the slurry.

2.2.2. Computed Fluid Dynamics Simulation

Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulation was performed using COMSOL Multiphysics 6.1 (COMSOL, Inc., Massachusetts, USA). Given that the flow direction of the liquid during slurry circulation was from the inlet to the outlet (as indicated by the red arrows in Figure e), the sagittal plane of the printer was extracted to simplify the 3D model into a 2D model, thereby reducing the computational load. The “Mixture ModelLaminar Flow” module in COMSOL Multiphysics 6.1 was employed to simulate the sedimentation behavior of the slurry under both noncirculation and circulation conditions.

2.2.3. Sedimentation Experiments of the Slurry

To evaluate the stability of the slurry before and after improvement, sedimentation experiments were conducted to visually compare the differences in sedimentation rates. A total of 500 mL of the prepared slurry was continuously shaken to ensure uniformity. From this, 30 mL of slurry was extracted and placed in a 50 mL transparent centrifuge tube labeled as 10# at 25 °C room temperature. After 1 h, another 30 mL of the continuously shaken slurry was extracted and placed into a centrifuge tube labeled as 9#. This process was repeated hourly up to the 10th hour, resulting in 10 centrifuge tubes, each representing sedimentation times ranging from 1 to 10 h. The supernatant in each centrifuge tube was carefully removed, allowing for the collection of the sedimented portion of the slurry in the tube.

2.2.4. Design of TPMS Porous Bioceramics

To create computer models for printing bioceramics, TPMS structures were designed using nTopology 4.22 (nTopology, Inc., USA). Three types of TPMS structures were employed: Gyroid, Diamond, and Schwarz-P. The designed ceramics were sliced by using Anycubic PhotonWorkshop software. Due to the volume shrinkage caused by the volatilization of resin and multilayer graphene during the sintering process, the linear sintering shrinkage ratio was determined to be 39% based on preliminary testing. Consequently, the green body models were scaled up linearly by a factor of 1/(1 – 39%), equivalent to 164%, within Anycubic PhotonWorkshop software.

2.2.5. 3D Printing of Ceramic Green Bodies

The overall process of ceramic preparation is shown in Scheme . Using the above proportions, ceramic green bodies were printed with the Anycubic M5S photopolymerization 3D printer, assisted by an SCS. Prior to printing, a custom-designed SCS compatible with the printer’s resin tank was fabricated using 3D printing (Figure ). The system included an inlet tube (Figure a), an outlet tube (Figure b), a diverter (Figure d), and a peristaltic pump. Both the inlet and outlet tubes were divided into eight subchannels at the entry and exit points. Each subchannel was designed with identical diameters and lengths to ensure uniform flow distribution.

The printing parameters were as follows: 5 bottom layers with a bottom exposure time of 200 s to ensure successful adhesion of the ceramic green bodies to the printing plate. The normal exposure time for layers was set to 4.0 s. Other printing parameters followed the default settings for water-washing resin: layer thickness of 50 μm and light-off time of 0.5 s. After printing, the ceramic green bodies were cleaned using deionized water to remove uncured slurry from the internal pores.

2.2.6. Materials Characterization of Ceramics

The green body was sintered at 1300 °C to obtain the final ceramic product, with the sintering curve provided in our previous study. First, the overall structure of the ceramic was observed with a stereomicroscope. For the theoretical density-based calculation, the porosity was derived using the formula:

Porosity=(1−ρbulkρtheoretical)×100%

where ρbulk represents the bulk density of the ceramic, calculated as ρbulk= W d/V, with W d denoting the dry weight of the specimen and V its geometric volume. The theoretical density (ρtheoretical) was obtained from crystallographic data or literature values for the fully dense ceramic composition. Measurements were performed on five parallelepiped specimens (φ = 5 mm, h = 5 mm) to ensure statistical reliability, with all dimensions verified using a digital micrometer (±1 μm accuracy).

The compressive strength was tested (Figure S1) using a universal materials testing machine (Meters Industrial Systems, Inc., China), and Young’s modulus was calculated based on the initial slope of the stress–strain curve. X-ray diffraction (XRD) spectra were obtained in the 2θ range of 5–40° using a Rigaku MAX-2600 X-ray diffractometer (Japan) with CuKα radiation. The crystallinity was calculated using the formula: [A cr/(A cr + A am)] × 100%, where A cr represents the area under the crystalline peaks and A am represents the area under the amorphous region.

2.2.7. Preparation of Bioceramic Extracts

The preparation of bioceramic extracts followed the ISO 10993-12 standard. Bioceramic samples (Figure S2) were sterilized by high-temperature steam sterilization and then immersed in serum-free MEM-α medium at a mass-to-volume ratio of 1 g/mL. The samples were incubated at 37 °C for 24 h. The resulting extracts were sterilized by filtration using a 0.22 μm filter membrane, aliquoted, and stored at 4 °C for use within 7 days.

2.2.8. Biocompatibility Test

Unless otherwise specified, all cells were cultured in MEM-α medium supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 1% penicillin-streptomycin with or without ceramic extract solution. Cultures were maintained in a humidified incubator at 37 °C with 5% CO2 and passaged at 80–90% confluence. In this study, MC3T3-E1 cells were employed to evaluate the in vitro biocompatibility of the ceramic scaffolds. All image-based quantitative analyses for in vivo experiments were performed using ImageJ software (NIH, Bethesda, USA).

Live/Dead staining: exponentially growing cells were seeded into 6-well plates at a density of 5 × 105 cells per well. The experimental group (EG) was cultured in medium supplemented with bioceramic extracts, while the control group (CG) was cultured in a standard medium. Staining was performed on days 1, 3, and 5 postseeding using 2 μM Calcein-AM (a live cell marker) and 4 μM propidium iodide (PI, a dead cell marker), followed by a 30 min incubation. After staining, cell images were captured using a fluorescence microscope.

Proliferation assessment (CCK-8 and EdU assays): cells in the logarithmic growth phase were seeded at a density of 5 × 103 cells/well in a 96-well plate with five replicates for each group. After incubation for 1, 3, and 5 days, 100 μL of culture medium containing 10% CCK-8 reagent was added to each well and incubated for 1 h. Absorbance (OD value) was measured at 450 nm using a microplate reader (BioTek, Synergy H1). Cells in the logarithmic growth phase were seeded at a density of 5 × 105 cells/well in a 6-well plate. After 48 h of incubation, the cells were incubated with 10 μM EdU solution for 2 h, followed by fixation with 4% paraformaldehyde. The EdU Click reaction was performed according to the manufacturer’s instructions. EdU labeling was observed under a fluorescence microscope, and the percentage of EdU-positive cells was calculated.

Migration ability assessment (Scratch and Transwell Assay): cells in the logarithmic growth phase were seeded at a density of 5 × 105 cells/well in a 6-well plate. The experimental group was treated with extract-treated culture medium, while the control group was cultured with plain culture medium. Cells were cultured until 100% confluence was achieved. A uniform scratch was made in the cell monolayer using a sterile 200 μL pipet tip. The cells were washed with PBS to remove floating cells and then observed under an inverted microscope. After 24 h of incubation with extract-treated medium containing 2% FBS and plain culture medium, respectively, the scratch closure was observed again, and the wound healing rate was calculated. Transwell chambers with an 8 μm pore size (Corning, USA) were placed in a 24-well plate. After digestion and centrifugation, cells were resuspended in a serum-free medium. A total of 2 × 104 cells were seeded into the upper chamber, while the lower chamber contained 600 μL of extract-treated medium with 2% FBS and plain culture medium as chemoattractants. The Transwell chambers were incubated at 37 °C in a 5% CO2 incubator for 24 h. After incubation, cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde, washed twice with PBS, and stained with 0.1% crystal violet for 10 min. After PBS washing to remove excess dye, the cells in the upper chamber were gently wiped off with a cotton swab. After natural air-drying, the cells in the lower chamber were observed under a microscope, and the number of migrated cells was counted.

2.2.9. Osteogenic Differentiation Assessment

The logarithmic phase cells were seeded at 2 × 105 cells per well in a 6-well plate and cultured in osteogenic induction medium consisting of MEM-α + 10% FBS + 10 mM β-glycerophosphate + 50 μg/mL ascorbic acid-2-phosphate + 10 nM dexamethasone. For the experimental group, the osteogenic induction medium was treated with extracts. The cells were cultured for 14 days.

ALP and ARS staining: cells were stained with Alizarin Red S and Alkaline Phosphatase kits following the manufacturer’s protocols, and images were captured.

qPCR: total RNA was extracted using the Trizol reagent, and cDNA was synthesized using the TaKaRa cDNA synthesis kit (RR047A, TaKaRa). A 10 μL reaction system was prepared using Fast Taq qPCR SYBR Green Mix, and gene expression levels of Col1 and OPN were quantified using the CFX96 real-time PCR system (Bio-Rad). GAPDH was used as an internal reference, and relative expression levels were calculated using the 2–ΔΔCT method (Tables S1 and S2).

Immunofluorescence: cells were cultured on coverslips placed in 6-well plates. After 14 days of induction, cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde, permeabilized with 0.5% Triton-X 100 for 15 min, and blocked with 3% BSA in PBS for 30 min. Primary antibodies (COL1, OPN) (COL1 1:100, Proteintech, 66716-1-lg; OPN 1:100, Proteintech, 80912-4-RR) were incubated overnight at 4 °C. On the following day, secondary fluorescent antibodies (Huaan, China, HA1121 and HA1126) were added and incubated for 1 h at room temperature. After DAPI staining, slides were mounted with antifade mounting medium (Solelybio, S2100–5 mL). Fluorescence intensity of osteogenic markers was quantified using a fluorescence microscope.

Western blot (WB): cells were lysed using 80 μL of RIPA buffer containing 1% PMSF to extract total protein. Protein concentration was quantified using the Bicinchoninic Acid assay. Equal amounts of protein were separated by SDS-PAGE and transferred to PVDF membranes. After blocking with 5% nonfat milk, membranes were incubated overnight with primary antibodies against COL1 and OPN (1:2500) and GAPDH as an internal control. Secondary antibody incubation was performed at room temperature for 1 h. Protein bands were visualized using an enhanced ECL kit (PK10003, Proteintech) and imaged using a ChemiDoc imaging system (Qinxiang, Shanghai). Gray value quantification was performed for further analysis.

2.2.10. Statistical Analysis

All experiments were repeated three times or more (n ≥ 3). Parametric statistical tests were applied under the assumption of a normal data distribution based on previous studies. Differences between two groups were assessed using Student’s t test, and comparisons among multiple groups were evaluated using one-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s post hoc test. All data are expressed as the mean ± standard deviation (SD). Statistical analyses were performed using SPSS 22.0 and GraphPad Prism 9.0.

3. Results

3.1. Computed Fluid Dynamics Simulation

As shown in Figure b,c, fluid dynamics simulations conducted using COMSOL Multiphysics 6.1 indicate that the introduction of the SCS significantly slowed down the sedimentation rate of the hydroxyapatite suspension. Under noncirculation conditions, significant sedimentation was observed after 5 h, and clear stratification occurred after 10 h (Figure c). In contrast, under circulation conditions, the slurry exhibited minimal sedimentation at the beginning of printing, and by 5 h into the printing process, sedimentation and flow reached dynamic equilibrium, with no significant changes observed even after 10 h of printing (Figure b).

3.2. Sedimentation Experiment

The sedimentation experiment results were consistent with those of the fluid dynamics simulations (Figure a). Under the condition without the circulation system, the slurry exhibited noticeable sedimentation within the first hour, and after 10 h, the sedimentation rate (sediment depth in the reservoir/total slurry depth in the reservoir × 100%) reached 13.3%. In contrast, the slurry with the circulation system showed a much lower sedimentation rate of only 1.6% over the same observation period, demonstrating the advantage of the circulation system in slowing down the sedimentation rate. After completing the sedimentation period, the centrifuge tube was emptied and allowed to stand for 1 h, during which a sludge-like sediment of ceramic slurry was observed at the bottom, as marked by the red dashed line.

3.3. 3D Printing and Sintering of Bioceramics

Using the improved 3D printing method described above, three types of TPMS porous structures (shown in Figure a) of HA-BG bioceramics were successfully fabricated. The printing process took less than 2 h per cycle, resulting in 220 ceramic green bodies. Except for a few green bodies near the circulation system inlet that showed deformities, the remaining ceramic green bodies were well-formed, achieving an average yield rate of 97.73% (Figure b). The sintered ceramics exhibited a uniform white appearance without noticeable deformation or cracking. After sintering, the porosities of the Gyroid, Diamond, and Schwarz-P ceramics were 67.9 ± 1.6%, 69.3 ± 1.4%, and 70.5 ± 1.7%, respectively. The pore size of the Gyroid ceramics reached approximately 50 μm, demonstrating the high precision of the printing method. The stereomicroscope images are shown in Figure c,d.

3.4. Material Characterization

XRD spectrum analysis (Figure d) showed that both HA and BG were preserved before and after sintering, with high-temperature sintering at 1300 °C promoting the formation of a multiphase material system. Scanning electron microscope (SEM) images, as shown in Figure a, b, c, e, revealed that all three ceramic structures possessed highly uniform Gyroid porous structures with well-connected pores, supporting material exchange inside and outside cells. The pore size ranged from 50 to 200 μm, which is suitable for cellular applications. At higher magnification, the nanostructural features of hydroxyapatite crystals were observed, with dense crystal structures achieved by sintering at 1300 °C.

3.5. Mechanical Properties Testing

The mechanical properties of the ceramics were evaluated through compression testing, and a typical stress–strain curve was plotted (Figure f). As shown in Figure g, the compressive strength of the solid cylindrical samples was 2.19 ± 0.34 MPa, while the Gyroid structure exhibited a compressive strength of 4.00 ± 0.42 MPa, the Diamond structure 3.47 ± 0.44 MPa, and the Schwarz structure 1.61 ± 0.37 MPa. The Young’s modulus was calculated from the initial slope of the stress–strain curve (Figure h). For the solid samples, it was 202.10 ± 16.51 MPa, for the Gyroid structure 453.00 ± 7.43 MPa, for the Diamond structure 341.50 ± 22.31 MPa, and for the Schwarz-P structure 303.40 ± 14.63 MPa.

3.6. Biocompatibility Test

Live/Dead staining (Figure a,c): on days 1, 3, and 5, the cells in both the experimental group (EG) and the control group (CG) predominantly exhibited green fluorescence, indicating live cells. The cell viability in both groups exceeded 95%, and no significant differences in cell activity were observed between the two groups at any time point (p > 0.05).

EdU staining (Figure b,e): the proportion of proliferating cells in the EG was 34.97 ± 9.65%, while the CG exhibited a proliferating cell proportion of 29.68 ± 6.25%. No significant difference was observed between the two groups in terms of proliferating cell proportion (p > 0.05).

CCK-8 assay (Figure i): the OD values for the EG were 0.46 ± 0.03, 0.88 ± 0.02, and 1.84 ± 0.11 on days 1, 3, and 5, respectively. For the CG, the corresponding OD values were 0.46 ± 0.01, 0.93 ± 0.05, and 1.77 ± 0.04. No significant differences in OD values were observed between the two groups at any time point (p > 0.05).

Transwell assay (Figure d,h): the number of migrated cells in the EG was 129.60 ± 10.97, while the CG showed 131.60 ± 20.50 migrated cells. No significant difference in cell migration was observed between the two groups (p > 0.05).

Scratch assay (Figure f,g): the area closure rate of the EG was 56.90 ± 9.53% at 0 h and 53.41 ± 7.51% at 24 h. No significant difference in the scratch healing rate was observed between the two groups (p > 0.05).

3.7. Osteogenic Differentiation Experiments

ALP and ARS staining (Figure a): both the EG and CG showed good ALP activity and mineralized nodule formation, with no significant differences between the groups, indicating no adverse effects of the ceramics on osteogenic differentiation.

qPCR analysis (Figure b): no statistically significant differences were observed in the expression levels of OPN and COL-1 between the EG and CG (ns), further validating that the ceramics do not affect osteogenesis-related gene expression.

Western blot analysis (Figure c,d): protein levels of OPN and COL-1 were comparable between the EG and CG (ns).

Immunofluorescence staining (Figure e,f): both groups exhibited strong COL-1 (red) and OPN (green) signals. Statistical analysis of fluorescence intensity revealed no significant differences between the groups (ns).

4. Discussion

The core objective of this study is to explore the role of the SCS in the 3D printing process of a low-viscosity ceramic slurry through computer simulations and actual sedimentation experiments, with the aim of improving the quality and stability of ceramic printing. The experimental results indicate that the SCS significantly prevents the sedimentation of the ceramic slurry throughout the printing process, thereby enhancing print quality and improving the accuracy and uniformity of the final product. It is worth noting that the flow rate setting of the circulation system is crucial; low flow rates lead to slow sedimentation, while excessively high flow rates cause slurry movement during the curing process, which can result in curing failure and deformation of the ceramic green body. Based on our preliminary experiments, a flow rate of 150 mL/min was found to be optimal for our 3D printer (Anycubic M5S), effectively preventing slurry sedimentation while ensuring a high yield (97.73%). In addition to the ceramic printing procedure mentioned in the Methods section, we also conducted a printing experiment lasting over 7 h (using a 10 μm layer thickness for higher Z-axis precision). Under the condition that the circulation system was active, no significant sedimentation of the slurry occurred, confirming that the circulation system effectively maintains slurry stability during extended 3D printing processes. To further evaluate the effectiveness of the SCS in maintaining slurry homogeneity, we conducted a comparative sedimentation study involving three different stabilization strategies: natural sedimentation without intervention, dispersant addition (4% w/v BYK-111), and the SCS system (Figure S3). The results clearly demonstrate that, over a 10 h period, the slurry treated with SCS exhibited the lowest sedimented mass percentage, significantly outperforming both the untreated group and the dispersant-stabilized group. While dispersants can partially inhibit particle settling by improving surface charge repulsion or viscosity, their effectiveness is limited in low-solid-content ceramic slurries and may introduce cytotoxicity or complicate sintering behavior. In contrast, the SCS provides a physical stabilization mechanism by periodically redistributing particles through recirculation without the need for chemical additives or continuous agitation. This noninvasive and energy-efficient approach enables a more consistent particle suspension over time, which is particularly beneficial for extended printing sessions and industrial-scale applications. These findings support the practical advantages of SCS in preserving slurry stability and improving the reliability of ceramic DLP printing. Furthermore, the fluid dynamics simulations conducted with COMSOL software provided theoretical support for this design, validating the accuracy of the experimental setup and offering a theoretical foundation for the future optimization of the printing process.

Shrinkage during sintering is commonly regarded as a drawback in ceramic printing due to the potential risks of deformation or cracking. Recent studies have highlighted the feasibility of using controlled shrinkage to improve dimensional fidelity and structural performance. , In contrast, our study proposes an alternative strategy: the linear shrinkage of ceramic sintering is not only compensated for but actively utilized as a manufacturing simplification tool. For example, if a final pore size of 50 μm is required and the shrinkage ratio is predictable (e.g., 50%), the pore size can be initially designed as 100 μm in the CAD model. This approach relies on maintaining slurry homogeneity through the use of stabilization strategies such as SCS, allowing for the fabrication of high-precision ceramics beyond the resolution limits of the 3D printer. Conceptually, this differs from traditional shrinkage suppression techniques by redefining shrinkage as a tunable process parameter rather than a technical limitation.

In addition to improving slurry uniformity and print resolution, the potential challenges in scaling up the SCS system for industrial applications should be carefully considered. First, although the recirculation pump consumes relatively low power, continuous or long-term use may increase energy demands in large-scale production environments. Second, the slurry circulation loop may face maintenance issues, such as clogging, deposition of particulate matter, or degradation of tubing materials over time. These factors could affect system reliability and require periodic cleaning or part replacement. Third, the integration of an external circulation system requires structural compatibility with existing DLP printers, which may vary widely in terms of build volume, resin vat design, and firmware control. To address these limitations, our system was designed with modularity and low complexity in mind, allowing it to be retrofitted to desktop printers without major hardware modifications. Future optimization efforts may include the development of self-cleaning channels, energy-efficient pumps, and plug-and-play hardware modules to enhance usability and minimize maintenance. These considerations will be critical for translating SCS from a laboratory setup to a robust industrial solution.

In this study, TPMS-structured ceramic materials demonstrated significant potential in bone tissue engineering. − TPMS structures possess high specific stiffness, excellent energy absorption capacity, and good biocompatibility, which have garnered widespread attention in the biomedical field, particularly in bone repair and tissue engineering applications. This study successfully fabricated ceramics with TPMS structures, achieving high porosity (around 70%) and a minimum pore size of 50 μm, a level of precision far exceeding that achievable by conventional digital light processing (DLP) 3D printing technologies. , In some traditional studies, despite 3D printing equipment having micron-level precision, the presence of nanoscale ceramic particles in the slurry causes light refraction effects, leading to scattering of the curing light during the printing process. , This scattering can disrupt the curing process and ultimately affect the accuracy of the printed product. To address this issue, this study employed multilayer graphene as a dispersing agent for ceramic slurry. Graphene not only possesses excellent thermal conductivity and light absorption properties but also decomposes completely during the sintering process, preventing any potential impurities in the final product. , The thermal conductivity of graphene helps quickly dissipate the heat generated by light cross-linking during printing, preventing localized overheating that could cause thermal curing issues. Additionally, its light absorption capability effectively reduces light scattering during exposure, enhancing printing accuracy and ensuring a high degree of consistency between the printed product and the design model. This approach effectively addresses the challenges of achieving high precision and uniformity in the ceramic 3D printing process, offering a promising solution for fabricating advanced bioceramics with TPMS structures for bone tissue engineering applications.

In terms of mechanical properties, the Gyroid- and Diamond-structured bioceramics fabricated in this study exhibit compressive strength and elastic modulus values that fall within the standard range for trabecular bone. This indicates that the produced ceramic scaffolds not only demonstrate excellent biocompatibility but also possess suitable mechanical properties, providing sufficient support for bone repair. Among them, the Gyroid structure demonstrated the highest compressive strength and the highest Young’s modulus, indicating its superior mechanical performance. This favorable combination of strength and elasticity helps to reduce the risk of stress shielding and promotes better mechanical integration with host tissue. The continuous, triply periodic curvature of the Gyroid architecture also offers smoother stress distribution and higher energy absorption, further supporting its selection as the optimal TPMS design for bone scaffold applications in this study. Furthermore, by adjusting the porosity and formulation of the design, the mechanical properties of these ceramic scaffolds can be tailored. The mechanical performance of bone repair materials is closely linked to their pore structure, making the design of pore architectures similar to that of trabecular bone crucial for the functionality of the materials. The ceramic scaffolds produced in this study meet the requirements for porosity and pore size control, which are essential for bone tissue engineering. This makes them highly promising for widespread application in bone repair and bone substitute materials, providing a solid foundation for their use in clinical settings.

In terms of biocompatibility, the bioceramic materials developed in this study also exhibit excellent performance. According to the X-ray diffraction (XRD) results, the main components of the ceramic scaffolds after sintering are hydroxyapatite (HA) and 45S5 bioactive glass, both of which are bioactive materials. Hydroxyapatite can be utilized by MC3T3-E1 cells and converted into the inorganic component of bone, while 45S5 bioactive glass is widely recognized for its ability to promote osteogenesis and angiogenesis. The XRD pattern also reveals the presence of characteristic peaks for secondary phases, particularly around the 2θ position of 30°. These peaks may correspond to sodium metasilicate hydrate (Na2H2SiO4(H2O)8) and sodium hexametaphosphate hydrate (Na2P6O18(H2O)6), which are likely formed during the sintering process. This indicates that the high-temperature reactions of the raw materials resulted in the formation of new phases, which could potentially impact the mechanical properties and biocompatibility of the final material. As a result, these bioceramic scaffolds have significant potential for application in bone tissue engineering. They can promote new bone formation during bone repair and aid in the restoration of damaged tissue function. The use of bioactive materials is crucial for accelerating bone tissue repair and enhancing the application of bone substitute materials, which holds great importance for the future development of advanced bone repair strategies.

However, despite the many positive outcomes of this study, there are still some limitations. Regarding the SCS, although it effectively enhances the stability of the printing process and improves the quality of the ceramic products, the design and implementation of this system add complexity to the printing procedure. To make this technology more user-friendly, 3D printer manufacturers could consider integrating the SCS into the printer’s design or offering it as an additional modular product for sale. This approach would reduce the operational difficulties that users may encounter during the printing process. Integration not only improves the user experience but also ensures the system’s stability. In terms of biological evaluation, while the results of this study demonstrate good biocompatibility and osteogenic differentiation potential of the ceramic materials, these findings are based on preliminary cell culture experiments and have not been validated through long-term in vivo studies. In practical bone tissue engineering applications, the performance of materials needs to be tested over a longer time scale and in more complex biological systems, especially in in vivo environments, which would more accurately reflect the material’s performance in real-world applications. Therefore, future research should expand to animal model experiments with long-term observation and evaluation of additional biological markers to further verify the bone repair ability and biocompatibility of the ceramic materials.

In addition, cellular biology research remains a critical direction for future studies. Understanding the specific impact of material surface properties, pore structures, and other factors on cell behavior is a key issue in the field of bone tissue engineering. As further visual confirmation, immunofluorescence staining (Figure S4) showed significantly enhanced osteoblast adhesion and cytoskeletal organization on the ceramic surface compared to the nonceramic area. In the future, we will further explore how to optimize cellular behavior through material design, particularly how adjustments to the pore structure, surface chemistry, and other factors can promote cell adhesion, proliferation, and differentiation. Moreover, the effect of different pore structures and sizes on angiogenesis is another important direction for future research. Angiogenesis plays a crucial role in the bone repair process, as the formation of blood vessels is essential for supplying nutrients and supporting tissue regeneration. − Therefore, optimizing the pore structure of ceramic materials to enhance vascular growth presents a significant challenge in future bone tissue engineering research. Addressing this challenge will be pivotal for advancing the development of more effective bone repair materials.

Given the immense potential of 3D printing technology in personalized medicine and customized implant design, the solution proposed in this study offers new perspectives for the development of novel biomaterials. With the continuous advancement of printing technology and material science, 3D printing not only enables mass production but also allows for the customization and manufacturing of implants tailored to individual patient needs. By optimizing the mechanical properties, pore structure, and bioactivity of ceramic materials, we believe that this technology can provide more personalized and efficient bone tissue repair solutions for patients, paving the way for more effective, patient-specific treatments in the future.

5. Conclusion

This study successfully developed an improved 3D printing technology by integrating an SCS to optimize the printing quality of low-viscosity ceramic slurries. Both computational simulations and experimental results demonstrated that the system significantly reduced the sedimentation rate of the slurry during printing, thereby enhancing the printing precision and improving the yield rate of the ceramic products. Using this technology, we achieved the low-cost and rapid fabrication of ceramics with high porosity, small pore sizes, and high precision, coupled with appropriate physical properties and mechanical performance similar to that of cancellous bone. Cell culture results indicated that the porous ceramics had no negative effects on the proliferation and osteogenic differentiation of MC3T3-E1 cells, highlighting their potential for applications in bone tissue engineering. Therefore, this study not only provides an effective solution to overcome the slurry sedimentation challenges during the 3D printing of bioceramics and improve printing quality but also demonstrates the potential applications of the fabricated bioceramics in bone tissue engineering. We anticipate that this research will offer new perspectives and approaches for the application of 3D printing technologies in the biomedical field, particularly in the fabrication of high-precision and functionalized bioceramics.
