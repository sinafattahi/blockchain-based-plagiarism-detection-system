Abstract

This study investigates the effectiveness of titanium dioxide nanoparticles (TiO2 NPs) on the uptake, accumulation, translocation, and phytoremediation efficiency of Cd by­(L.) Urban (). TiO2 NPs at a concentration of 100 mg/kg were applied to soils contaminated with Cd at varying levels (10, 100 mg/kg). The Cd concentration in the roots ofwas increased by 4 times when grown in soils containing both TiO2 NPs and Cd, compared with soil containing only Cd, indicating that TiO2 NPs play an important role in facilitating Cd removal from the soil. TiO2 aggregates were observed in the cortex cells and xylem regions in the roots. The adsorption of Cd onto TiO2 aggregates was confirmed by scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM)-energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) analysis. Once translocated, TiO2 aggregates were found to be distributed within the air space of mesophyll cells, and there is an internalization of TiO2 aggregates within the cytoplasm ofleaves. TiO2 NPs were observed to be transformed into rodlike aggregates when they penetrated through barriers such as cell walls and cell membranes in the transportation process. In addition, it is found that negatively charged TiO2 NPs were more efficiently translocated from roots to shoots ofthan positively charged ones. These findings suggested that the uptake, translocation, and distribution of TiO2 NPs were closely related to the properties of TiO2 NPs.

Introduction

Cd is one of the most toxic heavy metals, displaying considerable mobility within contaminated environments. According to the Dutch pollution standard and the World Health Organization (WHO) standard, the prescribed guideline values for Cd concentrations in soils are established at 0.8 and 0.35 mg/kg, and soil exhibiting Cd content exceeding 12.0 mg/kg signifies a notable degree of contamination. Soil Cd levels ranging from 3 to 8 mg/kg are acknowledged to exert toxic effects on plants. However, in some pollution cases, Cd levels exceeding 100 mg/kg have been found in Indonesia. Excessive Cd levels in the soil pose a significant threat to both flora and fauna, with detrimental effects on organs and the potential for carcinogenicity in human beings. Numerous research studies have reported on the utilization of phytoremediation as a green technology for remediating Cd-contaminated soil. Hyperaccumulator plant species are recognized as plants that naturally accumulate Cd in foliar tissues, exceeding a concentration of 100 mg/kg Cd of dry weight. Using hyperaccumulator plants is an effective strategy for phytoremediation; however, the limited bioavailability of pollutants and the capability of hyperaccumulator plants present constraints on their practical application. To overcome these limitations, studies have focused on the application of organic or inorganic amendments, as well as microorganisms in soil, aiming to enhance the efficiency of the phytoremediation processes. ,

Nanomaterials (NMs) offer potential for environmental remediation by acting as adsorbents and catalysts to detect and remove pollutants. Numerous NMs have been reported for wastewater treatment, including carbon-based NMs, silica-based NMs, nanoscale zerovalent iron (nZVI), and metal oxide-based NMs. As a promising photocatalyst, TiO2 nanoparticles (TiO2 NPs) have gained increasing attention in photodegrading organic pollutions and removing heavy metals such as As, Cd, Cu, Fe, and Pb or metalloids from wastewater. While the utilization of NMs in treating contaminated soil remains restricted, certain NMs have demonstrated promising traits for this purpose. For example, nZVI has been studied for removing heavy metals and metalloids such as Cd and Pb from contaminated soil, and this is possible because of the adsorption and ion reduction properties of nZVI. TiO2 NPs have been shown to have the potential to remove heavy metals and metalloids from soil or water via the phytoremediation approach as well. , For instance, it was reported that low concentrations of TiO2 NPs enhanced the phytoextraction capacity of­(L.) Moench for Sb in polluted soil. Additionally, TiO2 NPs were found to enhance As accumulation and improve the phytoremediation efficiency ofL. cultured in copper mine wastewater. The application of TiO2 NPs to Cd-polluted soil significantly increased the accumulation capacity ofL. Similar results were illustrated in soybean plants (L.) Merr, where nano-TiO2 particles significantly enhanced the uptake and accumulation of Cd and Cs. , TiO2 NPs have been shown to enhance photosynthesis efficiency, increase biomass, and promote root elongation. − The positive effects of TiO2 NPs on plant growth have been suggested as a contributing factor to their improved uptake characteristics for heavy metals in plants. It has been proposed that TiO2 NPs increase the photosynthesis rate and growth parameters in plants, mitigating Cd stress while enhancing Cd uptake. The proposed mechanism for TiO2 NPs’ interaction with plants also includes decreased pH in root exudates, leading to increased bioavailability of Cd in soil, decreased Cd toxicity, and increased Cd adsorption in plants. TiO2 NPs improve metal solubility by altering soil chemistry, increasing the availability of heavy metals for root uptake. They also enhance xylem loading and metal transport, increasing metal accumulation in aboveground tissues. This leads to a higher remediation efficiency, making the process possible for large-scale applications and also potentially shortening the time required for the remediation of the land. Conversely, TiO2 NPs decreased Cd uptake in roots and leaves of rice seedlings and reduced Cd toxicity. Contradictory findings highlight the diverse effects of TiO2 NPs on plant–metal interactions. These effects might be attributed to various physicochemical properties of TiO2 NPs, such as size and surface charge, as well as the specificity of plant species regarding the uptake behavior. For example, larger NPs (>20 nm) have been found to be taken up by plant roots in research studies, and NPs with at least one dimension less than 50 nm exhibit better uptake. This impact of material properties on the remediation behavior of plants is yet to be understood. While the impact of TiO2 NPs on Cd accumulation in plants has been investigated, the relationship between the TiO2 NPs’ uptake pathways, translocation, subcellular distribution in plants, and Cd accumulation in plants remains understudied. Thus, understanding the effects of TiO2 NPs on the uptake and distribution of Cd in plants could pave the way for the development of new strategies to enhance the phytoremediation efficiency of Cd-contaminated soil.

In this study,is selected for phytoremediation experiments conducted in a greenhouse., belonging to the family Apiaceae, is a perennial, stoloniferous plant. It has been identified as a promising candidate plant for Cd remediation based on a previous study where tropical plant species were screened for potential phytoremediation. has been demonstrated to have the ability to accumulate Cd, with a bioconcentration factor (BCF) more than 1 when grown on soil with 200 mg/kg Cd. It has also been recognized as a Cd hyperaccumulator plant in a siding lead–zinc mine. This is possible becausehas adapted to the extreme site by evolving mechanisms to cope with Cd in the polluted soil. However, research studies on enhancing Cd accumulation in nonmine typeare limited.is widely distributed in swampy areas of tropical and subtropical regions and can propagate through both the seedling and offspring ramets via clonal growth. The basal ramets can remain viable for more than 1 year. However, the low biomass of this species presents a challenge for its application in phytoremediation. Phytoremediation efficiency is also limited by low metal bioavailability and slow root-to-shoot translocation. While TiO2 NPs have shown potential in enhancing plant growth, their role in improving metal uptake, transportation, and interaction with pollutants remains unclear. Specifically, the impact of TiO2 NPs’ properties such as size and surface charge on their interaction with plants and metals requires further investigation. Therefore, in the present work, we aim to demonstrate the potential of applying TiO2 NPs as efficient amendments for Cd remediation in polluted soil through phytoremediation. The influence of TiO2 NPs on the uptake, accumulation, and translocation of Cd byplants was assessed. In addition, TiO2 NPs uptake and distribution at a cellular level have been studied. The application of TiO2 NPs in phytoremediation enhances heavy metal uptake and translocation by increasing metal bioavailability, promoting root-to-shoot transport. This study aims to explore how the properties of TiO2 NPs affect Cd uptake efficiency and their interactions with Cd in the plant–soil system. The strategy of TiO2 NPs-assisted Cd uptake in the soil will help overcome the limits of phytoremediation, such as long duration and low efficiency, and contribute to beneficial impacts on the sustainability of the environment.

Results and Discussion

Synthesis and Characterization of NPs

The average particle diameter of the TiO2 NPs synthesized by the hydrothermal reaction was 5.21 ± 0.11 nm (Figure d shows the size distribution). The TiO2 NPs prepared in this work were within the size exclusion limit (SEL) range (5–20 nm) of cell walls, making it possible for plants to take them up. The TiO2 NPs were applied to the soil to study their effect on Cd uptake in the phytoremediation process inplants.

The hydroxyl functional groups on the surface of TiO2 NPs could interact with metal ions in solution, and this interaction can be seen in Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectra. From the FTIR spectrum of TiO2 NPs (Figure b), the Ti–O–Ti bending mode could be seen in the range from 400 to 900 cm–1, the Ti–OH stretching mode could be observed at 1630 cm–1, and the band at 3415 cm–1 can be attributed to the stretching vibration of the −OH group. The interaction of Cd2+ ions with the surface of TiO2 NPs can be seen from FTIR analysis. In Figure b, the band at 1055 cm–1 is attributed to the formation of the Ti–O–Cd complex. Thus, the result confirms the formation of surface complexes between Cd2+ ions and TiO2 NPs.

Accumulation and Distribution of Cd in Plants

Roots

Figure b,c shows the contents of Cd inroots in different pretreated soil groups determined using ICP-OES. The TiO2 NPs promoted Cd accumulation from the soil to the roots, with the Cd10/NPs group showing significant enhancements after 2 and 6 weeks, as well as Cd100/NPs in every sampling time point compared with Cd-treated groups without any NPs. It is notable that the highest Cd values (24.13 mg/kg) accumulated in roots appeared in week 4 for the Cd10 groups without NPs in the soil. In the Cd10/NPs group, the highest Cd content (41.47 mg/kg) was found in week 2, which is twice as high as the Cd levels in the Cd10 group. This suggests that the presence of TiO2 NPs in the soil could shorten the time for roots to accumulate Cd, which is important for shortening the time for remediation. A decreasing trend for the Cd content in the Cd10 and Cd10/NPs groups with time was observed due to the translocation of Cd from the roots to the shoots in the plants. Cd accumulated in the roots of the Cd100 and Cd100/NPs groups showed a continuously increasing trend, maybe because the inflection point for the upward transfer of Cd had not yet appeared within 8 weeks. The highest Cd content (772.53 mg/kg) inroots was obtained by the plant grown on the Cd100/NPs soil. The increase is more than 90% compared with the Cd100 group (without NPs). The Cd concentration in the roots is influenced by the Cd concentration in the substrate, the bioavailability of Cd, and the duration of exposure to Cd. In this case, the time- and concentration-dependent characteristics of Cd absorption in roots could be responsible for the inconsistent Cd accumulation trends in different groups.

Several strategies were studied for improving the Cd uptake by plants, including genetic engineering, adding chelates like EDTA, and amendments such as chloride. Nanophytoremediation has been proposed as a strategy that integrates nanomaterials into phytoremediation applications. Reported materials include iron NPs, nanomagnetite, silicon NPs, CeO2 NPs, silica NPs, TiO2 NPs, etc. There is some published work on the effect of TiO2 NPs on phytoremediation. In general, it focused on two different directions with respect to the study of how TiO2 NPs helped in contaminated soil: (1) TiO2 NPs contributed to enhancing immobilization, decreasing bioavailability and uptake of the metal by the plants, enhancing reduction reaction (organic pollutants) and immobilization, and enhancing the efficiency of pollutant degradation. , (2) TiO2 also displays high Cd tolerance while enhancing plant growth, as previous studies reported that coapplication of TiO2 NPs and hyperaccumulator leads to efficient Cd removal from soil. As shown in Figure S1, TiO2 NPs enhance photosynthetic efficiency and water regulation under moderate Cd stress, supporting their potential role in improving plant tolerance and phytoremediation performance. Although similar findings have been reported with different plant species, previous studies primarily focused on bioparameter measurements and did not illustrate the interaction between TiO2 NPs and Cd. In this work, we tracked the distribution of both Cd and Ti in plant tissues and explored their interactions with pollutants. Employing TiO2 NPs in soil could be developed as an alternative method to enhance the Cd uptake in phytoremediation. These findings provide valuable insights for the design and development of novel NPs for enhanced phytoremediation in future studies and applications. According to previous work using molecular simulations, Cd ion adsorption onto TiO2 nanostructures is via a chemical bond, where Cd is bonded very strongly (E int interaction energy = −4.566 eV) to TiO2. The interaction of Cd ions with the O atoms on the TiO2 surface was typically via electrostatic attractions with partially covalent bonds, and the interaction between Cd2+ and Ti atoms was predicted to be electrostatic. It could be hypothesized that the Cd ions in soil initially contact electrostatically with the negatively charged sites on the surfaces of TiO2 NPs. In order to show that this is indeed the case, it is important to look at the correlation between TiO2 and Cd in the plant samples. Petioles and Laminas

The concentrations of Cd that accumulated in the petioles (Figure d,e) and lamias (Figure f,g) ofwere measured to understand the Cd translocation behavior in leaf tissues. Results show that the Cd content in leaves gradually increased with time and with increasing concentration in the treated soil. Cd was mainly accumulated in the leaf petiole rather than the lamina. The affinity of Cd for vascular bundles in petioles may contribute to the increased Cd content (the Cd fluorescence signal in the petiole is shown in Figure d). More Cd was found to have accumulated in the petiole of the Cd10 (56.13 mg/kg) and Cd100 (197.59 mg/kg) samples. The amount of Cd in the petioles at week 8 of plants with NPs (in Cd10/NPs and Cd100/NPs groups) was lower than that in plants without NPs. This could be because the transport of Cd through the petiole was faster since TF values (the ratio between lamina and petiole) were higher in the Cd/NPs groups (Table , TF (L/P) values). The petiole essentially serves as the transport pathway, which could result in the Cd content being rather transient, hence accounting for the observed variation. For the Cd content in the lamina,grown in soil with Cd and TiO2 NPs added had a significantly higher content than those without TiO2 NPs under Cd-contaminated soil conditions. In the Cd10/NPs and Cd100/NPs treatments, the highest concentration of Cd in the lamina, measuring 27.69 and 95.81 mg/kg, respectively, represents increases of 89 and 121% compared to treatments without TiO2 NPs.

These results show that the BCF values (BCF R/S and BCF L/S) of plants grown on soil with Cd/NPs treatment were higher than those with only Cd treatment. This suggests that TiO2 NPs facilitated Cd uptake and accumulation in roots and laminas of, which is consistent with the increased Cd content in roots and laminas. It is usually thought that plants with BCF values of more than 1 have abilities for heavy metal accumulation. The results revealed that TiO2 NPs enhanced the phytostabilization capability of Cd in roots and the phytoextraction ability for Cd removal from soil by plants. Increasing TF (L/P) values were found in Cd/NPs groups except for the Cd100/NPs samples collected at week 6. There might be formation of apoplastic barriers, which affect the Cd transport toward the shoots of plants since the TF values (TF P/R, TF L/R) were lower than 1 in the Cd100/NPs group. Also, reaching the accumulation limit or homeostasis of Cd concentrations inroots and shoots could be another explanation for this phenomenon.

The uptake, accumulation, and distribution of Cd in plant tissues are significant factors to consider in the evaluation of the ability of phytoremediation for plants and materials combination. Cd2+ ions are highly mobile and water-soluble. They can enter the cortex tissues via apoplastic and symplastic pathways to reach the xylem. To figure out Cd distribution and translocation in roots and shoots of, fresh samples were collected from the Cd100/NPs group at week 8 and stained with the Leadmium Green AM dye and then observed through fluorescence spectroscopy using a laser confocal scanning microscope.

Figure a shows Cd fluorescence in root; the strongest fluorescence signal was observed in the xylem in the stele. Apparently, xylem is the major part of the apoplastic region, which determines the Cd translocation from roots to shoots. In this study, we have observed that the trend at a low Cd dose (10 mg/kg) for Cd uptake with TiO2 NPs assistance is such that the uptake decreased with time, but at a high Cd dose (100 mg/kg), the uptake increased with time. This could be attributed to the mechanisms governing Cd uptake and translocation in roots over the experimental period. Cd uptake in plants occurs through a multistep process involving apoplastic accumulation, symplastic uptake, and xylem loading, each influencing the root-to-shoot translocation efficiency. Initially, Cd accumulates in the root apoplast through passive adsorption, where its retention is influenced by cell wall properties and pH-dependent interactions. Once inside the root cortex, Cd enters the cells by symplastic uptake through active transport, contributing to the radial transport of Cd in roots. The next step is xylem loading, a highly regulated process governed by cation exchange capacity and metal–ligand complexation, which facilitates Cd translocation to the shoots via transpiration. At low Cd doses (10 mg/kg),efficiently transported Cd into the shoots, reducing root retention over time. However, at high Cd doses (100 mg/kg), xylem loading may become saturated, leading to increased Cd retention in the roots as a protective mechanism to minimize toxicity in aerial tissues.

Inleaves, Cd fluorescence was found in vascular bundles, in the lower and upper epidermis, and also in the air spaces between mesophyll cells (Figure b–e). It is notable that there is a preferential Cd accumulation in large epidermis cells (storage cells which do not contain chloroplasts) rather than mesophyll cells containing chloroplasts, hence reducing the adverse impact of Cd on sensitive enzymes and photosynthetic apparatus. It has been shown that Cd initially accumulates in the cytoplasm of the epidermis cell, followed by a slow sequestration of Cd into the vacuole. Vacuolar sequestration is one of the important mechanisms in plants for reducing Cd toxicity and maintaining metal homeostasis. These findings are in line with our results, indicating that one significant strategy for Cd accumulation in plants is vacuolar sequestration in epidermal cells. With the addition of TiO2 NPs to the soil, Cd accumulation in laminas (mostly in vascular bundles and epidermal cells) was increased in.

Uptake and Translocation of TiO2 NPs in

To investigate the distribution and transportation pathways of TiO2 NPs withintissues, including roots and leaves, plant samples harvested from the Cd100/NPs group after an 8-week treatment were subjected to polymer fixation, dehydration, embedding, sectioning, and subsequent analysis using TEM. Figures and are STEM images showcasing transverse sections ofroots. Looking at Figure , one can see that the penetration of TiO2 nanoparticles in the cell wall and the cell membrane is evident in the cortex cells. In addition, the internalization of TiO2 aggregates has been observed in the tracheid cell of the xylem (Figure ). Small TiO2 NPs have a larger surface area, allowing more interaction with Cd ions. The smaller TiO2 NPs also contribute to the penetration of NPs into plant cell walls and membranes.

TiO2 NPs in soil could be adsorbed on the root surface. Then, TiO2 NPs can penetrate cell walls and be internalized by the cell membrane via endocytosis, or other protein and ion channels on the cell membrane. While the reported size exclusion limit (SEL) of cell wall pores ranges from 5 to 20 nm, it was reported that the threshold diameter for TiO2 NPs to accumulate in wheat root is 140 nm, and the size limit for transport to the stele and translocate to shoot in wheat is found to be 36 nm for TiO2 NPs. Hence, the size exclusion limit for different plant species can vary.

TiO2 NPs were found to cover the root epidermal surface, and intracellular NPs tended to form agglomerates near plasma membranes in rice after a 3-day incubation in the NP suspension. In this work, rodlike TiO2 NPs were observed when TiO2 NPs penetrated the cell wall (Figure a,b) and the cell membrane (Figure a). These TiO2 NPs were over 300 nm in length and 120–160 nm in diameter. Compared to TiO2 NPs applied on soil, the size of NPs increased significantly; these changes could be attributed to biotransformation. The reported mechanisms of transformation include aggregation, adsorption, biodegradation, dissolution, eco-/biocorona, redox reaction, sulfidation, etc. Since there was no transformation of TiO2 NPs reported before, we proposed that the mechanism of transformation of TiO2 NPs inplants is (1) dissolution of TiO2 NPs in the presence ofmetabolite, (2) followed by the precipitation of the rodlike crystals found inplants. In the transverse section of the same root sample, TiO2 aggregates (size ranging from 60 to 160 nm in diameter) were further internalized in the cytoplasm of cells along the outer cortex toward the central areas. This cell-to-cell transport implies that the TiO2 NPs uptake pathway fromroots could potentially take place via the symplastic pathways. Sufficient adhesive forces can lead to the attachment of NPs to cell membranes, facilitating their internalization by generating a temporary pore in the plasma membrane. Such complete internalization of nanomaterials is mainly dependent on the shape, size, and surface functional groups of them.

From the EDS spectrum, we can see that both Ti and Cd signals are located on the same sites in the cytoplasm of root cells; the element mapping found that Cd adsorbs to TiO2 NPs and aggregates, suggesting their synergistic transportation. This is an indication that TiO2 NPs are likely to be carriers for Cd uptake by theplant from soil to roots. The smaller NPs have the capacity to increase the permeability of plant cells by generating root pores due to their higher surface reactivity, resulting in high mineral and nutrient uptake. NPs are also recognized for creating nanoscale holes on the cell membranes that can range from 15 to 49 nm, thus facilitating their internal uptake in plants. In this case, the presence of “holes” within both cell walls and membranes could be hypothesized. Consequently, Cd could potentially enter plant cells through two routes: (1) by adsorbing onto TiO2 NPs, followed by taking up of Cd together with TiO2 NPs into the plant tissues; (2) taking up Cd also by directly traversing these “holes” created when TiO2 NPs penetrate cells. This is consistent with the previously observed increase in the Cd content found in plants when soil is amended with TiO2 NPs, i.e.,plants exhibit enhanced uptake and accumulation of Cd in their root parts compared with the unassisted groups (Figure b,c).

Once TiO2 NPs are taken up and internalized inroots, they have the potential to be transferred into the stelar region from where they move longitudinally through the xylem into the shoots, as evidenced by the observation of TiO2 NPs in the leaf tissues ofthrough STEM. Figure shows the distribution of the TiO2 aggregates within the leaf. Notably, a rodlike TiO2 nanoparticle was observed between two adjacent mesophyll cells (Figure a), and their length ranges from 230 to 290 nm, with a diameter of 80–140 nm. Remarkably, the TiO2 nanoparticle located within the intercellular space of neighboring cells, penetrating the cell walls, exhibited a shape that was similar to TiO2 NPs observed in the root (Figure a,b), both having rodlike shapes. It was reported that the shape of NPs can influence their uptake by plants. For instance, the internalization of AuNPs was observed for Au nanorods but not spherical AuNPs. Also, the behavior of these rod-shaped NPs may traverse the plant cell wall through a rotation-mediated mechanism, positioning them advantageously at acute angles relative to the plane of the cell wall. Figure b further illustrates the dispersion of TiO2 aggregates within the air space of mesophyll cells, along with their internalization within a mesophyll cell in theleaf. However, the morphology of these internalized TiO2 aggregates appeared irregular compared with that of TiO2 aggregates situated exterior to the cell, yet penetrating the cell wall.

Remarkably, the alteration in the shape of TiO2 aggregates before and after their internalization into plant cells, as observed in both root and leaf samples, displayed a consistent trend. The presence of TiO2 nanorods can be seen clearly during their traversal of barriers including cell walls and membranes; hence, this indicates that the rod-shaped morphology may potentially enhance the penetration process of the NPs. Subsequently, these NPs transformed into a more rounded configuration, leading to a reduction in the regularity of the aggregates’ shape. This finding implies the pivotal role of shape as a determining factor in influencing the translocation of nanoparticles through cellular structures.

Ti Accumulation Patterns inTissues

The variation of Ti contents inplant tissues over time in TiO2 NPs (100 mg/kg) assisted groups (Cd0/NPs, Cd10/NPs, and Cd100NPs) is depicted in Figure a–c. Compared with Cd10/NPs and Cd100NPs groups, the accumulation of Ti in roots occurred in the first 2 weeks in the Cd0/NPs group, followed by a decreasing trend due to their transportation upward to shoots. Correspondingly, a time-dependent increase in the Ti contents in the petiole and lamina was observed in the TiO2 NPs treatment group. It is noticeable that the concentration of Ti in leaves was lower than that in roots, possibly attributed to the restricted translocation of TiO2 NPs from the root to the shoot. As shown in Figure b,c, mostly Ti concentrations tend to increase with time in the leaf, but no obvious trend was found in the petiole. As mentioned earlier, this could be because the petiole is the transport channel for these NPs and, hence, the concentration may be varying quite substantially. This corresponds well to the Cd content found in the petiole.

Despite the increasing trend of Ti contents in Cd10/NPs and Cd100NPs groups, the Ti levels in the root remained lower compared to the dose applied to the soil (100 mg/kg). It has been reported that the presence of natural organic matter in soil can promote the aggregation of TiO2 NPs. Soil pH and ionic strength play a crucial role in the aggregation kinetics of TiO2 NPs. Thus, the limited mobility of large aggregates of TiO2 NPs in soil might contribute to the restricted availability of free TiO2 NPs forplant uptake. It is notable that higher Ti contents were taken up in plant roots in Cd100/NPs groups 2 weeks later. This phenomenon could be explained by the negative charge present in the root surface, which favored accumulating positively charged Cd100/NPs (Figure d) due to electrostatic attraction. However, this accumulation could be a disadvantage, as this may reduce the amount of TiO2 NPs for their upward translocation inplants. Cd adsorption on TiO2 NPs was detected in the STEM-EDS study ofroot (Figures and ). This complex may dissociate inside the root cells, releasing Cd2+ from the surface of TiO2 NPs and increasing the concentration of Cd in roots. TiO2 NPs that are smaller in size have a larger surface area, and in combination with the surface charge, this allows them to adsorb and form complex Cd2+ ions, preventing precipitation and maintaining ions in soluble forms that are more bioavailable for plant uptake. This process may occur through electrostatic attraction between Cd2+ ions and the negatively charged surface of the TiO2 NPs in this study. The increased bioavailable Cd in the root can then be transported to the shoot, as seen by the enhanced Cd content for TiO2 NPs-assisted groups (Figure ). It has been reported that positively charged CeO2 NPs were more associated with roots than negatively charged ones. Similar results have been shown in AuNPs with positive charge taken up in plant roots. The results are noteworthy as they revealed that negatively charged TiO2 NPs exhibited a higher efficiency of translocation from the root to the lamina compared to treatments involving the presence of both Cd and TiO2 NPs in the soil (Figure ). These results suggested that the surface charge of TiO2 NPs could serve as a pivotal parameter governing their translocation in plant tissues.

Conclusions

The work investigated the effect of TiO2 NPs on Cd bioaccumulation inby conducting pot experiments. Results revealed thatis capable of Cd phytoextraction with higher BCF and TF values. The introduction of TiO2 NPs in soil significantly increased Cd uptake and accumulation in roots and leaves. Cd accumulated in leaf laminas ofat lower Cd level treatment (10 mg/kg), while it was enriched in roots in higher Cd conditions (100 mg/kg). The Cd adsorption to TiO2 NPs is closely related to their synergistic transport from the soil to the root. However, after Cd and TiO2 NPs uptake and accumulation in roots, TiO2 NPs tended to stay in the roots with less translocation, while Cd continued to transport upward. Uptake, translocation, and distribution of Cd and TiO2 NPs at the organ, tissue, and cellular levels were investigated. Whilecould uptake Cd through both symplastic and apoplastic pathways, the plant accumulation of TiO2 NPs may mostly be through symplastic pathways. In the root and shoot cells of, TiO2 NPs and aggregates with a size greater than 100 nm were found, demonstrating that the threshold of the size limit to penetrate the plant cell wall and cell membrane might be greater than 100 nm rather than 5–20 nm. Rodlike TiO2 NPs were observed during the cell-to-cell translocation process in root and leaf cells. Although higher Ti contents were accumulated in the roots, TiO2 NP translocation from roots to shoots was found as well. Positively charged Cd100/NPs tend to accumulate in the root, while negatively charged TiO2 NPs were translocated from the root to the shoot. Understanding the parameters of NPs that influence NPs’ fate in plants is important for further application. Notably, size, shape, and surface charge could be critical properties of NPs for their uptake and translocation in plants. In summary, TiO2 NPs could play a significant role as carriers of Cd in the phytoremediation process, mainly contributing to Cd uptake and accumulation by roots from soil. The current study is a way forward to developing a TiO2 NPs-assisted Cd remediation strategy that employs plants in a greenhouse. Further research studies are needed under field conditions to understand the interaction of TiO2 NPs with plants grown in Cd-contaminated soil.

Materials and Methods

Synthesis and Characterization of TiO2 NPs

To prepare TiO2 NPs, 5 mL of TTIP was slowly added dropwise to 12.5 mL of aqueous 0.25 M NaCl solution containing 1 g of Pluronic P-123­(PEO20PPO70PEO20) triblock copolymer, followed by stirring of the mixed solution at 1000 rpm at room temperature, before transferring it into a 45 mL Teflon-lined hydrothermal autoclave. Hydrothermal reactions were performed for 6 h at 120 °C. The samples were then centrifuged at 10,000 rpm at 25 °C for 30 min and the supernatant decanted, followed by washing with 95% ethanol three times to remove all residual precursors and contaminants. The powdery product was then dried at 85 °C for 2 h.

The crystalline structure of the synthesized TiO2 NPs was determined using X-ray diffraction (XRD) Panalytical XPert Pro (Cu Kα = 1.5418 Å) grazing angle powder X-ray diffraction. The measurement program was run at a scan step size of 0.020° and a scan speed of 0.43° per min, within a scan range from 10 to 90°. The phase composition was calculated and analyzed by Topas 6 software upon fitting sample data peaks against those of anatase and brookite; the XRD pattern is shown in Figure a. The size of TiO2 NPs was determined by transmission electron microscopy (TEM; JEOL 2020 HR) at an accelerating voltage of 200 kV. The obtained TEM image of TiO2 NPs is shown in Figure c. ζ-Potential of TiO2 NPs was measured in deionized water using Nano Particle Analyzer SZ-100 (Horiba Scientific Ltd.). Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) spectra were recorded with NicoletTM Contimuum (Thermo Scientific) in the range of 4000–400 cm–1. For pot experiments, TiO2 NPs suspensions were prepared in Milli-Q water at a 100 mg/kg final concentration in dried soil and sonicated in a water bath for 30 min at 25 °C.

Plant Cultivation and Exposure Assay

(Figure a) seedlings were propagated in the NTU herb garden. For pot experiments, 50 g of dried soil (JIFFY FLORAFLEUR 002 UNIVERSAL POTTING SOIL) was measured in each pot, treated with various concentrations of Cd (CdSO4), and then spiked with the TiO2 NP suspension. In this study, Cd-free (Cd0, Cd0/NPs) soil and four other groups (listed in Table ) of Cd-treated soil (Cd10, Cd10/NPs, Cd100, Cd100/NPs) were planted withbasal ramet. Plants were watered daily with 25 mL of tap water to ensure the growth conditions in the greenhouse (National Institute of Education, NIE). Root and leaf samples were harvested biweekly over an experiment of 8 weeks. No significant difference in the physical characteristics ofwas observed between the different groups.

Measurement and Analysis of the Ti and Cd Contents

Dried plant samples were ground and digested in concentrated HNO3 using a microwave-assisted digester (Milestone Microwave Laboratory Systems, Germany). The Ti and Cd contents in the root and shoot parts of plants were determined by inductively coupled plasma-optical emission spectrometry (ICP-OES) (Optima 8300, PerkinElmer).

The phytoremediation efficiency of Cd inwas assessed by the bioconcentration factor (BCF) and translocation factor (TF) values, which are useful parameters for evaluating the abilities of plants in heavy metal uptake and accumulating. The BCF refers to the ratio of Cd contents in plant tissues (Root-R, Petiole-P, Lamina-L) to that in the soil (S) using the following equation

BCF=Cd concentration in plant tissue(mg/kg)Cd concentration in soil(mg/kg)

The TF is defined as the ratio between Cd concentrations in the plant shoot (petiole, lamina) and root (TF P/R, TF L/R), as well as lamina and petiole (TF L/P), calculated using the following formula

TF=Cd concentraion in shoot(mg/kg)Cd concentration in root(mg/kg),TF(L/P)=Cd concentration in lamina(mg/kg)Cd concentration in petiole(mg/kg)

TF values were also used to evaluate the translocation efficiency of Ti in plants.

Ultrastructural Observations

Freshroot and lamia samples from the plants of the Cd100/NPs group after 2 months were harvested. The plant samples were washed with 75% ethanol for 1 min before being cut into 1–2 mm3 slices. They were prefixed with 2.5% glutaraldehyde (GA) for 4 h at 4 °C and washed three times with 0.1 M phosphate buffer saline. Secondary fixation was conducted using 2% Osmium tetroxide for 2 h and washed three times with DI H2O. The fixed samples were progressively dehydrated using gradient ethanol (25, 50, 75, 95, and 100%) for 20 min each. Dehydrated samples were infiltrated and embedded with 100% acetone (1 h), 50% acetone containing 50% LR White (1 h), 25% acetone containing 75% LR White (1 h), and LR white resin (overnight). The specimens were transferred into embedding capsules, positioned at the tip of the capsules, followed by hardening at 60 °C for 48 h. The embedded plant samples were trimmed, sectioned using an ultramicrotome (UC7, Lecia Microsystems) to 100 nm slices, and put on a 200 mesh carbon-coated Ni grind (Ted Pella) for subsequent observation by a transmission electron microscope (TEM). JEOL 2100F TEM operated at 200 keV with a 100 mm2 windowless Oxford Ultim Max detector EDS was used to observe the distribution of TiO2 NPs and Cd in plant samples.

Analysis of Cd Distribution Using a Laser Scanning Confocal Microscope

To monitor the uptake, translocation, and distribution of Cd in fresh roots and leaves of, harvested plant samples were washed with DI water, cut, stained with the Cd-specific Leadmium Green AM dye (Invitrogen) for 1 h in the dark, and washed three times with assay buffer. Samples were imaged using a laser scanning confocal microscope (LSCM, Lecia TCS SP5). A laser excitation of 488 nm was used to locate the dye, and 633 nm was used for chlorophyll. The emission range was set to 500–550 nm for the Cd detection and 650–750 nm for chloroplast autofluorescence from the chlorophyll.
