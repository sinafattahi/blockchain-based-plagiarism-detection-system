Abstract

In the research on lateral vented explosions of gasoline vapor–air mixtures in pipelines with different opening pressures, the paper conducts a numerical simulation study of gasoline vapor–air mixture explosions in a rectangular pipeline with a length-to-diameter ratio of 15. By analyzing the explosion flame morphology, unburned fuel gas distribution, flow field structure, and overpressure variation characteristics, the results reveal that there are two different explosion modes with and without constraints, forming five typical overpressure peaks: P 1, P un, P ext, P 2, and P 3. There is a strong dynamic coupling relationship among explosion flame behavior, unburned fuel gas distribution, and flow field structure. The three overpressure peaks P 1, P un, and P 2 in the internal explosion field, as well as the explosion intensity index, external explosion time, and external explosion delay time, are all related to the opening pressure.

1. Introduction

Oil vapors, formed by the inevitable volatilization and leakage of oil products during production, storage, and transportation, pose a significant threat to personnel and property safety in the event of an explosion. To mitigate the risks associated with gas explosions, the explosion vent is widely recognized as the most cost-effective solution. When an explosion occurs, the rise in internal overpressure activates the explosion exhaust device, rapidly releasing the overpressure and flames from the space to the external environment. This process effectively reduces the destructive force of the explosion and safeguards the equipment and structural integrity. However, in elongated pipelines such as those in oil and gas pipeline engineering, natural gas pipelines, and coal mine roadwaysparticularly those with large length-to-diameter ratiosa combustible gas explosion can cause a sharp pressure rise at the pipeline end, even leading to detonation. − Venting at the pipeline end presents considerable challenges and yields suboptimal results. , In contrast, lateral venting, which occurs during the initial to midstages of ignition and explosion, can significantly reduce explosion-related losses.

Previous research has extensively investigated the explosive characteristics of flammable gases under various venting conditions. − For instance, Wang et al. conducted experiments on petroleum fuel vapor–air mixtures with different exhaust dimensions, revealing that as the exhaust coefficient Kv increases, the number of overpressure peaks decreases while the overpressure values rise. They determined the ideal exhaust coefficient range to be 4.560 ≤ Kv ≤ 8.585. Yu et al. examined the impact of lateral vent position and area on methane explosion characteristics within an unobstructed pipe, demonstrating that the effectiveness of lateral venting does not monotonically decrease with increasing distance between the lateral vent and the ignition point. Lu et al. explored the influence of hydrogen concentration on explosion venting, observing that variations in hydrogen concentration alter explosion intensity, which subsequently affects flame morphology, propagation velocity, and temperature distribution. Yang et al. performed a series of explosion venting experiments and numerical simulations on a large-scale test system, finding that adding a vent at the top of a pipe trench significantly reduces upstream overpressure and impulse, thereby mitigating overpressure effects.

Among the venting conditions, the opening pressure of the vent is a critical factor. Lv et al. investigated the effect of the opening pressure of an explosion vent surface (EVS) on methane deflagration characteristics in a vertical branch pipe, revealing that increased EVS opening pressure promotes pressure accumulation in horizontal pipes, while the exhaust effect in vertical branches attenuates the deflagration pressure peak. Zhang et al. studied propane–air discharge explosions in a pipe with a length-to-diameter ratio of 7 under static activation pressure (Pstat), discovering that higher Pstat values facilitate balanced exhaust and reduce the required exhaust area. Lu et al. examined the impact of burst pressure on hydrogen explosion venting in a 20 L cubic container, observing that as burst pressure increases, the maximum explosion pressures in both internal and external areas rise. During propagation, the flame’s surface becomes smoother, and the flame’s width and area increase correspondingly.

Compared with experiments, numerical simulations provide a richer array of venting parameters and enable more in-depth analysis. Li et al. employed numerical simulations to study the explosive characteristics of hydrogen and gasoline vapors in pipes with and without obstacles, finding that hydrogen explosions generate significantly more intense explosion parameters than gasoline vapor explosions. In pipes with obstacles, the rise in the explosion overpressure is more synchronized with combustion reactions, whereas this synchronization is less pronounced in unobstructed pipes. Sun et al. numerically investigated the venting parameters of hydrogen–air explosions in rectangular pipes, revealing that as vent pressure increases and vent size decreases, explosion intensity significantly rises. When flames pass through vents under high-pressure gradients, the flame velocity increases, leading to more intense turbulence and secondary external explosions. Cao et al. numerically simulated the venting process of ethylene–air mixtures, analyzing external flame propagation mechanisms using velocity, velocity vectors, and turbulent kinetic energy, thereby uncovering flame propagation processes under different concentrations and venting conditions. Pang et al. systematically analyzed the effects of opening pressure, opening time, and vent size on external explosion overpressure through numerical simulations, finding that as vent opening pressure and opening time increase, external explosion overpressure intensity significantly rises, external explosion locations become closer to the vent, and explosion duration shortens. Pan et al. numerically simulated lateral venting in a small rectangular channel with two symmetric ignitions, observing that two symmetric flames develop simultaneously until they merge. At this point, due to vortex flows, a hollow region forms near the channel bottom. Huang et al. numerically analyzed the impact of lateral vent ducts on flame propagation, overpressure, and flow field during explosions, revealing that upstream venting inhibits flame propagation while downstream venting may induce it.

Previous studies have primarily focused on the evolution of flame morphology in laterally vented pipelines. However, there is still a lack of adequate research on the diffusion of unburned gas and flame propagation during the venting process. Additionally, the key venting parameters, such as overpressure peaks, require further investigation. In this study, a numerical simulation approach is employed to analyze the interrelationships among explosion flame morphology, unburned gas distribution, flow field structure, and overpressure variations under different opening pressures. The aim is to uncover the dynamic mechanisms of lateral explosion venting of a gasoline vapor–air mixture in pipelines.

The gasoline vapor–air mixture explosion process involves both laminar and turbulent flows. Neither the laminar finite-rate model nor the eddy dissipation model alone can accurately describe the reaction rate. Therefore, the Finite-Rate/Eddy-Dissipation model is employed to determine the chemical reaction rate. During the calculation of species generation rates, both Arrhenius and eddy dissipation rates are computed with the smaller value being selected. 2.3. Computational Model

As shown in Figure , the computational model aligns with the experimental setup. The explosion pipeline has a cross-sectional dimension of 100 × 100 mm and a total length of 1500 mm, with a 100 × 100 mm vent located at the center of the upper wall. The three pressure monitoring points, PT1, PT2, and PT3, are shown in the figure. To observe external flame characteristics, the computational domain extends 300 mm in the positive and negative xz directions and 600 mm in the y direction from the vent center, forming an external area computational region of 0.6 × 0.6 × 0.6 m. The finite volume method is used for mesh discretization. The external area mesh size is twice that of the internal area. At an opening pressure of 0 kPa, grid independence verification is conducted on the internal area model with mesh sizes of 4 mm, 2 mm, and 1.5 mm. As listed in Table , results show that the 2 mm and 1.5 mm meshes yield consistent results. Considering the computational cost, a 2 mm mesh size is adopted, resulting in a total of 6.66 million mesh elements.

The initial temperature is set to 300 K, and the pressure is set to atmospheric pressure. A hemispherical ignition region with a radius of 5 mm is established at the center of the left side face of the pipeline. The Spark-Model is used for ignition, with a duration of 1 ms. The ignition energy is set at 1.5 J, the same as in the experiment. Typical gasoline vapor consists of over a hundred different components. To simplify the model and analysis, C7H16 and air premixed gas replace the gasoline vapor–air mixture in the experiment. Also, the chemical reaction is simplified to a single-step reaction: C7H16 + 11O2 = 7CO2 + 8H2O. Previous research , has shown that when the initial gasoline–vapor concentration is near the stoichiometric concentration, the explosion is most violent, and the peak overpressure is maximized. Therefore, in this study, the initial gasolinevapor mass fraction of the experimental and simulation was set at 6%, corresponding to an equivalence ratio of approximately 1. The internal area computational region is set to a wall boundary condition, while the external area computational region is set to a pressure outlet boundary condition. To simulate the venting processes under different opening pressures, a pressure monitoring point is placed at the vent center. When the pressure reaches the operating pressure, the vent boundary condition is changed from the wall to interior. Opening pressures are set to 0, 20, 40, and 60 kPa to mimic traditional weak structures like glass. The PISO algorithm is used for pressure–velocity coupling. The convection term is discretized using a second-order upwind format, and the diffusion term is discretized using a second-order central difference format. The time step is set to 10–5 s to ensure computational accuracy.

2.4. Model Validation

To validate the CFD model, experiments and numerical simulations of lateral vented gasoline vapor–air mixture explosions at operating pressures of 0 and 40 kPa are conducted. The flame structure and overpressure variations from the simulations are compared with the experimental results. In the experiments, when the operating pressure is 0 kPa, the pipeline vent is sealed with PVC film, which is punctured simultaneously with ignition to simulate venting at 0 kPa. When the operating pressure is 40 kPa, the pipeline vent is sealed with aluminum foil at a static burst pressure of 40 kPa.

Figure a compares the experimental and numerical simulation flame structures. Numerical simulations accurately reproduce the entire process of flame propagation from the internal hemispherical structure to the external venting. Figure b compares the experimental and numerical simulation overpressure variations at PT1. Both show a trend of slow increase, rapid increase, venting decline, external explosion rise, and oscillatory attenuation. However, due to the incomplete rupture of the vent film in the early stages of the experiment, fewer unburned fuel gases are initially released to the external area. Consequently, the maximum overpressure peak in the 0 kPa experiment (1.607 kPa) is 6.56% higher than the simulation result (1.508 kPa). For the 40 kPa case, the elasticity and surface tension of the aluminum foil result in an experimental maximum overpressure peak (45.882 kPa) that is 14.59% higher than the simulation result (40.039 kPa). Overall, the established numerical model can accurately describe the overpressure and flame propagation variations in actual lateral-vented gasoline vapor–air mixture explosions and is suitable for further analysis of gasoline vapor–air mixture venting characteristics under different opening pressures.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Dynamic Characteristics of Typical Parameters during Explosion

3.1.1. Dynamic Characteristics of Internal and External Overpressure

After ignition, the internal and external overpressure variations under different opening pressures exhibit similar trends. Taking P open = 40 kPa as an example, Figure shows that five pressure peaksP 1, P un, P ext, P 2, and P 3are generated in the internal and external areas after ignition. To analyze the coupling relationship among explosion overpressure, flame morphology, unburned fuel gas distribution, and flow field structure, the flame structure, unburned fuel gas distribution contour, and flow field velocity vector at the peak overpressure moments are extracted (Figure ). Additionally, a coupling diagram of the internal overpressure, flame volume change rate (combustion rate), and vent mass flow rate (venting rate) is plotted (Figure ). The explosion process is divided into six stages: “sealed space explosion,” “rapid venting,” “external explosion,” “external gas backflow,” “flame ‘blockage’,” and “pressure oscillation.”

During the sealed space explosion stage (0–39.5 ms), from ignition until the vent opens, the internal flame structure evolves from an “ellipsoidal” to a “finger-like” shape. Due to the sealed vent, this stage can be simplified as a constant-volume explosion. The overpressure rises exponentially and reaches the opening pressure at 39.5 ms, forming P 1. At PT1, PT2, and PT3, P 1 values are 40.04, 38.86, and 5.1 kPa, respectively.

In the rapid venting stage (39.5–41.5 ms), after the vent opens, the internal flow field accelerates toward the vent due to the significant pressure difference between the inside and outside. By 41.5 ms, the maximum velocity reaches 216 m/s. This process has two effects: (1) the flame front develops small wrinkles and becomes turbulent, accelerating internal combustion and causing pressure increases; (2) a large amount of unburned fuel gas is rapidly released from the vent, forming a mushroom-shaped gas cloud in the external area and causing pressure drops. At 41.5 ms, the vent mass flow rate reaches a maximum value of −1.66 kg/s (the negative value indicates outward venting) and forms P un. At PT1, PT2, and PT3, P un values are 22.55, 36.77, and 6.67 kPa, respectively. Compared to PT1, the PT2 region experiences less venting, resulting in a larger pressure peak.

During the external explosion stage (41.5–47.5 ms), the flame propagates to the external area and quickly ignites the external unburned fuel gas. By 47.5 ms, most of the external unburned fuel gas is consumed, generating a peak P ext = 3.11 kPa at PT3. Notably, P ext is not observed at PT2 because the external explosion pressure wave propagates into the pipeline and overlaps with internal pressure waves to form a peak of 5.12 kPa at PT1. Due to the distance and different propagation directions, the external explosion pressure wave dissipates before reaching PT2. Figure shows that during this stage, there are no significant inflection points in the internal combustion rate and venting rate, indirectly confirming that P ext originates from the external explosion.

In the external gas backflow stage (47.5–51.5 ms), the internal pipeline pressure first becomes negative due to the release of a large amount of unburned gas. The flow field velocity vector shows that a recirculation vortex forms in the upper left part of the vent. The mass flow rate reaches a positive maximum of 0.056 kg/s at 50.5 ms. Under the influence of backflow gas, internal combustion accelerates again, producing peak P 2 at 51.5 ms. At PT1, PT2, and PT3, P 2 values are 4.18, 16.78, and 3.11 kPa, respectively. At this point, only a small amount of unburned gas can propagate to the external area through the vent, resulting in a much larger P 2 peak at PT2 than at PT1.

During the flame “blockage” stage (51.5–60 ms), the flame has fully passed through the vent, dividing the pipeline into two regions. Behind the flame front is a large amount of high-temperature burned gas, which can be rapidly vented to the external area, causing pressure drops. In front of the flame front is low-temperature unburned gas, which is “blocked” by the flame front and is unable to vent. Under the influence of the flow field, it collides with the flame front and accelerates combustion, leading to pressure increases. By 60 ms, peak P 3 is generated. At PT2, P 3 is 16.4 kPa, significantly higher than those at PT1 (3.49 kPa) and PT3 (1.24 kPa).

In the “pressure oscillation” stage (after 60 ms), the flame continued to propagate to the right side of the pipeline. The precursor pressure wave reflected off the right wall and propagated to the vent for release. The pressure oscillated between positive and negative until the internal gasoline vapor–air mixture was completely consumed.

When P open = 0 kPa (Figure ), only three overpressure peaksP un, P ext, and P 2are observed at PT1. This is because the pipeline is initially open, and ignition directly enters the venting stage, eliminating P 1. Due to prolonged venting, a large amount of unburned fuel gas is expelled to the external area, reducing the internal right-side gasoline vapor concentration. After the external backflow and external explosion stages, the flame propagates beyond the vent. Since the internal right-side gasoline vapor concentration is low, the pressure increase from combustion is fully offset by venting, preventing the observation of P 3. The process directly transitions to the pressure oscillation stage. Notably, the external explosion is delayed until after the external backflow stage.

The pressure variation trends for P open = 20 and 60 kPa are similar to those for 40 kPa (Figure ). However, when P open = 20 kPa, the external explosion pressure is small, and the pressure wave dissipates before returning to the pipeline interior, so P ext is not observed. Additionally, due to lower internal pressure, the pressure increase from combustion is fully offset by venting, resulting in no significant P 3 peak.

3.1.2. Overpressure Rise Rate Characteristics

Figure shows the overpressure rise rates at different opening pressures and measurement points. Five oscillation peaks are observed under different opening pressures. Peak “O” results from the rapid venting of internal pressure after the vent opens; peaks “A” and “A′” arise from the coupling of flame combustion pressure increase and rapid venting; peak “B” is caused by the external explosion; peak “C” is due to the promotion of combustion pressure increase by vent backflow; and peak “D” is caused by flame “blockage” at the vent. It should be noted that peak “A” occurs between the vent opening and the formation of peak overpressure P un, so it is detected in all venting processes. However, peak “A′” occurs after P un forms, and its formation is related to the timing of the formation of the peak overpressures P ext and P 2. Therefore, peak “A′” is not always observed in every venting process.

The overpressure rise rate trends vary significantly across different measurement points. First, since peak “O” is generated by the rapid venting of internal pressure after the vent opens, it is only observed at PT3. Second, as the external explosion pressure wave dissipates before reaching PT2 due to reflections from the pipeline walls, peak “B” is not observed at PT3. Lastly, compared to internal measurement points PT1 and PT2, the overpressure rise rate peaks at the external PT3 are consistently larger. For an opening pressure of 60 kPa, the maximum overpressure rise rate reaches 48,009 kPa/s. Notably, the overpressure rise rate oscillations at PT2 are more pronounced than at other points with more significant pressure fluctuations. In practical applications, this area is prone to damage and requires reinforcement.

3.1.3. Flame Dynamic Characteristics

Figures and illustrate the flame structure (red), unburned fuel gas (blue), and flow field velocity vector (vector arrows) variations at different moments for opening pressures of 40 and 0 kPa, representing typical explosion modes with and without weak constraints in laterally vented pipelines.

When weak structural constraints are present, the flame structure undergoes the following stages: (1) “hemispherical” to “finger-like.” In the early explosion stage, the pipeline is sealed. The ignited flame diffuses smoothly in all directions, forming a hemispherical flame. As the flame encounters the pipeline walls, it transitions to a “finger-like” shape. (2) “Small-wrinkled finger-like.” After the vent opens, the flame accelerates under the pressure difference between the inside and outside. The flame front becomes wrinkled, increasing the flame area and combustion velocity. A large amount of unburned gas is released into the external area. Due to the velocity difference between the expelled gas and the surrounding stationary air, shear vortices form on both sides under the influence of Kelvin–Helmholtz instability, creating a mushroom-shaped unburned gas cloud. (3) “External diffusion-like.” When the flame propagates to the external area, it rapidly spreads horizontally and vertically under the external explosion’s influence. Unlike the symmetric mushroom-shaped flame described in the literature, the flame is stretched to the left. This is because the unburned gas from the pipeline leaks to the right side of the vent and propagates upward to the right. The gas is drawn into vortices on both sides of the propagation direction. The left-side gas is ignited, intensifying the leftward flame propagation. (4) “External left rotating-like.” The flame continued to propagate upward to the left. Under the combined effects of Rayleigh–Taylor and Kelvin–Helmholtz instabilities, a large-scale vortex forms in the lower-left region. The vortex action sustains the leftward flame propagation. (5) “Internal tulip to planar-like.” After the internal flame fully passes through the vent, the external unburned gas is nearly depleted, and the external flame dissipates. The flame front “blocks” the pipeline, turning the right side of the pipeline into a “sealed” environment. The internal flame propagation transitions from “tulip-like” to “planar.” As gasoline vapor is consumed, the flame brightness gradually dims and eventually disappears.

Compared with venting with weak constraints, venting without weak constraints (0 kPa) exhibits several distinct differences: (1) Unburned gas begins to leak from the ignition stage. Due to the small pressure difference across the vent, the horizontal and vertical flow field velocities are of the same order of magnitude. The unburned gas propagates to the upper right and forms a mushroom-shaped cloud, with a greater propagation distance. (2) When the flame reaches the vent, two characteristics emerge: ① Under the influence of Rayleigh–Taylor and Kelvin–Helmholtz instabilities, a left-biased ellipsoidal flame forms near the vent, with significant external gas backflow from the left side of the vent. ② The flame continues to propagate into the unburned gas in the upper right external area. (3) The external explosion occurs after the external gas backflow stage. At this point, the unburned gas in the upper right external area is fully ignited, forming two ellipsoidal flame structures. In engineering design, this highlights the need to focus on the near-left and far-right regions in external flame protection.

3.2. Distribution of Typical Parameters under Different Opening Pressures

3.2.1. Internal Overpressure Characteristics

Figure shows the peak overpressure, time to reach peak overpressure, and explosion intensity index (kg) at two internal measurement points (PT1 and PT2) under different opening pressures. The maximum values of P 1, P un, and P 2 exhibit a linear increase with a rising opening pressure, particularly at PT1. In contrast, P ext and P 3 show no significant changes. This is attributed to the formation mechanisms of these peaks. As the opening pressure increases, the sealed space explosion stage lasts longer, leading to higher P 1 and P un values. Consequently, the negative pressure after venting is lower, resulting in more intense external gas flow, and a larger P 2. P ext is primarily influenced by the external explosion, while P 3 is affected by the coupling of late-stage combustion and venting, with more complex influencing factors that cannot be directly linked to the opening pressure. When the average values of the peak overpressures P 1, P un, P 2, and P 3 at different opening pressures are compared, PT2 shows increases of 1.41%, 42.71%, 204.19%, and 421.23% over PT1. This is because the continuous reflection and superposition of pressure waves at the pipeline end lead to increased overpressure. After the explosion enters the flame “blocking” and “pressure oscillation” stages, this pressure increase effect becomes more significant. In contrast, the venting port area experiences lower peak overpressures due to continuous pressure release.

From the time to reach peak overpressure with constrained vents, the time to reach each peak increases linearly with rising opening pressure. For unconstrained vents, the time to reach P un, P ext, and P 2 exceeds that for P open = 60 kPa. This is due to the prolonged and extensive release of unburned gas under unconstrained conditions.

The explosion intensity index (K g) is a critical parameter for industrial design and is calculated using the formula Kg=(dp/dt)max×V3 , results show that higher opening pressures lead to greater explosion intensity indices. When P open = 60 kPa, K g reaches its maximum value of 10.2882 MPa·m/s. Notably, at P open = 0, 20, and 40 kPa, the explosion intensity index at PT2 is 102.67%, 3.14%, and 17.99% higher than that at PT1, respectively. However, at P open = 60 kPa, the explosion intensity index at PT2 is 30.55% lower than that at PT1. This discrepancy arises because the long length-to-diameter ratio (L/D = 15) of the internal pipeline causes the pressure wave to propagate rapidly under the influence of the rapidly changing flame field and unburned gas flow field, leading to asynchronous variations in the explosion intensity index across different measurement points.

3.2.2. External Explosion Characteristics

Figure shows the external average turbulent kinetic energy, external unburned gas volume, and overpressure time series at PT3 under different opening pressures. After the vent opens, the external average turbulent kinetic energy rapidly increases to a peak and then quickly declines. For opening pressures of 40 and 60 kPa, the average turbulent kinetic energy peaks are 3.9 and 7.48 times higher than at 0 kPa, respectively. The unburned gas volume is calculated as the region where the gasoline vapor fraction exceeds the lower explosion limit (3%). Results indicate that the unburned gas volume initially increases rapidly. After the flame propagates to the vent, the unburned gas is rapidly consumed by combustion. The external unburned gas volume peaks are 0.00481 m3, 0.00346 m3, 0.00389 m3, and 0.00324 m3 for P open = 0, 20, 40, and 60 kPa, respectively. As the opening pressure increases, the duration of the external unburned gas decreases. Under unconstrained conditions, the unburned gas cloud propagated externally for approximately 85.5 ms. For P open = 20, 40, and 60 kPa, the durations are 25, 16.5, and 12.5 ms, respectively, representing reductions of 70.76%, 80.7%, and 85.38%. For the external area, the most hazardous scenario is the occurrence of an “external explosion.” Turbulent kinetic energy, a key parameter for quantifying flow field irregularity, can quantitatively describe the state of the external flow field. Therefore, the moment when the average turbulent kinetic energy of the external area reaches its peak is defined as the “external explosion” moment. It can be seen that when the “external explosion” occurs, the external unburned gas is ignited, leading to a sharp reduction in unburned gas volume and a rapid rise in external overpressure. The turbulent kinetic energy increases sharply and reaches its maximum.

Define the time interval between the opening moment of the explosion vent (T open) and the external explosion (T ext) as the occurrence time of the external explosion delay (T delay), T delay provides a relative safety window for personnel and equipment protection in practical applications. Figure shows that with constrained vents, as the opening pressure increases, the external explosion time lengthens, while T delay shortens, and the reduction rate slows. For P open = 20 kPa, the delay time is 12 ms. For P open = 40 and 60 kPa, T delay decreases by 33.4% and 54.2%, respectively. This is because higher opening pressures delay vent opening, allowing the flame front to approach closer to the vent. After venting, the larger pressure difference accelerates flame release, thereby reducing the external explosion delay time. Under unconstrained conditions, more unburned gas is released to the external area, resulting in a later external explosion.

4. Conclusions

Through numerical simulations of lateral vented gasoline vapor–air mixture explosions in pipelines under different opening pressures, the following conclusions can drawn:

  • (1)

    The internal and external overpressure and flame structure exhibit two distinct modes with and without constraints. With constraints, the explosion process is divided into six stages: “sealed space explosion,” “rapid venting,” “external explosion,” “external gas backflow,” “flame ‘blockage’,” and “pressure oscillation,” forming five typical overpressure peaks. Without constraints, the process is divided into four stages: “rapid venting,” “external gas backflow,” “external explosion,” and “pressure oscillation,” forming three typical overpressure peaks.

  • (2)

    Different overpressure peaks arise from different factors. P 1 is solely related to the opening pressure, P ext is associated with the external explosion, while P un, P 2, and P 3 are influenced by the coupling of flame volume changes and vent mass flow rate during various explosion stages. There is a strong dynamic coupling relationship between explosion flame behavior, unburned gas distribution, and flow field structure.

  • (3)

    The three internal overpressure peaks P 1, P un, and P 2 show a linear increase with rising opening pressure, whereas P ext and P 3 exhibit no significant changes. The explosion intensity index also increases with higher opening pressures, the trends for different measurement points are not entirely synchronized.

  • (4)

    When an external explosion occurs, a large amount of unburned gas is ignited, leading to a sharp reduction in unburned gas volume. The turbulent kinetic energy increases sharply and reaches its maximum, and a rapid rise in external overpressure. As the opening pressure increases, the external explosion time lengthens, while the external explosion delay time shortens.
