Abstract

Nanobentonite particles were used to investigate the elimination of nickel­(II) and copper­(II) at concentrations between 50 and 300 mg L–1. Batch adsorption tests were conducted to examine the impacts of several factors, including pH, contact time, initial metal ions concentration, adsorbent dose, and temperatures. It was found that the first 20 min of metal ion removal was very fast, and the majority of the reaction was completed. The contact time was 30 min. The optimum pH was found to be 5.0 and 6.0 for copper­(II) and nickel­(II). We carefully evaluated how temperature affected the kinetics and equilibrium of adsorption on nanobentonite particles. The adsorption rate increased as the temperature rose, which is consistent with an exothermic process. The data were subjected to Langmuir, Freundlich, and Dubinin–Radushkevich isotherms at various temperatures. The best explanation for the data was found to be the Langmuir adsorption model. Adsorption kinetics has been investigated using pseudo-first and pseudo-second orders. The data fit best with a pseudo-second-order mechanism. Studies have been done on thermodynamic parameters including ΔH°, ΔG°, and ΔS°. FTIR spectra were used to study the role of functional groups on the surface of nanobentonite in removing heavy metal ions.

1. Introduction

The uncontrolled disposal of metal ions has led to serious water resource pollution. Many heavy metals such as lead, cobalt, copper, and zinc are global pollutants. These metals are toxic and can cause cancer, and they can harm human health and aquatic ecosystems when they enter the water and food chain. Therefore, recent studies have focused on developing new adsorbents that can selectively and quickly adsorb harmful metal ions. Wastewater from various industries, such as metallurgy, leather, chemical production, mining, and battery manufacturing, contains one or more of these metal ions. It is crucial to remove these metal ions from wastewater before discharging them into the environment because they can contaminate the food chain through waste emissions into water bodies. Water pollution is a relevant issue that has attracted the attention of scientists. Moreover, water is essential for all biological and industrial processes, and any change in its quality will affect the environmental balance. Although water covers 75% of the Earth’s crust, only 0.7% is freshwater. The main source of freshwater in Egypt is the Nile River. However, relying on it continuously will create serious problems in the future. The World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that one-third of people worldwide lack access to clean water and that the problem is getting worse as a result of rising water demand. , Humans depend mainly on their ability to manage natural resources. Nanobentonite is an efficient adsorbent for heavy metals due to its unique properties. Its high surface area, arising from its nanoscale size, provides numerous active sites for metal ion adsorption, enhancing its capacity for heavy metal removal. Its layered silicate structure facilitates ion exchange, trapping metal ions within its layers, while surface functional groups like hydroxyls and silanols enable strong interactions with heavy metals. Nanobentonite’s negative surface charge creates a high affinity for positively charged transition metals, supporting effective adsorption. Additionally, as a natural, abundant, and cost-effective material, nanobentonite offers environmental and economic advantages for water purification and wastewater treatment. Hence, new methods should be proposed to purify water and enhance its quality to cope with overpopulation. So the goal of this research is to remove heavy metals from wastewater to reuse it.

2.2. Operating Method

Because nanobentonite is so tiny, batch procedures are employed. As a result, it has not had an impact on the batch sample collection at intervals. Shaking nanobentonite and a solution containing 100 ppm of copper­(II) and nickel­(II) was used to conduct these studies. To determine the ideal pH for each metal solution, a range of pH values, from 2 to 8, was investigated for each solution. NaOH and nitric acid were used to change the pH. The study involved varying the temperature (303, 313, 323, and 333 K), contact time (3, 10, 15, 20, and 30 min), initial concentrations of copper­(II) and nickel­(II) (50, 100, 150, 200, and 300 mg/L), and dosage of nanobentonite (0.01, 0.03, 0.05, 0.08, and 0.1 g L–1). An aliquot of 0.25 mL was taken from the reaction solution at different intervals for analysis. At regular intervals, the samples were collected. The atomic absorption spectrophotometer was then used to analyze them. The precipitate materials were extracted from the solution by filtering it. Using an atomic absorption spectrophotometer, the nanobentonite was examined both before and after the ions of copper­(II) and nickel­(II) were adsorbed.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. pH’s Impact

Metal sorption depends on pH, which affects both the metal chemistry in solution and the availability of binding sites on the sorbent’s functional groups. The pH of the metal ion solution was adjusted by adding dilute NaOH or HNO3 solution. The initial concentrations of copper­(II) and nickel­(II) were 100 mg/L, and the adsorption time was 30 min to reach equilibrium. Figure shows how the starting pH affects the adsorption process. The adsorbed amount of copper and nickel ions increases with higher pH because of the negative surface charge of nanobentonite at high pH values, with maximum adsorption capacity at pH 5.0 for Cu­(II) and pH 6.0 for Ni­(II). However, as Figure shows, increasing the pH from 2.0 to 8.0 enhanced the removal of Ni­(II) and Cu­(II). In acidic conditions, most of the functional groups of the components are protonated, leaving few ionized groups available. Adsorption in acid medium can be explained by competition between proton-metal species. The maximum pH uptake value can be attributed to the free lone pair of electrons on deprotonated oxygen atoms, which can coordinate with metal ions.

Figure indicates that when pH increases, the percentage of adsorbent copper­(II) and nickel­(II) ions increases to a maximum of 10. It is associated with an increase in pH, which induces the replacement of hydrogen ions on the surface of the micro bentonite with metal ions, resulting in increased adsorption efficacy. Adsorption studies on the influence of adsorbent quantity, beginning concentration, and contact time for copper­(II) and nickel­(II) ions were conducted since the optimum pH values for copper­(II) and nickel­(II) adsorption by nanobentonite are 5.0 and 6.0, respectively.

3.2. Effect of Contact Time

The metal ions in each sample were measured by atomic absorption spectroscopy after 30 min of adsorption. The adsorption experiment was repeated three times, and the findings were averaged. The data were recorded and the metal ion removal over time was plotted. The adsorbent removed the metal ions from the solution in 30 min. So, 30 min was chosen as the optimal contact time. Figures , , , and show how the metal ion removal depends on the contact time. Five different initial concentrations (50, 100, 150, 200, and 300 mg/L) were used on 0.1 g of nanobentonite. The adsorption efficiency was calculated from the difference in metal content before and after equilibrium adsorption. Metal ion removal increased with time and reached a maximum after 30 min. This means that the metals in the solution decreased a lot in the first 30 min, and the removal was almost complete after 30 min. The metal ion removal can be divided into two stages: one with a high removal rate and one with a low removal rate. This is because at the beginning, there were many free adsorption sites for copper­(II) and nickel­(II) to bind to. The equilibrium time for copper­(II) and nickel­(II) adsorption on nanobentonite was determined by the constant adsorption capacity for all of the concentrations tested. Therefore, the initial fast rate may be due to surface adsorption. First, the surface was empty and the reaction went faster. But later, the adsorbate molecules entered the pores and got adsorbed there, which is called intraparticle diffusion.

It was also found that the higher the primary metal ion concentration, the more metal ions were absorbed, or q t (mg/g). The kinetic experiment showed that copper­(II) and nickel­(II) adsorption on nanobentonite was a two-step process. First, there was a fast adsorption of copper­(II) and nickel­(II) to the external surface. Then, there was slow intraparticle diffusion inside the adsorbent. This means that the adsorbent particles had two different types of binding sites for the metal ions.

3.3. Effect of Adsorbent Dose

By shifting the amount of nanobentonite from 0.01 to 0.1 g while holding other variables (such as pH, temperature (Figure ), agitation speed, initial copper­(II) and nickel­(II) ions concentration, and contact time) constant, we studied the dependence of the adsorption of cations copper­(II) and nickel­(II) on nanobentonite. Figures and demonstrate that employing nanobentonite as the adsorption dosage increased the percent removal of copper­(II) and nickel­(II) ions from 64 to 77% for copper­(II) and from 85.4 to 95.2% for nickel­(II). Figures and demonstrate that, for fixed initial concentrations, the quantity of adsorbed metal ions decreases as the adsorbate dose is increased (Figure ).

For the rest of the study, 0.1 g of adsorbent dosage is considered as optimum dosage for copper­(II) and nickel­(II) removal using nanobentonite.

3.4. The Effect of Initial Copper and Nickel Ion Concentrations

The copper and nickel removal percentage dropped from 100 to 80% as the initial copper and nickel ion concentrations rose from 50 to 300 ppm. This is because at lower metal ion concentrations, there were enough adsorption sites for complete adsorption. But at higher metal ion concentrations, there were not enough adsorption sites, so the metal sorption percentage decreased. The metal ion concentration affects the adsorption capacity. , Figure shows that the metal ion amount, q e (mg/g), increased with increasing initial concentration. Copper­(II) and nickel­(II) ions were adsorbed at dissimilar initial concentrations from 50 to 300 mg/L at pH 5.0 and 6.0, respectively, for 30 min at 303 K, 200 rpm, and 0.1 g of nanobentonite. Figures and , respectively, show that the metal ion amount, q e (mg/g), improved by increasing the initial concentration.

The initial metal ion concentration determined % removal. This means that metal ions formed a monolayer on the surface of nanobentonite.

3.5. Temperature’s Impact

The text explains how temperature affects the adsorption process. Higher temperature lowers the solution’s viscosity, which speeds up the diffusion of the adsorbate molecules into the adsorbent’s outer layer and increases the adsorption (Figure ). Also, temperature changes can alter the adsorbent’s equilibrium adsorption capacity. Therefore, studying how temperature influences the adsorption process can reveal standard Gibbs free energy, enthalpy, and entropy changes of the adsorption. The batch adsorption experiments used different temperatures from 303 to 333 K, with constant initial concentrations of 100 mg/L and 200 rpm for copper­(II) and nickel­(II) ions, respectively; optimal pH values of 5.0 and 6.0 for copper and nickel, respectively; and 0.1 g of nanobentonite (Figures and ). The results showed that temperature enhanced the adsorption capacity of nanobentonite for both copper­(II) and nickel­(II) ions (Figure ). This is because higher temperature increases the movement and interaction of the ions with the adsorbent, as well as the formation of active surface sites, or the intraparticle diffusion rate of the ions. Using the following equations, the text additionally computes the thermodynamic parameters from the adsorption equilibrium data at various temperatures, including standard Gibbs free energy (ΔG°), standard enthalpy change (ΔH°), and standard entropy change (ΔS°)

The thermodynamic parameters ΔG°, ΔH°, and ΔS° from the adsorption equilibrium data at different temperatures using equations and plots in Figure and Table . The positive ΔS° means more disorder at the interface during adsorption, which makes the system more random − . Figure shows that more ions are adsorbed at higher temperature because the ions move and interact more with the adsorbent’s active sites. Other researchers have also found similar trends for aqueous phase adsorption. In other words, from eq , the entropic term TΔS° decides the sign of ΔG°. The positive value of ΔH° means that the sorption processes are exothermic. Moreover, the negative values of ΔG° show the spontaneity of the sorption processes. The value of ΔG° drops with temperature, which indicates that the reaction is more spontaneous at higher temperatures. Therefore, a conclusion can be made that the adsorption process of copper­(II) and nickel­(II) ions are intraparticle diffusions.

4.1. The Kinetic Model with Pseudo-First Order

The earliest known equation for the adsorption rate based on the adsorption capacity is the Lagergren pseudo-first-order model, which can be expressed linearly as

ln(qe−qt)=ln(qe)−k1t 8

where t (min) is the duration, k 1 (min–1) is the pseudo-first-order adsorption rate coefficient, and q e (mg/g) and q t (mg/g) are the values of amount adsorbed per unit mass at equilibrium and at any time, respectively. The values of k 1 and q e are listed in Figures and and in Table and can be determined from the slope and intercept of the linear plot of ln­(q e – q t) vs t.

To fit the experimental data to eq , one must know the value of q e. A comparison between the q e values produced from plots of ln (q e–q t) vs t 20, 21 and the experimentally found q e values is the ultimate test of the accuracy of eq . There is a discrepancy in the equilibrium adsorption capacity (q e) between the computed and experimental data, and the correlation coefficients for the pseudo-first-order kinetic model are low, suggesting a subpar pseudo-first-order fit to the experimental data.

4.2. The Kinetic Model with Pseudo-Second Order

H o and Mackay , were then used to analyze the kinetic data, assuming pseudo-second-order adsorption. This is how the linear form is expressed.

tqt=1k2qe2+1qet 9

where k 2 is the pseudo-second-order (mg g–1 min–1) adsorption rate constant. Plotting t/q t against t should show a linear relationship, from which q e and k 2 can be obtained by calculating the plot’s slope and intercept, respectively (q e = 1/slope and k 2 = (slope)2/intercept). Figures and show this relationship.

The two models previously described can be evaluated for applicability using each of the linear plots depicted in Figures and . These graphs were used to calculate the correlation coefficient, or R 2, which quantified how applicable each model was. The kinetic rate constants derived from the first- and second-order pseudokinetic models are displayed in Table . The correlation coefficient, R 2, is relatively high (>0.9979) for the pseudo-second-order adsorption model, and the adsorption capabilities calculated by the model are likewise close to those established by experiments. Unfortunately, the R 2 values for the pseudo-first order are insufficient. So a pseudo-second-order model of the removal kinetic of nickel­(II) and copper­(II) indicates that chemisorption governs the adsorption process, i.e., the rate-limiting step is the creation of chemical bonds between the metal ions and the active sites on the surface of the nanobentonite. According to this concept, there is a strong interaction between the adsorbent and the adsorbate since the adsorption capacity is related to the square of the number of active sites occupied. As a result, the pseudo-second-order adsorption model was shown to be more appropriate for modeling nickel and copper adsorption kinetics on nanobentonite.

The mechanism of the reaction can be interpreted as the first step involves the surface of nanobentonite particles, which have functional groups such as silanol (−Si–OH) and aluminol (−Al–OH), being approached by copper (Cu2+) and nickel (Ni2+) ions (Figure ). The metal ions can approach the reactive sites in anticipation of ion exchange or bonding because these functional groups on nanobentonite are ready for interaction. Once close, chemisorption takes place, generating inner-sphere complexes as Cu2+ and Ni2+ ions replace naturally existing cations like magnesium (Mg2+) and sodium (Na+) on the surface. The metal ions and the surface of the nanobentonite form solid, long-lasting chemical bonds as a result of this process. van der Waals forces enable the reversible binding of extra metal ions to the surface during weaker physical adsorption, also referred to as physisorption. Strong chemisorption and lesser physisorption work together to guarantee long-lasting and adaptable interactions between the metal ions and the nanobentonite.

5. Equilibrium Adsorption Study

The adsorption isotherm shows how much of a substance sticks to an adsorbent at a fixed temperature and how much stays in the solution. It helps us understand how solutes and sorbents behave. We need a good model to design and improve adsorption processes. Some models are Langmuir, Freundlich, Dubinin–Radushkevich, Redlich–Peterson, Sips, and Temkin. We used the first three models to test our data.

5.1. Langmuir Model

The Langmuir model describes how a substance adsorbs on a surface with incomplete number of equal sites. , It makes some assumptions: (1) The adsorbent has specific sites for sorption; (2) copper­(II) and nickel­(II) take one site each; (3) the adsorbent can only hold so much of the substance (at equilibrium); and (4) all sites are the same and have the same energy. The model also ignores the movement of the substance on the surface. The model helps us find the maximum amount of adsorption when the surface is fully covered. We use this equation to apply the model

Ceqe=1qmax.b+Ceqmax 10

where q e is how much metal ion is on the adsorbent (mg/g), C e is how much metal ion is in the solution, q max is how much the adsorbent can hold, and b is a constant. Figure shows graphs of C e/q e versus time. We used a formula and the graphs to get the Langmuir parameters using C e. We calculated b and q max from the slope and intercept of the graphs. High R 2 values show that the Langmuir model fits well with these adsorptions.

The Langmuir isotherm’s key properties can be stated in terms of a dimensionless constant separation factor (R L), which is defined as

RL=11+bCo 11

where C o represents the metal ion’s starting concentration. The R L values reflect the sort of isotherm, which might be unfavorable (R L > 1), linear (R L = 1), favorable (0 < R L < 1), or irreversible (R L = 0). The R L values for Cu­(II) ion adsorption ranged from 0.74 to 0.32, whereas Ni­(II) ion adsorption ranged from 0.54 to 0.16. Thus, the adsorption process is advantageous.

5.2. Freundlich Model

The Freundlich isotherm model was the first to characterize the sorption process. The theory applies to adsorption on heterogeneous surfaces where adsorbed molecules interact, and the Freundlich equation demonstrates that sorption energy reduces exponentially as an adsorbent’s sorption cores are completed. This isotherm is a linear equation that explains heterogeneous systems.

log⁡qe=log⁡Kf+1nlogCe 12

where K f and 1/n are the Freundlich constants for adsorption capacity and intensity, respectively. Figure depicts the linear plot of log q e versus log C e, with the constants 1/n and K f computed from the slope and intercept, respectively, and then gathered in Table . The value of 1/n smaller than 1 indicates favorable adsorption and confirms the heterogeneity of the adsorbent. It also implies that there is a strong link between heavy metal ions and nanobentonite.

5.3. Dubinin–Radushkevich (D-R) Isotherm Model

The D-R model is a formula that describes how a substance fills the pores of an adsorbent. It works for physical adsorption processes and allows for more than one layer of adsorption. The linear form of the D-R isotherm model is written as

ln⁡qe=ln⁡qs−Kadε2 13

The theoretical isotherm saturation capacity (q s) is expressed in mg/g, the gas constant R (J/mol·K) is the D-R isotherm constant (mol2/J2), the absolute temperature is T (K), and the Polanyi potential is equal to RT ln (1 + 1Ce ).

A linear relationship between ln q e and ε2 is depicted in Figure . K ad (mol2/J2) is the plot’s slope, and Table ’s intercept provides the sorption capacity q s (mg/g). When the adsorbate is moved from infinity in the solution to the surface of the solid, the constant K ad can be used to compute the mean free energy E (kJ/mol) for adsorption per molecule.

E=1/(2Kad)1/2 14

This parameter provides details on the type of adsorption, such as physical or chemical ion exchange. When E is less than 8 kJ/mol, the adsorption process is physical; however, when E is between 8 and 16 kJ/mol, chemical ion exchange is the method used in the adsorption process. 6. Infrared Spectra of Nanobentonite

The FTIR spectra of nanobentonite before and after adsorbing copper­(II) and nickel­(II) were measured in the 500–4000 cm–1 range to identify the deviations in the functional groups of the adsorbent. Figure shows the spectra, which include clay phase and impurity bands. The bands at 3629.54 and 3417.92 cm–1 indicate hydroxyl groups, while the bands at 3417.92 and 1633.36 cm–1 suggest water hydration in the adsorbent. The band at 3622.5 cm–1 is assigned to interlayer hydrogen bonding in clay. The bands near 1030.16, 912, and 796.72 cm–1 correspond to Si–O–Si, Al2OH, and MgAlOH vibrations, respectively, indicating the presence of montmorillonite. The bands at 616.77, 516.48, and 460.6 cm–1 are due to Si–O vibrations of quartz.

Because of the presence of numerous elements, the coupled vibrations are noticeable. Si–O stretching vibrations were detected at 616.77, 516.48, and 460.6 cm–1 in the infrared examinations of bentonite, indicating the presence of quartz. The presence of quartz is also supported by the occurrence of the δ (Si–O) and v (Si–O–Si) bands. The vibrations detected at 914.5 cm–1 suggest that hematite might be present.

7. Advantages of Using Nanobentonite

Nanobentonite is a low-cost and naturally abundant adsorbent, making it an attractive alternative to more expensive compounds such as activated carbon and chitosan, which require complicated preparation. Its layered silicate structure allows for efficient ion exchange, allowing Cu­(II) and Ni­(II) ions to be trapped an advantage over materials such as charcoal that lack this property. Furthermore, unlike zeolite, nanobentonite’s surface is easily changed with functional groups, increasing its reactivity and adsorption effectiveness. It also has great thermal and chemical stability, making it more lasting in harsh environments than chitosan, which is biodegradable and less stable in some applications. Nanobentonite’s enhanced ion exchange capability allows for more efficient heavy metal removal, outperforming biochar as a natural and environmentally sustainable substance.

8. Conclusions

In this work, ions of copper­(II) and nickel­(II) were extracted from aqueous solutions using nanobentonite. The effects of temperature, adsorbent dose, beginning metal ion concentration, contact time, pH, and batch adsorption studies were investigated. More than 98% of copper­(II) and nickel­(II) were eliminated under ideal circumstances (contact duration = 15 min, pH = 5.0 and 6.0 for copper­(II) and nickel­(II), and nanobentonite concentration = 0.01 mg/L). The adsorption kinetics were found to be better described by a pseudo-second-order adsorption model. For the adsorption of copper­(II) and nickel­(II) ions, the Langmuir adsorption model showed the optimum R L values in the ranges of 0.66–0.88, 0.06–0.88, and 0.686–0.89, respectively. The heterogeneity of the adsorbent was confirmed, and a favorable adsorption process was shown by the Freundlich value of 1/n being smaller than 1. The exothermic reaction is revealed by the thermodynamic data (ΔG, ΔH, and ΔS). The nanobentonite’s FTIR spectra were recorded both prior to and during the adsorption of copper­(II) and nickel­(II).
