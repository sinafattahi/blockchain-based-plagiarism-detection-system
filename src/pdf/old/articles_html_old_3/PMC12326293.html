ABSTRACT

Nanoplanktonic diatoms (2–20 μm) are a significant yet historically understudied component of marine ecosystems. We investigated three recently isolated nanoplanktonic diatoms from the Northwest Atlantic Ocean (NWA): Minidiscus spinulatus, Mediolabrus comicus, and Minidiscus trioculatus. Using Oxford Nanopore sequencing, we assembled and annotated their complete chloroplast and mitochondrial genomes. Pangenome analyses revealed that Minidiscus species consistently clustered more closely with select Thalassiosira species, whereas M. comicus formed a sister clade with Skeletonema. Circularized chloroplast genomes allowed us to characterize the full‐length 16S ribosomal RNAs for each isolate, thereby leading to higher resolution of these taxa in preexisting 16S metabarcoding data. During our study, M. spinulatus was primarily restricted to the Bedford Basin. In contrast, both M. trioculatus and M. comicus had larger geographic ranges extending to the Labrador Sea, and in the case of M. comicus, to the Canadian Arctic Gateway. Weekly metabarcoding from the coastal Bedford Basin, N.S., Canada (2014–2022), revealed a seasonal succession of nanoplanktonic taxa, with Minidiscus trioculatus dominating in the early months, followed by M. comicus and M. spinulatus . Our results highlight the critical value of phytoplankton isolations and organelle genomics for expanding our understanding of the diversity and biogeography of nanoplanktonic diatoms.

Keywords: Arctic, chloroplast genomes, eDNA, Labrador Sea, marine microbiology, Mediolabrus, microbial ecology, Minidiscus, mitochondrial genomes, scotia shelf

1. Introduction

Phytoplankton are vital contributors to biogeochemical cycles, including ocean carbon export, due to their role as primary producers. Diatoms are unicellular phytoplankton with silica shells that occur in marine environments worldwide, contributing around 20% of global oxygen production (Nelson et al. 1995; Falkowski et al. 1998). While conservative estimates suggest that ~12,000 identified species of diatoms exist with another 8,000 yet to be discovered, other estimates suggest there could be as many as 200,000 species (Guiry 2012). Diatom size classes include picoplankton (< 2 μm), nanoplankton (2–20 μm), and microplankton (> 20 μm) (Guiry 2012; Leblanc et al. 2018). The ecological significance of nanoplanktonic diatoms is underscored by recent Tara Oceans metabarcoding data, which show that diatoms were most abundant in the 3–20 μm size class (Malviya et al. 2016; Leblanc et al. 2018; Pierella Karlusich et al. 2025).

Thalassiosiraceae is a family of centric diatoms composed of 26 different genera, including Thalassiosira, Conticriba, Porosira, Minidiscus, and Mediolabrus. First discovered in 1973 by Hasle (Round et al. 1990; Guiry and Guiry 2024; Hasle 1973), Minidiscus and Mediolabrus spp. are the smallest known centric diatoms, ranging from < 2–8 μm, and are known to exist as either solitary cells, in aggregations, or sometimes in chains formed from the central fultoportulae (Arsenieff et al. 2020; Kaczmarska et al. 2009; Leblanc et al. 2018). Importantly, recent taxonomic reclassification has placed some Minidiscus species into the new genus Mediolabrus (Li et al. 2020) based on morphological and phylogenetic differences. When reclassifying some Minidiscus as Mediolabrus based on morphology, it was found that Minidiscus has at least one fultoportula in the valve center close to a rimoportula, whereas Mediolabrus only has a single rimoportula concentrating processes in the valve center, with no fultoportula near the rimoportula (Li et al. 2020). Phylogenetic trees further demonstrated that Minidiscus is not a monophyletic group; the Minidiscus are closely related to Thalassiosira species, while the Mediolabrus are a sister clade to the Skeletonema genus. According to Algaebase [as of April 2025], there are nine known Minidiscus species and three known Mediolabrus species (Guiry and Guiry 2022, 2024).

Minidiscus and Mediolabrus have been observed using metabarcoding in various locations across mid‐ and high‐latitude sites, as well as well‐mixed environments [e.g., within the Northwest Atlantic (NWA)] (Leblanc et al. 2018; Bolaños et al. 2020; Robicheau et al. 2022; Daniels et al. 2015; Aké‐Castillo et al. 2001; Pesant et al. 2015; Fernandes and Correr‐Da‐Silva 2020; Malviya et al. 2016). According to the metabarcoding of the V4 18S ribosomal RNA (rRNA) completed by the Tara Oceans expedition, Minidiscus (encompassing both Minidiscus and Mediolabrus) is the 10th most abundant diatom genus in the global ocean (Malviya et al. 2016; Leblanc et al. 2018; Pierella Karlusich et al. 2025). More locally within the NWA, genetic markers targeting the highly variable regions (V4V5 and V6V8) of 16S rRNA have identified that amplicon sequence variants (ASVs) in the Minidiscus genus rank among the top ten most common diatom genera in the Bedford Basin (BB) and the Scotian Shelf (SS) from 2014 to 2017 (Robicheau et al. 2022).

Due to their size, it was previously assumed that nanoplanktonic diatoms were primarily remineralized in the microbial loop; however, recent observations have revealed that they can form large blooms and contribute to carbon export with high sinking rates (Bolaños et al. 2020; Leblanc et al. 2018; Malviya et al. 2016; Arsenieff et al. 2020). Carbon export is further supported through the identification of frustules in Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) images of sediment traps following a large diatom bloom dominated by Mediolabrus comicus and Minidiscus trioculatus in the Mediterranean Sea (Leblanc et al. 2018). These findings suggest that, unlike previous assumptions, nanoplanktonic diatoms may play a significant role in carbon export that has been historically underestimated due to size‐limited sampling methods (Leblanc et al. 2018). The higher proportion of unassigned reads in smaller size fractions (3/5–20 μm) reflects the limited representation of nanoplanktonic diatoms in genetic reference databases, in contrast to diatoms that have been identified in larger size fractions (Pierella Karlusich et al. 2025). It is therefore critical that we investigate nanoplanktonic diatoms and discover more about their distribution, phylogeny, and physiology.

Distinguishing between centric nanoplanktonic diatoms is difficult using conventional light microscopy, as demonstrated by the recent reclassification of Minidiscus species. It is clear that molecular information in conjunction with enhanced morphological investigations (i.e., using SEM) are needed to more accurately identify these microbes (Li et al. 2020). Oftentimes, smaller barcodes of DNA are unable to distinguish organisms down to the species‐level. Full mitochondrial (mt) and chloroplast (cp) genomes have been proposed as potential ‘super‐barcodes’ that can be used to effectively distinguish between organisms (Wu et al. 2021). As the cost decreases due to the increasing efficiency of molecular methods, using longer nuclear or plastid molecular markers to examine microbial communities is becoming feasible (Wu et al. 2021).

This work explores three nanoplanktonic diatoms in the Thalassiosiraceae family that were isolated from water collected in the BB and the SS, both from within the NWA: Minidiscus spinulatus (BB), Minidiscus trioculatus (SS), and Mediolabrus comicus (SS) (the third species previously being known as Minidiscus comicus). Prior work by Mordret et al. (2024) identified strains AGSB‐0017 (NCBI Accession: OR922670) and AGSB‐0018 (NCBI Accession: OR922671) as Mediolabrus comicus and Minidiscus trioculatus, respectively, using long rRNA fragments that included 18S, ITS, and 28S rRNA gene regions. We report the complete organellar genomes from these three Northwest Atlantic isolates, revealing differences in genome organization compared to previously sequenced organellar genomes in the Thalassiosirales order. Additionally, phylogenomic trees completed with concatenated alignments of protein‐coding genes re‐emphasize the classification of M. comicus as more closely related to the Skeletonema genus and the classification of Minidiscus species as closer to select Thalassiosira. The complete cp sequences also allowed us to revisit the distribution of these three species using highly resolved (to the species‐level) environmental 16S rRNA data from the BB, SS, Labrador Sea (LS), and Canadian Arctic Gateway (CAG). Overall, this study broadens our understanding of the phylogeny, evolution, and distribution of nanoplanktonic centric diatoms in the Northwest Atlantic Ocean.

2. Materials and Methods

2.1. Sampling, Isolation, and Culture Maintenance

Diatoms were isolated from NWA coastal seawater in the BB and SS (Figure S1). Minidiscus spinulatus (AGSB‐0477) was isolated from seawater collected at the Compass Buoy Station sampled through the Fisheries and Oceans (DFO) Bedford Basin Monitoring Program (BBMP; Bedford Basin Monitoring Program) [44°41′37″ N, 63°38′25″ W] on July 25, 2018 (NS, Canada) (Li et al. 1998). For this isolate, 1 m seawater was plated on Artificial Seawater (ASW) media with 0.2 μM NaNO3 plus 1.2% w/v agarose and was incubated at 15°C ± 1°C via a 12 h:12 h light:dark cycle at an irradiance of 100 μmol/m2/s. Minidiscus spinulatus was maintained for several years on the above medium by transferring it monthly. The M. trioculatus (AGSB‐0018) and M. comicus (AGSB‐0017) strains were isolated from seawater samples collected with a CTD‐Rosette at chlorophyll maximum depths (28 m) on August 8, 2021, during the COOGER‐2021 research cruise [45°30′37.70″ N, 60°39′5.47″ W] (Mordret et al. 2024). Both strains were isolated by dilution series with L1 medium (NCMA/Bigelow Laboratory for Ocean Sciences, USA) and maintained in liquid culture at a 16:8 h light–dark cycle under 30 μmol of light (Mordret et al. 2024).

In preparation for genomics work, the three strains were individually grown in 50 mL liquid cultures of L1 made with 0.2 μm filtered seawater [at ~34 ppt salinity] (NCMA/Bigelow Laboratory for Ocean Sciences, USA). Phytoplankton isolates were treated with an antibiotic mixture at a final concentration of 100 μg/mL penicillin G, 25 μg/mL dihydrostreptomycin sulfate, and 25 μg/mL gentamycin sulfate, minimizing bacterial contamination. Each strain was transferred every 2–3 weeks and maintained at 16°C ± 1°C. All three cultures are available in the Algal Genomics and Synthetic Biology (AGSB) culture collection maintained by the National Research Council of Canada (NRC) in Halifax, Nova Scotia.

2.2. Nucleic Acid Extraction and Genomic Sequencing

Isolates were grown to 1–2 × 109 cells L−1 and syringe‐filtered onto 3 μm nucleopore filters (Millipore, Germany). DNA was extracted from the cells using the enhanced lysis procedure described by Zorz et al. (2019) and the DNeasy Plant Extraction Kit (Qiagen, Germany). DNA was further concentrated using the Genomic DNA Clean & Concentrator‐10 kit D4011 (Zymo Research, USA). Sample concentration and purity were assessed using a NanoDrop 2000 (Thermo Scientific, USA) and a Qubit 4 fluorometer via a High‐Sensitivity dsDNA Quantification Assay kit (Thermo Scientific, USA). Extracted genomic DNA was then sequenced using a Nanopore Ligation Sequencing Kit V14 (SQK‐LSK114) and a flow cell (R10.4.1) for each isolate according to the manufacturer's instructions (Noakes et al. 2019; Wang et al. 2021). Nanopore sequencing started with 1402–1491 pores available and ran for 32–48 h depending on the isolate, with runs resulting in 10.99–15.59 Gb and 2.84–5.56 M reads of data (Data S1).

Sequences were base‐called from FAST5 files and converted to FASTQ files. Porechop (v0.2.4) was used to trim adapters, filtlong (v0.2.1) to filter out low‐quality sequences, and then raven (v6.10.0) and raven‐assembler (v1.6.1) were used to assemble long reads into contigs with default parameters (Wick et al. 2017; Vaser and Šikić 2020, 2021). Once assembled, the long‐read polishing programs pepper (v8.0) and medaka (v1.11.3) were used to polish the contigs (Shafin et al. 2021; Lee et al. 2021). Putative homopolymer‐associated frameshifts, commonly observed with nanopore sequencing, were manually inspected and corrected in Geneious Prime 2024.0.7. In Geneious, contigs with low GC‐content (29.1%–31.3%) were identified as mt and cp sequences, some of which were completed; their circularity was confirmed using the program GetOrganelle (v1.7.7.1) (Jin et al. 2020). The cp genomes of M. trioculatus and M. comicus contained self‐overlapping regions with multiple inverted repeats, which were manually resolved in Geneious to produce circular organellar genomes.

2.3. Phylogenetic Tree of Related Species From the 18S rRNA Gene

A phylogenetic tree of the 18S rRNA gene was constructed using 92 related species to determine the evolutionary relationships of the cultured isolates and to classify the AGSB‐0477 culture, which had not been identified elsewhere. The 18S rRNA sequences were aligned using MUSCLE with default parameters, employing the PPP algorithm (Edgar 2004). To determine the best‐fit model of nucleotide substitution, we used ModelFinder implemented in IQ‐TREE2 (v2.3.6), which identified GTR + F + I + R3 as the optimal model based on the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) (Minh et al. 2020; Kalyaanamoorthy et al. 2017). Maximum likelihood analysis was first performed using IQ‐TREE2 with 1000 ultrafast bootstrap replicates and model selection restricted to models compatible with MrBayes for later comparison. Additional maximum likelihood analysis was carried out using PHYML (v. 3.0) with 1000 bootstrap replicates under a GTR substitution model (Guindon et al. 2005, 2010). Bayesian inference was performed in MrBayes (v. 3.2.6) under the GTR + G model, with four Markov chains run for 1,100,000 generations, sampling every 200 generations (Huelsenbeck and Ronquist 2001; Guindon et al. 2005, 2010). The IQ‐TREE2 software was used as the basis of the phylogenetic tree, and the PHYML bootstraps and Bayesian inferences were added as additional supports using Inkscape (v. 1.1.2). Biomass preparation and DNA extraction for this analysis were the same as that described below for genomic sequencing. The 18S rRNA genes were amplified and sequenced using the methods described by Mordret et al. (2024).

2.4. Organellar Genome Annotation and Comparison

Organellar genomes were first annotated using MFannot with the codon table for “Mold, Protozoan, Coelenterate Mitochondrial and Mycoplasma/Spiroplasma Code” selected for mt and the codon table “Bacterial, Archaeal, and Plant Plastid Code” selected for cps (Lang et al. 2023). The MFannot output was converted to GenBank format using the AGAT v1.4.2 toolkit to convert the files to GFF3 format and the EMBOSS server to convert GFF3 files to GenBank format (Rice et al. 2000). RNAweasel and MFannot were used to check tRNAs, introns (group I and group II), rnpB, rrn5, and rns (Greiner et al. 2019; Tillich et al. 2017). The annotations were subsequently checked using a variety of tools, including tRNAscan‐SE (v2.0.7) software and ARAGORN to identify introns in the mt (maximum length = 3000 bp) and Basic Local Alignment Search Tool (BLAST) against diatom reference genomes (Laslett and Canback 2004; Chan and Lowe 2019; Tillich et al. 2017). For cps, references included Minidiscus spinulatus, Conticribra weissflogii, Roundia cardiophora, Thalassiosira oceanica CCMP1005 and Thalassiosira pseudonana and for mt, this included Skeletonema marinoi and Thalassiosira pseudonana (Tillich et al. 2017). Annotations were visualized on circular genome maps using OGDRAW v1.3.1 (Figure S2) and with the software program Proksee (v2.0) (Figure 2) (Greiner et al. 2019; Grant et al. 2023). A BLAST comparison of each organellar genome to the two other isolates was conducted using the “BLAST” feature of the Proksee software (Grant et al. 2023).

2.5. Comparison of Organellar Genomes Through Concatenated Phylogenomic and Pangenomic Analyses

All available complete mt and cp genomes from the Thalassiosirales taxonomic order were downloaded from Genbank (Clark et al. 2016). For the mt genomes, this resulted in 16 mt genomes, including the three provided in this study (Table S2) (Clark et al. 2016). For these 16 genomes, we constructed a concatenated phylogenetic tree of 28 protein‐coding mt genes (see Table S4 for gene list) (Cox et al. 2024). For the cp genomes, identical sequences of the same species were removed, which resulted in 37 complete cp genomes obtained from Genbank—34 previously available and the three obtained herein (Table S3) (Clark et al. 2016). Among these genomes is the previously mentioned Minidiscus spinulatus, isolated from Jiaozhou Bay, China (Nordberg et al. 2014; Wang and Chen 2022). There were 97 protein‐coding genes selected for the concatenated alignment and phylogenetic tree (see Table S5 for the gene list) (Cox et al. 2024).

In the case of the phylogenetic trees, there were two organisms from the Lithodesmiales order used as an outgroup: Helicotheca tamesis and Lithodesmium undulatum . Organellar genomes were converted to contigs databases using the Anvi'o function ‘anvi‐gen‐contigs‐database’ (Eren et al. 2015). Each genome database was then annotated with a custom HMM profile created from select protein‐coding genes in the cp (97) or mt (25) (Tables S4 and S5). The function ‘anvi‐run‐hmms’ was run on each of the contig databases, and then the concatenated alignment was retrieved using ‘anvi‐get‐sequences‐for‐hmm‐hits’ and the MUSCLE alignment algorithm (Eren et al. 2015; Edgar 2004). The model for each of the partitions was determined using ModelFinder in IQ‐TREE2 (v2.3.6), which applied different best‐fit models (e.g., TIM3 + F + I + R3, GTR + F + R4) to each partitioned gene set based on the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) (Minh et al. 2020; Kalyaanamoorthy et al. 2017). In both the mt and cp trees, the maximum likelihood analysis was conducted using IQ‐TREE2 with 1000 ultrafast bootstrap replicates (Minh et al. 2020). To further compare the similarity of the organisms, the same contigs databases were used in the Anvi'o pangenome pipeline (Eren et al. 2015). Additionally, the average nucleotide identity (ANI) was calculated using the fastANI algorithm (Hernández‐Salmerón and Moreno‐Hagelsieb 2022).

2.6. Sample Collection for Environmental Metabarcoding Analyses

The distribution of nanoplanktonic diatom isolates in the NWA was examined using metabarcoding datasets and related environmental data compiled over multiple years from various regions, thanks to the work of many research groups. These datasets included weekly sampling through the BBMP and multiple expeditions to the SS through the DFO Atlantic Zone Monitoring Program (AZMP; Atlantic Zone Monitoring Program (AZMP)), LS through the DFO Atlantic Zone Off‐Shelf Monitoring Program (AZOMP; Atlantic Zone Off‐Shelf Monitoring Program (AZOMP)) and the Ocean Frontier Institute's Northwest Atlantic Biological Carbon Pump project (NWA‐BCP; The Northwest Atlantic Biological Carbon Pump) and the CAG through the GEOTRACES program (GEOTRACES) (Table 1) (Li et al. 1998; Therriault et al. 1998; Casault et al. 2024; Yashayaev et al. 2021). Due to the various sampling expeditions, some discrepancies in biomass sampling methods are important to take into account. The biomass samples for the SS, LS, and CAG expeditions were all size‐fractionated through large 3 μm (L) and small 0.2 μm (S) filters. In the case of the NWA‐BCP CE expedition to the LS, there were also marine snow catcher deployments at various sites with corresponding eDNA data. In the case of the BBMP, the filtering methods to collect biomass for DNA extraction have varied over that time. For some years (2014–2017, 2022), there was no size fractionation of the water sample, and therefore the sample was filtered through one 0.2 μm filter (W). However, from 2018 to 2021, the samples were size‐fractionated with the large 3 μm (L) and small 0.2 μm filters (S). To have comparable data across the years 2014–2022, the relative abundance of the sample was reconstituted using DNA concentration as a proxy for biomass (SL) (Haider et al. 2025).

2.7. Corresponding Oceanographic Data and Flow Cytometry Measurements

The BBMP is a long‐term weekly time series that has characterized microbial communities using flow cytometry since the 1990s (Li and Dickie 2001; Li 1998; Cullen et al. 2007). In 2014, environmental DNA (eDNA) metabarcoding was incorporated into the monitoring efforts to further investigate microbial diversity. In this work, we used BBMP oceanographic measurements and flow cytometry measurements to make ecological predictions related to the prevalence of our three isolates of interest. Samples for the BBMP were collected at depths of 1, 5, 10, and 60 m. Environmental parameters, including temperature, chlorophyll a, and nutrient concentrations, were obtained from the publicly available dataset maintained by the Bedford Institute of Oceanography (BIO) (Bedford Basin Monitoring Program). For consistency, flow cytometry cell counts (> 35 μm) for 2014–2017 were obtained from Robicheau et al. (2022), while counts for 2018–2022 were conducted in the LaRoche lab using a BD Accuri flow cytometer (2018–2021) and a Novocyte flow cytometer (2021–2022) from Agilent Technologies. In both cases, a threshold of < 450 was set for Chlorophyll‐H (proxy for chlorophyll), and approximate size gates of < 3, 3–10, and > 10 μm were established for forward scatter via Novocyte QC particles with a known size and fluorescence profile (Agilent Technologies).

2.8. Data Analyses for the Distribution of the Three Isolates

To investigate the prevalence of the three isolates in environmental genomic data, cp genomes were aligned to the 16S rRNA sequence available from M. spinulatus (Wang and Chen 2022). Identified 16S rRNA sequences were then aligned to ASVs for the V4V5 and V6V8 16S rRNA variable regions from environmental metabarcoding datasets (Data S2). Datasets from all of the expeditions were analyzed with the same pipeline as in Robicheau et al. (2022), with the only difference being that the ASV data was not rarefied (Robicheau et al. 2022; Comeau et al. 2017, 2011; Willis et al. 2019; Bolyen et al. 2019; Decelle et al. 2015; Quast et al. 2013; Walters et al. 2016; Parada et al. 2016; Amir et al. 2017). In determining the presence of these organisms at all geographic locations, a single identification of the nanoplanktonic diatom 16S barcode within the 1–20 m depth range at a set of geographic coordinates in a given year was considered a detection of the species at that site. To quantify the prevalence of the three diatoms in the photosynthetic community within a water sample, relative abundances were calculated as the percentage of total cp 16S reads in that sample.

Downstream data analysis and visualization were conducted using R (v4.4.2) and RStudio (v2024.09.1 + 394) with various packages (R Core Team 2021; RStudio Team 2020). Tidyverse (v2.2.0), tidyr (v1.3.1), and dplyr (v1.1.4) packages were used for initial data cleaning and filtering, and the microbiome (v1.28.0) and phyloseq (v2.12.1) packages were used to handle the microbial data (Wickham et al. 2019, 2023; Sudarshan 2012–2019; McMurdie and Holmes 2013; Boehmke 2016). The redundancy analysis (RDA) using the environmental variables in the BBMP was performed using vegan (v2.6‐10) and microbiome (v1.28.0) packages (Sudarshan 2012–2019; Dixon 2003). ASV feature tables were first CLR transformed with the ‘microbiome::transform()’ function (Sudarshan 2012–2019). All numerical environmental variables with sufficient data were initially retained (1530 out of 1736 samples). The environmental variables were checked for correlation using heatmaps, and variables were selected based on interest; highly correlated variables (|> 0.7|) were then removed (Figure S11). Standardization of the numerical values was performed using ‘deconstand()’ which scaled the data to a mean of zero, and then the RDA was conducted using the ‘rda()’ function (Dixon 2003). The significance of the RDA and each environmental variable was done using the ‘anova()’ function with 999 permutations. Variables deemed insignificant were discarded, resulting in a reduced RDA that focused on select oceanographic measurements (salinity, temperature, oxygen), nutrients (nitrate, ammonia), chlorophyll a and flow cytometry measurements (nanoeukaryotes and Synechococcus).

3. Results

3.1. NWA Isolates Are Small Nanoplanktonic Diatoms Belonging to Thalassiosirales

Isolates were initially identified as small centric diatoms based on cell morphology (Figure 1). A phylogenetic analysis of 18S rRNA sequences again confirmed the taxonomic placement of Minidiscus trioculatus [AGSB‐0018; OR22670] and Mediolabrus comicus [AGSB‐0017; OR22671] (Mordret et al. 2024) and further showed that the BB isolate was Minidiscus spinulatus [AGSB‐0477; PV640934] (Figure 1). Specifically, AGSB‐0477 clustered closely with MN528624‐Minidiscus spinulatus (100% alignment, 99% bootstrap support) and KY912618‐Minidiscus proschkinae (previously Thalassiosira proschkinae; 100% alignment, 99 bootstraps), AGSB‐0017 clustered with MN528623‐Mediolabrus comicus (99.9% alignment, 100% bootstrap support), and AGSB‐0018 clustered with FJ590768‐Minidiscus trioculatus (100% alignment, 100% bootstrap support) (Li et al. 2020). Minidiscus spinulatus and Minidiscus proschkinae have identical 18S regions, and the AGSB‐0477 isolate was further characterized as M. spinulatus through additional phylogenetic trees, including its cp genome (presented later in Figure 5). Mediolabrus appeared as a sister clade of Skeletonema, and Minidiscus as a sister clade to a group containing Cyclostephanos, Stephanodiscus, Pantocsekiella, Discostella, and Thalassiosira hispida. In general, the Minidiscus isolates fell within a much broader clade containing many Thalassiosira species. The taxa in the Thalassiosira genus appear to form two different clusters in the phylogeny, one of which clusters more closely with the Minidiscus clade and one that forms its own cluster, dissimilar to both the Mediolabrus and Minidiscus clades.

3.2. Distinct Mitochondrial Genome Characteristics Among the Three Isolates

Mitochondrial (mt) genomes of Minidiscus spinulatus, Minidiscus trioculatus, and Mediolabrus comicus exhibited similar GC‐content, at 29.1%, 31.0%, and 30.5% GC, respectively. Their mt genomes, however, varied in size from 49,654 bp, 53,164 bp, to 79,303 bp for M. spinulatus , M. comicus, and M. trioculatus, respectively (Table 2). The regions of sequence contributing to the M. trioculatus' longer 79,303 bp mt genome include a non‐coding region (13% of the mt genome), which is primarily composed of repeats in addition to seven introns in its cox1 gene (Figure 2). The three isolates shared a relatively conserved gene synteny, as is typical for species within the same order (Wang et al. 2022). In all three, the nad11 gene was not split into nad11a and nad11b, which is typical of species in the Order Mediophyceae (Wang et al. 2022). Notably, the tatA gene was present only in M. comicus and absent in both M. trioculatus and M. spinulatus .

3.3. NWA Isolates Have Unique Features in Their Chloroplast Genomic Repertoire Compared to Other Sequenced Thalassiosirales

Chloroplast genome size was 126,734, 135,507, and 138,031 bp for M. comicus, M. trioculatus, and M. spinulatus , respectively (Figure 3, Table 3). Sequenced chloroplast genomes further exhibited similar GC content, with M. spinulatus and M. comicus both at 30.8% GC, while M. trioculatus was slightly higher at 31.1% GC. The large single‐copy (LSC) region varied in size among the species, with M. spinulatus having the largest at 70,509 bp, followed by M. trioculatus at 66,399 bp and M. comicus at 65,377 bp (Table 3). The inverted repeat (IR) regions also differed, with M. spinulatus and M. comicus having similar lengths of 18,185 and 17,315 bp, respectively, whereas M. trioculatus had a substantially smaller IR region at 11,795 bp. The percentage of each genome attributed to coding sequences varied, with M. trioculatus at 68.7%, M. spinulatus at 71.9%, and M. comicus at 76.4%. 3.4. Pan‐Organellar Genome Analyses of Thalassiosirales Diatoms

Further pangenomic analyses of Thalassiosirales mt genomes identified 112 gene clusters, of which 24 (21%) were present in all 16 genomes, forming the core mt genome, while the remaining 88 (79%) belonged to the accessory genome (Figure 4A; Data S3). In the accessory genome, 62 were unique to only one organism (70% of the accessory genome), and of these, 28 (45%) were singleton gene clusters belonging to M. trioculatus. The 79,302 bp genome of M. trioculatus is 1.79 times larger than the average of the 16 currently sequenced Thalassiosirales genomes at 44,285 [+/− 9709]. Minidiscus trioculatus also had the highest number of gene clusters (62, average = 40.6) and the highest GC‐content (31.0% GC, average = 29.7% GC).

In the case of the cp pangenome for the 37 Thalassiosirales genomes, 189 gene clusters were found, of which 112 (59%) were a part of the core genome and 77 (41%) were identified as the accessory genome; that latter includes 47/77 (61%) that were singleton gene clusters (Figure 4B; Data S3). Minidiscus trioculatus had six singleton gene clusters, M. comicus had zero, and the NWA M. spinulatus isolate had one. The OL855995 M. spinulatus had two singleton gene clusters, showing regional‐specific diversity between the two isolates (Wang and Chen 2022). Measuring the cp genomes' average nucleotide identities (ANI), Minidiscus spinulatus has 99.7% ANI to OL855995 M. spinulatus and 91.5% ANI to M. trioculatus. Interestingly, M. trioculatus had a slightly higher ANI when compared to the NWA M. spinulatus (91.6%) than to the OL855995 M. spinulatus (91.2%).

In both organellar pangenomes (Figure 4), as expected, isolates of the same species had highly similar ANIs (Data S4). The ANIs of the cluster including Skeletonema and Mediolabrus had overall higher similarity than the cluster of Minidiscus and Thalassiosira. Overall, the cp genomes had closer similarity than the mt genomes, ranging from 83.1% to ~100% ANI with isolates of the same species in comparison to 80.9% to ~100% with isolates of the same species (Data S4). In both cases, Cluster 1, comprising Skeletonema species and Mediolabrus comicus, exhibited a closer genetic relationship within the cluster compared to Cluster 2, which included the genera Thalassiosira and Minidiscus (Figure 4). This indicates that the genomes in Cluster 1 are more highly conserved than those in Cluster 2 in terms of ANI.

3.5. Phylogenomic Analyses of Thalassiosirales Organellar Genome DNA Re‐Emphasizes Prior Classification

Concatenated alignments and trees for a suite of protein‐coding (PC) genes were generated for currently available data to further explore the phylogenomic relationships of the three new isolates within the Thalassiosirales order (one analysis using mt PC genes, the other cp PC genes). Both trees further emphasized the characterization of these three isolates as Minidiscus and Mediolabrus species, respectively (Figure 5). The Minidiscus species clustered closely together (at 99%–100% bootstrap support), and the previously found clade separation was demonstrated as the M. comicus isolate formed a sister clade with the Skeletonema genus (at 100% bootstrap support), and the Minidiscus species were either within the same clade or a sister clade to members of the Thalassiosira genus. Thus, the concatenated alignments from the mt and cp genomes (i.e., the ‘super‐barcodes’) re‐emphasize the clade separation of Mediolabrus and Minidiscus (Li et al. 2020).

3.6. Thalassiosirales Isolates Exhibit Differing Biogeography and Succession in the NWA

Seagoing expeditions in the NWA (AZMP, AZOMP, NWA‐BCP CE, and GEOTRACES) characterized the distribution of the three isolates using 16S rRNA metabarcoding. The presence and absence of the ASVs pertaining to the isolates in the surface photosynthetic community (1–20 m) were determined by subsetting the dataset to include only samples collected within that depth range (Figure 6). When considering all samples across all depths and sampling methods, the ASVs were detected at more sites (Figure S4). Additionally, the proportions of the surface ASVs (1–20 m) to the rest of the photosynthetic community were determined and plotted (Figure S5). Of all these off‐shore samples, M. spinulatus was only detected at one location off the SS in the Fall of 2017 (Figure 6). In the sample at this location, it accounted for approximately 0.9% of the total photosynthetic community. In contrast, both M. trioculatus and M. comicus were commonly found at various locations and seasons. It is important to note that M. trioculatus was not detected in samples collected at any depth during the BELAS‐1 expedition (LS) and in any surface samples (1–20 m) in the AZOMP22 (LS) mission; hence, in 2022 this species had limited presence in the Central Labrador Basin and Greenland shelf (Figure 6; Figure S4). Mediolabrus comicus was detected at the surface in all expeditions except during AZOMP22 in 2022 (Figure S3; Figure 6). In the 2015 cruise to the CAG, M. trioculatus was also only found at the Southern site in the Central LS, while M. comicus was more frequently detected at northern sites, specifically within Baffin Bay and the Canadian Arctic Archipelago (Figure 6). This demonstrates that M. comicus potentially has a more northern geographic range than M. trioculatus. When comparing the relative proportions of phytoplankton communities on the SS in the fall versus spring, M. trioculatus was generally more abundant in the spring, while M. comicus appeared in greater proportions in the fall (Figure S5).

While the expeditions above provide insight on the geographic range of the taxa in the NWA, in the BB (Halifax, N.S.), weekly 16S rRNA data provide greater resolution of when the taxa were present in‐shore between 2014 and 2022. When observed, these nanoplankton occupied a higher proportion of the photosynthetic community at 1 m than at 60 m (Figure 7A). However, all three isolates were found throughout the water column at the four depths sampled in the BB (Figure S6). For size‐fractionated years (> 3 μM versus 0.2–3 μM), the nanoplankton tended to occupy a larger proportion of the community in the > 3 μM fraction (Figure S7).

Using the BBMP data, we found that the three nanoplanktonic diatoms each had a unique temporal pattern, with individual species tending to dominate different times of the year (Figure 7A). Although M. spinulatus was only detected in one off‐shore sample (Figure 6), it was observed consistently in the BB, occupying a large relative proportion of the phytoplankton community in the second half of the year (generally Jun–Dec). In comparison, M. trioculatus was consistently present mainly in the first half of the year (Jan–Jun) and was then followed by M. comicus and M. spinulatus , with the former of the two taxa appearing slightly earlier than the other (Figure 7D). Minidiscus spinulatus and M. trioculatus rarely dominated the photosynthetic community simultaneously, indicating that the two species may be adapted to conditions and/or dynamics of opposite seasons at the in‐shore BBMP site. Mediolabrus comicus appears more frequently in the second half of the year but also coexists with M. trioculatus in winter and spring, preceding the dominance of M. spinulatus (Figure 7D). A dot plot of the relative abundance of each isolate by month is visualized in Figure S9 to demonstrate sample‐level variability. The seasonal patterns observed in the BBMP environmental data suggest that temperature (via seasonal change) plays a key role in driving succession across the three studied taxa; M. trioculatus dominates at lower temperatures (between −1°C and 5°C), and M. spinulatus dominates at higher temperatures, overlapping with M. comicus (at > 8°C; Figure 7E). To further investigate this, the temperature ranges were plotted for each isolate. The individual species are shown with distinct distributions, and the pairwise Wilcoxon rank‐sum tests (with Benjamini–Hochberg correction) indicate that each species' temperature range is significantly different, as evidenced by the low p‐values (p < 8.1e‐09) (Figure S10).

To further explore the relationship between environmental variables and the nanoplanktonic diatoms, we performed an RDA using metadata from the BBMP (Figure 7F). The RDA model was statistically significant (ANOVA, 1000 permutations, p < 0.001), with all selected environmental variables also showing significance (p < 0.01). The first axis (RDA1) explained 21.0% of the total variance and captured 90.2% of the constrained variance within the model, while RDA2 explained 1.6% of the total variance and 6.8% of the constrained variance. Temperature (9.365 variance, F = 312.50, p < 0.001) was the most influential variable, explaining the largest proportion of variance in the dataset, followed by ammonia (1.565 variance, F = 52.23, p < 0.001) and nitrate (1.076 variance, F = 35.92, p < 0.001). The impact of temperature on the RDA model re‐emphasizes our succession and temperature results (Figure 7A–E). Minidiscus spinulatus and M. comicus had higher RDA1 values; separation on this axis also correlates with warmer temperatures toward positive RDA1, whereas M. trioculatus, with its negative RDA1 value, exhibited the opposite trend, suggesting a preference for cooler conditions. In contrast, the RDA2 axis was most influenced by stronger correlations with chlorophyll a, followed by nitrate, oxygen, and Synechococcus, suggesting nutrient availability and bloom conditions as potential controls for RDA2. Mediolabrus comicus was more positively associated with RDA2 (1.94) in contrast to M. spinulatus (0.06), suggesting a potential preference for M. comicus for higher bioavailable nitrogen and less biomass as indicated by chlorophyll a (Figure 7F).

4. Discussion

4.1. Contribution of New Organellar Genomes for Three Nanoplanktonic Diatoms

Our work contributes new genomic information to the current repertoire of diatom organellar genomes. There are currently 266 species in the Thalassiosiraceae family, and only 19 cp genomes (16 species) and 8 mt genomes are available for this diverse diatom family (Guiry and Guiry 2015). Here we present the complete cp and mt genomes for M. spinulatus , M. trioculatus, and M. comicus isolates from the NWA. Currently, a complete cp genome (OL855995) is available for an M. spinulatus strain from Jiaozhou Bay (China), while only a partial cp genome is available for M. trioculatus (strain CCMP495) (Nordberg et al. 2014; Wang and Chen 2022). Despite being from two distinct oceanographic regions, the Minidiscus spinulatus strains from the NWA and Jiaozhou Bay have highly similar cp genomes (ANI = 99.7%) (Figure 4, Figure S12). Recently established based on phylogenetic and morphological data (Li et al. 2020), the genus Mediolabrus remains under investigation, and our work advances understanding of its genetic and ecological distinctions from Minidiscus.

4.2. Phylogenomic Classification of Three Isolates

Recent studies have emphasized the power of organelle genomes in resolving fine‐scale phylogenetic relationships and uncovering unexpected evolutionary patterns within diatoms. For example, Jeong and Lee (2024) demonstrated that cp and mt phylogenies often conflict with traditional morphological classifications. In our case, using cp and mt genomes as super‐barcodes reaffirmed the two distinct clades of Minidiscus and Mediolabrus previously identified by Li et al. (2020) with more robust support. Multiple methods, including the partial 18S rRNA phylogeny (1051 bp), separate concatenated gene trees of protein‐coding regions from both the mt genomes and cp genomes, and pangenome analyses, consistently showed that Mediolabrus comicus clustered with Skeletonema species. In contrast, in the 18S rRNA tree, Minidiscus clustered closer to a group containing Cyclostephanos, Stephanodiscus, Pantocsekiella, Discostella, and Thalassiosira species (Li et al. 2020). The same species were not available for the concatenated tree of the Thalassiosirales organellar genomes; however, Minidiscus still clustered closer to related Thalassiosira than Skeletonema. With additional sequencing of Thalassiosirales species' organellar genomes, the phylogenetic relationship of Minidiscus to other genera will be further revealed.

Cell size is a crucial factor that shapes various aspects of phytoplankton physiology, ecology, and evolution, influencing traits such as nutrient acquisition, photophysiology, and grazing pressures (Finkel et al. 2010). For further context, we compiled a supplemental dataset of cell sizes for each species included in the three phylogenetic trees from various sources (Tables S1–S3). Within the 18S rRNA tree, cell sizes varied across different taxonomic groupings, highlighting that smaller cell size is presumably not a monophyletic trait within the Thalassiosirales. For example, the sister clade to Minidiscus exhibited a range of 2.5–54 μm, with an average of 15.7 μm, while the sister clade to Mediolabrus, Skeletonema menzelli, showed a range of 2–10 μm, with the closely related Skeletonema species averaging 7.6 μm (range 2–21 μm). Additionally, more distantly related clades outside the nanoplanktonic diatoms generally had larger cell sizes, with average cell size (within the clades) ranging from 13.1 to 55.8 μm. However, there were also several species with small cell sizes that did not cluster closely with the nanoplankton (e.g., Thalassiosira profunda with a size range of 2–5 μm).

4.3. Larger Mitogenome in M. trioculatus is due to Nucleotide Repeats and cox1 Introns

Organellar genome comparisons for members of the Order Thalassiosirales revealed that M. trioculatus has a much larger mt genome than its close relatives. Within Thalassiosiraceae, the currently observed mt genome lengths range from 39,089 bp in Thalassiosira pseudonana to 51,728 bp in Thalassiosira rotula . Interestingly, M. trioculatus has the largest currently reported mt genome in the Thalassiosiraceae family and is more similar to two pennate diatoms, Phaeodactylum tricornutum and Halamphora calidilacuna, which are 77,356 and 103,605 bp, respectively (Oudot‐Le Secq and Green 2011; Pogoda et al. 2019). The extended length of the M. trioculatus mt genome is due to two main features: the long non‐coding region and seven introns located within the cox1 gene.

Both M. trioculatus and M. spinulatus possess non‐coding regions, measuring 10,301 and 7490 bp, respectively. While non‐coding regions are uncommon in diatom mt genomes, researchers have previously reported them. For example, P. tricornutum has a non‐coding region of 35,454 bp that is composed of directed repeats, and T. pseudonana has a 4970 bp repeat region (Oudot‐Le Secq and Green 2011). In P. tricornutum , repeats account for 46% of the mt genome, whereas in M. trioculatus and M. spinulatus , repeats make up 13%–15% of the mt genome. In contrast to P. tricornutum , M. trioculatus also has seven introns in the cox1 gene, which account for 24.5% (19,435 bp) of the genome. Mediolabrus comicus similarly has two introns in the cox1 gene, while M. spinulatus has none. The presence of group II introns within the cox1 gene has been observed in other diatoms, such as H. calidilacuna and H. coffeaeformis (Pogoda et al. 2019). The cox1 gene is the most commonly used marker for animals and is often referred to as the ‘barcode of life’ (Nakazato and Jinbo 2022). Our data supports Pogoda et al. (2019), who argue that the introns found in the cox1 gene may partially explain the varying success in amplifying the cox1 gene in diatoms. The lack of non‐coding regions in M. comicus mirrors the compact mitochondrial genomes of Skeletonema species, but unlike Skeletonema, M. comicus possesses four introns. Differences in non‐coding region lengths and intron numbers among closely related species' mt genomes highlight the need for broader organellar genome sequencing in diatoms to better understand the distribution of mobile genetic elements and assess the reliability of potential biomarkers (i.e., cox1) across taxonomic groups.

4.4. Patterns in Gene Retention and Loss Across the Three Isolates

Results from this study contribute to our collective knowledge on gene retention and loss in diatoms. In the mt genomes, the tatA gene was present in M. comicus but absent in both of the Minidiscus isolates. The tatA gene is involved in the twin‐arginine translocation pathway that translocates proteins across membranes (Cline and Theg 2007; Bennewitz et al. 2020). Loss of tatA has previously been found in two Coscinodiscophyceae species, Guinardia striata and Actinocyclus sp., making the two Minidiscus species the first diatoms in the Mediophyceae class to lack the tatA gene (Wang et al. 2022). Moreover, in all three mt genomes, the nad11 gene was present as a single, uninterrupted sequence, consistent with most other mt genomes within the class Mediophyceae. The only currently known exception in Mediophyceae is Odontella aurita var. minima, in which nad11 is split into two segments (Wang et al. 2022).

The cp genomes contained a total of 31 tRNAs, comprising 28 distinct tRNAs when accounting for duplicates in the inverted repeat (IR) regions. All three species shared tRNA‐Ala and tRNA‐Ile in their IR regions. However, while M. spinulatus and M. comicus also contained tRNA‐Leu, Minidiscus trioculatus differed by featuring a duplicated tRNA‐Phe. The total tRNAs match what has been previously found in the Skeletonema genus and are close to the 30 total tRNAs in most currently known Thalassiosira cp genomes (Liu, Chen, et al. 2021; Liu, Xu, et al. 2021). Regarding overlapping gene pairs, the three isolates exhibited the same four gene overlaps that have been consistently observed across all Thalassiosira species (Table 3) (Liu, Chen, et al. 2021). The petF (ferredoxin) gene plays a role in electron transport during photosynthesis and has been transferred from the cp to the nuclear genome in select species, including some Thalassiosira species, likely enabling them to better tolerate iron‐limited environments (Lommer et al. 2010; Liu, Chen, et al. 2021). However, this endosymbiotic gene transfer has not occurred in any of the three isolates herein.

4.5. Organellar Genome Sequencing Refines Distributions of Isolated Thalassiosirales Nanoplanktonic Diatoms in the NWA That Are Derived by eDNA Metabarcoding

A major benefit of sequencing chloroplast genomes from isolated cultures is the detection of full‐length 16S rRNA gene sequences, which enables the strain‐specific identification of eDNA metabarcoding ASVs. Previously, Robicheau et al. (2022) described the succession of two Minidiscus ASVs (differing by 1 bp) throughout the year, which were classified as M. trioculatus based on available reference databases. When reinvestigated, the 16S rRNA sequences from the cp genomes revealed that one of those ASVs is M. spinulatus , highlighting the fundamental principle that microbial community datasets can only be correctly interpreted to the degree that reference databases can be relied on. Ongoing dedication to sequencing uncharacterized nuclear and plastid genomes of important phytoplankton species is crucial for enhancing the accuracy of microbial community analyses.

4.6. Situating the NWA Distribution of Isolates Within Global Biogeographic Patterns

Global monitoring programs using metabarcoding (primarily 18S rRNA), microscopy, and flow cytometry have identified Minidiscus and Mediolabrus as key players across a range of ecosystems. The Tara Oceans expedition (2009–2013) found that Minidiscus (encompassing Mediolabrus) was one of the most commonly found diatoms with a high level of occupancy determined by both the V4 and V9 18S rRNA markers (Pierella Karlusich et al. 2025; Malviya et al. 2016). In Tara Oceans, Minidiscus were detected primarily in the surface ocean, with some detection at the deep chlorophyll maximum (DCM) (Pierella Karlusich et al. 2025; Malviya et al. 2016). These genera have also been detected in various localized monitoring programs; these include the NBay Plankton Time Series in Narragansett Bay, Rhode Island (2008–2014); the Elbe Estuary in Northern Germany (2021–2022); the LTER‐MC program in the Gulf of Naples (1984–2010, 2011–2013); the SOMLIT‐Astan monitoring program in the Western English Channel (2009–2016); the DeWeX program in the Gulf of Lions, Mediterranean Sea (2013); a survey in Marian Cove, Antarctica (January–February 2019); and natural salt marsh pools in California, USA (2018) (Fontaine and Rynearson 2023; Martens, Biederbick, et al. 2024; Martens, Russnak, et al. 2024; Zingone et al. 2023; Caracciolo, Berney, et al. 2022; Caracciolo, Rigaut‐Jalabert, et al. 2022; Jeon et al. 2023; Arsenieff et al. 2020; Vineis et al. 2025; Leblanc et al. 2018).

Our work highlights the presence of these nanoplanktonic diatoms on the SS and in the LS—areas that have not been sampled by the global Tara Oceans project. Moreover, the detection of M. comicus in the CAG in 2015 reflects earlier records of Minidiscus species in the region (Pierella Karlusich et al. 2025; Malviya et al. 2016). The distribution and seasonal patterns of these nanoplanktonic diatoms suggest that the M. spinulatus isolate from the Northwest Atlantic (NWA) is primarily restricted to the in‐shore BB and is not commonly found in open ocean areas. The other known circularized cp genome for this species comes from Jiaozhou Bay (China), which is also close to the coastline (Wang and Chen 2022). In contrast, M. trioculatus and M. comicus appear to be more broadly distributed across the NWA. The seasonal trends observed in the BB (Figure 7) are reflected in all years of sampling the SS region: M. trioculatus tends to be more abundant in the spring, whereas M. comicus is more prevalent in the fall (Figure S5).

4.7. Comparative Seasonal Dynamics of Nanoplanktonic Diatom Genotypes Across Diverse Coastal Systems

The succession of different Minidiscus genotypes has been observed across multiple monitoring programs, reflecting strong seasonal and regional patterns. In Narragansett Bay (2008–2014), Minidiscus and Mediolabrus species exhibited distinct seasonal trends: M. spinulatus was more abundant from January to June, M. trioculatus appeared mainly between March and June, and two Mediolabrus comicus ASVs displayed distinct seasonal peaks—one in late fall and another from May to November (Fontaine and Rynearson 2023). In the Gulf of Naples (LTER‐MC station, 1984–2010), M. comicus showed a bimodal pattern, appearing twice annually (Zingone et al. 2023); this pattern was also confirmed with 18S rRNA data (2011–2013) at both LTER‐MC and the SOMLIT‐Astan station in the Western English Channel, despite differing environmental conditions (Caracciolo, Berney, et al. 2022). Extended monitoring at SOMLIT‐Astan (2009–2016) further revealed species‐specific seasonality: Minidiscus variabilis occurred year‐round, M. spinulatus peaked in the second half of the year, and M. comicus dominated from November to March (Caracciolo, Rigaut‐Jalabert, et al. 2022). In the Elbe Estuary (2021–2022), eight Minidiscus genotypes were identified, with three dominating ( M. spinulatus genotypes 1 and 2, and M. comicus), showing salinity‐dependent distributions (Martens, Biederbick, et al. 2024; Martens, Russnak, et al. 2024). These results demonstrate that not all nanoplanktonic diatoms in the Minidiscus and Mediolabrus genera display the same seasonal patterns and are likely controlled by environmental factors and/or seasonal shifts specific to each study area. Moreover, these studies provide evidence that different genotypes of the same species have been shown to have different seasonal patterns (Fontaine and Rynearson 2023; Martens, Russnak, et al. 2024; Martens, Biederbick, et al. 2024).

The BBMP's weekly resolution provides a clearer view of the succession of the three isolates with nine years of metabarcoding data (Figure 7). The bimodal seasonal pattern similar to that observed in the Gulf of Naples and the Western English Channel was observed in the BB in 2014, 2016, 2017, 2018, and 2022 (Figure S6) (Caracciolo, Berney, et al. 2022). In the BBMP, Minidiscus spinulatus peaked in the second half of the year, similar to the M. spinulatus identified in the SOMLIT‐Astan time series and dissimilar to the trend in Narragansett Bay (Caracciolo, Berney, et al. 2022; Fontaine and Rynearson 2023). We observed M. trioculatus almost exclusively in the first half of the year, from January to June, which is a larger but similar window to that observed in Narragansett Bay (March–June) (Fontaine and Rynearson 2023).

4.8. Evidence for Biological Carbon Export by Nanoplanktonic Diatoms in the Northwest Atlantic

It was previously assumed that nanoplanktonic diatoms, due to their small size, were predominantly remineralized within the microbial loop; however, our findings align with more recent studies demonstrating that these diatoms are capable of contributing significantly to carbon export (Leblanc et al. 2018; Malviya et al. 2016; Bolaños et al. 2020). Notably, earlier work has identified species M. spinulatus , M. trioculatus, M. variabilis, and M. comicus within benthic communities along Nova Scotia's coast, suggesting some potential for sedimentary deposition (Krumhansl et al. 2025). Our study extends this knowledge by detecting these species in deeper off‐shore waters, indicating an increased potential for long‐term carbon storage. Although there were no sediment samples taken for any of the off‐shore expeditions, the ASVs for our isolates were often detected below the euphotic layer (> 150 m) (Guidi et al. 2016). On the SS, both M. trioculatus and M. comicus were found throughout the sampled water column from surface to 300 m, and in the LS, Mediolabrus was found in various water samples from the surface to 1000 m. In the Arctic sampling of 2015, both M. comicus and M. trioculatus were found as deep as 1400 m in the LS, and M. comicus was notably detected as deep as 2300 m in the CAG. These observations suggest that nanoplanktonic diatoms may not only escape remineralization in the upper water column but also reach deep ocean layers, enhancing their role in vertical carbon flux.

4.9. Environmental Drivers of Nanoplanktonic Diatom Abundance in the NWA and Implications for Future Bloom Dynamics

Temperature was the key factor shaping the relative abundance of the three nanoplankton ASVs in the BB, with M. trioculatus favoring colder waters and M. spinulatus and M. comicus more abundant in warmer conditions (Figure 7). A comparable seasonal pattern was observed on the SS, where M. trioculatus was more dominant in the colder spring months, while M. comicus became more prevalent in the warmer fall. Our findings from both the BB and SS suggest that M. spinulatus and M. comicus thrive under warm, high‐biomass conditions, indicating greater resilience to future ocean warming compared to M. trioculatus. A notable contradictory finding is the broader distribution of M. comicus into the LS and CAG. If temperature were the primary driver, the cold‐adapted M. trioculatus would be expected to dominate these regions. This discrepancy highlights the complexity of environmental influences, as temperature is not only a direct factor but is also correlated with other oceanographic processes such as stratification, nutrient availability, light availability, and the concentration of dissolved gases. It is therefore possible that other environmental factors may play a more important role in shaping the species distributions in the LS and CAG. Alternatively, cold‐adapted M. comicus strains may exist, with genomic differences undetectable by the 16S region alone. Similar strain‐level variation has been observed using the 18S rRNA gene, where M. comicus ASVs exhibit distinct seasonal patterns in Narragansett Bay (Fontaine and Rynearson 2023).

Previous surveys have identified intense blooms and blooming capabilities of Minidiscus. During a study in Marian Cove, Antarctica, nano‐diatoms (including Minidiscus) made up approximately 20% of the total phytoplankton abundance, with Minidiscus itself reaching high concentrations ranging from 2.7 × 105 to 5.6 × 106 cells L−1 (Jeon et al. 2023). The highest Minidiscus abundances were generally observed near the glacier, associated with surface haline stratification and low‐light conditions. Another bloom was observed in the Mediterranean Sea; these species also reached very high abundances during an April expedition, with cell concentrations of 4–6 × 106 cells L−1 at several stations (Leblanc et al. 2018). Moreover, in a coastal upwelling experiment, two Minidiscus ASVs were identified as stronger blooming species in the mesocosm (Vineis et al. 2025). Although our work does not show direct evidence of large nanoplanktonic blooms in the NWA, the detection of these diatoms across depths, seasons, and years demonstrates the persistent presence of these species in the NWA. Research in the BB has demonstrated that smaller cells tend to dominate under conditions that depart from the typical seasonal cycles (i.e., increased temperature) (Robicheau et al. 2022). We can therefore hypothesize that as climate change continues to cause changes to the ocean, such as rising temperatures, intensified stratification, and altered nutrient availability, small‐sized phytoplankton may become more dominant and potentially contribute to intense blooms as observed previously (Leblanc et al. 2018; Jeon et al. 2023; Bolaños et al. 2020).

5. Conclusions

Here we presented the organellar genomes for three nanoplanktonic diatoms isolated in the NWA. The pangenomic analyses of the organellar genomes reaffirmed the distinct Minidiscus and Mediolabrus clades proposed by Li et al. (2020). Additionally, we identified unique features that contributed to the longer length of the M. trioculatus mt genome, including a non‐coding region and seven introns within the cox1 gene. Patterns of gene retention and loss were observed in the organellar genomes, such as tatA loss in the Minidiscus isolates' mt genomes and petF retention in all three isolates' cp genomes. The cp genomes from the isolated cultures enabled precise identification of diatom species through the re‐investigation of previous 16S rRNA metabarcoding from the NWA. In the BB, we identified seasonal preferences, primarily driven by temperature, that influenced the abundance of the isolates throughout the year. On the SS and in the open NWA, we found M. comicus and M. trioculatus below the euphotic zone and as deep as 1400 m, which indicates their potential contribution to the biological carbon pump. The consistent detection of the isolates across depths, seasons, and regions suggests persistent presence in the NWA; however, no indication of intense Minidiscus or Mediolabrus‐dominated blooms was found. Projected shifts in ocean conditions due to climate change will likely favor smaller phytoplankton, and these isolates are therefore candidates for nanoplanktonic diatom‐dominated blooms in the future.
