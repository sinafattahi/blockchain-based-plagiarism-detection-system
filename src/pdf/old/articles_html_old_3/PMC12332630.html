Abstract

Nuclear receptors (NRs) play pivotal roles in functionally diverse cell signaling cascades, regulating metabolism and homeostasis. This study introduces a broadly applicable molecular imaging platform for NR activities based on four rationally designed single-chain bioluminescent probes named P1–P4. As all the ligand binding domains (LBDs) of NRs are highly conserved, the probe portfolio was exemplified using the LBD of peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor γ (PPARγ-LBD), i.e., the LBD was centrally positioned and fused with luciferases and/or fluorescent proteins based on the schemes of the protein-fragment complementation assay (PCA), molecular strain (MS) probe, circular permutation (CP) probe, and bioluminescence resonance energy transfer (BRET) system. In the developing process, we identified the optimal dissection sites for the marine luciferase RLuc for PCA systems and validated them in in vitro and in vivo studies in response to various ligands. Among the probes, P3 and P4 series probes exhibited strong BL intensities in response to a PPARγ agonist with signal-to-background ratios of maximally 14-fold. The animal study using furimazine (FMZ) substrate analogs such as Ad-FMZ showed that the probes can sustain agonist-dependent BL signals for up to 24 h in animal models. Considering the biological importance of NRs, the molecular imaging platform with the portfolio of probes developed in this study can contribute to interrogate many NR-related cell signaling pathways by replacing the PPARγ-LBD with LBDs from other NRs in the probes.

1. Introduction

Nuclear receptors (NRs) are one of the most abundant classes of transcriptional factors acting as molecular switches in regulating the cellular signaling pathway in normal and pathological conditions. NRs provide a direct link between signaling molecules that regulate homeostasis, reproduction, development, and metabolism. ,

NRs consist of three functionally distinct domains called the N-terminal domain (NTD), DNA-binding domain (DBD), and ligand-binding domain (LBD). The NTD is variable among NRs, whereas the LBD is highly conserved. In addition, the LBD is mainly responsible for the key roles in recognizing the endogenous ligand as a first responder of the signal transduction pathways. This unique feature of the LBD inspired us to create a portfolio of four rationally designed single-chain bioluminescent probes based on the LBD of NRs. Considering that the size and functions of the LBD of NRs are similar to each other, we speculated that a portfolio of probes based on a particular NR could generalize the applicability of the probes for other NR-LBD-based molecular imaging probes.

As a representative of NRs, we paid attention to peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor (PPAR), which controls a great number of gene networks involved in adipogenesis, lipid metabolism, inflammation, and maintenance of metabolic homeostasis. PPAR has three isoforms, PPARα, PPARβ, and PPARγ, each containing several domains including the LBD, i.e., PPAR-LBD. The ligand-induced conformational changes in the PPAR-LBD control the downstream regulatory functions via its homo- and hetero dimerizations with retinoid X receptor (RXR) and by recruiting coactivators. , This PPAR–RXR dimerization was previously investigated using a bioluminescence resonance energy transfer (BRET) assay. Because of diverse functions in the biological activities, PPAR has been persistently investigated as a drug target in various diseases. Some PPAR agonists have been approved as clinical agents for the treatment of diabetes and dyslipidemia. , Thus, it is important to have a proper evaluation of PPAR activities in vivo and in vitro for identifying potential ligands with no or minimal adverse activities using high-throughput assays before they can be used for clinical applications.

Conventionally, NR activities have been determined preclinically using mammalian two-hybrid assays, where the NR-LBD is fused to the GAL4-DNA binding domain of yeast and VP16 transactivator domain of HSV1 (GAL4-DBD-LBD-VP16). This fusion protein is designed to convert ligand activities of NRs eventually to the expression of a reporter protein (luciferase) codelivered with the system. Thus, the ligand activity is converted to the expression levels of a reporter protein, such as firefly luciferase. The expression levels are finally determined using luciferin, a specific substrate for luciferase. NR activities have also been investigated using time-resolved fluorescence resonance energy transfer (TR-FRET) system for screening potential NR ligands, where the NR-LBD is fused to GAL4-DBD. − All the methods generally require a long stimulation time, typically 12–24 h, to accumulate sufficient amount of reporter proteins to quantify and decode NR activity. This feature limits the determination of the temporal dynamics of molecular events of interest by the receptor and its respective ligand. Moreover, the detectable molecular events are confined only to nuclear proteins by using this approach.

Alternatively, ligand-activated NR–coactivator interaction has been indexed with various cell-free assay systems such as immunohistochemistry, surface plasmon resonance (SPR), and real-time quantitative reverse transcriptase polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR). , However, these assays are simply designed to represent a snapshot of a complex biological process under artificially designed experimental conditions. Further, the results do not reflect the complexity of the contexts in cells within living subjects.

We previously demonstrated the fundamental strategies for imaging molecular events in live mammalian cells using “single-chain” bioluminescence (BL) probes. − These single-chain probes are uniquely designed by integrating both functions of ligand recognition and optical reporter within a single fusion protein. We have applied for determining biological activities of steroid hormones such as androgen, estrogen, and cortisol upon their interactions with respective receptors. − We also invented a concept of “molecular strain probes” for indexing molecular strains appended on marine luciferases by ligand-activated protein–protein interactions (PPIs), where a full-length luciferase is sandwiched between two proteins of interest with the use of small flexible linkers. , However, these probes can conceptually determine only the antiparallel PPIs, since the protein partners assembled within the single-chain probe can only interact antiparallelly. Another problem with the single-chain strain probe is the background BL intensities, which are relatively high because full-length luciferases are embedded in the probe backbone, where high backgound signals are developed by the reporter under normal physiological conditions.

In the present study, we leveraged the great physiological impact of NRs and developed a portfolio of four representative molecular imaging probes based on the ligand-induced conformational changes, coactivator recruitment, and receptor homo- and heterodimerizations. The probes were contructed by centering NR-LBD on tandemly modified luciferase and/or a fluorescent protein. We designed the probes based on the schemes of protein-fragment complementation assay (PCA), molecular strain (MS) probe, circular permutation (CP) probe, and BRET system, as illustrated in Figure A. We exemplified the utility of the portfolio with the PPARγ-LBD, which is useful for imaging the intracellular molecular activities of NRs in vitro and in vivo. In the probe developing process, we have specified the insights used to develop “single-chain” probes through optimizing (i) the dissection sites of Renilla luciferase (RLuc) for the protein-fragment complementation-based probes and (ii) the interface regions between the domains of the resonance energy transfer-based probes for improving the signal-to-background (S/B) ratios. The ligand-activated conformation changes of PPARγ-LBD and the corresponding working mechanism of the probes are computationally modeled to address the rational design. We finally elucidated in vivo imaging of the probe in animals using xenografts expressing the optical probes. Considering the impact of NRs as a triggering mechanism in many important molecular events in cells, the present study guides on how to image the ligand-activated cell signaling pathways of NRs in cells and in animal models.

2. Materials and Methods

2.1. Materials

Native coelenterazine (nCTZ) was purchased from Nanolight (Pinetop, AZ, USA). The CTZ analogs, T1, T2, TS1, and TS2, were obtained from our previous study, while coelenterazine-h (CTZh) was purchased from FujiFilm Wako Pure Chemical Co. (Osaka, Japan). The furimazine (FMZ) analogs, pivaloyl-FMZ (Piv-FMZ) and 1-adamantanecarbonyl-FMZ (Ad-FMZ), were from our previous studies. , The ligands of PPARγ such as GW1929, rosiglitazone, pioglitazone hydrochloride, and rosiglitazone maleate were obtained from FujiFilm Wako Pure Chemical Co. The cDNA constructs of various marine luciferases cloned in pcDNA3.1­(+) vector backbone were obtained from our previous studies, i.e., Renilla luciferase 8.6-535 (RLuc8.6-535), Renilla luciferase 8.6-535SG (RLuc86SG), Artificial luciferase 16 (ALuc16, GenBank MF817967), and Artificial luciferase 23 (ALuc23; GenBank MF817968). ,

The cDNAs encoding human PPARγ-LBD (193–475 AA or 204–475 AA; Genbank X90563), the Src homology-2 (SH2) domain derived from v-Src (150–248 AA), mNeptune C158D, near-infrared fluorescent protein (iRFP), and the Ornithine decarboxylase-PEST sequence were custom-synthesized by Eurofins Genomics (Tokyo, Japan) based on the sequence information available on the public database, National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI).

BALB/c nude mice (female, 6 weeks old, ca. 16 g) were obtained from Charles River (Wilmington, MA). The Administrative Panel on Laboratory Animal Care of Stanford University approved all procedures using laboratory animals used in this study, and all experiments were conducted in accordance with the Guidelines for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals (APLAC-26748, Approval Date: September 11, 2023).

2.2. Modeling of the Molecular Structure of PPARγ-LBD

We obtained PPARγ-LBD structure information from the AlphaFold database (https://alphafold.ebi.ac.uk, AF-P37231). The N-terminal region was rotated to make contacts with the helix 9 (H9) as it is not bound to DNA. The agonist was placed based on the crystal structure (PDB: 2PRG). The unbound structure used in this study was based on the antagonist-bound structure (PDB: 6C5T) with the above N-terminal region that was manually placed.

2.3. Construction of a Series of Plasmids Encoding Single-Chain Probes to Image PPARγ Activities

The single-chain probes categorized into Groups 1–4 in Figure A were generated by a tandem linkage of cDNA fragments, as shown in Figure B,C: in Group 1 (P1), the cDNA fragments encoding the N- and C-terminal domains of ALuc16 (19–129 AA for N-ALuc16 and 130–212 AA for C-ALuc16) were generated by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) to introduce the specific dissection sites HindIII/KpnI and BamHI/XhoI at the 5′ and 3′ ends, respectively. Separately, the cDNA fragment encoding PPARγ-LBD (193–475 AA) comprising a glycine-serine (GS) linker sequence was synthesized by PCR to introduce the specific dissection sites KpnI/BamHI at the 5′ and 3′ ends, respectively. After PCR, the cDNA blocks were sequentially digested with the respective sets of restriction enzymes, purified, ligated, and subcloned into the appropriately digested pcDNA3.1­(+) vector backbone (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA).

The design of the P1 probe was inspired by an original single-chain probe concept based on the ″protein-fragment complementation assay (PCA)″. In this design, a full-length luciferase was dissected into N- and C-terminal fragments, between of which PPARγ-LBD was sandwiched. In the presence of an agonist, the PPARγ-LBD undergoes an internal conformation change and results in the reconstitution of the adjacent N- and C-terminal fragments. Consequently, the BL activities are ligand-dependently resurrected. In the P1 design, the N- and C-terminal fragments of ALuc16 (19–129 and 130–212 AA) were used because these fragments were one of the most successful fragments for PCA based on our previous study.

The Group 2 (P2) version of probe was created from the idea of nongenomic activities of steroid hormones using “molecular strain (MS)” probes. , For the construction of plasmid vector, cDNA fragment encoding PPARγ-LBD (193–475 AA) comprising a GS linker sequence at the C-terminus with the unique restriction enzyme sites such as HindIII and BamHI at the 5′ and 3′ ends, respectively, was generated by PCR. In parallel, cDNA fragments encoding full-length ALuc23 and the SH2 domain of v-Src (150–248 AA) with unique restriction enzyme sites, BamHI/KpnI and KpnI/XhoI, at the 5′ and 3′ ends, respectively, were synthesized by PCR. The PCR-amplified cDNA blocks were stepwise digested with the respective sets of restriction enzymes, purified, ligated, and subcloned into the corresponding restriction enzyme-digested pcDNA3.1­(+) vector backbone (Invitrogen) to construct a P2 probe (Figure C).

It was previously reported that ligand-activated PPARγ-LBD is phosphorylated and binds the SH2 domain of coactivators including v-Src. Hence, we designed the P2 version probe to exploit this PPI mechanism and constructed the single-chain probe carrying PPARγ-LBD and SH2 domain flanking the full-length ALuc23 on either side. The hinge region tyrosine of α-Helix 12 of PPARγ-LBD is phosphorylated and recognized by the adjacent SH2 domain. This intramolecular PPI elevates the BL intensity by appending strain to ALuc23 (Figure A).

In the Group 3 version of probes (P3), the cDNA fragments encoding the dissection pairs of RLuc8.6-535 were generated by PCR as stated previously to introduce the unique restriction enzyme sites BamHI/AgeI and AgeI/KpnI at the 5′ and 3′ ends, respectively, as shown in Figure S1. In the fabrication of the cDNA construct for P3.2, the cDNA templates encoding the α-helical LXXLL and LXXII motifs were obtained from our precedent study, where L, I, and X mean leucine, isoleucine, and any amino acid, respectively. After PCR amplification, the cDNA fragments were digested stepwise with the respective sets of restriction enzymes, purified, ligated, and subcloned into the corresponding restriction enzyme-digested pcDNA3.1­(+) vector backbone (Invitrogen) to construct a set of P3 probes.

The P3 probes were designed based on the “circular permutation (CP)” method, which essentially combined ideas from the P1 probe and our previous glucocorticoid receptor (GR) probe. In this strategy, RLuc8.6-535 was dissected into N- and C-terminal fragments, which were deployed in the C- and N-terminal order and fused via a GS linker to create circular permutated RLuc8.6-535. These luciferase fragments were then sandwiched between the LXXLL motif and PPARγ-LBD. In the presence of the ligand agonist, PPARγ-LBD binds the LXXLL motif. This intramolecular folding enhances the BL intensities of reconstituted RLuc8.6-535. We prepared two different versions of P3 probes: one named P3.1 was constructed without the LXXII consensus motif of corepressors of NRs, while the other one named P3.2 was with the LXXII motif. We attempted the consensus motif “LXXII” of corepressors of NRs with the hope of improving the signal-to-background (S/B) ratios through reducing the background intensities derived from nonspecific binding between the probe components as speculated in our previous study.

For the construction of Group 4 (P4) probes, cDNA fragments encoding a fluorescent protein (mNeptune (C158D) or iRFP) and a marine luciferase (NanoLuc or RLuc86SG) were created by PCR to introduce the specific dissection sites, HindIII/BamHI and KpnI/XhoI, at the 5′ and 3′ ends, respectively. In parallel, a cDNA fragment encoding PPARγ-LBD or GS-linker-fused PPARγ-LBD was generated by PCR to make the unique dissection sites, BamHI/KpnI, at the 5′ and 3′ ends, respectively. After PCR amplification, the cDNA fragments were stepwise digested with the respective sets of restriction enzymes, purified, ligated, and subcloned into the corresponding restriction enzyme-digested pcDNA3.1­(+) vector backbone (Invitrogen) to construct the P4 version of probes.

The P4 probes were inspired by the basic design of our previous reported concept of BRET probes. , The key components of P4 probes are luciferase and fluorescent protein as the resonance energy donor and acceptor, respectively, between which PPARγ-LBD was inserted. In the P4-series probes upon agonistic ligand-mediated activation, PPARγ-LBD transforms through conformational changes of H12 and enhances the BRET efficiency by proximally positioning the fluorescent and luciferase proteins.

The primers used for the PCR reactions were listed in Table S1. We tested the fidelity of the cDNA constructs by a genetic sequence analyzer (Eurofin Genomics) before using them for further experiments.

2.4. Characterization of the Imaging Probes (P1–P4) for Studying PPARγ Activities

All versions of the probes were characterized in mammalian cells according to the following method. We used COS-7 cells grown in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM) supplemented with 10% (v/v) FBS and 1% (v/v) P/S in 6-well microplates. We transiently transfected the cells with pcDNA3.1­(+) vector encoding P1, P2, P3.1, or P3.2 probes. Twenty-four h post transfection, we subcultured the cells to a 96-well black-wall clear bottom microplate to study the ligand-induced activation of the probes. The cells in the wells were stimulated overnight using 10–6 M GW1929 or this vehicle (0.1% (v/v) DMSO dissolved in PBS). The culture media in the wells were carefully eliminated, and the remaining cells in the wells were simultaneously immersed with nCTZ dissolved in PBS. The corresponding BL intensities from the live cells were immediately determined with an IVIS Spectrum imaging system (PerkinElmer, Waltham, MA, USA) and analyzed with Living Image software version 4.7, as shown in Figure A. The S/B ratios were calculated by dividing the BL intensity of the ligand-stimulated cells (signal) with that without ligand stimulation (background).

As P3.1 showed the highest absolute BL intensity and S/B ratio in response to the PPARγ agonist, whereas probes P1 and P2 (Groups 1 and 2) did not elevate the BL signal, and we further investigated the optimal dissection site for RLuc8.6-535 inside the P3.1 probe, as illustrated in Figure B,C.

COS-7 cells were grown in DMEM supplemented with 10% (v/v) FBS and 1% (v/v) P/S in 6-well microplates for transfection experiments. The cells were then transiently transfected with pcDNA3.1­(+) vector encoding each P3-series of probes developed with the N- and C-terminal fragments of RLuc8.6-535 at different dissection sites. Twenty-four h post transfection, the cells were subcultured into a 96-well black-wall clear bottom microplate. The cells in the wells were overnight stimulated using 10–6 M GW1929 or this vehicle. The cells were lysed in 40 μL of lysis buffer (Promega) by incubating for 15 min at room temperature. The cell lysates were simultaneously mixed with 40 μL of nCTZ dissolved in PBS using a 12-channel microplate, and the corresponding BL images were immediately determined using an IVIS Spectrum optical imaging system (PerkinElmer).

2.5. Characterization of P4-Series of BRET Probes for Imaging PPARγ Activities

The optical properties of the P4-series of BRET probes were determined in the presence or absence of an agonist GW1929 (Figure ). We used COS-7 cells transfected with plasmid vectors encoding probes P4.1–P4.6 by following the same protocol stated above in Figure A. The cells in the wells of 96-well black-frame microplates were stimulated with the vehicle (0 h) or 10 μM of GW1929 for 1, 5, and 16 h. The culture media in the wells were removed, and the wells were simultaneously treated with 40 μL of nCTZ dissolved in PBS using a 12-channel micropipette. The corresponding BL images were determined with the IVIS Spectrum imaging system (PerkinElmer) and analyzed with Living Image software version 4.7.

2.6. Determination of the Sensor Performance of P3.1 and P4.1 according to CTZ Analogs

We also tested specificity of various CTZ analogs in the sensor performance using probes P3.1 and P4.4 stably expressed in COS-7 cells (Figure ). We cultured COS-7 cells in the DMEM complete medium in 10 cm culture dishes. The cells were transiently transfected with pcDNA3.1­(+) vector encoding the probes P3.1 or P4.4, where the Neomycin ORF in the original vector backbone was replaced with puromycin antibiotic-coding gene. The cells were incubated for 24 h in a CO2 incubator (5% (v/v) CO2 with 95% (v/v) O2), and then, the culture media were replaced with fresh medium containing 1 μg/mL puromycin. The puromycin-resistant clones were selected by culturing for 2 weeks and used for further experiments.

COS-7 cells stably expressing P3.1 or P4.4 were seeded in a 96-well microplate and incubated overnight. The cells in the wells were then stimulated overnight using 10–6 M GW1929 or its vehicle (0.1% (v/v) DMSO). The wells were then conceptually divided into two sections: The culture media in the first section were eliminated completely, and the remaining cells were lysed with 40 μL of the lysis buffer by incubating for 20 min, whereas the wells in the second section remained sealed for preventing evaporation after completely decanting the culture media. Separately, a series of substrate solutions were prepared by dissolving the stocks of nCTZ or its analogs (CTZh, T1, T2, TS1, or TS2) in PBS buffer to a final concentration of 100 μM. The wells were then simultaneously injected with 40 μL of the substrate solutions using a 12-channel micropipette, and the corresponding BL images were obtained using the IVIS Spectrum imaging system and analyzed with Living Image software version 4.7.

In parallel, the live COS-7 cells containing P4.4 probe according to CTZ analogs were prepared as shown in the above paragraphs of this section, and the corresponding BL spectra were determined by the following method. The live cells were harvested by trypsinization and centrifugation. The precipitated cells were resuspended with a fresh PBS buffer and counted with an automatic cell counter (Countess 3, Thermo Fisher Scientific). The cell number was finally adjusted to 2 × 10 6 /mL. Forty μL of the cell solution was deployed into 200 μL PCR tubes, and the corresponding BL spectra were measured with a precision spectrophotometer (AB-1850, ATTO, Tokyo, Japan) (integration time: 30 s) immediately after injection of 40 μL of each substrate solution.

2.7. BL Imaging of GW1929 Activity with P4.4 in Living Mice

We used BALB/C mice implanted with cells expressing the P4.4 probe for in vivo imaging of PPARγ activity in response to agonist GW1929. The BL images of mice bearing xenografts of the P4.4 probe were monitored after administration of caged luciferins and PPARγ agonist GW1929 (Figure ).

COS-7 cells stably expressing the P4.4 probe were implanted into female BALB/c mice (5–7 weeks old). To image the tumor sites in vivo, the mice were randomly divided into two groups. The left tumors received PBS, while the right tumors were intratumorally (i.t.) injected with 20 μL of GW1929 (1 μM). The two groups were administered intraperitoneally (i.p.) with 100 μL of a caged luciferin, Piv-FMZ or Ad-FMZ in PBS, respectively (n = 3 per group). These animals were anesthetized with 2% (v/v) isoflurane in (1 L/min oxygen), and the BL images were acquired at various time points using the LagoX-Spectra optical imaging system (Spectral Instruments Imaging, Tucson, AZ, USA), i.e., at 5 min, 30 min, 1, 2, and 4 h post ligand injection (30 s integration per image). The images were analyzed using Living Image software, version 4.7. Regions of interest (ROIs) were drawn around each xenograft site, and the total number of photons within each ROI was recorded. The ROI position and size were held constant across all of the images.

To analyze the ex vivo images, after 2 h postinjection (p.i.) of the substrates i.t. or intravenously (i.v.), the mouse organs of each group were exercised and imaged over time, and the corresponding images were analyzed using the same Living Image software as described above.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Design of Single-Chain Probes for Imaging Ligand-Driven PPARγ Activities

To date, we have devoted systematic efforts in creating unique single-chain bioluminescent imaging systems for studying various PPIs in cells. ,,, In this study, for the first time, we applied the basic concepts of single-chain probes to image ligand-driven PPARγ activities in mammalian cells and animal models, as illustrated in Figure A,B. The probe designs and working mechanisms have been briefly described in Section of the Methods.

3.2. Characterization of Single-Chain Probes for Studying PPARγ Activities

We determined the absolute BL intensities and the S/B ratios of probes P1, P2, P3.1, and P3.2 in the presence or absence of the PPARγ agonist GW1929 (Figure A). The highest BL intensities were found with P3.1, and its S/B ratio was ca. 2.9, whereas no significant BL signals were observed with the probes P1 and P2 in both the presence and absence of GW1929.

It is difficult to pinpoint the exact reason for the poor performance of the P1 and P2 probes in measuring ligand-driven PPARγ activities, although similar molecular designs reported in our previous studies have successfully measured ohter steroid hormone activities. − This may suggest that the ligand-activated PPARγ-LBD adopts a unique conformation, which could be distinct from those of other NRs such as the androgen receptor (AR) and estrogen receptor (ER), potentially requiring optimal linker lengths and amino acid sequences different from those used in our previous probe designs. However, the primary objective of this study was to develop a broadly applicable molecular imaging platform for NR activities with optimal designs and linkers. For instance, the relative position and distance between the N- and C-termini of the PPARγ-LBD upon ligand activation may be unique and different from those observed in the LBDs of the other NRs. Even minor variations in the linker length can markedly influence the sensor performance of P1 and P2. Therefore, the overall performance of P1 and P2 could likely be improved through further optimization of probe designs, including adjustments to linker lengths, component arrangement, and interface regions of the PPARγ-LBD.

3.3. Characterization of Optimal Dissection Sites for the P3-Series Probes

To optimize PCA-based single-chain probes (P3-series), we investigated suitable dissection sites within RLuc8.6-535, which are essential for improving both S/B ratios and absolute optical intensities of the probes. A series of N- and C-terminal fragments of RLuc8.6-535 derived from various dissection sites were deployed between the LXXLL motif and PPARγ-LBD (Figures B,C and S1).

We tested the functionality of the probes in transiently transfected COS-7 cells upon exposure to PPARγ agonist GW1929. The results showed a significant level of BL intensities from the P3-series of probes carrying the N- and C-terminal fragments based on the dissection sites of RLuc8.6-535 at 209/210 AA; 225/226 AA; 228/229 AA; and 245/246 AA. The highest BL intensities were produced from the probe carrying the N- and C-terminal fragments, which were dissected at 228/229 AA, followed by the pair dissected at 209/210 AA. Interestingly, the distance between the dissection sites was 17 AA (Figure C). We named the single-chain probe carrying the N- and C-terminal fragments of RLuc8.6-535 dissected at 228 and 229 AA as P3.1.

It is noteworthy that the four probes constructed using the dissection sites at 209/210, 225/226, 229/230, and 245/246 with high BL intensities are located in the loop region of RLuc8.6-535 as shown in Figure B. In contrast, the dissection sites of the low intensity probes are mostly located within secondary structures positioned within α-helices and β-strand (with the exception of 249/250) of RLuc8.6-535. The overall results can be explained with the statement that disruption of the secondary structures of the luciferase protein can destabilize the enzyme structure upon it being located around the active site, resulting in the poor reconstitution of the reporter activity. We also noted that the split sites 209/210 and 228/229 each harbor glycine residues (G210 and G229), whose inherent flexibility and neutrality are likely to contribute to the superior BL intensities by providing a margin for the approximation of the split interface.

Although the PCA-based single-chain probes showcase a bioassay based on NR-LBD, the overall S/B ratios and optical performance fell short of our expectations. Hence, we further designed BRET-based probes as a new architecture based on the ligand-induced structural changes to NR-LBD.

3.4. Characterization of P4-Series BRET Probes for Imaging PPARγ Activities

Furthermore, we tested the sensor properties of the BRET-based P4-series of probes in COS-7 cells (Figure ). The sensor properties of probes P4.1 through 4.4 were evaluated after exposure to ligand GW1929 for different time points. The strongest S/B ratios appeared after overnight stimulation with GW1929, followed by a 5 h exposure. Although the BRET probes themselves reflect real-time PPIs, the ligand GW1929 contains hydrophilic functional groups (pK a 3.91), and it takes a long time to pass through the cell membrane of animal cells to show the intended ligand-mediated NR activation. This is why many previous publications used such long stimulation times such as 9 h and 24 h in their experimental protocols with GW1929. The present study also showed the highest effect with the longest stimulation time; however, it also showed efficient early activation.

On the other hand, the probe P4.3 showed slightly enhanced background BL intensities with the poor S/B ratios. We observed the BRET signal after screening using a 640 nm bandpass filter, which is the expected emission peak of mNeptune C162D in P4-series probes. Among the various P-series probes, P4.2 and P4.4 showed the highest S/B ratios (Figure B).

We further constructed P4.5 and P4.6 through tagging a PEST sequence or replacing the fluorescent protein and luciferase with iRFP and RLuc86SG, respectively, to the P4.4 probe. The PEST sequence was introduced to the probes to promote proteasomal degradation, shortening probe half-life and lowering the background intensity. iRFP and RLuc86SG were chosen because of their red-shifted optical properties. Interestingly, the agonist facilitated a reduction in the BL intensities of probe P4.5 by half of the negative control, whereas the same agonist boosted the BL intensities of probe P4.6 by 1.5-fold (Figure C). Ultimately, P4.4 was selected for the following experiments in Figure because it showed the highest S/B ratio and the strongest optical intensities.

3.5. CTZ Analogs Significantly Modulate the Sensor Performance of P3.1 and P4.4 Probes

To evaluate substrate contribution to the sensor performance, we tested P3.1 (PCA probe) and P4.4 (BRET probe) using a range of coelenterazine (CTZ) analogs in both live cells and lysates (Figure ). The overall BL intensities of probe P3.1 were higher in live cells than in lysates. In live cells, CTZh yielded the highest absolute BL intensity, followed by T1 and nCTZ. The lowest BL intensity was recorded for P3.1 probes with substrates TS1 and TS2 (Figure A). These results indicate that the split-luciferase based on RLuc8.6-535 positioned within the P3.1 probe shows the best enzymatic performance with substrate CTZh. This result aligns with our previous report where we have observed the strongest BL intensities with substrate CTZh for RLuc86SG, compared to the same with other CTZ analogs. The chemical structure of CTZh also explains that the highest BL intensity is partly contributed by the fast diffusion of CTZh into the live cells, facilitated by (i) the absence of the hydroxy group (OH) at the C-2 position (high hydrophobicity) and (ii) the absence of the double bond at the C-6 position of the CTZh backbone (low bulkiness).

The overall S/B ratios appeared to be predominantly influenced by variance in the baseline intensities in the absence of a ligand. T1 and nCTZ delivered the highest S/B ratios (∼6.8 fold) for P3.1 due to reduced background signal (0.3 × 106 p/s/cm2/sr) in live cells, whereas CTZh, despite high BL, showed lower S/B (∼2.0 fold) owing to elevated baseline (2.3 × 106 p/s/cm2/sr).

It remains challenging to fully elucidate why the S/B ratios are diverse, according to the substrate–probe P3.1 combinations. The S/B ratios as a consequence of the substrate–probe P3.1 reaction can be influenced by many factors, e.g., the functional groups of each substrate determine (i) the specificity to the luciferase present within the probes, (ii) the hydrophobicity of the substrate facilitating permeability across the plasma membrane, and (iii) the steric hindrance upon substrate interaction with the luciferase in the probes. All the intrinsic factors of the substrate collectively determine the unique strength of the signal intensities and the S/B ratios of the substrate–P3.1 pairs.

We also tested the BL spectral properties of the BRET probes. In Figure B, we show that the peak positions and intensities of the BL spectra of P4.4 probe are heavily influenced by CTZ analogs. The strongest BL spectrum for the probe was obtained with the substrate CTZh followed by S5 and nCTZ. The spectral peaks obtained for probe P4.4 with nCTZ and CTZh were in blue wavelengths, 468 and 473 nm, respectively. The most red-shifted peak of 507 nm was obtained for the same probe with the substrate S5. The wavelength gap between nCTZ and S5 (around 39 nm) is attributable to the presence of the sulfur (S) ankle at the C-8 position that is the only unique feature of substrate S5 compared to nCTZ. Hence, the red-shifted BL spectrum of the P4.4 probe with S5 can be interpreted as the spectral shift solely contributed by the extended π-conjugation of the sulfur at the C-8 position. This result corresponds with those of the previous study using S-series of CTZ analogs.

The P4-series BRET probes are conceptually different from a conventional NanoBRET platform using a NanoLuc-labeled target protein or a split-NanoLuc-labeled protein pair. , In the present probes, the target protein (PPARγ-LBD) is designed to be sandwiched between a fluorescent protein and a luciferase (i.e., an integrated form), whereas the NanoBRET probes make use of simply NanoLuc-labeled protein(s) (i.e., two separated molecule form). These two designsintegrated versus separatedoffer complementary advantages depending on the PPI context under investigation.

3.6. In Vivo Imaging of the P4.4 BRET Probe in Response to the Treatment of Agonist GW1929 in a Mouse Model

We further conducted in vivo imaging of the GW1929-driven PPARγ activity in COS-7 xenografts expressing the P4.4 probe. We used specific substrates such as Piv-FMZ and Ad-FMZ, where Piv and Ad groups serve as protecting groups, which were introduced at the C-3 position of FMZ via an ether linkage (Figure ). FMZ was originally optimized for NanoLuc, but we used it in this study because of its small size and compatibility with the protecting groups.

We used mice bearing tumor xenografts on both lower flanks of the mouse for this study. After i.p. administration of Piv-FMZ, the left tumor injected with PBS did not show any increase in the BL signal, whereas the right tumor that received i.t. injection of the GW1929 ligand exhibited long-lasting and high BL signals (Figure B). In the right tumor sites, the BL intensity significantly increased at 30 min p.i. and peaked at 120 min p.i. and became progressively weaker after 240 min. A similar trend was observed with another substrate, Ad-FMZ (Figure C). ROI measurements of the BL signals at each time point for the mouse groups are presented as bar graphs adjacent to the corresponding mouse image panels. These data clearly indicated that Ad-FMZ is a better substrate than Piv-FMZ with respect to signal stability and durability; specifically, Ad-FMZ displays approximately 2-fold higher BL signals at all the measurement time points p.i. with GW1929 than with PBS. Ad-FMZ shows considerable BL intensity even after 24 h p.i.

It is interesting to discuss why Ad-FMZ is more durable than Piv-FMZ. Although both protective groups, Ad and Piv, are linked to the same imidazopyrazinone backbone of FMZ via an ester linker, Ad group is more bulky and lipophilic than Piv group and easily stabilized by binding albumin as shown in refs and . These chemical properties should delay the hydrolysis of Ad-FMZ in animal models compared to the case of Piv-FMZ and thus result in the longer durability of Ad-FMZ than that of Piv-FMZ in animal models.

At the early time points (within 5 min), Piv-FMZ exhibited a high auto luminescence baseline. As discussed in previous studies, this elevated baseline is likely attributable to the initial instability of the imidazopyrazinone backbone in the serum and plasma. The acute and nonspecific oxidation of this backbone generates BL signals that decay quickly, known as not conducive to prolonged real-time imaging in vitro and in vivo. , Stabilization of the imidazopyrazinone structure can be achieved by introducing protecting groups at the reaction site, such as Ad-FMZ and Piv-FMZ. However, the Piv group offers less steric protection effects compared to the Ad group. This chemical difference reasonably explains the higher auto luminescence baseline observed for Piv-FMZ, particularly at the early time points in the in vivo experiments.

One may consider that animal experiments using only 6 mice (n = 3) may have an insufficient sample size and can affect the reliability of the conclusions. However, in most probe imaging studies, we observed a minimum variability between animal imaging results. Hence, we used fewer numbers in this study to address the use of these probes for in vivo imaging applications. Although preclinical experiments are often conducted with five mice to achieve statistical significance, in this study, we utilized a minimal number of mice (n = 3) to draw reasonable conclusions.

We obtained corresponding ex vivo images of tumors 24 h p.i. to determine the potential distribution of tumor cells and the biodistribution of the substrates (Figure B inset “a” (the bottom panel) and Figure C inset “a” (the bottom panel)). The BL images indicated that BL signals corresponding to the tumors were weak, but upon activation by GW1929, the tumors showed a brighter signal compared to the one activated by PBS. We did not observe any nonspecific signal due to substrate autoluminescence in other tissues and organs, which is common with FMZ and its analogs.

The ex vivo results support the following conclusion: (i) the in vivo BL signals are specific to the activation by the ligand GW1929 compared to the negative controls; (ii) the i.t. injection of ligand GW1929 does not result in significant nonspecific activation in the contralateral tumor; and (iii) the two FMZ analogs (Piv-FMZ and Ad-FMZ) are useful for imaging with a long circulation time of up to 24 h while preserving activity specific to the luciferase expressed by the tumor cells. We consider that the reason for the nondiffused nature of GW1929 could be partially due to the ligand formulation (using mineral oil and PEG400) used for the study.

Overall, the in vivo imaging study clearly demonstrated that the P4.4 probe provides an improved BL intensity that can be imaged by the use of long-acting substrates such as Ad-FMZ for up to 24 h without reinjection. This P4.4-based in vivo imaging provides an orthogonal methodology to directly assess ligand-activated PPARγ activities and offers advantages over conventional in vivo imaging methods using a Fluorine-18-labeled PPARγ ligand and a PPAR-targeting carrier with fluorescent Cyanine 5.5 particularly in terms of quantification and phototoxicity-free imaging.

4. Conclusion

NRs are one of the most abundant classes of transcription factors present in mammalian systems, acting as molecular switches that directly connect signaling molecules. Hence, the NR-based imaging probes facilitate preclinical investigations of the molecular events in cells under both normal and abnormal metabolic conditions.

In the present study, we developed a molecular imaging platform with four differently designed “single-chain” bioluminescent probes that can be readily adapted for other molecular imaging applications. We demonstrated four different categories of unique molecular imaging probes in vitro and in vivo. We reported efficient molecular imaging probes such as P3.1 and P4.4 that can assay ligand-driven PPARγ activity with high sensitivity and specificity. In the developing process, we identified the prominent dissection sites for RLuc that interestingly appear at every 17 AA distance in the AA sequence. We also found that the P4 series of probes efficiently generate optical signals whose S/B ratios are up to 14-fold. This study also demonstrated long-term in vivo imaging of ligand-driven PPARγ activity in living mice using the optical probes and novel long-acting substrates Piv-FMZ and Ad-FMZ. The entire probe development process above provides guidance on how to develop efficient single-chain BL probes based on NRs.

As noted in the introduction, NR activity has conventionally been assessed using mammalian two-hybrid assays and SPR. BRET systems and other probe platforms have also been developed but are validated in limited case studies. Given the importance of NR-LBDs, the assay platform developed in this study demonstrates how to bioassay the NR-related molecular events in cell signaling pathways under various physiological conditions, including in living animal models. Given that the size and function of NR LBDs are highly conserved, researchers can readily develop their own probe portfolios by simply replacing PPARγ-LBD with other LBDs, including isoforms such as PPARα-LBD and PPARβ-LBD. In addition to the general significance of NRs, PPARs have been especially investigated as a drug target in various diseases. For example, several PPAR agonists are currently being used as clinical agents for the treatment of such as diabetes and dyslipidemia. The assay portfolio developed in this study contributes to the rapid preclinical evaluation of PPAR ligand activity both in vivo and in vitro prior to potential clinical applications.
