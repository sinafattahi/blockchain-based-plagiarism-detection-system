Abstract

For cementing operations in oil and gas wells to maintain the cement sheath integrity, effective fluid loss management is essential. In order to improve fluid loss management in cement slurries, this study examines the mechanism of action of a new copolymer fluid loss additive (FLA) made of sodium styrenesulfonate (SSS), N-methacrylamide (HAM), and itaconic acid (IA). The performance evaluation results indicate that under conditions of 180 °C and saturated brine, the water loss can still be controlled within 70 mL, and the cement exhibits excellent settling stability. After additives were added, the compressive strength of the cement samples in freshwater, semi-saturated brine, and saturated brine systems decreased by 15.9%, 25.3%, and 20.6%, respectively, compared to the control samples. Although there is a certain degree of reduction, these values still meet industry standards and do not affect on-site construction. The mechanism of action of the SSS/HAM/IA copolymer is elucidated through comprehensive testing, including total organic carbon test, zeta potential, environmental scanning electron microscopy test, mercury intrusion porosimetry, and X-ray diffraction analysis. The copolymer’s adsorption onto cement particles dynamically alters the zeta potential of the cement slurry system, with the extent of adsorption directly correlating with fluid loss reduction. As the hydration reaction progresses, the copolymer’s adsorption on cement particles increases, enhancing the system’s stability without altering the composition of the cement hydration products. This study contributes to the development of an advanced FLA, paving the way for more efficient and reliable cementing operations in the oil and gas industry.

1. Introduction

Cementing operations are critical to the integrity and longevity of oil and gas wells, with fluid loss control being a key factor in ensuring successful cement placement. Fluid loss additives (FLAs) are essential components in cement slurries, designed to minimize the loss of water to the surrounding formation during the cementing process. − Over the years, significant advancements have been made in the development of synthetic polymer-based FLAs, which have shown superior performance in controlling fluid loss under high-temperature and high-pressure scenarios.

Recent studies have explored various aspects of FLAs, including their adsorption behavior, impact on cement hydration, and formation of filter cakes. For instance, Li et al. investigated the use of zwitterionic copolymers as FLAs, demonstrating their effectiveness in reducing fluid loss by forming a dense filter cake and enhancing the stability of cement slurries under high-temperature conditions. Similarly, Zhang et al. synthesized a novel terpolymer FLA and found that its adsorption on cement particles significantly reduced fluid loss by increasing the viscosity of the cement slurry and blocking the pores in the filter cake. Wang et al. explored the role of electrostatic interactions between FLAs and cement particles, showing that the adsorption of negatively charged polymers on positively charged cement surfaces plays a crucial role in controlling fluid loss. Furthermore, Liu et al. investigated the impact of FLAs on the microstructure of cement hydration products, revealing that the addition of FLAs not only reduces fluid loss but also promotes the formation of a more compact and durable cement matrix.

Despite these advancements, several gaps remain in the current understanding of FLA mechanisms. Most studies focus on specific FLAs, often employing limited testing methods, which restrict the generalizability of the findings. Moreover, the complex nature of cement hydration poses challenges in accurately modeling the interaction between FLAs and the cement particles. This lack of a comprehensive theoretical framework hinders the development of more effective FLAs tailored to specific well conditions. In view of these challenges, this study prepared a copolymer FLA with significant advantages in controlling the water loss of a cement slurry, high temperature resistance, salt resistance, compressive strength, and settlement stability, and explored its working mechanism in depth. By analyzing the adsorption behavior, zeta potential change, and microstructure change of the copolymer, this FLA revealed how to control fluid loss in a cement slurry under different temperature and salinity conditions. In addition to providing useful insights for improving cement slurry compositions under high-temperature and high-salinity conditions, our findings advance our basic understanding of FLA processes.

2.2. Preparation of FLA

After dissolving the three monomers of sodium styrenesulfonate, N-methyl acrylamide, and itaconic acid (IA) in a beaker with a molar ratio of 1:1.5:0.5, the pH of the mixture was brought to 8 by adding calcium hydroxide solution. Add the monomer solution to a 250 mL three-necked flask, and the mass concentration of the monomer is 20%. Weigh 0.8% of the total monomer mass as an initiator (K2S2O8) and transfer it into a constant-pressure separatory funnel. Pass nitrogen through the apparatus to remove air and prevent oxygen from consuming the initiator and radicals. When the temperature of the experimental setup reaches 60 °C, the stopcock on the separatory funnel is opened, and the initiator is slowly added to the three-necked flask. Control of the addition rate ensures a uniform and stable reaction process. After the addition is complete, close the nitrogen valve. Stop the reaction after 4 h, and cool the flask to obtain a colorless, slightly viscous copolymer product. Extract and purify the product by adding anhydrous ethanol and, after drying and grinding, obtain the polymer powder.

2.3. Performance Evaluation

2.3.1. Preparation of Cement Slurry

In compliance with the trial requirements, an electronic balance was used to weigh class G oil well cement, quartz sand, cement admixture, and water. Next, the water-filled pulp cup was placed on the stirrer to start the motor, and the stirrer speed was turned to 4000 rpm ± 200 rpm. Then, the cement admixture is thoroughly mixed with the cement, and the cement or cement admixture mixture is uniformly added to the mixing cup within 15 s. After the mixture was all added to the mixing cup, the speed of the mixer was turned to 12000 rpm ± 500 rpm, and stirring was continued for 35 s ± 1 s. After the mixture was churned, the cement slurry required for the experiment was obtained.

2.3.2. Water Loss Performance

A high-temperature and high-pressure water loss meter, an OWC-9310 system, was used to conduct a static water loss test. The cement slurry is poured into the water loss meter cup according to the API RP 10B 2013 Oil Well Cement Experimental Method. The water loss meter cup was inflated to 6.7 MPa, and the filtrate was collected under differential pressure for 30 min at the present experimental temperature.

2.3.3. Compressive Strength Test

According to the experimental requirements, the prepared cement slurry was poured into the test mold coated with lubricating oil. Then, in order to remove air from the cement slurry and keep it from compromising the cement stone’s compressive strength, the slurry in the mold was stirred more than 30 times with a handmade, fine copper wire. Next, the test mold was covered with a glass plate and secured with a thin rope. The bound test mold was rinsed with tap water. The test mold was placed in a curing device at a specified temperature. When the curing temperature is below or equal to 90 °C, the test mold is placed in a water bath for curing. If the curing temperature exceeds 90 °C, the mold containing the sample is placed in a high-temperature and high-pressure curing chamber for curing. After the curing time reaches the experimental age, demold the sample and use a pressure testing machine to test the compressive strength of the sample within 45 min after demolding. The force needed to destroy the sample divided by the minimal cross-sectional area of the sample in contact with the pressure testing machine’s bearing plate yields the sample’s compressive strength value. 2.3.4. Cement Settling Stability Test

The settling stability of the cement slurry significantly affects its performance. The testing method is as follows: pour the cured cement slurry into a 500 mL graduated cylinder to the calibration mark and let it stand for 2 h. Measure the densities of the top, middle, and bottom portions of the cement slurry in the cylinder using a densitometer after it has stood. The degree of settling stability of the cement slurry is indicated by the size of the difference among these three density values.

2.4. Mechanism Study

2.4.1. Inherent Properties of Polymers

2.4.1.1. Thermogravimetic Analysis

This paper uses a DSC823 TGA/SDTA85/e-type thermal analyzer (Mettler Toledo, Switzerland) to analyze the thermal stability, thermal decomposition process, and ultimate heat resistance of the copolymers. The experimental procedure is as follows: After the instrument is calibrated, 5–10 mg of copolymer powder is weighed on an analytical balance and placed in a ceramic crucible. Then, place the crucible in the thermal analyzer and, under nitrogen protection, increase the temperature at a rate of 10 °C/min from 40 to 600 °C. Perform the test according to the operating specifications. After the experiment, we obtained the thermogravimetric curve of the copolymer.

2.4.1.2. Gel Permeation Chromatography Analysis

In this paper, the method used to determine the relative molecular mass and its distribution of the synthesized copolymer is gel permeation chromatography (GPC); a gel substance with different pore sizes is used as the chromatographic column. The copolymer components of different molecular weights are separated by the gel substance through their varying obstructive effects. Smaller molecules can pass through all of the pores in the gel and are separated first. As the molecular weight increases, the number of pores through which they pass decreases, and the time required for separation gradually increases. The Alliance e2695 gel chromatograph was used to analyze the copolymer, dissolving the copolymer powder in a solvent, and testing it using an IC761 ion chromatograph. The relative molecular mass and the distribution of the copolymer were analyzed based on the obtained test results.

2.4.1.3. Scanning Electron Microscopy Analysis

The microstructure of the copolymer solution was examined in this work using an ambient scanning electron microscope of the Quanta 450 type, a cement slurry, and a cement stone containing a copolymer desiccant, so as to study the mechanism of action, salt resistance, and temperature resistance of the desiccant from a microscopic perspective.

2.4.2. Analysis of Interaction between the Water Loss Agent and Cement Particles

2.4.2.1. Total Organic Carbon Test

The concentration of the copolymer that is not adsorbed by the cement particles can be obtained by the filtrate collected by the water loss test. The adsorption amount of copolymer FLA on the cement particles can be determined according to the consumption method. For the cement slurry containing the copolymer FLA, the content of the copolymer that is not adsorbed in the filtrate can be obtained by total organic carbon (TOC) analysis using a model TOC-VCPH instrument from Japan Shimazu Co., Ltd. Thus, the amount of copolymer adsorbed in the cement slurry can be calculated.

2.4.2.2. Zeta Potential Test

In order to investigate the effect of the polymer on the potential between the cement particle surface and the dispersion solution, a cement slurry of different polymer FLA contents was prepared. The zeta potential was tested after the hydration reaction was carried out at different times. The zeta potential of the cement slurry system was measured using a Zeta PALS 190 Plus Zeta potentiometer from Brookhaven, USA. The measured potential value is the mean of the five readings. Usually, the applied operating voltage falls between 50 and 150 mV.

2.4.2.3. Environmental Scanning Electron Microscopy Test

To find out how the FLA affected the cement slurry’s hydration, the experiment examined the microscopic morphology of a freshwater cement slurry system with a 4% fluid loss addition applied at 10, 30, and 50 min. The manufactured cement slurry was removed for testing after being freeze-dried with liquid nitrogen on a stage. The Quanta 450 environmental scanning electron microscope, manufactured by FEI in the United States, was employed.

2.4.2.4. Microscopic Morphology of Cement Stone

Cement pastes with 0% and 4% copolymers made with freshwater, semi-saturated brine, and saturated brine were taken out and dried after curing in a 90 °C water bath for 3 days in order to ascertain the copolymer’s and the cement particles’ mode of action. After the material was prepared into thin slices and a gold spray was applied, the microscopic morphology was examined.

2.4.2.5. Mercury Intrusion Porosimetry Test

After curing the blank cement slurry and cement slurry with 4% FLA under a 90 °C water bath for 3 days, the samples were taken out and dried in an oven at 60 °C. Then, the porosity and pore surface area of different cement stones were tested by an AutoPore IV9500 mercury press.

2.4.2.6. X-ray Diffraction (XRD) Test

In order to carry out elemental analysis of the cement stone powder containing the copolymer FLA, freshwater cement stone containing 0%, 1%, 2%, 4%, and 6% copolymers was ground into a powder using a mortar. The test was carried out with a model AL-2700B X-ray diffractometer from Dandong Aolong Ray Instrument Group Co., Ltd. With Cu Kα as the radiation source, the accelerating voltage is 40 kV, the current is 200 mA, the scanning angle (2θ) is in the range of 0° to 70°, the step length is 0.02°, and the scanning residence time is 4 s.

2.4.2.7. Cement Stone Thermogravimetric Test

Ground into a powder were freshwater cement stone, semi-saturated brine cement stone, and saturated brine cement stone with 0%, 4%, and 6% copolymers, respectively. Then, a small amount of powder sample (5–10 mg) is weighed and put into a thermal analyzer (model DSC823 TGA/SDTA85/e from METTLER TOLEDO, Switzerland). Under N2 protection, at a heating rate of 20 °C/min, the TG-DTG curve of cement stone powder was obtained from 40 to 1000 °C.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Assessment of Performance

3.1.1. Control of Fluid Loss

For the cement slurry system, the change in fluid loss under different loading conditions at 90 °C, 120 °C, 150 °C, and 180 °C was examined using SSS/HAM/IA as the FLA. The experimental results are shown in Figure . The following cement slurry mix is used to evaluate the fluid loss performance: SSS/HAM/IA + 44% freshwater +0.5% dispersion +1% retarder +35% silica sand + Class G oil well cement.

As shown in Figure , under the same water loss reduction dosage conditions, the water loss gradually increases with the rise in the test temperature. At 180 °C, when the dosage is 3%, the water loss can be controlled within 100 mL; when the dosage is 6%, it can be controlled within 60 mL. This indicates that the copolymer FLA has excellent high-temperature resistance and can be effectively applied to freshwater cement slurry systems at 180 °C.

All three cement slurry systems see a progressive increase in water loss as the temperature rises, even with the same dosage of the FLA. The water loss in the cement slurry grows with the slurry’s salt content when the test temperature and FLA dose are the same. Meanwhile, at 180 °C, when the FLA dosage exceeds 4%, the water loss in all three cement slurry systems can still be kept below 100 mL. This is mainly due to the damage to copolymer molecular chains caused by temperature and salt content. As the temperature rises and the salt content in the system increases, the copolymer molecular chains are damaged, thereby affecting the water loss reduction performance. On the other hand, since the sulfonic acid groups and carboxyl groups in the main chain of copolymer molecules are anionic groups, their presence in the cement slurry weakens hydration, causing molecular chain curling and contraction, which reduces the adsorption between the copolymer molecular chains and cement particles, ultimately leading to an increase in water loss. The polymer SSS/HAM/IA is highly resistant to salts at temperatures up to 180 °C. It successfully controls sludge water loss.

3.1.3. Compressive Strength of Cement Stone

Compressive strength is one of the primary indicators affecting cementing safety and determining the quality of cementing. Therefore, it is essential to ensure that the cement stone reaches the specified compressive strength value. To study the impact of synthesized copolymer water reducers on the compressive strength of cement stones, tests were conducted on the compressive strength values of water reducers at different dosages and temperatures after curing for 3 days in a freshwater-based cement slurry, a semi-saturated brine-based cement slurry, and a saturated brine-based cement slurry. Figures – present the findings.

In the three cement paste systems, as the amount of copolymer water reducer increases, after 3 days of curing, the cement stone’s compressive strength ratings all exhibit varied degrees of reduction. The cement stone’s compressive strength also exhibits a declining tendency as the curing temperature rises under the identical copolymer water reducer dosage settings. When the water reducer dosage is 6% and the curing temperature is 90 °C, in freshwater, semi-saturated brine, and saturated brine, the cement stone’s relative compressive strengths are 23.34, 19.23, and 18.01 MPa. Compared to the blank sample paste at the same age, dosage, and experimental temperature, their compressive strengths have decreased by 15.9%, 25.3%, and 20.6%. This indicates that the addition of a water reducer causes a slight decrease in the compressive strength of the cement stone, and its impact on the saturated brine cement stone is greater than on the freshwater cement stone. However, the test results also show that the development of compressive strength in the three types of cement stones meets the requirements specified by standards and can satisfy the needs of on-site construction.

3.1.4. Settling Stability of Cement Slurry

The quality of cementing is greatly impacted by the cement slurry’s settling stability. Excessive density discrepancies between the top and lower portions of the cement slurry might result from inadequate settling stability, causing unequal compressive strength at different levels of the cement annulus, which in turn affects the quality and safety of the cementing. Therefore, researching how water reducers affect the cement slurry’s capacity to settle is crucial. The density variations among the upper, middle, and lower portions of the cement slurry can be used to determine the settling stability of the slurry.

Tables – show how the settling stability of the three cement slurry systems gradually increases with the addition of water-reducing agents. When the dosage reaches 6%, there is no density difference among the top, middle, and bottom layers. This effect is primarily caused by the fact that many cement particles adsorb onto the surface of high-molecular-weight copolymers, increasing the cement particles’ resistance to movement. Copolymers also increase the electrostatic repulsive potential energy between cement particles, while decreasing the attractive potential energy.

3.2. Working Mechanism

3.2.1. Self-Assembly Properties of Copolymers

3.2.1.1. Thermogravimetic Analysis

As shown in Figure , the thermal loss of copolymer desiccants is mainly divided into four stages. In the first stage, from 45 to 153 °C, the weight loss of copolymer desiccants is relatively small, approximately 9.95%. The primary cause of this weight reduction is the volatilization of crystalline water and small molecular impurities in the polymer. In the second stage, from 153 to 408 °C, the weight loss is about 9.43%. The main reason for the decrease in copolymer quality is the thermal decomposition of copolymers with lower molecular weights and certain groups (amide groups and sulfonic acid groups) within the copolymer chains. In the third stage, from 408 to 506 °C, the weight loss reaches 20%. During this stage, the weight loss of copolymers is significant due to partial chain scission of the copolymer main chain at this temperature, with most amide groups, sulfonic acid groups, and carboxyl groups in the molecules undergoing decomposition. The weight loss in the fourth stage occurs after 506 °C; within this temperature range, the copolymer main chain begins large-scale scission and carbonization, with its weight continuing to decrease as the temperature rises, ultimately leaving behind charred residues. Correspondingly, the DTG curves show peaks at 84 °C, 186 °C, 278 °C, 398 °C, and 436 °C, representing the thermal decomposition temperatures of water, carboxyl groups, amide groups, and sulfonic acid groups, respectively. The highest decomposition rate occurs between 435 and 436 °C, which corresponds to the cleavage of the copolymer main chain.

From the above analysis, it is known that after 408 °C, the main chain of the copolymer begins to degrade thermally. The main chain of the water loss agent in the copolymer is damaged and starts to lose its intended properties. Therefore, from this temperature onward, the copolymer no longer has a water loss effect. This suggests that the water loss agent has exceptional heat resistance, with a thermal degradation temperature of at least 408 °C.

3.2.1.2. GPC Analysis

The molecular weight and the distribution of relative molecular weights significantly influence many properties of copolymers. Generally, M z, M w, M η, and M n are the four fundamental parameters used to express the relative molecular weight of copolymers. The polydispersity index (DPI) is used to represent the distribution of the relative molecular weight in homogeneous copolymers. From the structure and properties of polymers, we know that the relationship between relative molecular weights is M z ≥ M w ≥ M η ≥ M n. A higher DPI indicates a more dispersed relative molecular weight of the copolymer, while a lower DPI indicates a more concentrated relative molecular weight. When the DPI equals 1, it indicates that the copolymer has a monodisperse state. The relative molecular weight and distribution of the polymer were ascertained in this experiment using volume exclusion chromatography; the test findings are displayed in Table .

As shown in Table , the copolymer has a relatively high molecular weight, with a number-average molecular weight of 17.10 × 104 g/mol, a weight-average molecular weight of 26.98 × 104 g/mol, and a z-average molecular weight of 43.17 × 104 g/mol. However, its molecular weight distribution is relatively narrow (with a polydispersity index of 1.598). This is advantageous for enhancing the copolymer’s ability to reduce water loss as a FLA. If the copolymer has an abnormally high molecular weight, it can cause additional adsorption sites on the cement particles when employed as a FLA. This can result in a thicker hydration layer and higher viscosity of the cement slurry, which is bad for pumping. Conversely, if the copolymer has an excessively low molecular weight, the shorter molecular chains mean fewer adsorption sites, making it harder for the copolymer to adsorb the surrounding cement particles, thus increasing the water loss and reducing the water loss reduction effect. A wider molecular weight distribution (larger polydispersity index) indicates that the copolymer contains both low- and high-molecular-weight polymers, which results in poorer water loss reduction performance compared to copolymers with a narrower molecular weight distribution.

3.2.1.3. Microscopic Morphology Analysis of Copolymers

The environmental scanning electron microscopy (ESEM) image of the copolymer solution is shown in Figure , where (a) is a panoramic view obtained by magnifying 200 times after dissolving the copolymer in deionized water and (b), (c), and (d) are local microscopic images. As can be seen from Figure , the copolymer dissolved in deionized water primarily exhibits a network structure in the form of a film layer with pores of varying sizes and uneven distribution within it. When the copolymer is further magnified to 1000 times, the microscopic morphology of different parts of the polymer solution is as shown in Figure b,c,d, where the polymer mainly presents a network and film-like structure distribution. The reason for this microscopic morphology is the entanglement and curling of polymer molecular chains. Cement particles in the slurry adsorb onto the surface of the polymer film and fill the gaps between the layers when the copolymer and cement slurry come into contact. In addition, the copolymer’s multilayered structure may adsorb a lot of cement particles and stop the free water of the slurry from evaporating. As a result, this structure is essential for minimizing the cement slurry’s water loss.

3.2.2. Interface Interaction Features

3.2.2.1. TOC Test

Figure illustrates the negative correlation between the amount of copolymer adsorbed on the cement particles and the fluid loss in the cement paste system. A significant fluid loss indicates that the cement particles’ surface has little copolymer adsorbed on it. A significant amount of copolymer will be adsorbed on the cement particles’ surface if there is little water loss.

According to the aforementioned research, the quantity of copolymer adsorption in the cement paste system is strongly correlated to the amount of water loss. Thus, it can be said that the FLA copolymer’s mode of action mostly relies on its adsorption onto the cement particles. Furthermore, the main basis of adsorption is the electrostatic interaction between the positively charged Stern layer formed during the hydration of the cement particles and the positively charged –SO3– and −COO– groups of the anionic copolymer. This experimental result is consistent with the conclusions reached by Johann Plank. , Additionally, the zeta potential test of the cement paste containing the copolymer shows that the FLA copolymer is adsorbed on the surface of the cement particles. Figure illustrates how the cement paste system’s negative potential rises with an increase in FLA copolymer concentration.

3.2.2.2. Zeta Potential Test

As the reaction time increases, the zeta potential value for a pure cement slurry (FLA-0) gradually decreases from its initial positive value during cement hydration, as shown in Figure . When the reaction occurs to 20 min, the potential value becomes negative, and its zeta value is −0.45 mV. After 30 min, the value of the cement slurry tends to be stable. The main cause of this phenomenon is various hydration products generated during the hydration reaction. When the hydration reaction begins, the surface of the cement’s main components, tricalcium silicate and dicalcium silicate, is negatively charged, while the surface of tricalcium aluminate is positively charged. However, the hydration rate of tricalcium aluminate is faster than that of tricalcium silicate and dicalcium silicate, thus making the surface of the cement slurry positively charged. As time progresses, the negative charge generated by the continuous hydration of tricalcium silicate and dicalcium silicate is neutralized with the positive charge generated by the hydration of tricalcium aluminate, so the zeta potential value of the cement slurry system is continuously lowered. While bulk hydration processes continue, the zeta potential of the slurry system stabilizes after 30 min of hydration, signifying the development of electrokinetic equilibrium at particle surfaces.

The addition of the FLA copolymer to the cement paste resulted in a system with a lower zeta potential than the pure cement paste. As the amount of FLA copolymer increased, the zeta potential of the system decreased. The zeta potential stabilized at 30 min of hydration. This phenomenon shows that the copolymers lose their electronegativity and that the FLA copolymers are adsorbed on the cement particle surface in large quantities. These electron-negative copolymer molecules produce a strong negative charge on the cement particle surface, and the more copolymers that are adsorbed on the cement particle surface, the more noticeable the system’s zeta potential decrease. The aforementioned investigation demonstrates that the copolymer FLA mostly affects cement particles by adsorption on their surface.

3.2.2.3. ESEM Test of Cement Slurry Containing the FLA

A microscopic topography of the cement slurry with varying hydration durations is shown in Figure , which shows that the cement is less hydrated when the hydration reaction starts and that cement particles are visible when magnified 5000 times (Figure b). At the same time, the copolymer is mainly made of a film floating on the surface of the cement particles (Figure a). A small amount of the copolymer film is embedded in the cement particles (Figure b). The cement particles progressively diminish as the hydration reaction continues, and the hydration product C–S–H, which takes the shape of a network gel, eventually forms. At this time, the copolymer floating on the surface is reduced (Figure c), and the copolymer film is mainly adsorbed on the surface of cement particles and hydration products (Figure d). When the hydration reaction reached 50 min, the copolymer film floating on the surface had completely disappeared, and at this time, the copolymer film was adsorbed on the surface of the cement particles in a large amount.

The aforementioned investigation indicates that as the hydration reaction advances, the copolymer’s interaction with the cement particles and the hydration product gradually increases. To create contact, the copolymer’s molecular chain is mostly adsorbed on the cement particle’s surface.

3.2.2.4. Microscopic Morphology of Cement Stone Containing FLA

The results of the energy-dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) analyses of the cement stone containing different concentrations of the FLA copolymer are shown in Figures and .

It can be observed from Figure that different morphologies of hydration products are widely distributed in various regions of the cement stone. The main hydration products are calcium hydroxide (Ca (OH)2), calcium-hydrated monosulfoaluminate (AFm), columnar crystal of calcium aluminite (AFt), and calcium silicate gel (C–S–H), in which the hydrated calcium has a layered structure, hydrated monosulfide calcium aluminate has a layered structure, AFt has a columnar structure, and C–S–H has a network gel structure. Moreover, several forms of hydration products are widely distributed in the various areas of cement stone. On the other hand, in the cement stone without copolymer FLA (Figure a,c,e), there are a small number of holes and microcracks, which are due to the pores formed by the pores, capillary pores, and hydration product C–S–H gel produced during cement hydration.

When the copolymer was added to the cement, it was apparent in the SEM image that the copolymer film or particles adhered to the surface of the cement hydration product (Figure b,d,f). Compared with pure cement stone, the cement stone structure containing a copolymer is denser, and no pores and microcracks are observed in the SEM image. The reason for this phenomenon is that the negatively charged copolymer particles or the film are adsorbed on the surface of the cement hydration product , and filled with pores and microcracks generated during the hydration of the cement.

Furthermore, the structure is less dense than that of the freshwater cement stone including the copolymer FLA, according to the SEM images of the semi-saturated brine cement stone and the saturated brine cement stone containing the copolymer FLA, but it is denser than the structure of three kinds of cement stones without copolymers. It indicates that NaCl only partially destroys the structure of the copolymer and indicates that the synthesized copolymer has a good salt resistance.

EDS spectrum analysis shows that pure cement stone mainly contains O, Si, Ca, and other elements (as shown in Figure a, where C, N, and other elements are mainly derived from additives such as dispersants added in the process of preparing cement slurry). For the semi-saturated brine cement stone without a copolymer (Figure c) and the saturated brine cement stone without a copolymer (Figure e), the Na and Cl elements are derived from NaCl added during the preparation of cement slurry. When the copolymer was added to the cement, EDS spectrum analysis showed that the cement stone mainly contained C, N, O, Na, Si, S, and Ca and other elements (such as Figure b,d,f), wherein C, N, and S are derived from a copolymer. This demonstrates that the copolymer does not change the cement hydration product but only brings the elements contained in it. The adsorption of negatively charged copolymer films on the surface of cement particles is the most significant impact of copolymers on cement hydration, according to the data above.

Because the total weight loss might somewhat indicate the cement’s level of hydration, adding the copolymer FLA can help the cement hydrate. As the content of the FLA increases, the promotion effect is obvious. ,

3.3. Role of FLA

Calcium oxide, silica, alumina, and iron oxide are the primary constituents of the Class G oil well cement. The cement’s hydrated products mostly consist of calcium silicate gel with an amorphous network structure, lamellar crystalline low-sulfur hydrated calcium sulfoaluminate, columnar crystalline ettringite, and layered crystal hydrogen calcium oxide. The zeta potential of the pure cement slurry system is positive, according to the zeta test results, indicating that the hydration product contains a positive charge. The inclusion of a group, such as an amide group, carboxyl group, or sulfonic acid group, makes the copolymer FLA negatively charged. As seen in Figure , when the FLA comes into contact with the hydrated cement particles, the polymer chain is adsorbed on their surface as a result of electrostatic attraction, altering the cement particles’ surface charge.

According to the TOC test, the quantity of copolymer adsorption on the cement particle surface is correlated with the cement slurry’s water loss. The slurry loses less water as the quantity of adsorption rises. Water loss can be reduced by the copolymer because the polymer chains adsorb onto the cement particles. The copolymer adsorbed on cement particle surfaces increases the dynamic viscosity of the slurry, which can reduce the rate of the slurry water loss. Additionally, when the copolymer content rises, the copolymer adsorbed on the cement particle’s surface increases, and the polymer molecular chain tends to be coiled. As seen in Figure , the adsorbed layer’s thickness also grows. This not only makes it easier to fill the spaces between the cement particles but also stops the pores that are created when the cement particles hydrate. As a result, it restricts the filter cake’s rate of penetration, blocks the slurry’s water loss channel, and thickens the cement slurry system as a whole. Additionally, the copolymer’s film structure helps to retain the free water in the slurry, which raises the water molecules’ resistance to movement and lowers the cement slurry’s water loss.

4. Conclusion

The SSS/HAM/IA copolymer demonstrates exceptional performance as a fluid loss control agent in cement slurries across a wide range of conditions, including temperatures from 90 to 180 °C, and in environments with freshwater, semi-saturated brine, and saturated brine. This novel copolymer exhibits superior fluid loss control, thermal stability, and salt resistance, making it a highly effective solution for cementing operations under challenging oil and gas well conditions. The innovative mechanism of the SSS/HAM/IA copolymer is elucidated through comprehensive testing, including TOC, zeta potential, XRD, and ESEM analyses. The copolymer’s adsorption onto cement particles dynamically alters the zeta potential of the cement slurry system, with the extent of adsorption directly correlating with fluid loss reduction. As the hydration reaction progresses, the copolymer’s adsorption on cement particles increases, enhancing the system’s stability without altering the composition of the cement hydration products. Furthermore, the copolymer promotes cement particle hydration, contributing to the overall strength and durability of the cement matrix. The primary mechanism by which the SSS/HAM/IA copolymer controls fluid loss is through its adsorption onto cement particles, which increases the dynamic viscosity of the slurry and reduces the rate of free water filtration. Additionally, the copolymer fills pores and microcracks within the cement structure, forming a dense, low-permeability filter cake that further inhibits fluid loss.

In summary, the SSS/HAM/IA copolymer represents a groundbreaking advancement in fluid loss control agents for cementing operations. Its ability to maintain performance under extreme temperatures and salinity conditions, coupled with its unique adsorption and pore-filling mechanisms, sets a new standard for FLAs in the oil and gas industry. Despite the significant achievements of this study, there is still room for further in-depth research. On one hand, more field application tests should be conducted to verify the long-term stability and reliability of the copolymer in actual oil and gas well cementing operations, assessing its adaptability to different geological conditions and well conditions. On the other hand, the relationship between the copolymer structure and performance can be further explored. By optimizing the composition and structure of the copolymer through molecular design, we can enhance its temperature and salt resistance as well as compatibility with other cement additives. Additionally, combining advanced computational simulation techniques to gain a deeper understanding of the interaction mechanisms between copolymers and cement particles at the microscopic level will provide theoretical support for developing more efficient cement slurry FLAs.
