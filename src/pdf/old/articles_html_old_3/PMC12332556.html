Abstract

Medicinal plants, known for their bioactive compounds, have long been utilized for the treatment of various diseases, including diabetes. A novel compound, distachionate (DST), was isolated as a yellowish powder from , referred to as snowbush. It is known to possess pronounced antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities mediated through the modulation of COX-2 and inflammatory cytokines. Based on its diverse medicinal uses of the parent plant and the lack of data for its effectiveness in diabetes, this study has been aimed at evaluating the antidiabetic efficacy of DST and DST-loaded ZnO nanoparticles embedded in chitosan (DSTNC) using high fructose (HF) and streptozotocin (HF + STZ)-administered diabetic rats. DSTNC was prepared and characterized by using particle size, zeta potential, Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy, X-ray diffraction, and atomic force microscopy techniques. In vivo antidiabetic effects of DST and DSTNC were determined in Wistar rats (180–220 g). Diabetes was induced in rats by administration of HF for 6 weeks, and at the end of the fifth week, streptozotocin (STZ, 35 mg kg–1 i.p.) was injected. DST, DSTNC, and standard metformin (200 mg kg–1) were administered orally for 6 weeks. Administration of DST (5, 10, and 15 mg kg–1) and DSTNC (5, 10, and 15 mg kg–1) markedly increased (p < 0.001) serum insulin and high-density lipoprotein levels, while a notable (p < 0.001) reduction in glucose, total cholesterol, triglycerides (TG), glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c), low-density lipoprotein, and markers of liver and kidney function was observed when compared with the data of only HF + STZ-administered animals. Additionally, this treatment has also influenced inflammatory markers like interleukin 6 (IL-6) and tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-α) and adipocytokines, including chemerin, adiponectin, and leptin. DST- and DSTNC-treated animals exhibited marked (p < 0.001) improvement in catalase (CAT) and superoxide dismutase levels (Units mg–1), while they caused a marked (p < 0.001) reduction in malondialdehyde levels (nmol g–1) in liver and pancreatic tissues. Histopathological examination showed restoration of tissue structures in the pancreas, liver, kidney, heart, and aortic specimens of the animals treated with DST and DSTNC compared with the data of only HF + STZ-fed diabetic animals. The mRNA expression studies revealed the upregulation of glucose transporter 4, pancreatic duodenal homeobox 1 (PDX-1), and sirtuin 1 (SIRT1) and downregulation of c-Jun N-terminal kinase (Jun N-terminal kinase) and a disintegrin and metalloprotease 17, highlighting their role in the improvement of glucose homeostasis, β cell dysfunction, insulin signaling pathway, inflammation, and insulin resistance. This study shows that DST and DST nanocomposites possess antidiabetic properties mediated likely via modulation of glycemic, oxidative stress, adipocytokines, and inflammatory markers. Thus, this study provides a rationale for the antidiabetic potential of DST and DST nanocomposites.

1. Introduction

Diabetes is the most prevalent metabolic disorder, posing significant global medical challenges today. It represents a group of metabolic disorders characterized by chronic hyperglycemia. According to the International Diabetes Federation Atlas 2021, diabetes affects 10.5% of the global adult population aged 20 to 79. Projections indicate that by 2045, this number will rise to 783 million, equivalent to one in every eight adults. The nature of the disease is evolving, with type 2 diabetes estimated to have consumed 12% of global healthcare spending in 2010, totaling $376 billion, which is projected to increase to $490 billion by 2030. The World Health Organization predicts that diabetes is expected to be the seventh leading cause of death by 2030. Pakistan has a notably high prevalence rate of 39.19%. Recent epidemiological data show Pakistan’s diabetes landscape has worsened, with age-adjusted prevalence of 30.8% in urban and 25.4% in rural areas, making it among the countries with the highest diabetes burden in South Asia. Approximately 37.1% of Pakistani adults with diabetes remain undiagnosed, highlighting significant gaps in screening and early intervention.

Hyperglycemia in diabetes triggers oxidative stress through glucose auto-oxidation and mitochondrial dysfunction, leading to excessive free radical accumulation and reduced antioxidant defense. Intake of high fructose (HF) + STZ leads to partial pancreatic β-cell dysfunction. The induction of diabetes results in elevated blood glucose, reduced insulin secretion or its impaired action, elevated cytokine-mediated inflammation (IL-6 and TNF-α), increased oxidative stress, and abnormal lipid profile.

Type 2 diabetes represents a complex metabolic disorder characterized by chronic hyperglycemia arising from the intricate interplay between insulin resistance and progressive pancreatic β-cell dysfunction. The pathophysiology involves multiple interconnected mechanisms: initially, peripheral tissues develop insulin resistance, prompting compensatory β-cell hyperfunction and hyperinsulinemia to maintain glucose homeostasis. However, sustained metabolic stress from hyperglycemia, hyperlipidemia, and oxidative damage leads to β-cell dysfunction through endoplasmic reticulum stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, and activation of apoptotic pathways. Environmental factors, including nutritional excess, physical inactivity, and gut dysbiosis, perpetuate this pathological cascade by promoting chronic low-grade inflammation, lipotoxicity, and glucotoxicity. Furthermore, epigenetic modifications and metabolic memory phenomena ensure the persistence of diabetic complications even after glycemic control is achieved. The progressive nature of T2DM reflects the cumulative impact of these multifactorial pathways, ultimately resulting in inadequate insulin secretion relative to metabolic demand and the establishment of chronic hyperglycemia with its associated micro- and macrovascular complications.

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) are crucial in driving the progression of insulin resistance by disrupting the insulin signaling pathway. Glucose transporter 4 (GLUT-4) is crucial for insulin-regulated glucose uptake in adipose tissue and muscle and is often diminished in diabetics, leading to hyperglycemia. It activates the Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK) pathway, reducing insulin gene expression and β-cell function by decreasing PDX-1 activity through its nucleo-cytoplasmic translocation. This process likely contributes to β-cell dysfunction in diabetes. The early growth response-1 (EGR-1) transcription factor, which regulates genes such as PDX-1 when mutated, can impair β-cell function and cause hyperglycemia. A disintegrin and metalloproteinases (ADAMs) are proteins involved in cell signaling by shedding molecules such as cytokines and growth factors, and a disintegrin and metalloprotease 17 (ADAM17) is closely linked to obesity, inflammation, and diabetes through its substrate, TNF-α. SIRT1 mediates glucose and lipid metabolism by exerting deacetylase activity on various substrates. In pancreatic β-cells, SIRT1 enhances insulin secretion while offering protection against oxidative stress and inflammation, thus positively influencing metabolic pathways and insulin signaling.

Over 800 medicinal plants worldwide, such as , , , and , are known to contain bioactive compounds like alkaloids, flavonoids, and terpenoids with the potential to enhance insulin secretion, improve insulin sensitivity, and regulate blood glucose levels. These plants offer promising prospects for safer and cost-effective diabetes management and highlight the importance of continued research for innovative therapies. ,

In our laboratory, the data of an earlier study confirmed the structure of the compound as 2-amino-6,7,8-trimethoxy-4a,5,6,7,8,9-hexahydro-2H-benzoannulen-3-yl 3,4,5-trimethoxybenzoate, identifying it as a novel natural product. The compound was named “distachionate (DST)” in reference to the species , a shrub characterized by its purple, green, and white leaves, belonging to the genus within the Phyllanthaceae family. DST, with the chemical formula C24H33NO8 and a molecular weight of 463.22, was obtained as a yellowish powder. Notably, has been recognized for its therapeutic potential, particularly in the management of oxidative stress and inflammation.

Nanotechnology, involving the modification of substances at the nanoscale, has diverse applications in medicine, chemistry, and other fields. Nanoparticles, with at least one dimension less than 100 nm, are utilized in various applications due to their unique properties. Zinc oxide nanoparticles (ZnO NPs), known for their biocompatibility and nontoxicity, are used in drug delivery, biological labeling, and therapeutic agents for diverse diseases including diabetes treatments. Chitosan (CS), a biopolymer derived from chitin, has strong antidiabetic and antimicrobial properties. It interacts with DNA, inhibits RNA production, and affects glucose metabolism, making it a valuable component in drug delivery systems.

Pharmacological treatments for diabetes include agents designed to enhance insulin sensitivity, promote insulin secretion, and improve glucose uptake; however, their prolonged use is associated with varied adverse effects. This has spurred interest in alternative therapies, particularly herbal remedies or phyto-based compounds, which have been used in chronic ailments for centuries with better efficacy and fewer side effects. Despite the promising antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties of DST derived from , its potential in diabetes management remains underexplored yet; this study has been designed to assess the antidiabetic potential of DST and DST-loaded ZnO nanoparticles embedded in chitosan (DSTNC) in HF + STZ-fed rats. Further, the effect of the administration of DST and DSTNC was explored on the candidate genes of diabetes development and its progression including GLUT-4, ADAM17, SIRT1, JNK, and PDX-1, thus highlighting the effect of treatment on glucose and lipid metabolism and beta cell function.

2. Results

2.12.1. Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopic Analysis of Distachionate-Loaded ZnO Nanoparticles Embedded in Chitosan (DSTNC)

The functional groups in DSTNC were identified using FTIR spectroscopy, and the results are displayed in Figure . The spectrum exhibited several distinct peaks corresponding to various functional groups attached to or interacting with the DSTNC. The FTIR spectrum for CS was observed at 3439 cm–1, which is attributed to the N–H stretching vibrations of amine (NH2) groups, suggesting the presence of amino functionalities, possibly from residual precursors or capping agents. The peak at 2900 cm–1 corresponds to C–H stretching, indicating the existence of alkyl chains on the nanoparticle surface. The band at 1625 cm–1 is assigned to N–H bending vibrations, further confirming the presence of amine groups. Additionally, prominent peaks at 1508 cm–1 and 1274 cm–1 correspond to CO stretching and bending vibrations, respectively, indicative of carbonyl-containing compounds such as carboxyl or ester groups in the CS. A distinct peak at 1365 cm–1 is associated with OCH3 bending vibrations, pointing toward the presence of methoxy groups in DST. The absorption band at 1129 cm–1 is assigned to O–H bending vibrations, suggesting the presence of hydroxyl groups, which may arise from surface hydroxylation or adsorbed moisture. Critically, a strong and sharp peak observed at 600 cm–1 is characteristic of the Zn–O bond stretching vibrations, confirming the successful formation of the DSTNC.

These results collectively indicate that the synthesized DSTNC possesses various surface functional groups, including amine, hydroxyl, carbonyl, and methoxy groups, likely due to residual reactants or stabilizing agents used during synthesis. The presence of these functional groups may play a significant role in enhancing the stability and potential biofunctionalization of DSTNC.

2.22.2. X-ray Diffraction Analysis of Distachionate-Loaded ZnO Nanoparticles Embedded in Chitosan (DSTNC)

The XRD patterns of DSTNC displayed multiple Bragg reflection peaks corresponding to ZnO, CS, and DST, as shown in Figure . Distinct diffraction peaks were observed at 2θ angles of 31.8° and 34.5°, corresponding to the (100) and (002) planes of the hexagonal ZnO crystal structure, in agreement with the standard ZnO pattern. A characteristic peak for CS appeared at 2θ = 19.8° and 26° in the DSTNC composite, which demonstrates the semicrystalline structure of CS. Additionally, DST contains OCH3 and COOH groups that have characteristic peaks a 2θ angles of 47.5° and 10°, respectively.

2.3. Particle Size Distribution and Stability Analysis of Distachionate-Loaded ZnO Nanoparticles Embedded in Chitosan (DSTNC)

DSTNC exhibited particle sizes of 158.1 nm and 1778 dnm Z-average sizes with a 1 polydispersity index (PDI) (Figure a). The surface charge and stability of the DSTNC were evaluated using zeta potential measurements. The zeta potential distribution is shown in Figure b. The recorded zeta potential value was −7.17 mV, with a standard deviation of 6.96 mV, indicating a moderately stable colloidal suspension. The distribution profile revealed two distinct peaks. The major peak was observed at −9.28 mV, accounting for 81.4% of the total area, suggesting that the majority of the nanoparticles carry a negative surface charge. A minor peak appeared at +4.38 mV, representing 18.6%, which might be attributed to a small population of differently charged or agglomerated particles. The negative zeta potential reflects the presence of negatively charged functional groups on the nanoparticle surface, which is consistent with the FTIR findings that indicated surface hydroxyl and amine groups. Although a zeta potential value beyond ±30 mV is typically considered ideal for high colloidal stability, the observed value indicates an acceptable dispersion with a tendency for mild aggregation over time.

2.4. Atomic Force Microscopy Analysis of Distachionate-Loaded ZnO Nanoparticles Embedded in Chitosan (DSTNC)

Atomic force microscopy (AFM) generates high-resolution images of the three-dimensional topology of a sample surface. 2.5. Acute Toxicity Testing of Distachionate and Distachionate-Loaded ZnO Nanoparticles Embedded in Chitosan (DSTNC)

At a maximum dose of 2000 mg kg–1 of DST and DSTNC, animals revealed the absence of toxic effects. All the animals remained alive without any impaired activity, disability, or death.

3. Discussion

Diabetes mellitus is a long-term endocrine disorder representing sustained hyperglycemia, which disrupts the metabolism of carbohydrates, proteins, and fat. Various oral antidiabetic agents, in conjunction with insulin, are used to manage the disease. Sulfonylureas, such as glibenclamide, lower blood glucose by encouraging pancreatic β-cells to secrete additional insulin, decreasing hepatic insulin clearance and suppressing glucagon secretion. They also inhibit hepatic gluconeogenesis.

As established an in internationally recognized study and in our lab, the HF + STZ model effectively simulates type 2 diabetes by replicating insulin resistance, β-cell dysfunction, oxidative stress, and inflammation. HF consumption promotes insulin resistance through hepatic lipogenesis, oxidative stress, and systemic inflammation, disrupting insulin receptor signaling pathways. This prediabetic condition is worsened by low-dose STZ, which targets pancreatic β-cells via GLUT2 transporters, causing partial β-cell destruction. This model mimics human type 2 diabetes with moderate hyperglycemia, insulin resistance, and residual insulin secretion. , Recent research validates this model’s utility.

DST, isolated from , showed significant α-glucosidase and α-amylase inhibitory activities, which are critical in controlling hyperglycemia. Studies have also indicated that methanolic extract (aerial parts) of shows significant cytotoxic, broncho relaxant, spasmolytic, antidiabetic, and anti-Alzheimer’s activity.

Coating metal oxide nanoparticles with the biopolymer CS has become a key focus in the field of polymer nanotechnology. The surface coating of CS on DST-loaded ZnO nanoparticles was done and characterized through XRD, FTIR, DLS, and zeta potential analysis. Similarly, the characterization of the intelligent drugs was validated using spectral techniques, such as FTIR, XRD, and DLS, along with morphological analyses, including AFM. The FTIR study showed peaks detected at 3439 cm–1, 2900 cm–1, 1625 cm–1, 1508 cm–1, 1365 cm–1, 1274 cm–1, 1129 cm–1, and 600 cm–1, confirming the successful synthesis of functionalized DSTNC. The XRD profile of DSTNC was observed at 2θ angles of 31.8°, 34.5°, 19.8°, 26°, 47.5°, and 10°, which showed the semicrystalline structure of CS and hexagonal structure of ZnO, corresponding to the confirmation of ZnO, CS, and DST successful formation. Differential light scattering measurements indicated that the DSTNC had a size of 158.1 nm. Additionally, AFM was employed to capture high-resolution images of the DSTNC that demonstrate three-dimensional surface topology, hydrophobicity, and the nanometer scale.

The current study was designed to evaluate the therapeutic potential of DST and its nanocomposite, DSTNC, in ameliorating the metabolic disturbances associated with HF + STZ-induced diabetes in rats. The findings from this study have highlighted the significant impact of both DST and DSTNC in reducing hyperglycemia, improving insulin sensitivity, and restoring the balance in lipid metabolism and inflammatory biomarkers. Additionally, these compounds demonstrated pronounced antioxidant effects and mitigated oxidative stress with histopathological evidence supporting their protective role in preventing organ damage.

There is a common belief that natural herbal medicines and naturally derived compounds are relatively safe compared to purely synthetic-origin compounds. The acute toxicity study revealed that DST and DSTNC were well-tolerated at doses as high as 2000 mg kg–1, with no observable signs of toxicity, disability, or mortality. This confirms the safety profile of DST and DSTNC, which is essential for the translation of these findings into the potential for clinical applications.

Administration of HF combined with STZ for 42 days led to glucose dysregulation, insulin insensitivity, β-cell damage, and changes in inflammatory mediators (IL-6 and TNF-α), markers of oxidative damage (SOD, CAT, and MDA), adipokines (chemerin, leptin, and adiponectin), and key genetic markers for diabetes (JNK, SIRT-1, GLUT-4, ADAM-17, and PDX1). Administration of DST and DSTNC starting on the 42nd day of the induction period led to decreased weight gain and food and fluid intake in HF + STZ-administered rats compared to normoglycemic controls. Both insulin and leptin regulate the long-term energy balance in the central nervous system. Since HF does not trigger insulin secretion from pancreatic β cells, consuming HF results in lower postmeal insulin levels compared to glucose. As leptin production is controlled by the insulin response, HF consumption also lowers circulating leptin levels. This combined reduction in insulin and leptin in individuals consuming HF diets may increase the risk of weight gain and related metabolic issues. The DC group exhibited a significant body weight gain over the duration of the study, a common manifestation of metabolic disturbances in diabetes. Notably, treatment with DST and DSTNC resulted in a marked (p < 0.001) decrease in body weight, comparable with the effects of the standard antidiabetic treatment, metformin. Moreover, DST and DSTNC significantly reduced food and fluid intake in diabetic rats, suggesting progress in satiety and possibly better glycemic control, similar to the effects seen on the part of metformin.

HF intake leads to hyperlipidemia. A substantial increase in hepatic de novo lipogenesis is a key factor contributing to the metabolic burden associated with HF consumption. Consequently, elevated plasma free fatty acids (FFAs), TG, and vLDL-TG levels result in hyperlipidemia and promote TG accumulation in extrahepatic tissues and organs. Additionally, excessive FFAs impair pancreatic function, thus hindering insulin production needed to counteract hyperglycemia. Elevated FFAs directly activate macrophages, prompting the excretion of inflammatory cytokines, with TNF-α playing a key role in stimulating other cytokines. This inflammatory response contributes to insulin resistance in peripheral tissues. TNF-α, produced by monocytes and macrophages, contributes to peripheral insulin resistance and insulin secretion. Research has established a direct link between TNF-α and type 2 diabetes. IL-6, mainly released by the visceral adipose tissue, affects glucose metabolism. While IL-6 may help regulate glucose in healthy individuals, it likely worsens inflammation and insulin resistance in obesity, highlighting its key role in diabetes development. , In the current investigation, diabetic rats exhibited elevated levels of inflammatory biomarkers, for instance, IL-6 and TNF-α. Treatment with DST and DSTNC caused a significant (p < 0.001) reduction in the levels of these inflammatory markers, suggesting that these compounds possess prominent anti-inflammatory properties.

One of the hallmarks of diabetes is dyslipidemia, characterized by impaired levels of TC, LDL, and TG, and decreased HDL levels. Insulin deficiency or resistance results in elevated FFA accumulation in the liver, which converts them into TGs, causing higher vLDL production. , This increase in vLDL and TG levels activates lecithin acyl-cholesterol transferase and lipoprotein lipase, reducing HDL and increasing LDL levels. Excessive fructose consumption promotes endogenous lipogenesis, bypassing glycolysis regulation and enhancing lipogenic substrates, leading to TG synthesis, liver fat accumulation, insulin resistance, and vLDL production. Lipids are crucial in the development of complications associated with diabetes mellitus. The current research revealed that both DST and DSTNC markedly reduced TG, TC, and LDL levels while causing an increase in HDL levels in diabetic rats. The improvements in the lipid profile observed with DST and DSTNC were comparable to those seen with metformin, underscoring their potential in managing diabetic dyslipidemia.

Serum metabolic indicators, such as ALT, creatinine, AST, and urea, were markedly elevated in DC animals, reflecting liver and kidney dysfunction, common complications in diabetes. However, DST and DSTNC treatments resulted in significant (p < 0.001) reductions in these parameters, suggesting that these compounds have protective effects on both liver and kidney function. Renal damage is linked to abnormal urea and creatinine levels, often caused by the excessive generation of ROS in diabetes, which leads to renal impairment. In the current investigation, treatment with DST and DSTNC markedly restored serum creatinine and urea levels to normal levels, likely by attenuating ROS levels in the body.

Glycemic control, as indicated by blood glucose, insulin, and HbA1c levels, was markedly improved in DST- and DSTNC-treated rats. Both compounds, particularly at higher doses, significantly (p < 0.001) decreased levels of HbA1c and blood glucose while elevating levels of insulin, indicating improved pancreatic function and enhanced insulin sensitivity. Elevated HbA1c levels are also attributed to the failure of pancreatic beta cells.

Adipose tissue, a vital form of connective tissue, is essential for managing insulin sensitivity and energy homeostasis. Its main role is to store surplus nutrients as TGs and release FFAs during periods of fasting. Adipose tissue also secretes various endocrine and signaling factors referred to as adipocytokines. Leptin, which is directly linked to TG levels in adipose tissue, controls hunger and regulates food intake and body weight. Adiponectin provides hypoglycemic, antiatherogenic, and inflammation-modulating benefits. Chemerin, a recently identified adipokine, has endocrine, paracrine, and autocrine functions, influencing glucose and lipid regulation, immune response, angiogenesis, inflammation, and blood pressure regulation. In this study, the reduction in adipocytokines, including chemerin and leptin, and the restoration of adiponectin levels further support the anti-inflammatory and insulin-sensitizing effects of DST and DSTNC.

Oxidative stress, resulting from an imbalance between antioxidants and ROS, plays a significant role in diabetes, particularly in vascular complications. High blood glucose levels trigger ROS production, damaging lipids, proteins, and DNA and leading to beta-cell dysfunction and diabetic complications like retinopathy and nephropathy. Key enzymes with antioxidant properties, like SOD and CAT, are often reduced in diabetes, worsening oxidative damage and contributing to disease progression. Lipid peroxidation and biomarkers such as MDA further reflect this oxidative damage. In this study, administration of DST and DSTNC significantly reduced oxidative stress and enhanced the activity of antioxidant enzymes in the treatment groups compared with the HF-fed diabetic rats. Both compounds increased antioxidant enzyme levels (CAT and SOD) and decreased MDA levels in various tissues, indicating enhanced antioxidant defenses.

Diabetes is associated with tissue-specific damage, with liver histological changes ranging from hepatic steatosis to steatohepatitis and hepatic fibrosis. It markedly raises the possibility of mortality as a result of the frequent development of nephropathy, which may evolve into chronic kidney failure, often leading to dialysis or kidney transplantation. Histological analysis further confirmed the protective effects of DST and DSTNC on organ tissues. In diabetic rats, HF + STZ exposure caused structural damage to the pancreas, liver, kidney, heart, and aorta, as evidenced by disorganized tissue architecture, inflammation, and lipid accumulation. However, DST and DSTNC treatments resulted in significant improvements in tissue structure including the regeneration of pancreatic β-cells, reduced hepatocyte damage, normalized kidney function, and improved heart and aorta tissue integrity.

In this study, the effects of DST and DSTNC on the expression of key genes involved in diabetes were investigated using qRT-PCR. Molecularly, the HF + STZ model exhibits upregulation of ADAM17 and JNK with downregulation of SIRT1, PDX-1, and GLUT-4. ADAM17, a metalloproteinase that cleaves membrane-bound TNF-α, enhances cytokine release, worsening inflammation and contributing to insulin resistance. JNK links oxidative stress to insulin resistance by disrupting insulin signaling and promoting β-cell apoptosis. SIRT1 regulates mitochondrial function, antioxidant defense, and insulin sensitivity; its suppression exacerbates oxidative damage and impairs insulin signaling. PDX-1, crucial for insulin gene transcription, is downregulated in STZ-induced injury, reducing insulin synthesis. GLUT-4 downregulation in muscle and adipose tissues leads to a reduced glucose uptake and hyperglycemia. This evidence collectively supports the model’s relevance in replicating human diabetes-associated metabolic and inflammatory disturbances (Figure ). Notably, DSTNC exhibited a regulatory effect stronger than that of DST, particularly in enhancing the expression of SIRT1, PDX-1, and GLUT-4 and suppressing ADAM17 and JNK at higher doses. This suggests that nanoparticle-based delivery enhances the antidiabetic properties of DST, providing a more potent therapeutic strategy for modulating key pathways involved in diabetes. These results support the potential of DSTNC as an improved formulation for diabetes management by targeting molecular pathways associated with insulin sensitivity and glucose regulation.

Taken together, this study implies that DST and DSTNC offer substantial therapeutic potential in the regulation of diabetes and its complications. Their ability to improve glycemic control, enhance lipid metabolism, reduce inflammation and oxidative stress, and protect against organ damage thus highlights their promising potential for further exploration in the development of new antidiabetic drug leads. Future research is deemed to clarify the molecular mechanisms behind these effects and to assess the long-term safety and effectiveness of DST and DSTNC in clinical settings.

4. Conclusion

These findings show that DST and DST nanocomposites possess antidiabetic properties possibly mediated via modulation of the glycemic indicator (glucose, serum insulin, and HbA1c), inflammatory mediator (IL-6 and TNF-α), oxidative stress marker (SOD, CAT, and MDA), and adipocytokine (chemerin, leptin, and adiponectin) levels. Further, the administration of DST and DST nanocomposites displayed improvement in insulin signaling, glucose and lipid metabolism, and inflammation by causing upregulation of the mRNA expression of GLUT-4, PDX-1, and SIRT1 and downregulation of the mRNA expression of JNK and ADAM17. Thus, this study provides a scientific basis for the therapeutic potential of DST and DST nanocomposites in diabetes.

Stock solutions of DST and DSTNC were prepared in 1% DMSO (v/v) and 1% Tween-80 (w/v).

5.2. Animal Model

Fifty-four Wistar rats weighing 180–220 g and aged 5–6 weeks were procured and housed in a ventilated animal facility at the Department of Pharmaceutical Sciences, Government College University, Faisalabad (GCUF). They were maintained at ambient temperature under a 12 h light/dark cycle and habituated for 7 days with ad libitum food and water. Diabetes was induced by administering 20% high-fructose drinking water for 6 weeks. The GCUF Ethical Review Committee provided ethical clearance for this study (ref. No. GCUF/ERC/50).

5.3. Experimental Procedure for ZnO Nanoparticle Synthesis

ZnO NPs were synthesized by using the chemical precipitation method. Initially, a 1 M solution of zinc sulfate heptahydrate was stirred vigorously with the help of a magnetic stirrer. Subsequently, a 2 M sodium hydroxide solution was added gradually, dropwise, to the mixture. Continuous stirring was maintained for 18 h, resulting in the formation of a substantial white precipitate. The precipitate was separated via filtration followed by thorough washing with distilled water and ethanol to eliminate any impurities. It was then dried in a hot air oven at 100 °C for 8 h. Finally, the dried material was ground into a fine powder using a mortar and pestle.

5.4. Development of Distachionate-Loaded ZnO Nanoparticles

To begin, 30 mg of DST was dissolved in either 150 μL of absolute ethanol or 1N NaOH. This solution was then diluted with 10 mL of an ethanol–water mixture (50:50, v/v) at room temperature. Subsequently, 30 mg of ZnO NPs was added to the hormone solution, followed by stirring at 700 rpm for varying durations of 30, 60, 120, 180, and 240 min to facilitate hormone loading. After stirring, the mixtures were left to stand undisturbed for 24 h. The resulting suspensions were centrifuged at 10,000 rpm for 10 min to collect the DST-loaded ZnO nanoparticles. The collected nanoparticles were then placed into Eppendorf tubes, frozen at −20 °C, and subsequently freeze-dried using a lyophilizer (Christ, Germany) prior to further use.

5.5. Development of Distachionate–ZnO Nanoparticles Embedded in a Chitosan Matrix (DSTNC)

A 0.25 g sample of CS was suspended in 25 mL of 5% (v/v) acetic acid and stirred intensively for 30 min. In a separate step, 5 mg of synthesized DSTNC was ultrasonically treated in distilled water for 30 min before being introduced into the CS solution and stirred at 80 °C for 1 h. Dropwise addition of a freshly prepared 1 M NaOH solution was carried out until complete precipitation occurred. The precipitate was centrifuged, washed with deionized water and ethanol, and dried at 120 °C for 2 h.

5.65.6. Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy

FTIR spectroscopy was used to identify the functional groups present in the synthesized nanoparticles. The FTIR spectra were recorded using a JASCO FTIR-6600 spectrometer within the range of 500–4000 cm–1. The nanoparticle samples, prepared in powder form, were analyzed directly in ATR mode.

5.75.7. X-ray Diffraction

The structural and phase properties of the synthesized materials were examined by using the XRD technique. The measurements were performed with a Bruker D8 Advance diffractometer, operating at a scan rate of 1.2° per minute over a 2θ range of 10° to 80°. Copper Kα radiation (λ = 1.54056 Å) was utilized as the X-ray source, generated at 40 kV and 40 mA. The average particle size was estimated by applying Scherer’s equation.

5.8. Dynamic Light Scattering

The particle size of the nanoparticles was determined using a 90Plus Particle Size Analyzer (Brookhaven Instruments Corp., NY, USA) based on photon correlation spectroscopy, also known as dynamic light scattering. Prior to measurement, each sample was diluted with distilled water to ensure the appropriate scattering intensity. The analysis was performed at 25 °C, with the mean particle diameter and PDI calculated from ten measurements taken at a 90° scattering angle.

5.9. Zeta Potential

The dispersion stability of the nanoparticles was assessed by measuring their zeta potential using a Zetasizer Nano ZS instrument (Malvern, the Netherlands). The analysis was conducted at 25 °C, with each measurement performed in triplicate.

5.10. Atomic Force Microscopy

The surface roughness of the sample was analyzed using an atomic force microscope (Park Systems Korea, NX10).

5.11. Experiment Design

A total of 54 rats of either sex were divided into 9 equal groups, each group containing 6 rats. Group 1: nondiabetic negative control (NC); Group 2: untreated DC; Group 3: diabetic rats treated with STD (metformin, 200 mg kg–1); Group 4: diabetic animals with DST 5 mg kg–1; Group 5: diabetic animals treated with DST 10 mg kg–1; Group 6: diabetic animals treated with DST 15 mg kg–1; Group 7: DSTNC 5 mg kg–1; Group 8: DSTNC 10 mg kg–1; and Group 9: DSTNC 15 mg kg–1. The standard diet consisted of a total of ∼90.72 kg: flour, 34.90 kg; chokar, 34.90 kg; molasses, 1.05 kg; salt, 0.52 kg; Metavit-Super L, 0.23 kg; potassium metabisulfite, 0.10 kg; oil, 3.49 kg; fish meal, 15.71 kg; and powdered milk, 13.96 kg. All the groups except group 1 were treated with 20% HF in drinking water for 6 weeks. According to the protocol, except for group 1, all other groups received a single modest dosage of STZ (35 mg kg–1, i.p.) at the end of the fifth week. In 0.1 M citrate buffer, STZ was dissolved (pH 4). When diabetes was established after 6 weeks, treatment with DST and DSTNC was given orally while being dissolved in deionized water for 6 weeks.

5.12. Acute Toxicity Studies

Acute toxicity was assessed through an in vivo evaluation, with a single intake of 1000 mg kg–1 and 2000 mg kg–1 doses orally, as per the OECD 425 guidelines. The rats were monitored over a period of 24 h for signs of toxicity, including agitation, lacrimation, changes in general behavior, respiration, and mortality.

5.13. Assessment of Weight Changes, Food Intake, and Fluid Consumption

Throughout the experimental duration, the rise in weight of each group’s rats was measured using an electronic weighing machine (Sartorius-Power, USA) to evaluate the influence of the HF in water and STZ. Each rat’s daily food consumption was quantified across all groups to assess the diet’s effects. Drinking water was supplied in calibrated bottles for measurement of daily consumption.

5.14. Biochemical Analysis

Upon completion of the 12 week experiment, animals underwent an 18 h fasting period before being euthanized under deep anesthesia achieved through inhalation of isoflurane (5%–10% v/wt) within a controlled environment. Blood samples for HbA1c and glucose measurements were collected via a cardiac puncture using EDTA tubes containing heparin and sodium citrate. Tissues were obtained through dissection, rinsed in an ice-cooled saline solution, and mixed thoroughly in phosphate buffer (0.1 M, pH 7.5). The resulting homogenate samples were spun at 3000 rpm for 10 min to collect the supernatants. Serum was separated from blood samples by centrifuging at 4000 rpm for 10 min. All serum and tissue homogenates were kept at −80 °C for subsequent biochemical analyses.

5.20. Evaluation of Antioxidant Activity

The free radical scavenging activity of DST and DSTNC was measured by DPPH, FRAP, and ABTS methods. A widely accepted method for evaluating free radical scavenging activity, the DPPH assay, is frequently used in antioxidant research. In this method, a stock solution of DPPH is prepared by dissolving 4 mg of DPPH in 100 mL of methanol. The DPPH solution is then mixed with different concentrations of the DST and DSTNC (5, 10, and 15 mg kg–1), and the mixture is agitated and stored at room temperature for 60 min. The absorbance of the solution is measured at 517 nm using a UV spectrophotometer. A negative control consisting of DPPH and methanol is used for comparison. The percentage of inhibition or scavenging activity is calculated by the formula

scavengingeffect(%)=[(AC−AS)/AC]×100

where AC is the absorbance of the negative control and AS is the absorbance of the sample. GraphPad Prism version 8.01 (GraphPad Software, San Diego, CA, USA) was used to determine IC50 values, which indicated the concentration needed to inhibit 50% of the DPPH radicals.

The ABTS assay was conducted as per the protocol outlined by Böhm et al., with absorbance measured at 734 nm. The FRAP assay followed the method defined by Yuan et al., and absorbance was recorded at 700 nm.

5.21. Histopathological Examination

Histopathological examination was conducted by preserving the liver, kidneys, aorta, pancreas, and heart of rats in 10% formalin. The organs were dyed with eosin and hematoxylin, sectioned using a microtome (Leica, Germany), and examined under a light microscope (ACCU-SCOPE 3001-LED Digital Microscope, USA).
