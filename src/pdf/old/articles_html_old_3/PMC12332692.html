Abstract

The pore-expanding modification of zeolites is an important means to improve their catalytic performance. This study demonstrates the postsynthesis of Ti-Sil-BEA zeolites via silanization-modified parent BEA zeolite and its application in catalyzing epoxidation reactions of dicyclopentadiene (DCPD). The zeolites were characterized by BET, X-ray diffraction, scanning electron microscopy, transmission electron microscopy, UV–vis spectroscopy, and X-ray electron spectroscopy. The impact of silanizing reagents on the crystallization of BEA zeolites as well as pore-expanding was studied. It is revealed that the addition of N-[3-(Trimethoxysilyl)­propyl]­ethylenediamine (TSPED) before crystallization of *BEA-type zeolites can effectively introduce mesoporous structures without impacting the integrity of the zeolite crystals (resulting in an augmentation of the mesopore volume by 55% and emergence of mesopores with a diameter of 11 nm). Furthermore, the introduction of TSPED led to the reduction of zeolite size, and the change of the mesoporous structure of Ti-Sil-BEA improved its catalytic performance for the DCPD epoxidation reaction; the DCPD conversion was increased by 11.5%. The dicyclopentadiene dioxide (DCPDDO) selectivity was increased by 52.5%.

1. Introduction

Dicyclopentadiene dioxide (DCPDDO) has been identified as a potential additive for the synthesis of specialty epoxy resins. The addition of DCPDDO has shown enhancement in the light resistance, heat resistance, electrical insulation, and hardness of epoxy resins. , DCPDDO is synthesized through the epoxidation of dicyclopentadiene (DCPD), which can be isolated from the C5 products of petroleum cracking. Currently, the primary industrial method for producing DCPDDO involves the oxidation of peracetic acid. However, this method suffers a safety risk due to the unstable nature of peracetic acid. Additionally, the use of sulfuric acid as a catalyst is associated with corrosion of equipment and environmental pollution. Therefore, it is necessary to modify the current production process to enable the production of DCPDDO more environmentally friendly and efficiently.

Epoxidation of olefins via hydrogen peroxide catalyzed by titanium-containing heteroatom zeolite is regarded as a clean and efficient process. , Titanium-containing heteroatom zeolites such as TS-1, Ti-BEA, and Ti-MOR are an important branch of molecular sieve materials and have been widely used in catalytic oxidation reactions. They can be employed to produce a variety of high-value-added fine chemicals. , Among them, TS-1 zeolite is the earliest prepared and applied titanium-containing heteroatom zeolite industrially in the world. It possesses an MFI-type topology (10-membered ring, [100] 0.51 × 0.55 nm ↔ [010] 0.53 × 0.56 nm). This relatively small pore structure is suitable for oxidation reactions involving small molecules, such as hydroxylation of aromatic hydrocarbons, epoxidation of propylene, and ammoximation of cyclohexanone. − However, the molecular size of DCPD is relatively large, making it difficult for DCPD to diffuse into the pores of the TS-1 zeolite. To improve the diffusion efficiency of DCPD as well as its products, a zeolite catalyst with larger pore structures is a better choice. For example, Ti-BEA (*BEA topology) zeolite, with its three-dimensional 12-membered ring pore structure ([100] 0.66 × 0.67 nm ↔ [001] 0.56 × 0.56 nm), is often used to catalyze the epoxidation of large-molecule olefins. −

To further enhance the catalytic performance of zeolites, various modification methods have been explored for molecular sieves. These approaches primarily include creating hierarchical structures within zeolite crystals, nanocrystallization, adjusting the hydrophilicity and hydrophobicity of the pore system, modulating the microenvironment of titanium active sites, and introducing hierarchical pore structures. ,− Among these, the introduction of abundant mesoporous structures into zeolite frameworks is an effective strategy to improve the accessibility of reactants to active sites and further enhance the diffusion efficiency of the products. For instance, Pech reviewed the performance comparison of conventional titanosilicate zeolites, hierarchical titanosilicate zeolites, lamellar titanosilicate zeolites, and mesoporous titanosilicates. It appeared that the best catalytic performance is achieved when the pore size of a catalyst and the molecular size of the substrate suit. Steven L. Suib reviewed the structural characteristics of preparation methods of different mesoporous materials. Cross-linking many functional groups (such as amines, strongly acidic ionic liquids, sulfonates, triphenylphosphine, pyridine, sulfonates, and similar substances) to porous polymers can effectively improve the activity of catalysts. Seungyeop Lee et al. utilized a dual-phase surfactant during the crystallization process to synthesize hierarchical MFI, MTW, *MRE, and *BEA zeolites with sponge-like morphologies. This method successfully introduced mesoporous structures into the zeolites, significantly increasing their specific surface area and pore volume and greatly improving their adsorption efficiency for neohexane. Natalia Suárez et al. employed HF/NH4F solution to etch and expand the mesopores of BEA zeolite. While the modified zeolite retained a high relative crystallinity, its mesoporous volume increased by 80%. However, the presence of F– in the modification system posed challenges for subsequent processing and environmental concerns. Heqin Yang et al. prepared hierarchical BEA zeolite using a dry gel conversion method, which resulted in the formation of distinct new mesoporous structures and reduced the size of zeolite crystallites. However, this method led to a decrease in the crystallinity of the hierarchical BEA zeolite and the formation of amorphous Al. Another effective approach involves adding pore-expanding agents during zeolite crystallization. For example, María del Mar Alonso-Doncel et al. prepared dendritic ZSM-5 zeolite by adding long-chain organosilanes (C14, C18) to ZSM-5. Dendritic ZSM-5 zeolite has an abundant mesoporous structure as well as balanced Brønsted/Lewis acidity. Another example is the addition of N,N-Diethyl-3-(trimethoxysilyl)­propan-1-amine during the crystallization of TS-1 zeolite yielded TS-SP zeolite with a sponge-like morphology. This method significantly increased the volume of mesopores in the TS-1 zeolite and enhanced its catalytic performance in the epoxidation of methyl oleate. The modification process using a silanization reagent needed to be introduced into the homogeneous titanium–silicon sol system prior to the hydrothermal crystallization of zeolites. However, the direct hydrothermal synthesis of Ti-BEA zeolite required the introduction of HF/NaF as mineralizing agents. , These mineralizing agents were subsequently removed through calcination, a process that led to the release of fluorine into the atmosphere and resulted in environmental pollution. For Ti-BEA zeolite, it is not advisable to employ the same modification method as that used for TS-1 zeolite. Therefore, it is necessary to develop a new pore-expansion method for Ti-BEA zeolite to achieve the preparation environmental friendly.

In this study, to achieve efficient epoxidation of DCPD, we designed and synthesized a Ti-Sil-BEA zeolite with abundant mesoporous structures. To accomplish this, first we synthesized Al-BEA zeolite by employing a silanization reagent during the hydrothermal crystallization process. The silanization reagent acted as a pore-directing agent and led to the formation of Al-Sil-BEA with wide mesoporous dimensions. Subsequently, Ti-Sil-BEA was prepared using a solid–liquid postsynthesis titanium insertion method, and its physicochemical properties were analyzed. The introduction of different silanization reagents had varying effects on the crystallization process of the zeolite, leading to the formation of new mesoporous structures with different sizes and dimensions, as well as changes in the size of zeolite crystallites. Ultimately, the most suitable silanization reagent for pore expansion was identified. These findings provide insights and understanding into zeolite pore-expansion methods and have positive implications for improving the efficiency of epoxidation reactions involving large-molecule olefins.

2. Experimental Section

2.1. Materials and Reagents

Tetraethyl orthosilicate (TEOS, 99%, Beijing InnoChem Science & Technology Co., Ltd.), Tetrabutyl titanate (TEOT, 99%, Beijing InnoChem Science & Technology Co., Ltd.), Tetraethylammonium hydroxide (TEAOH, 35%, Beijing InnoChem Science & Technology Co., Ltd.), Sodium hydroxide (NaOH, 96%, Shanghai Aladdin Biochemical Technology Co., Ltd.), Aluminum hydroxide (Al­(OH)3, 99%, Shanghai Aladdin Biochemical Technology Co., Ltd.), Nitric acid (HNO3, 65%, Tianjin Damao Chemical Reagent Partnership Enterprise (Limited Partnership)), Titanium tetrachloride (TiCl4, 99%, Beijing InnoChem Science & Technology Co., Ltd.), Ethyl alcohol (EtOH, 99.9%, Beijing InnoChem Science & Technology Co., Ltd.), Dimethyldichlorosilane (DCDMS, 98%, Beijing InnoChem Science & Technology Co., Ltd.), Methyltrimethoxy-silane (TMMOS, 98%, Shanghai Aladdin Biochemical Technology Co., Ltd.), N-Methylaminopropyltrimethoxysilane (TMMPPS, 98%, Shanghai Aladdin Biochemical Technology Co., Ltd.), [3-(N,N-Dimethylamino)­propyl]­trimethoxysilane (DMPTS, 96%, TCI (Shanghai) Development Co., Ltd.), N-[3-(trimethoxysilyl)­propyl]­ethylenediamine (TSPED, 95%, TCI (Shanghai) Development Co., Ltd.), 3-Mercaptopropyltriethoxysilane (MPTES, 98%, Beijing InnoChem Science & Technology Co., Ltd.), Bis­(trimethoxysilylpropyl)­amine (BTSPA, 98%), Dicyclopentadiene (DCPD, 97%, Beijing InnoChem Science & Technology Co., Ltd.), hydrogen peroxide (H2O2, 30%, Tianjin Damao Chemical Reagent Partnership Enterprise (Limited Partnership)), Tert-Butanol (TBA, 99%, Beijing InnoChem Science & Technology Co., Ltd.), and Ammonium chloride (NH4Cl, 99%, Shanghai Aladdin Biochemical Technology Co., Ltd.).

2.2. Catalyst Preparation

Preparation of solid silica gel: TEOS was utilized as the silica source, while TEAOH was employed as the templating agent and alkali source. The reagents, i.e., TEOS, TEAOH, and water, were added sequentially to a beaker. The molar ratio of Si to TEAOH was 1:0.15. Following thorough stirring at room temperature, the mixture was heated to 80 °C with continuous stirring to evaporate the alcohol and maintained for 3 h, and the liquid sol was obtained. It was then stirred at 55 °C until the sol solidified. Following this, the solid sol was dried in an oven at 80 °C for 12 h, resulting in the attainment of solid silica gel, characterized by a water–silica ratio of less than 1.

Preparation of sodium meta-aluminate solution: Sodium hydroxide, aluminum hydroxide, and water were added to a stainless steel container and placed on an induction cooker to achieve boiling and dissolution. Upon completion of the dissolution process, the solution was transferred to a measuring cylinder followed by the addition of boiled water to establish the desired volume. The sodium meta-aluminate solution obtained had a composition of n(NaOH): n(Al­(OH)3): n(H2O) = 1.875:1:20.

Hydrothermal synthesis of expanded pore Al-BEA molecular sieves: In the crystallization kettle, the previously prepared solid silica powder, ground to more than 40 mesh, sodium meta-aluminate solution, silanization reagent, and water were added sequentially. The material in the kettle exhibited a final composition of n(Si): n(Al): n(TEAOH): n(Na): n(silanization reagent): n(H2O) = 1:0.08:0.15:0.15:0.025:6. The kettle was sealed into a homogeneous reactor at a speed of 10 rpm, and the temperature was increased to 120 °C at a rate of 1 °C/min. The crystallization process was carried out for 72 h. Subsequently, the crystallization was carried out at a speed of 10 rpm, and then the temperature was increased to 145 °C at a rate of 1 °C/min. The crystallization kettle was hermetically sealed and transferred into a homogeneous reactor at a speed of 10 rpm. The temperature was then increased to 120 °C at a rate of 1 °C/min for 24 h and subsequently to 145 °C for a duration of 72 h. The crystallized slurry was subjected to filtration followed by washing and drying in an oven at 110 °C for a period of 4 h. Thereafter, the samples were roasted in a muffle furnace at 550 °C for a duration of 3 h. The silylated, pore-expanded Al-BEA zeolites thus obtained were labeled as Al-Sil-BEA-X (X representing the abbreviation of the introduced silanization reagent, including DCDMS, TMMOS, TMMPPS, DMPTS, TSPED, MPTES, and BTSPA).

Preparation of DeAl-BEA and DeAl-Sil-BEA-X: Al-BEA and Al-Sil-BEA-X zeolites were used as raw materials, prepared by the acid dealuminization method. Al-BEA was stirred and mixed with a 13 mol/L HNO3 solution at a mass ratio of 1:41 and stirred at 100 °C for 24 h to remove skeletal aluminum. Subsequently, the mixture was filtered and washed with deionized water until reaching a neutral pH. It was then dried at 110 °C for 4 h. The resulting solid was stirred with a 0.1 mol/L HNO3 solution (prepared by diluting 65 wt % nitric acid) in accordance with a mass ratio of 1:10. This was stirred at 80 °C for 2 h to remove nonskeletal aluminum. Subsequently, the mixture was filtered and washed until it reached a neutral pH with deionized water. It was then dried at 110 °C for 4 h and calcined at 550 °C for 3 h. This process yielded DeAl-BEA zeolites with nested hydroxyl defects.

Preparation of Ti-BEA and Ti-Sil-BEA-X: Ti-BEA and Ti-Sil-BEA-X zeolites were prepared by the liquid-phase post-insertion of titanium method. − TiCl4 was chosen as the titanium source, and anhydrous ethanol was used as the solvent, and the steps of preparation were as follows: First, ethanol and TiCl4 were combined and stirred for 20 min at 60 °C in order to produce Ti­(Cl)­4–n (OEt) n (0 < n < 4). Subsequently, DeAl-BEA and DeAl-Sil-BEA-X zeolites were added to a mixed solution of ethanol and TiCl4 (the molar ratio of TiCl4 to Si atoms in DeAl-BEA or DeAl-Sil-BEA-X was 0.02). The volumetric mass ratio of ethanol to DeAl-BEA or DeAl-Sil-BEA-X was 20, and the solution was stirred for 2 h at 60 °C. At the conclusion of the heating period, the solvent was removed via rotary evaporation (60 °C, −0.1 MPa), and the resulting material was dried for 4 h at 110 °C. Finally, Ti-BEA and Ti-Sil-BEA-X zeolites were obtained through calcination at 550 °C for 3 h.

2.3. Catalyst Performance

The epoxidation of DCPD is a multistep reaction. DCPD reacts with H2O2 to form two different monoepoxy dicyclopentadienes (1,2-DCPDMO and 5,6-DCPDMO) (Scheme a,b). Subsequently, 1,2-DCPDMO and 5,6-DCPDMO further react with H2O2 to produce DCPDDO (Scheme c,d). Experimental evaluation of the Ti-BEA zeolite-catalyzed DCPD epoxidation reaction was conducted by using a reflux condenser under atmospheric pressure. In a typical evaluation, 0.2 g of catalyst, 300 mmol of solvent, 15 mmol of DCPD, 37.5 mmol of H2O2, and 0.02 g of NH4Cl were added into a 100 mL round-bottomed flask in sequential. The reaction was carried out at 60 °C for 8 h. Subsequently, the solids were filtered, and the liquid-phase products were collected and analyzed by using gas chromatography (Agilent 7890 GC). 3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Effect of Different Silanization Reagents on the Crystallization of BEA Molecular Sieves

The structural and chemical characteristics of Al-BEA, in conjunction with the samples obtained through treatment with various silanization reagents, were subjected to XRD and XRF analysis. The XRD spectra are depicted in Figure . The calculated relative crystallinity and chemical composition are listed in Table . It is noteworthy that the samples of Al-Sil-BEA-DCDMS and Al-Sil-BEA-BTSPA exhibit no discernible characteristic diffraction peaks of *BEA topology. As demonstrated in Table , entries 2 and 8, the relative crystallinity of the samples is low. The predominant structures observed are amorphous SiO2 and Al2O3, in contrast to the other samples, which display clear characteristic peaks indicative of crystallization and exhibit high relative crystallinity. Among the samples examined, Al-Sil-BEA-TSPED exhibited the highest relative crystallinity (Table , entry 6). The introduction of TSPED had a minimal impact on the crystallization of Al-BEA zeolite. The addition of silanization reagents has been observed to enhance the Si:Al ratio of the crystallized zeolites. This finding suggests that the addition of silanization reagents influences the amount of aluminum present within the zeolite structure.

The addition of silanization reagents has been demonstrated to exert a substantial influence on the mesoporous structure of the zeolites. As illustrated in Figure a, the N2 adsorption and desorption curves of Al-BEA, along with those obtained from samples subjected to diverse silanization reagents, are presented. The calculated values of the specific surface area and pore volume for each sample are also given in Table . The Al-Sil-BEA-DCDMS and Al-Sil-BEA-BTSPA samples show hysteresis loops at p/p° > 0.8 because they present an amorphous structure. Their specific surface areas and pore volumes are significantly smaller than those of the parent Al-BEA (Table , entries 2 and 8). The adsorption/desorption curves of the Al-Sil-BEA-TMMOS and Al-Sil-BEA-TMMPPS samples are basically free of hysteresis loops (the calculated specific surface areas and pore volumes of the mesoporous structures also show a significant decrease (Table , entries 3 and 4)). The findings suggest that the addition of TMMOS and TMMPPS during zeolite crystallization impedes the formation of zeolite mesoporous structures. Conversely, Al-BEA, Al-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, and Al-Sil-BEA-MPTES, which possess more complete crystal structures, are classified as H4 type. Al-Sil-BEA-TSPED, on the other hand, is categorized as H3 type. , This finding suggests the presence of an irregular mesoporous structure. A significant increase in mesopore volumes is observed for all three (Table , entries 5–7). Among them, the pore volume of Al-Sil-BEA-TSPED increased the most (by about 55%, Table , entry 6). The pore size distributions of the eight Al-BEA zeolites are illustrated in Figure b. It is evident that a modest mesoporous structure, measuring approximately 5 nm, is present in the parent Ti-BEA zeolite. The introduction of DMPTS and MPTES resulted in the formation of additional mesoporous structures with a diameter of 4.8 nm within the zeolite structure. However, the introduction of TSPED results in the formation of new mesoporous structures with a larger pore size (11 nm). Consequently, the addition of TSPED during the synthesis of Ti-BEA zeolite appears to be a strategy for optimizing the introduction of mesoporous structures.

The effect of silanization reagents on zeolite synthesis can also be visualized from the morphology of the samples. TEM photographs of Ti-BEA zeolite, in addition to samples that have been treated with a variety of silanization reagents, are presented in Figure . Figure a illustrates the morphology of the parent Al-BEA zeolite. It can be seen as a monolithic, compactly stacked crystal structure. Figure b,h illustrates Al-Sil-BEA-DCDMS and Al-Sil-BEA-BTSPA, which consist of distinctly dispersed amorphous small particles. Al-Sil-BEA-TSPED (Figure f) exhibits the most pronounced intracrystalline mesoporous structure. It also has a looser overall structure than the parent Al-BEA zeolite as well as Al-Sil-BEA-TMMOS (Figure c), Al-Sil-BEA-TMMPPS (Figure d), Al-Sil-BEA-DMPTS (Figure e), and Al-Sil-BEA-MPTES (Figure g). SEM photographs of Ti-BEA zeolites in addition to samples that have been treated with differing silanization reagents are presented in Figure . With the exception of Al-Sil-BEA-DCDMS (Figure b) and Al-Sil-BEA-BTSPA (Figure h), whose microstructures are characterized by irregular amorphous structures, the grains of the remaining zeolite samples demonstrate a spherical shape. The grain size of Al-Sil-BEA-TSPED (Figure f) is also significantly smaller than that of Al-BEA, which is also favorable to reduce the diffusion distance of the substances during the reaction process.

3.2. Effect of Mesopore Introduction on Molecular Sieve Postinsertion Titanium

Al-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, Al-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and Al-Sil-BEA-MPTES samples with pronounced mesoporous structures were prepared by dealuminizing and then inserting titanium to prepare Ti-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and Ti-Sil-BEA-MPTES. Ti-BEA was used as a comparative sample (obtained by dealumination of Al-BEA followed by insertion of Ti). The data of the dealuminated samples are presented in the Supporting Information. The Si/Al of DeAl-BEA, DeAl-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, DeAl-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and DeAl-Sil-BEA-MPTES exceeds 500 (Table , entries 9–12). The 27Al NMR spectra (Figure S1) show the characteristic peaks of 50 ppm (skeletal four-coordinated Al) and 0 ppm (nonskeletal six-coordinated Al) for both DeAl-BEA and DeAl-Sil-BEA-TSPED. The characteristic peaks at 50 and 0 ppm disappeared for both DeAl-BEA and DeAl-Sil-BEA-TSPED after dealumination treatment. This finding suggests that Al has been successfully removed from both two zeolites. Furthermore, DeAl-BEA, DeAl-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, DeAl-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and DeAl-Sil-BEA-MPTES maintain the topology of *BEA. However, their relative crystallinity is slightly lower than that of the parent BEA zeolite (Figure S2, Table S1, entries 1 and 2). The XRD spectra of Ti-BEA, Ti-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and Ti-Sil-BEA-MPTES are shown in Figure . Four samples retain the topology of *BEA. The relative crystallinity of the four samples decreases further, while the content of inserted Ti is essentially the same (Table , entries 9–12).

The N2 adsorption and desorption curves of Ti-BEA, Ti-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and Ti-Sil-BEA-MPTES are presented in Figure a. These samples exhibit a hysteresis loop type consistent with that observed in Al-BEA, Al-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, Al-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and Al-Sil-BEA-MPTES (Figure a) and DeAl-BEA, DeAl-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, DeAl-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and DeAl-Sil-BEA-MPTES (Figure S3a). The enhancement in the specific surface area and the pore volume of DeAl-BEA, DeAl-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, DeAl-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and DeAl-Sil-BEA-MPTES is ascribed to the removal of Al, leading to the formation of novel nested hydroxyl groups within the zeolite structure. The reoccupation of the position of the nested hydroxyl groups by Ti resulted in a decrease in the Ti-BEA, Ti-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and Ti-Sil-BEA-MPTES. The pore size distribution curves of Ti-BEA, Ti-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and Ti-Sil-BEA-MPTES are given in Figure b. A comparison of the pore size distribution curves before and after dealumination (Figure b and S3a) reveals that the mesoporous pore size of Ti-BEA and Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED remained largely unchanged upon the substitution of Ti. However, the pore sizes of Ti-Sil-BEA-DMPTS and Ti-Sil-BEA-MPTES were slightly enlarged (5.5 nm). Concurrently, DeAl-BEA, DeAl-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, DeAl-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and DeAl-Sil-BEA-MPTES, Ti-BEA, Ti-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and Ti-Sil-BEA-MPTES exhibited a morphology analogous to that of the parent sample (Figures S4 and S5).

The content of hydroxyl groups in zeolites can affect the concentration of H2O2 in the zeolite pores, thereby affecting the effect of olefin epoxidation. The 1H NMR spectra of Ti-BEA, Ti-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and Ti-Sil-BEA-MPTES are given in Figure . All four samples show significant signals at 1.4 and 1.7 ppm, which are attributed to Si–OH species in the samples. Among them, the strongest signal was observed for Ti-BEA. Ti-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and Ti-Sil-BEA-MPTES showed similar signal intensity. This indicates that Ti-BEA has the largest hydroxyl content, and the three grades of porous zeolites have similar hydroxyl content.

Ti species have been identified as active sites for catalyzing epoxidation reactions. The characterization of Ti species distribution is an important tool and prerequisite to relating the catalytic properties of zeolites. UV–vis spectroscopy provides information about the coordination state of Ti species based on metal charge transfer interactions between O atoms (ligands) and Ti­(IV). The UV–vis spectra of Ti are sensitive to the coordination chemical environment in which it is found. The coordination of disparate substances with Ti has been demonstrated to result in the generation and shift of UV–vis spectral peaks. UV–vis spectra have been shown to provide insights into the distribution of Ti species within the bulk phase of titanium-containing heteroatomic zeolites. The characteristic peak at 210 nm is attributed to the four-coordinated skeletal Ti species of titanium-containing heteroatomic zeolites. The 270 nm peak corresponds to the six-coordinated Ti species. − The absorption band of anatase species appears at near 320 nm. The UV–vis as well as the split-peak fitted spectra of Ti-BEA, Ti-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and Ti-Sil-BEA-MPTES are given in Figure . The percentage of the characteristic peak area of each Ti species in UV–vis of Ti-BEA, Ti-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and Ti-Sil-BEA-MPTES is given in Table . The peak area proportion of the tetra-coordinated titanium species in the four zeolites ranged from 51.4% to 63.4%. Among them, Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED has the lowest proportion of tetra-coordinated titanium species. On the contrary, for six-coordinated titanium species and anatase species, Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED was higher than the remaining three zeolites. However, the difference in the distribution of titanium species among the four zeolite samples was found to be small.

3.3. Effect of Silanated Pore-Expanding Zeolites on the Catalytic DCPD Epoxidation Reaction

DCPD is a prototypical macromolecular olefin. It possesses a distinctive bicyclic structure, accompanied by two CC bonds of varying structures. One is located on the cyclopentene ring (CP bond) and the other on the norbornene ring (NB bond) (Scheme S1). The main reaction pathway and the main byproduct generation pathway of the DCPD epoxidation reaction system are shown in Figure S6. The epoxidation products are monoepoxy dicyclopentadiene (1,2-DCPDMO and 5,6-DCPDMO) and dicyclopentadiene dioxygenated (DCPDDO); the byproducts are mainly alcohols produced by the hydrolysis of unoxidized double bonds of monoepoxy dicyclopentadiene and diols produced by hydrolysis of epoxides.

The curves of Ti-BEA, Ti-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and Ti-Sil-BEA-MPTES for DCPD conversion with time are given in Figure a. It can be seen that the DCPD conversion of all four zeolites increased with the increase of reaction time; Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED had the highest initial activity (75.8%). The conversion rate stabilized after 5 h. The highest initial activity (75.8%) was observed for Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED. The highest conversion rate (99.8%) was still achieved after 10 h. The reaction rate was also higher than that of the remaining three species. Ti-Sil-BEA-DMPTS and Ti-Sil-BEA-MPTES have similar reaction curves. However, the initial activity and the activity at 10 h were lower than that of Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED, while the parent Ti-BEA showed the lowest catalytic activity. Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED showed an 11.5% increase in the conversion rate compared to Ti-BEA. The TON values are given in Figure b, and it can also be seen that the zeolites after silanization were able to improve the TON values of the reaction effectively. The variation of selectivity of individual products with time is given in Figure c–f. The selectivities of 1,2-DCPDMO and 5,6-DCPDMO of the four molecular sieves decreased gradually with time, and the selectivities of DCPDDO and other products increased gradually with time. The selectivities of 1,2-DCPDMO and 5,6-DCPDMO of Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED were the lowest among the four zeolites. Meanwhile, the selectivity of DCPDDO was the highest among the four zeolites, which was 52.5% more selective than that of DCPDDO from Ti-BEA. The selectivities of other products of the four molecular sieves were basically the same. Combined with the above results, it can be shown that the introduction of mesopores can effectively improve the efficiency of the DCPD epoxidation reaction when the distribution of active Ti species and the number of hydroxyl groups of zeolites are similar and the larger mesopores can further promote the secondary epoxidation reaction of 1,2-DCPDMO and 5,6-DCPDMO.

4. Conclusion

This study demonstrates that the addition of appropriate silanization reagents during the hydrothermal crystallization of *BEA-type zeolites can effectively introduce hierarchical pore structure in the zeolite. The silanization reagents for pore expansion were screened. (1) TSPED is a suitable pore-expanding reagent for *BEA-type zeolites. XRD spectroscopy has revealed that the introduction of TMMOS, TMMPPS, DMPTS, TSPED, and MPTES into BEA zeolite synthesis has a negligible effect on the zeolite crystallization. It was observed from BET and TEM results that the introduction of DMPTS, TSPED, and MPTES led to an increase in the zeolite’s mesoporous structure, with the introduction of TSPED being the most significant. SEM analysis results revealed that Al-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, Al-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and Al-Sil-BEA-MPTES exhibited spherical morphology with a significantly smaller size compared to Al-BEA. (2) Ti-BEA, Ti-Sil-BEA-DMPTS, Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED, and Ti-Sil-BEA-MPTES were prepared by acid dealumination and postinsertion of titanium, and the physical properties of the four zeolites (degree of crystallization, pore structure) were largely maintained. The Ti species distribution exhibited remarkable consistency between the bulk phase (as characterized by UV–vis) and the surface (as characterized by XPS). Furthermore, Ti-Sil-BEA-TSPED demonstrated the best DCPD epoxidation reaction performance for the treatment: the DCPD conversion was increased by 11.5%. At the same time, the DCPDDO selectivity was improved by 52.5%. These findings serve to complement the zeolite pore-expanding modification approach. Consequently, these findings provide novel concepts for enhancing the epoxidation reaction efficiency of large molecular olefins.
