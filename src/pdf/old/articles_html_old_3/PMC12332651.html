Abstract

Titanium alloy plays an important role in many high-tech and industrial fields, and its surface processing is vital for expanding the application range and improving its performance. In this work, an effective method for the fabrication of superhydrophobic micro/nanostructures on a titanium alloy surface by a femtosecond laser with ink-assisted light absorption is proposed. The effect of processing parameters of laser cross-line scanning on the morphology and contact angle of surface micro/nanostructures of titanium alloy is revealed. Meanwhile, the change of surface chemical composition of the titanium alloy surface processed by a femtosecond laser is investigated, indicating the surface passivation caused by oxidation during laser processing, which will enhance its corrosion resistance. The greatest contact angle of 152° is successfully attained, giving the titanium alloy’s surface exceptional hydrophobic qualities. This work proves the ability of femtosecond laser processing on regulating micro/nanostructure and wetting property of the titanium alloy surface, which can further promote the application potential of titanium alloy in aerospace, marine engineering, biomedicine, and other fields.

Introduction

Titanium alloy is a high-performance alloy material based on titanium metal with the addition of other elements. It combines the many great qualities of titanium with the unique qualities of the additional elements, giving it low density and high strength that are comparable to those of high-strength alloy steels, but at a far lower weight. , At the same time, it also has good high-temperature resistance and biocompatibility and has important applications in the manufacture of high-temperature components of aero-engines and human implants in medical apparatus such as artificial joints and dental implants. From the overall architecture of the aircraft to the key components, titanium alloys can be seen everywhere. Its low density makes the aircraft effective in reducing its own weight, which not only reduces fuel consumption and reduced operating costs but also extends range and increases payload capacity, making flight more efficient, economical, and environmentally friendly. At the same time, the high strength and excellent toughness of titanium alloys endow components such as aircraft girders, landing gear, and key support structures of spacecraft with tough ″bones″, − enabling them to remain stable in extremely complex stress environments and cope with various high-intensity challenges during flight and space exploration. In addition, in the face of the high-temperature environment inside the aero engine and the harsh conditions such as space radiation, titanium alloys exhibit good high temperature resistance, corrosion resistance, and fatigue resistance. Titanium alloy surface processing is a key step in expanding its application range and improving its performance, and its importance cannot be ignored. By machining the surface of a titanium alloy, it is possible to modify the physical and chemical properties of its surface, thus optimizing its performance in different environments. For example, precise regulation of surface roughness and chemical composition can greatly enhance its hydrophobic properties, enabling it to maintain good condition in humid or corrosive environments, which is of great significance in the fields of marine engineering and chemical engineering. , At the same time, special surface processing can also enhance the wear resistance of titanium alloys, extend their service life, and maintain their stable and efficient operation.

Titanium alloy surface processing methods are diverse, including mechanical processing, chemical treatment, physical vapor deposition, and laser processing. Machining, such as cutting and grinding, , can achieve high-precision surfaces, but it will generate residual stress and work hardening on the surface of titanium alloys, which may reduce their corrosion resistance and fatigue properties, and the processing efficiency is relatively low. Chemical etching in chemical treatment , can realize the processing of complex patterns, but chemical reagents may cause pollution to the environment, and it is difficult to control the surface roughness after treatment. Vapor deposition can deposit various coatings on the surface of titanium alloys to improve their hardness, wear resistance, and corrosion resistance, but the cost of equipment is high, and the combination of coating and substrate is sometimes not ideal. , Laser processing technology is writing a new chapter in the field of titanium alloys, and its advantages and potential are eye-catching. Compared with traditional processing techniques, laser processing can handle titanium alloys with high precision and complexity due to its highly focused energy beam. At the microscopic level, lasers can precisely engrave various microstructures, whether it is complex textures or precise patterns, which offer infinite possibilities for enhancing the functionality of titanium alloys. , In industrial production, the noncontact characteristics of laser processing avoid tool wear and machining stress in traditional machining, greatly reducing damage to titanium alloy materials and improving product yield and production efficiency. Furthermore, by precisely controlling the laser parameters, it is also possible to precisely adjust the surface hardness, roughness, and other properties of titanium alloys to meet the individual needs of different application scenarios. , From the perspective of future development, with the continuous innovation and breakthrough of laser technology, its application potential in titanium alloy processing will be further unlocked. On the one hand, laser processing is expected to be combined with advanced material design concepts to create titanium alloy materials with new properties and functions, such as intelligent responsive materials and high-strength lightweight structural materials, providing key support for emerging industries such as new energy vehicles and high-end equipment manufacturing. On the other hand, the high efficiency and high precision characteristics of laser processing will promote the application of titanium alloys in mass customization production, meet the market demand for personalized and high-performance titanium alloy products, and further expand the market space of titanium alloys, making them shine in more fields and become an important force to promote the development of modern manufacturing.

In recent years, with the continuous development of materials science and surface engineering technology, the preparation of superhydrophobic surfaces has stood out for its unique properties such as self-cleaning, anti-icing, and drag reduction. − The preparation and application of titanium alloy hydrophobic surfaces have become a hot field, and numerous research results continue to emerge, laying a solid foundation for their large-scale application in different industries. Compared with previous studies, our method offers a novel integration of ink-assisted light absorption and femtosecond laser structuring. For instance, Manoj et al. demonstrated a one-step approach to fabricate superhydrophobic titanium surfaces but lacked precise control over chemical surface composition. Ding et al. focused on laser texturing yet did not address chemical modifications. Cheng et al. emphasized laser texturing but did not explore multilayer ink-assisted deposition. Our work uniquely combines topographical control with chemical modification through carbon and fluorine deposition, enabling high-performance, multifunctional surfaces for aerospace and biomedical applications. In this paper, the relationship between different femtosecond laser parameters on the microstructure morphology and wettability of titanium alloy surface was studied, and the chemical composition changes of the titanium alloy surface before and after laser processing were analyzed. It is found that surface passivation caused by oxidation during laser processing enhances the corrosion resistance. The results show that after optimizing the processing parameters, the maximum contact angle of the titanium alloy surface reaches 152°, showing excellent superhydrophobic properties. In the future, with the development of laser processing technology, the deepening of multidisciplinary cross-research, and the expansion of new application fields, titanium alloy hydrophobic materials are expected to play a greater role in more fields and make greater contributions to the technological progress and development of related industries.

Materials and Methods

Sample Pretreatment

The material used in this experiment is TC4 titanium alloy with a size of 50 mm × 50 mm × 10 mm. First, we use 100#, 240#, 400#, 1000#, and 2000# SIC sandpaper to remove impurities and organic pollutants from the surface of the material. Then, the polished sample was placed in a mixed liquid containing deionized water and absolute ethanol for ultrasonic cleaning for 15 min. After cleaning, it was placed in a 120 °C vacuum drying oven for 20 min. Then, the mixture was washed with clean paper and cooled naturally. Finally, ink was applied evenly. The brand of ink used is ″Beijing Yidege″, and its main ingredients are water, carbon black, bone glue, borneol, dyes, fragrances, adhesives, and preservatives. No heavy metal additives or toxic solvents were present. The ink was selected specifically for its high optical absorption and thermal decomposition characteristics under femtosecond laser irradiation. Importantly, postprocessing surface analysis confirmed that the residual chemical species include TiO2, TiC, and fluorinated groups. All of these are known to be chemically stable and biocompatible. This ensures that the processed surface is suitable for potential biomedical and aerospace use.

Femtosecond Laser Processing

The laser processing diagram is shown in Figure . The laser used is from the Wuhan Huari Precision Laser joint stock company, model HR-Sci-fs-5. Its wavelength, pulse width, and repetition rate are 1035 nm, 125 fs, and 45 MHz, respectively. In this paper, the optical path structure of a femtosecond laser processing system is reported. The laser beam is focused by a plano-convex lens with a focal length of 100 mm, and the diameter of the laser light spot is about 16 μm. The experimental process diagram is shown in Figure . The main reason for choosing ink to cover the titanium alloy surface is to improve the laser absorption rate. Generally, femtosecond laser processing requires high energy density to form the required structure and texture on the metal surface, and ink can enhance the absorption efficiency of the laser, so that low power can be used for fine processing.

Corresponding relationships of the pulse energy and pulse peak power with laser power (measured directly by a power meter) were calculated. The calculation process is shown in the Supporting Information. It revealed that the pulse energy corresponding to the laser power of 1W was 22.22 nJ, and the pulse peak power corresponding to the laser power of 1 W was 1.78 × 105 W. The number of pulses per spot as the function of the scanning speed was calculated. , The calculation process is shown in the Supporting Information. It revealed that the number of laser pulses delivered per unit length (spot diameter) was NP1D=3.45×106v (the numerical unit of v was mm/s). The summary table reporting the relevant laser parameters explored in the study is shown in Table S2.

Characterization of Surface Micro/Nanostructures

The changes in surface morphology under different laser parameters were observed by field emission scanning electron microscopy (GeminiSEM 300, Zeiss, Germany). The chemical composition of the titanium alloy surface before and after laser processing was characterized by means of energy spectrometry (attached in a scanning electron microscope), X-ray diffraction (D8 ADVANCE, Brooke, Germany), X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (ESCALAB XI+, Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA), and other means in terms of elemental content, physical phase composition, and changes in surface groups. The surface wettability of the sample was characterized by an optical contact angle meter (OCA20, DataPhysics, Germany).

Results and Discussion

Typical Ablative Surface Morphology of Titanium Alloy by Femtosecond Laser Processing

The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images in Figure illustrate the evolution of the surface topography of titanium alloy substrates treated by femtosecond lasers with different processing parameters. The laser processing parameters including laser power, corresponding pulse energy and pulse peak power, scanning spacing, scanning speed, corresponding number of laser pulses delivered per spot, and number of scans are organized in Table S2. Figure a1–d1 illustrates the evolution of the surface topography of titanium alloy substrates treated by femtosecond lasers with different laser powers of 2.88, 2.41, 2.09, and 1.73 W, respectively, while maintaining the scanning spacing of 120 μm, a scanning speed of 25 mm/s, and one scan. Figure a1 shows notable ablation with densely dispersed microstructures and the distinct grid pattern. Significant melting and resolidification brought on by high energy input created microparticles and microtracks that formed textured surface morphology. Figure b1 shows that the overall grid pattern remained clear and the ablated areas remained pronounced. The significantly weakened contrast between the processed area and the unprocessed area, as shown in Figure c1–d1, indicated weakened surface changes, which was reduced laser ablation effect. It revealed the impact of the laser power on surface topography. In the process of laser processing, the laser energy is first absorbed by the material surface and forms a high-temperature plasma layer. This plasma layer will not only absorb part of the laser energy but also affect the propagation path of the laser through the plasma shielding effect, thus changing the effective deposition of energy. Under the action of high-power laser, the plasma shielding effect can change the distribution of laser energy deposition, resulting in the increase of surface temperature and significant morphological changes. − Figure a2–d2 illustrates the effect of the number of scans (1, 4, 8, and 16, respectively) on the surface topography of titanium alloy substrates treated by femtosecond lasers with the laser power of 2.88 W, scanning speed of 25 mm/s, and scanning spacing of 120 μm. When it was scanned once, the surface exhibited a grid pattern with moderate ablation (Figure a2). The microstructures were mainly composed of sporadic molten materials, indicating limited surface energy accumulation. Repeated laser scans can contribute to the accumulation of heat and removal of material. The morphological features displayed additional improvement following eight scans (Figure c2). The ablated regions displayed more intricate microstructures, such as dense particles and ridges. The more substantial material change resulted from the increased energy deposition caused by repetitive scanning. After 16 scans (Figure d2), the surface showed significant damage of the grid structures because too much energy built up. The earlier structures were partly destroyed, with molten areas taking over and creating very uneven microstructures with uneven textures. Figure a3–d3 depicts the surface topography of titanium alloys treated by a femtosecond laser with different scanning spacings (60, 80, 100, and 120 μm, respectively) at 2.88 W laser power, 25 mm/s scanning speed, and one scan. In Figure a3, the laser ablation lines overlapped significantly, resulting in a highly continuous and uniform ablation pattern. The processed tracks were dense. The microstructural features included melted materials and resolidified particles, which can contribute to the formation of surfaces with textured appearance. In Figure b3, the overlap of the ablation lines decreased as the scanning spacing increased to 80 μm. The tracks remained continuous. The microstructures remained distinct, and the surface texture was moderate, indicating a balance between ablation efficiency and surface coverage. At scanning spacing of 100 μm (Figure c3), the ablation lines began to separate, resulting in discontinuous tracks. At a scanning spacing of 120 μm (Figure d3), the ablation lines separated well and the tracks separated. The overall textures were less uniform. Small scanning spacing resulted in highly textured and continuous patterns due to extensive line overlap, while large scanning spacing resulted in isolated ablative features with reduced texture. Figure a4–d4 shows the effect of different scanning speeds (100, 150, 200, and 300 mm/s, respectively) on the surface topography of femtosecond laser-treated titanium alloys, at 2.88 W laser power, 80 μm scan spacing, and one scan. At a scanning speed of 100 mm/s, the laser residence time was relatively high, resulting in significant energy deposition per unit area. This process created well-defined ablation features. At the scanning speed of 150 mm/s, the energy input per unit area was diminished. The prominence degree of the microstructures was relatively low. The considerable ablation was effectively suppressed, and the ablation degree was significantly decreased when the scanning speed approached 200 mm/s. This is because the higher scanning speed further shortens the interaction between the laser and the material. The decrease in laser action duration is directly linked to a clear decreasing trend in the ablation marks on the material surface, at both the macroscopic and microscopic levels. The ablation degree decreases as a result of the action time being shortened, which restricts the accumulation of energy on the material surface and the propagation of the thermal effect from the standpoint of energy transfer and material response. The heating of the material surface by a laser will lead to the rapid rise of the temperature in the local area, while the surrounding area of the laser action area will rely on heat conduction to dissipate the heat and form a temperature gradient. The existence of the temperature gradient determines the thermal expansion, melting, evaporation, and recrystallization of the material and then affects the evolution of the surface morphology. − The laser’s residence duration on the material surface per unit area was significantly reduced when the scanning speed was raised to the maximum value of 300 mm/s (Figure d4), and the energy deposition impact on the material surface tended to be reduced. Lower scanning speed can give the laser and material a longer time window to interact, allowing for a more complete deposition of energy per unit area. This results in the material’s surface developing wider track. On the other hand, fast scanning speed significantly shortens the laser–material contact time, which results in substantial alterations to the material’s surface microstructures and the uneven surface morphology.

In order to quantitatively describe the influence of laser processing parameters on processed tracks. The track width and depth were measured and statistically analyzed based on Figure and ultradepth-of-field microscope. Figure shows the influence law of laser processing parameters on the track width. It indicated that the track width of laser processing increased with the increase of the laser power and number of scans, decreased with the increase of scanning speed, and basically remained unchanged with the change of scanning spacing. These were attributed to the fact that the laser energy per unit area deposited on the material surface, increased with the increase of laser power and number of scans, decreased with the increase of scanning speed, and was not affected by scanning spacing. The increase of laser energy per unit area deposited on the material surface was conducive to the heat accumulation effect, which was conducive to the extension of thermal damage of the material in width direction, , and vice versa. Due to the high repetition rate of 45 MHz and weak single pulse energy (nJ), it was not conducive to nonthermal ablation but more conducive to the accumulation of thermal effect, which highlighted the role of incubation on the temperature distribution and was conducive to the extension of thermal damage of the material in width direction. , Hence, it was not conducive to the extension of thermal damage of the material in the depth direction, and shallow ablation was achieved. Therefore, the track depth was relatively shallow, and its sensitivity to the changes in laser processing parameters was very weak. Basically, the influence law of laser processing parameters on the depth cannot be obtained. Figure S1 shows a clear and typical contour of the track depth, which indicated a track depth of 2.2 μm. Similarly, due to the shallow track depth, the dimension in the height direction of the microstructures was small, hence small roughness of microstructures and its change, and therefore the influence law of laser processing parameters on the roughness cannot be obtained. The average surface roughness of multiple laser-processed surfaces was about Sa = 1.2 μm and Sq = 1.5 μm.

Figure a demonstrates the variation of the average area of the chevron-like micro/nanostructures with laser power. As the laser power increases from 1.36 to 2.88 W, the average areas of the chevron-like structures on the surface of the titanium alloy are 37,520.50, 39,727.53, 28,400.17, 24,022.64, and 16,562.63 μm2. It can be seen that the average size of the chevron-like structures decreases gradually with the increase in laser power, which is mainly due to the fact that higher laser power causes more photons to be absorbed by the material, leading to more efficient material removal, which results in a decrease in the average size and area. In addition, the plasma generated during femtosecond laser processing is able to absorb additional laser energy, increasing the rate of material removal. Figure b demonstrates the variation of the average area of the chevron-like micro- and nanostructures with the number of scans. As the number of scans increased from 1 to 16, the average areas of the chevron-like structures on the surface of the titanium alloy were 38,434.13, 26,129.57, 15,049.92, 11,829.44, and 10,648.13 μm2. It can be observed that with fewer scans, there is a lower energy accumulation in the laser-processed area and there is a lesser amount of material removal. When the number of scans is in the range of two to four scans, the material removal rate and the size of the micro/nanostructures change faster. After the number of scans reaches 8, the size of the micro/nanostructure gradually decreases and the material removal rate tends to stabilize. Multiple scans lead to an increase in energy density in the local region, and when the energy exceeds the vaporization threshold of the titanium alloy, the material is directly vaporized rather than melted and solidified, which reduces the redistribution time of the molten metal. At the same time, under repeated laser impacts, most of the material is concentrated at the bottom of the track and a small amount of molten metal is splashed to the outside of the track by recoil pressure, leading to a reduction in the size of the structure. Figure c demonstrates the variation of the average area of the chevron-like micro/nanostructures with scanning spacing. As the scanning spacing increases from 20 to 120 μm, the average area of the chevron-like micro/nanostructures on the surface of the titanium alloy is 699.91, 3927.32, 6390.87, 14,655.72, 19,732.23, and 40,423.01 μm2, respectively. The micro/nanostructures are gradually enlarged as the scanning spacing increases. During femtosecond laser processing, the scanning spacing directly affects the overlap density of energy deposition. A smaller scanning spacing increases the overlap area between the laser spots, leading to an enhanced energy accumulation effect, which triggers more intense material vaporization and plasma eruption and inhibits the growth of the micro/nanostructures. Increasing the scanning spacing reduces the overlap of the laser spots, the energy distribution is more diffuse, and the material has more time for thermal relaxation and energy exchange in the pulse gap, which promotes the hydrodynamic motion of the molten metal and the formation of larger micro/nanostructures. Figure d demonstrates the variation of the average area of the chevron-like micro/nanostructures with scanning spacing. As the scanning speed increases from 25 to 300 mm/s, the average areas of the chevron-like structures on the surface of the titanium alloy are 13,887.39, 14,335.95, 13,478.32, 12,651.39, 14,582.30, and 12,681.26 μm2, respectively. It can be seen that with the increase of the scanning speed, the change in the size of the micro/nanostructures size changes is small, which is due to the fact that the phase transformation and ablation behavior of titanium alloys are limited by the energy density threshold. When the scanning speed is varied within a specific range, the energy density may remain within the threshold window, resulting in saturation of the material response. High-speed scanning reduces heat accumulation but shortens cooling time, and the balance between the two stabilizes the molten metal flow range. In addition, the rapid generation of nanoscale oxide layers on the surface of titanium alloys under high-speed scanning can limit the expansion of the molten zone due to differences in their melting points.

Surface Wettability of Femtosecond Laser-Processed Titanium Alloys

The dependence of the contact angles of the titanium alloy surface treated by laser on the key parameters of femtosecond laser processing (laser power, number of scans, scan spacing, and scan speed) is shown in Figure a–d. As shown in Figure a, the scan spacing is 120 μm, the scanning speed is 25 mm/s, and the number of scans is 1; as the laser power increases from 1.01 to 2.88 W, the contact angle gradually increases from 126.0 to 141.8°. The research shows that the increase in laser power results in the formation of more pronounced micro/nanostructures, which enhance the hydrophobicity of the surface through the “airbag effect”. At the same time, laser processing may induce changes in chemical surface composition, such as the formation of titanium oxide layers, which further affect surface wettability. Figure b shows the effect of the number of scans on the contact angle at different scan spacings (20, 60, and 120 μm); the laser power is 2.88 W, and the scanning speed is 25 mm/s. The laser energy is more evenly dispersed under a few scans, which aids in the formation of notable surface microstructures. However, several scans may cause the structure to undergo the ″ablation effect″, which lowers the contact angle. Figure c depicts the change trend of the contact angle with the scan spacing under different scanning speeds; the laser power is 2.88 W, and the number of scans is 1. The contact angle increases first and then decreases with the increase of the scan spacing and reaches its peak at the scan spacing of 80 μm. A reduced scanning spacing results in a denser microstructure. Nevertheless, excessive heat effect may flatten the surface morphology, lowering the contact angle. Reduced surface homogeneity, inadequate microstructure, and eventually decreased hydrophobicity can be caused by excessive scan spacing. Figure d shows the change of the contact angle with scanning speed under different scan spacing conditions; the laser power is 2.88 W, and the number of scans is 1. The contact angle gradually increases and then decreases as the scanning speeds increases, reaching a maximum contact angle at approximately 200 mm/s. Lower scanning speeds can cause surface micro/nanostructures to be destroyed and overmachined, which lowers the contact angle. The surface topography is created weakly by inadequate laser residence time when the scanning speed is too high (200 mm/s above), which lowers the contact angle.

Surface Composition Analysis of Femtosecond Laser-Processed Titanium Alloy

The X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) full spectra of the titanium alloy surfaces in the original state (black line), after femtosecond laser processing with a thin ink layer assistance (green line), and after femtosecond laser processing with thick-ink layer assistance (red line) are shown in Figure . The XPS spectra of the original titanium alloy surface are dominated by the characteristic peaks of Ti 2p, O 1s, and C 1s. The Ti 2p peak indicates the significant presence of titanium in the matrix, while the O 1s peak reflects the formation of surface titanium oxide, which is a common chemical characteristic of titanium alloys in natural environments. In addition, the appearance of the Al 2p peak further confirmed that the aluminum component in the titanium alloy matrix participates in the formation of the surface oxide film. These natural oxide layers not only provide basic corrosion resistance but also provide high surface energy for subsequent laser processing. A new chemical composition signal appears in the XPS spectrum after the titanium alloy is coated with a thin ink layer and laser processed. Fluorine is usually derived from fluorosilane, and the localized high temperature during laser processing promotes the deposition of these components on the surface. The low surface energy characteristics of fluorine play a crucial role in enhancing the hydrophobic properties of surfaces. Meanwhile, under the laser heating action, the organic materials in the ink break down and polymerize, partially transforming into stable carbon-based materials that are deposited on the surface. As the thickness of the ink layer increases, a significant increase in the C 1s signal indicates that a higher proportion of carbon-based organic matter is enriched on the surface. The carbon-rich properties of this surface may make it more stable and resistant to corrosion. Ti 2p and O 1s signals were also enhanced. This shows that when the ink layer is thick, the titanium alloy reacts more violently with the oxygen, and the Ti element in the titanium alloy has a higher oxygen affinity, which makes it easy to form more stable oxide layers, resulting in an increase in the oxidation state signal of the surface Ti. At the same time, more oxides and hydroxyl groups are adsorbed on the surface. This phenomenon is the result of the laser thermal effect promoting chemical reactions. The XPS full spectrum revealed the significant effect of laser processing combined with different thickness ink layers on the surface chemical modification of laser-processed titanium alloys. Both the ink layer and fluorosilane successfully introduced fluorine-containing and carbon-based chemical components, which effectively improved surface function by reducing surface energy and enhancing surface chemical stability.

Moreover, Figure shows the high-resolution XPS spectrograms of C 1s, O 1s, and Ti 2p on the titanium alloy surface after laser processing (Figure a–c) and the original titanium alloy surface (Figure d–f). Through comparative analysis, the regulation effect of laser processing on the surface chemical composition and oxidation state of titanium alloys can be revealed in depth. After laser processing, a significant variety of peak signals appeared in the C 1s spectroscopy (Figure a), including CF3 (293.78 eV), CF2 (291.58 eV), CO (288.55 eV), C–O (286.25 eV), and C–C (284.8 eV). Among these, the appearance of CF2 and CF3 peaks reflects the introduction of fluorine-containing chemical components on the surface by laser processing, and these fluorine-containing functional groups provide a significant basis for enhancing the hydrophobic properties of titanium alloy surfaces by reducing the surface free energy. Furthermore, the presence of CO and C–O signals is indicative of the formation of oxidized carbon-based compounds, which may be attributable to laser-induced oxidation. This is characterized by the presence of components on the surface that oxidize to form aldehyde groups or carboxyl groups under the action of the local high temperature of the laser. The surface after laser processing obviously has more complex chemical functional groups, which lay the foundation for subsequent surface functionalization applications. The C 1s spectroscopy of the original titanium alloy surface (Figure d) is relatively simple and mainly consists of C–C (284.8 eV) and a small amount of C–O (286.11 eV). The intensity of the C–C peak is found to be dominant, indicating that the material surface is primarily covered by carbon-based organic pollutants in the environment. These pollutants are typically derived from organic particles present in the atmosphere or surface-adsorbed hydrocarbons, which manifest as a singular physical adsorption layer rather than a functionalized chemical layer that is formed upon laser processing. After laser processing, the O 1s spectroscopy (Figure b) can be divided into two parts: C–O (532.69 eV) and metal oxide (531.37 eV). The enhancement of the C–O peak is consistent with the formation of carbon oxide-based compounds in the spectroscopy of the C element, indicating that during laser processing, organic matter in the ink layer or the environment is oxidized to form more oxidation groups. These groups combine with titanium oxide to form a stable mixed oxide layer. The intensity of the metal oxide signal (531.37 eV) increased significantly in comparison to the original surface, indicating that laser processing produced a denser and thicker titanium oxide layer on the surface. The localized high temperature results in rapid oxidation of the titanium surface, forming a continuous TiO2 film, which plays a key role in improving the surface’s corrosion resistance and chemical stability. The O 1s spectroscopy of the original surface (Figure e) also shows C–O and metal oxide signals, but the C–O signal intensity is low, indicating that the content of carbon oxide-based species on the original surface is low. The peak intensity of the metal oxide is also significantly lower than that of the laser-processed surface, which may be due to the thin and uneven distribution of the naturally formed titanium oxide layer. After laser processing, Ti 2p spectroscopy (Figure c) clearly shows the characteristic peaks of TiO2:Ti 2p3/2 (459.14 eV) and Ti 2p1/2 (464.91 eV), and it was accompanied by a clear satellite peak. These peaks indicate that laser processing has formed a high-purity TiO2 layer on the surface. This fully oxidized titanium surface is essential for enhancing corrosion resistance, oxidation resistance, and biocompatibility. In addition, the Ti 2p spectroscopy of the original surface (Figure f) shows a small amount of titanium nitride (453.2 eV) signal in addition to the characteristic peak of TiO2. Laser processing can effectively change the chemical composition and oxidation state of the surface of titanium alloys and achieve surface functionalization and performance optimization by introducing fluorine-containing groups and carbon oxide-based compounds and constructing a complete TiO2 layer. The results show that the formation of a TiO2 film plays an important role in improving the surface hydrophobicity. The surface of TiO2 can affect the contact angle of water droplets through the change of surface functional groups, so as to improve the hydrophobicity of the surface. − These chemical changes have laid an important foundation for the application of titanium alloy surfaces in the fields of hydrophobicity, corrosion resistance, and biomedicine.

The XRD pattern of the original surface of the titanium alloy is shown in Figure a. It shows that the principal diffraction peaks belong to the crystal structures of α-Ti and β-Ti, with α-Ti as the dominant phase and β-Ti as the secondary phase. The characteristic diffraction peaks of α-Ti appear clearly at (100), (002), (101), (102), (103), etc. The characteristic peaks of β-Ti appear obviously at (110). The position and strength of these distinctive peaks reveal that the surface of the original titanium alloy has high crystallinity, indicating a typical metallic titanium phase structure. The diffraction peaks show that α-Ti has a peak strength significantly larger than that of β-Ti. This suggests that the α phase is the most stable and dominant structure in titanium alloys under their original condition. Figure b shows the XRD pattern of titanium alloy processed by a femtosecond laser after covering a thin ink layer. The image shows diffraction peaks of α-Ti and β-Ti, as well as typical peaks of TiO2 and Ti2O3 when compared to those of the original surface (Figure a). The characteristic diffraction peaks of TiO2 appear at the (012) and (004) crystal planes, while the diffraction peaks of Ti2O3 are mainly concentrated at the (113) crystal planes. The development of these peaks suggests that an apparent oxidation reaction occurs during femtosecond laser processing in the presence of thin ink coating, culminating in the production of a stable titanium dioxide phase. This oxidation reaction could be caused by two factors: first, the laser action causes a sharp increase in surface temperature, prompting the titanium alloy to chemically react with oxygen; second, the oxygen element in the thin ink layer acts as a source of support for the oxidation reaction, exacerbating the process. Although the oxide phase appears on the surface, the α-Ti and β-Ti phases still exist, showing that the titanium alloy’s crystal structure is not totally oxidized, and the oxidation reaction only occurs on the surface. Figure c shows the XRD pattern of a titanium alloy surface processed by a femtosecond laser after covering a thick ink layer. It can be seen that a new diffraction peak TiC appears, and typical crystal planes include (111) and (200). In addition, the characteristic peaks of the original α-Ti and β-Ti are significantly weakened, indicating that the laser processing has a more significant impact on the crystal structure of titanium metal under the condition of thick ink coating. The thick ink layer contains more carbon sources. During femtosecond laser processing, carbon and titanium react at high temperatures to form a stable TiC phase. It is found that TiC forms a strengthening phase in the metal matrix, which can reduce the pores and cracks in the metal matrix and further improve its corrosion resistance. Moreover, TiC is a very hard material, and its hardness is second only to diamond. Therefore, when TiC particles are distributed in the alloy matrix, the wear resistance of the matrix can be significantly improved. − Concurrently, titanium oxides produce Ti6O oxide, which might be reliant on Ti6O production in an oxygen-poor environment. When processed under a thick ink layer, the oxidation reaction may be weakened due to insufficient oxygen content, and titanium metal easily reacts with a small amount of oxygen to form Ti6O. More carbon sources are produced during processing under thick ink layers, which may restrict the overlay effect’s ability to diffuse surface oxygen and speed up the carbonization process.

The pristine titanium alloy surface was first analyzed by energy spectroscopy (EDS) of the face-scanning spectra, and Figure a demonstrates a map of the compositional content of the surface. It can be clearly observed that a large amount of titanium elements is present in the pristine surface with a weight percentage of 84.58%. In addition, aluminum elements accounted for 5.01% by weight, which is basically in line with the theoretical elemental composition of the TC4 titanium alloy and verifies the basic compositional composition of the alloy. Figure b demonstrates the surface composition content map after application of a layer of ink and laser processing. It can be seen that the weight percentages of Ti, C, O, and F elements on the sample surface are 57.18, 15.62, 14.11, and 9.53%, respectively. Compared with the pristine surface, the Ti element decreased by 27.4%, and the C, O, and F elements increased by 11.34, 8.13, and 9.46%, respectively. The increase in the weight percentage and distribution of carbon and oxygen elements after laser processing indicates that a significant oxidized film was formed during laser processing and that carbon has penetrated into the surface. In addition, the significant increase in the fluorine element originated from the fluorosilane modification performed after laser processing, which would leave part of the fluorine element on the surface of the titanium alloy. Figure c demonstrates the surface composition content map after applying three layers of ink and laser processing. It can be seen that the weight percentages of Ti, C, O, and F elements on the sample surface are 65.59, 18.32, 12.13, and 0.24%, respectively. Compared to the pristine surface, the element Ti has decreased by 18.99% and the elements C, O, and F have increased by 14.04, 6.15, and 0.17%, respectively. Compared with the sample in Figure b, after the addition of the ink layer, the content of elemental Ti increased, the elemental carbon increased, and the elemental oxygen decreased, which further verifies that the increase of the ink layer promotes the participation of oxygen in the reaction, which in turn leads to the formation of more oxidized film on the surface.

The Raman spectra of the titanium alloy surface before and after laser processing are shown in Figure . It can be seen that two obvious peaks appeared on the surface of the processed titanium alloy: the D peak located at 1346.80 cm–1 and the G peak located at 1592.84 cm–1. The D peak was caused by the SP3 heterogeneous carbon or defects, and the G peak was caused by the vibration of the SP2 heterogeneous carbon, which corresponded to the disordered structure of the carbon material and graphitized carbon, respectively. During laser processing, organic materials (dyes, resins, etc.) in the ink may undergo pyrolytic carbonization, generating defect-containing disordered carbon (D peak) and graphitized carbon (G peak). In addition, the carbon-based materials (carbon black, graphite, etc.) contained in the ink undergo a carbonization reaction under the action of the high laser energy, which makes part of the organic materials generate amorphous carbon or nanocarbon structures, while part of the incompletely pyrolyzed carbon particles are deposited on the surface of the titanium alloy to form a composite layer. In addition, a small peak appeared at 446.54 cm–1, which represented the generation of TiO2 on the surface of the titanium alloy. The comprehensive analysis shows that when the laser energy is applied to the surface of the titanium alloy, carbonization, oxidation, and lattice modification are triggered at the same time.

The surface topography of the titanium alloy with optimal hydrophobicity after femtosecond laser processing is shown in Figure . At this time, the processing parameters are laser power of 2.88 W, number of scans of 1, scan spacing of 80 μm, and scanning speed of 200 mm/s. Figure a shows the overall topography of the titanium alloy surface after laser processing, and regular grid-like micro/nanostructures can be observed. These grid structures are formed by the intersecting of laser scanning paths, exhibiting significant uniformity and consistency and reflecting the stability and controllability of laser processing. The surface of the inner area of the grid is more uniform, and the structure of the edge part is more significant. The surface contact angle is 152.0°, indicating that the surface had superhydrophobic properties. This significant hydrophobicity can be attributed to the multiscale characteristics of the surface topography after laser processing. These characteristics include the synergistic effects of the micrometer-scale grid structure and nanoparticle distribution as well as the formation of low surface energy chemicals. The high-magnification SEM image in Figure b makes it possible to see the edges’ microstructural characteristics. There is local melting and a microporous structure, and the edge portion of the grid has a rougher surface morphology than the center. This implies that the laser’s energy distribution at the edge region might be marginally higher than in the center, leading to localized material overheating and melting, followed by quick cooling to create irregular microstructures. Figure c further magnifies the inner region of the grid. As can be observed, the surface is made up of molten clusters of different sizes that are micrometers in size. These clusters are created when the molten material is rapidly cooled and solidified by a laser. This structure indicates that the evaporation, melting, and redeposition of local materials during laser processing result in the formation of microscale particles and clusters on the surface. These cluster structures significantly increase the surface roughness and surface specific area, providing favorable conditions for the formation of a stable balloon layer when the droplets contact the surface. Figure d shows a higher-magnification SEM image of the surface topography, allowing the distribution of nanoscale particles to be clearly observed. The nanoparticles are uniformly coated on the surface, further enhancing the surface roughness. The introduction of nanoparticle structure reduces the actual contact area between the droplet and the solid surface, further strengthening the hydrophobicity.

Conclusions

In this study, micro/nanostructures were successfully created by scanning the titanium alloy substrate covering the ink layer with a femtosecond laser. The development of micro/nano structures is facilitated by the more efficient transfer of laser energy to the titanium alloy’s surface, which is made possible by the ink’s absorption properties. The influence of laser processing parameters on the morphology and contact angle of micro/nanostructures was studied systematically. Through energy spectrum analysis, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy, and X-ray diffractometer analysis to characterize the surface chemical composition before and after laser processing, it is found that different titanium substances are generated during laser processing due to oxidation and carbonization of a large number of carbon sources in the ink layer. These modifications can result in the deposition of their products on the surface of micro/nanostructures, which can alter the surface’s chemical characteristics and impact the contact angle and hydrophobicity of titanium alloy surfaces. The results show that after optimizing the processing parameters, the maximum contact angle of the titanium alloy surface reaches 152°, showing excellent superhydrophobic properties. These study findings have wide-ranging potential applications in the domains of aerospace, marine engineering, and biomedicine and offer significant technical assistance for the design and development of hydrophobic surfaces made of titanium alloy.
