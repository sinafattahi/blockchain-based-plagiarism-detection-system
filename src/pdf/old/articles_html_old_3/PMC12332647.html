Influence of Functional Group Insertion on Clay Stabilizing Efficiencies of Antiswelling Agents: A Molecular Dynamics Study

Abstract

Clay minerals in oil and gas fields are prone to swelling, impacting wellbore stability and causing difficulties in water injection. Quaternary ammonium type cationic antiswelling agents have become important clay swelling inhibitors due to their electrostatic adsorption capabilities. Quantum mechanical density functional theory (DFT) calculations show that the local charges of quaternary ammonium and ethylene groups can be adjusted by inserting different functional groups into the molecule. However, the impact of the molecular charge distribution on the antiswelling effect has not been fully revealed. This study tests 21 surfactants and polymers based on vinylbenzyltrimethylammonium, methyl ester trimethylammonium, and amido trimethylammonium structures through functional group insertion using quantum mechanics calculations (QM) and molecular dynamics simulations (MD). The correlation between charge distribution and changes in montmorillonite layer spacing is explored. By adjusting the molecular structure, a series of charge distributions are obtained to evaluate their impact on antiswelling performance. The results show that the partial charge on the α-C and the β-CH2 had the most significant impact on the antiswelling ability of the agent, with Spearman coefficients as high as −0.431 and −0.434, respectively, while the charge on the nitrogen atom had a weaker effect. Polar functional groups such as amido groups exhibit strong antiswelling properties, whereas hydrophobic functional groups are less effective. This study provides a theoretical basis for the molecular design of highly efficient antiswelling agents, which is of great significance for the time- and cost-efficient selection and development of antiswelling agents for water-sensitive reservoirs.

Introduction

During drilling and oilfield development, clay swelling has always been one of the main challenges affecting wellbore stability and oil layer permeability. In medium and highly water-sensitive reservoirs, where montmorillonite content is high, water molecules easily enter the interlayers of clay minerals, leading to increased interlayer spacing, wellbore collapse, formation clogging, and impaired reservoir permeability. , To mitigate these issues, antiswelling agents are widely employed, with cationic polymer-based agents showing the most promise due to their ability to neutralize negatively charged clay particles through electrostatic interactions and compress the double electric layer. −

Quaternary ammonium-based inhibitors have emerged as particularly effective antiswelling agents. Extensive research has demonstrated their capabilities: Sun showed that HD-1 type agents could restore core permeability by nearly 90%. Zhang et al. found double quaternary ammonium salts achieved over 50% antiswelling rates at 2% concentration. Li developed hyperbranched polyester amides with terminal quaternary ammonium groups showing excellent performance. Further developments include dendritic quaternary ammonium salts by Cai et al. and Sa et al., , while Yu and Sun, respectively classified cationic polymers into “extroverted” and “introverted” types for targeting different swelling mechanisms. , Additional studies have explored hydrophobic quaternary ammonium inhibitors, synergistic effects with inorganic salts, and structural modifications involving hydrophobic groups. ,

Complementing experimental studies, molecular dynamics simulations have provided crucial insights into swelling mechanisms. , Bains et al. revealed that effective inhibitors should be linear long-chain molecules with hydrophobic and hydrophilic regions. Hensen demonstrated the quantitative relationship between charge and swelling behavior, and Li et al. showed how ion type and humidity affect migration rates in montmorillonite. Recent work has expanded to ionic liquid inhibitors and established that molecular charge distribution significantly affects cationic surfactant efficiency.

Despite these advances, a critical knowledge gap remains: the limited understanding of how molecular charge distribution affects quaternary ammonium inhibitor performance. Current antiswelling agent design relies heavily on empirical approaches rather than theoretical guidance, and quantum mechanical calculations have not yet been systematically applied to optimize these molecules. This gap is particularly significant because the fundamental mechanism involves electrostatic interactions with negatively charged clay surfaces, making charge distribution optimization crucial for developing more effective inhibitors.

To address this knowledge gap, this study systematically investigates how molecular charge distribution affects antiswelling performance by introducing different functional groups (carboxyl, ketone, and thioketone) into three typical quaternary ammonium structures. Using molecular dynamics simulations, we analyze the interaction between modified antiswelling agent molecules and montmorillonite surfaces to reveal the impact of charge distribution on adsorption behavior. This research provides theoretical guidance for optimizing quaternary ammonium antiswelling agent design, potentially reducing synthesis and evaluation costs for antiswelling agent applications in water-sensitive reservoirs in oil and gas field development.

Methods

Molecular Dynamics Simulations

The three cationic antiswelling agent structures involved in this study are methyl ester trimethylammonium polymer, amide trimethylammonium polymer, and polyvinyl phenyl trimethylammonium. Due to the size restriction inherent with the MD approach, all three agents were constructed with a polymerization degree of three, that is, each antiswelling agent contains three monomers, as shown in Figure .

All simulations were performed using the molecular dynamics software GROMACS (version 2023.3), with the CHARMM force field describing all organic molecules, and the SPC-FW model was used to describe water molecules. The CLAYFF force field was chosen for the montmorillonite blocks. The interaction between cationic antiswelling agents and the montmorillonite surface is expressed through electrostatic interactions and van der Waals forces. Long-range electrostatic interactions are calculated using the Particle Mesh Ewald (PME) method, and van der Waals forces are represented by the Lennard-Jones potential with a cutoff distance of 1.4 nm, with periodic boundary conditions in all three dimensions. Each simulation system contains 36 cationic antiswelling agent molecules, which are adsorbed on the montmorillonite surface due to the net negative charges of the surface, and an additional two Na+ ions are added to balance the charge. By analyzing the MD trajectories, it is possible to infer the molecular adsorption behavior and changes in interlayer distance.

The total simulation time for each group was 40 ns. The initial simulation was performed using the NVT ensemble, and the system temperature was controlled at 303 K using the Nosé-Hoover thermostat with a time constant of 1.0 ps and a time step of 1.5 fs. A simulation of 20 ns was conducted to allow sufficient adsorption of the antiswelling agent molecules on the montmorillonite surface. Subsequently, the NPT ensemble was used, and pressure coupling was performed for 10 ns using the Berendsen pressure coupling method, with a system pressure of 1 bar and a pressure coupling time constant of 2.0 ps. This was followed by continuing the simulation for another 10 ns using the Parrinello–Rahman pressure coupling method, also with a system pressure of 1 bar and a pressure coupling time constant of 2.0 ps, to ensure system equilibration. ,

Furthermore, to understand the charge distribution of cationic polymers and their interaction with clay surfaces, Gaussian16 software at the B3LYP/6–31G­(d) level , was used to perform the Density Functional Theory (DFT) calculations on the antiswelling agent molecules. Considering the water phase environment in practical applications, the Polarized Continuum Model (PCM) was used to simulate the water solvent effect to more accurately reflect the partial charges in the solvent and their influence on molecular interactions. ,

The size of the montmorillonite sheet layer is 3.011 nm × 3.832 nm × 0.656 nm. In order to accurately simulate the layered structure of actual clay, this study arranged five montmorillonite sheets in parallel along the Z-axis direction to construct a multilayer montmorillonite model. The layers were named M1, M2, M3, M4, and M5 respectively, making it possible to record the movement of each montmorillonite sheet layer in various directions in the subsequent simulation. As shown in Figure , the cationic antiswelling agent added in the system is adsorbed on and between the clay surfaces due to the electrostatic force.

The size of the box is 3.138 nm × 8.000 nm × 17.000 nm. In the MD study of Li et al., periodic boundary conditions were applied in the X and Y directions of the montmorillonite sheet layer, and the clay sheet completely occupies the XY plane, causing the water molecules unable to escape the space between two neighboring montmorillonite sheet layers (Figure left), nor can additional water molecules enter this space. In other words, the interlayer spacing of montmorillonite is restricted by the fixed number of residing water molecules, making it challenging to evaluate the actual effect of the antiswelling agent. To avoid this drawback, a larger Z-axis size was designed in this study to provide more water molecules, and to give sufficient space so that the montmorillonite layers can move freely. The size of the box is based on the five layers of montmorillonite sheets being placed perpendicularly along the Z-axis, with sufficient space flowing out between adjacent montmorillonite sheets for the flow of water supply molecules. In addition, the size of the simulation box along the X-axis was expanded beyond the X dimension of the montmorillonite layers, breaking their periodicity along the X direction. This is to enable the water molecules between the montmorillonite sheets to enter and exit the interlayer space, making it possible for dynamic changes in layer spacing (Figure right).

To quantitatively analyze the performance of antiswelling agents, the antiswelling effect of each agent was evaluated by calculating the change in montmorillonite interlayer spacing within the 40 ns simulation time. The expansion distance of montmorillonite sheets is determined as the average value of the expansion distances of the five layers of montmorillonite. By comparing with a blank control group in the absence of any antiswelling agent, the inhibition capability of cationic antiswelling agents on montmorillonite swelling can be quantified.

Influence of Molecular Structure on Efficiencies of Antiswelling Agents

To explore the influence of functional groups in the molecular structure of antiswelling agents on their performance, different functional groups were inserted near the methyl ester trimethylammonium polymer, the amide trimethylammonium polymer, and the polyethylene phenyl trimethylammonium quaternary ammonium group, as shown in Figure . Nineteen new cationic polymer structures were constructed. For the convenience of subsequent analysis, the α and β positions near the cationic group are defined as follows: the α position refers to the three methyl groups directly connected to the quaternary ammonium nitrogen, while the β position represents the methylene group connected to the quaternary ammonium nitrogen. Positions of the α and β carbon and hydrogens as well as the naming of their 14 combinations, hereafter referred to as subgroups, are demonstrated in Figure .

Previous studies have shown that insertion of functional groups can effectively regulate the charge distribution of neighboring α-C and the H atoms, thereby adjusting the relevant properties of the organic cations.

With density functional theory (DFT) calculations, the partial charge distribution of each newly constructed molecule was obtained. Changes in charge distribution caused by the insertion of functional groups may significantly enhance or inhibit the electrostatic adsorption capacity of antiswelling agents on clay mineral surfaces. To quantitatively evaluate the influence of different functional groups, Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient and Pearson’s linear correlation coefficient were used to assess the impact of charge distribution on the antiswelling efficiency of antiswelling agents. Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient is suitable for detecting nonlinear but ordered data relationships and is not sensitive to outliers; in contrast, Pearson’s linear correlation coefficient is applicable for the analysis of linear relationships with normal distribution and no significant outliers. The calculation and comparison of the two correlation coefficients were showcased in the work by Hauke and Kossowski. , By comparing the charge distribution at various sites between the original reference antiswelling agents and modified antiswelling agents and analyzing the changes in the distances between montmorillonite layers M1, M2, M3, M4, and M5, the correlation between charge distribution and antiswelling performance was explored.

Results and Discussion

Influence of Functional Groups on Charge Distribution of Antiswelling Agents

As the core region of antiswelling agents, the nitrogen atom of the quaternary ammonium group is significantly affected by the polarity and electron-donating properties of neighboring functional groups. The partial charge calculation results show that the charge of the quaternary ammonium nitrogen atom fluctuates greatly due to the influence of different functional groups introduced. For example, the charge on the N atom (referred to as N+ hereafter as defined in Figure a) in each antiswelling agent molecule increased by 20–60% (Figure ). This may be because of the electron-attracting effect of the oxygen on the ketone group, resulting in an increase in the positive charge of N+. However, in molecules with thioketone groups introduced, the average charge of N+ decreased by 0.27.

For the αβ-C subgroup, with the introduction of O atoms, S atoms, and ketone groups, the charge values show a clear increasing trend. Particularly, for the polyethylbenzene trimethylammonium molecules (labeled as c in Figure ), the average negative charge value of the αβ-C part decreases by 1.058. The thioketone group, S atom, and O atom all have high electron-attracting or lone pair electron-repelling effects. These characteristics affect the redistribution of charges within the molecule. Due to the high electronegativity of sulfur and oxygen, they are prone to attract the surrounding electrons through the inductive effect, resulting in a decrease in electron density around αβ-C, which manifests as an increase in the charge values of these carbon atoms. The β-C subsection shows a similar charge change to that of αβ-C. Unlike methyl ester trimethylammonium polymers and polyethylbenzene trimethylammonium, the amide-based trimethylammonium polymer antiswelling agents show opposite results, with a general increase in absolute charge values (more negative). The reason may be because of the amide group (−CONH−). The amide group itself has a conjugation effect and can stabilize the redistribution of the electron cloud. The nitrogen atom and oxygen atom in the amide group have lone pair electrons and can produce conjugation and inductive effects with other functional groups in the molecular structure (such as thioketone groups, oxygen, and sulfur), pushing electron density toward αβ-C, thereby increasing the negative charge at these positions in the amide-based trimethylammonium polymer molecules. The carbon atoms in the benzene ring do not have lone pair electrons, instead, they just form a closed π electron system and cannot effectively push electron density toward αβ-C. Therefore, the benzene ring has a limited effect on the charge distribution of the αβ-C subgroup. Even if electron-withdrawing or electron-donating functional groups are introduced, due to spatial hindrance, the inductive effect upon the αβ-C subgroup is weak, resulting in no obvious charge variation. The inductive effect of functional groups gradually weakens with increasing distance, therefore the charge distribution at the α-C position is less directly affected by functional groups that are farther away. For example, the electron-withdrawing or electron-donating effects of the amide group or carboxyl group are mainly reflected in areas closer in their vicinity (β-C) and have less influence on the α-C subgroup.

The α-H subgroup generally shows a higher partial charge after the introduction of highly polar functional groups, an example being the sulfone group, in the presence of which the charge value of the α-H part increases significantly. This is due to the high electronegativity of the sulfone group. When the electron density around α-H decreases, its charge value increases. The methyl ester trimethylammonium polymer is completely opposite to other antiswelling agents. The average positive charge value of α-H decreases by 0.4. This may be because the electron-withdrawing effect of the carboxyl group is relatively limited and there is no strong conjugation effect. It is also notable that the charge value of the β-C subgroup increased. The strong electronegativity and inductive effect of the sulfone group lead to the attraction of the electron density of the methyl (α) subgroup. The decrease in the charge value on the β-H subgroup means that the electron density at these positions has increased. The introduced functional groups such as the benzene ring, carboxyl group, and ketone group do not substantially attract electrons. Instead, they may push electrons toward β-H, increasing its electron density. This may be because the π electron cloud of the benzene ring has a certain repulsive effect, forcing some electron density to move toward the adjacent β position, thus increasing the electron density of β-H.

With the introduction of different functional groups, the total charge on the TC subgroup exhibits an overall upward trend, which may be because most of the functional groups have affinity for electrons which leads to inductive effect, resulting in a slight decrease in electron density in the quaternary ammonium group and ethylene region. To further explore the correlation between the change in charge values and the antiswelling efficiency of the antiswelling agent, Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient and the linear correlation coefficient between the two were calculated.

Effect of Charge Distribution on Antiswelling Efficiency

A Spearman correlation analysis was conducted between the charges on different subgroups in the quaternary ammonium group and the antiswelling effect of the antiswelling agent. The results are shown in Figure . Comparison of the expansion distance between modified antiexpansion agent and pure water montmorillonite is shown in Figure .

As shown in Figure , the Spearman coefficient of the partial charge on N+ is relatively low (ρ = 0.2173), indicating that the charge on N+ subgroup does not pose a significant impact on the antiswelling effect.

In comparison, the Spearman correlation coefficient of the β-C subgroup is −0.4108 (Figure ), and the corresponding linear correlation coefficient is −0.3773, both showing a negative correlation. In other words, the more negative the charge on the β-C subgroup, the better the antiswelling effect of montmorillonite. The αβ-C subgroup has a Spearman correlation coefficient ρ = −0.44 and a linear correlation coefficient r = 0.40, exhibiting the highest correlation among the structures constructed in this work. It is worth noting that Figure indicates that the charge variation range of α-C is relatively weak upon tuning of the molecular structure, and the Spearman correlation of α-C is lower than that of β-C. It is therefore clear that the dominant contributor for the relatively high correlation coefficient of αβ-C is β-C. The reason may be that the charge distribution at the β position is relatively flexible and more susceptible to the inductive effect of external functional groups. In other words, due to the linking position (between the quaternary ammonium group and the functional groups) of the β subgroup, its electron distribution can more sensitively respond to the electrical changes of surrounding functional groups. As for α-C subgroups, they are closer to the core structure of the molecule, and its charge distribution is strongly constrained by the quaternary ammonium portion of the molecule, and therefore its contribution to the antiswelling effect is relatively lower.

Among the methyl and methylene subgroups, the one with the highest correlation coefficient is β-CH2, with ρ = – 0.44 and r = −0.35, as shown in Figure . The more significant correlation coefficient of β-CH2 is mainly due to its relatively flexible charge distribution which is susceptible to the inductive effect of external functional groups. This allows the negative charge of β-CH2 to more effectively participate in the electrostatic interaction with the montmorillonite surface, enhancing the adsorption of the antiswelling agent on montmorillonite, thereby better stabilizing the interlayer spacing and inhibiting swelling.

With the above analyses, it can be seen that the subgroups whose charges have only weak impacts on the antiswelling effect are mostly due to the dispersion of charge distribution or limitations imposed by the molecular structure, which prevents the effective transfer of positive charges to the outer structure of the molecule, resulting in weakened adsorption on the montmorillonite surface. In contrast, those with stronger correlations indicate that by adjusting the local charges of the molecule, especially the concentrated charge distribution in αβ-C and αβ-H, the adsorption of the antiswelling agents can be effectively enhanced (Figure ). This suggests that in molecular design, the performance of the antiswelling agents can be further improved by optimizing the concentration of charges, and that the charges on αβ-C and αβ-H subgroups can function as potential indicators.

Influence of Functional Groups on Clay Interlayer Distance Stability

To evaluate the effect of the antiswelling agent’s performance, the standard deviation was used to assess the fluctuation in the distance between neighboring montmorillonite sheets. A smaller standard deviation indicates less variation in interlayer spacing, suggesting a stronger clay stabilizing effect of the antiswelling agent; conversely, a larger standard deviation indicates poorer inhibitory effects of the antiswelling agent.

In Figure , the antiswelling agents containing carboxyl groups and benzene rings exhibit the lowest standard deviations, at 0.0627 and 0.0780, respectively, indicating that these molecules can form stable adsorptions between montmorillonite layers, resulting in less fluctuation in interlayer spacing and showing relatively strong inhibitory effects. By contrast, the standard deviation of molecules containing thioketone groups is 0.1076, at a medium level, indicating their limited inhibitory effect on montmorillonite interlayer spacing. The electronegativity and electron-withdrawing effect of thioketone groups are moderate, which can enhance the polarity of the molecule to some extent, and thus form moderate electrostatic adsorption with the montmorillonite surface, partially reducing the fluctuation of interlayer spacing. However, their adsorption strength is not sufficient to completely stabilize the interlayer spacing, so their effect is inferior to that of carboxyl and benzene ring functional groups.

The molecular structures containing sulfone, ketone groups and sulfur have higher standard deviations, showing greater fluctuations in interlayer spacing, which indicates their relatively weak inhibitory effects. Especially for the structures containing sulfone groups, their standard deviations reach the highest, indicating the weakest effect in terms of interlayer spacing stability. This may be due to the strong electron-withdrawing effects of these functional groups, leading to an uneven charge distribution within the molecule, with electron density concentrated in specific areas, weakening the overall adsorption of the molecule on the clay surface. Therefore, these highly polar or strongly inductive functional groups are not necessarily ideal for stabilizing the interlayer spacing of montmorillonite sheets.

Conclusions

In this study, the in-depth exploration on the influence of different functional groups on the charge distribution of quaternary ammonium type antiswelling agents and their effects on the inhibition of interlayer swelling of montmorillonite was conducted through molecular dynamics simulations.

By inserting different functional groups near the quaternary ammonium group, 19 antiswelling agent molecules were constructed based on three reference structures, and the distribution of atomic charges within the molecules was calculated using quantum mechanics. The results show that the introduction of different functional groups significantly affects the charge distribution of the quaternary ammonium nitrogen atom, α-CH3, and β-CH2. Through correlation analysis of charge distribution and antiswelling effect, the following conclusions can be drawn:

  • Functional group insertion significantly alters the charge on the quaternary ammonium nitrogen (N+); notably, ketone groups increase N+ charge, while thioketone groups decrease it.

  • Molecular charge distribution, particularly at the αβ-C subgroup, is markedly affected by functional groups. Oxygen/sulfur atoms and ketone groups increase αβ-C charge (inductive effect), and amide groups increase its absolute charge (conjugation effect). In contrast, benzene rings have limited influence on αβ-C charge, and the α-C position is less directly impacted.

  • Highly polar functional groups generally increase α-H charge density; for instance, sulfone groups enhance α-H positive charge, whereas carboxyl groups (in methyl ester trimethylammonium polymers) reduce it.

  • A strong negative correlation exists between the antiswelling effect and charges on β-C (ρ = −0.41) and β-CH2 (ρ = −0.44) subgroups, suggesting that increased absolute charge at these sites enhances montmorillonite adsorption. α-C and β-H charges show weaker correlations.

  • Molecules with carboxyl or benzene groups, exhibiting uniform charge distribution and moderate polarity, provide the best montmorillonite interlayer stabilization. Thioketone-containing agents show limited efficacy, while those with sulfone, ketone, or sulfur groups perform poorly in stabilizing interlayer distance.

This research provides an important theoretical basis for the rational design and development of more efficient antiswelling agents. The practicality of molecular dynamics simulation in accelerating this process is particularly notable. The computer simulation screening of a series of candidate molecules is expected to be completed within 2–4 weeks, which contrasts sharply with the 6–12 months usually required for the synthesis and evaluation of the same experiments. Future research will focus on the synthesis of these computationally prioritized antiswelling agents and the subsequent verification of their experimental performance. This crucial step will help further confirm the reliability and practical application potential of the simulation guidance method, ultimately aiming to provide an effective technical solution for alleviating the widespread clay swelling problem in oil and gas field operations.
