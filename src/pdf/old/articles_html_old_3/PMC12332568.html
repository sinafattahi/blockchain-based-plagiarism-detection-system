Abstract

This study presents a comprehensive computational investigation into the separation of CH4/CO2 gas mixtures using nanochannels based on graphene, silicon carbide (SiC), and boron nitride (BN). Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were employed to evaluate the adsorption dynamics, permeation rates, and separation efficiency of these two-dimensional materials under varying pressures and configurations. Graphene exhibited superior CH4 permeability due to its atomic thickness and high surface energy, though selectivity was limited by weak interaction with CO2. SiC demonstrated moderate gas permeability with enhanced CO2 selectivity, attributed to its stronger adsorption sites and balanced transport dynamics. BN displayed exceptional impermeability, effectively acting as a molecular sieve for CO2, driven by its high surface polarity and robust barrier properties. The diffusion coefficients and interaction energies revealed a strong material dependence, with CO2 exhibiting higher diffusion and adsorption on polar surfaces. At the same time, CH4 showed reduced mobility due to weaker van der Waals interactions. These findings underscore the significance of nanoscale structural control and surface engineering in optimizing gas separation performance. The insights provided by this study lay a robust foundation for designing hybrid nanochannel architectures, which leverage the unique properties of each material to achieve advanced separation systems. This work holds promise for developing scalable, energy-efficient membranes for clean energy applications, carbon capture, and industrial gas processing.

1. Introduction

The separation of methane (CH4) and carbon dioxide (CO2) gas mixtures is a critical process various industrial applications, such as the purification of natural gas, the refining of biogas, and the storage and capture of carbon dioxide. − Methane functions as a crucial fuel and energy source, whereas carbon dioxide is a prominent greenhouse gas contributing to global climate change. The increasing need for clean energy and environmental sustainability has heightened the pursuit of efficient and economical techniques for gas separation. , Traditional separation methods, including cryogenic distillation, chemical adsorption, and pressure swing adsorption (PSA), are prevalent yet frequently linked to substantial energy expenditure and operational intricacy. − This has generated significant research interest in enhanced membrane-based separation technologies, especially those employing nanoscale materials with customized characteristics. ,

Two-dimensional (2D) nanomaterials, including graphene, boron nitride (BN), and silicon carbide (SiC), have emerged as viable alternatives for gas separation owing to their distinctive structural, mechanical, and chemical properties. , Graphene, consisting of a monolayer of sp2-hybridized carbon atoms organized in a hexagonal lattice, demonstrates outstanding mechanical strength, significant chemical stability, and extraordinary gas permeability owing to its atomic thickness. − Nonetheless, its intrinsic hydrophobicity and restricted chemical activity may diminish its gas separation selectivity. This constraint has generated interest in surface modification approaches, including functionalization, chemical doping, and defect engineering, to improve graphene’s selective gas transport characteristics.

Boron nitride, which has a structure akin to graphene but is composed of alternating boron and nitrogen atoms, provides unique benefits owing to its polar surface and chemical inertness. ,

The polar surfaces enhance CO2 adsorption through interactions with its quadrupole moment, despite CO2 being a nonpolar molecule, while rejecting nonpolar gases such as CH4. This feature renders BN especially appealing for applications necessitating selective CO2 capture. Furthermore, BN membranes exhibit exceptional thermal and chemical resilience, rendering them appropriate for demanding operational conditions. Silicon carbide, a covalent combination of silicon and carbon, possesses remarkable mechanical strength, tolerance to high temperatures, and chemical stability. These characteristics allow SiC-based membranes to operate efficiently under harsh conditions, while surface changes can improve their gas separation efficacy. ,

The molecular processes regulating gas transport through these nanomaterials are determined by size exclusion, adsorption intensity, and surface–gas interactions. Due to the similar molecular sizes of CH4 and CO2, with kinetic diameters of 3.8 Å and 3.3 Å, respectively, effective separation necessitates meticulous control of pore size and surface engineering. It should be mentioned that the kinetic diameter is the smallest effective diameter of a molecule, which determines its ability to pass through pores or channels. , Conventional polymeric membranes frequently encounter a compromise between permeability and selectivity, constraining their industrial utility. Conversely, 2D materials facilitate the fabrication of ultrathin membranes with precisely regulated pore structures, permitting selective molecular sieving and improved gas permeability. ,

Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations have demonstrated significant utility in examining gas transport within nanomaterials. MD simulations, through the modeling of atomic-level interactions, yield comprehensive insights into adsorption dynamics, permeation rates, and selectivity processes across many operating circumstances. , The simulations employ parameters like the Lennard-Jones potential to represent van der Waals interactions and electrostatic forces between gas molecules and the membrane surface. , Temperature, pressure, and gas concentrations can be adjusted to replicate realistic conditions, allowing researchers to refine membrane design before experimental testing.

Recent studies underscore the potential of tailoring these 2D nanomaterials for enhanced CH4/CO2 separation by leveraging their intrinsic properties. For instance, porous graphene membranes with angstrom-sized pores, created via UV-induced oxidative etching, have demonstrated selective sieving of CO2 over CH4, as shown by Koenig et al., due to precise size exclusion. Similarly, Roy et al. report that h-BN’s polar B–N bonds and high thermal conductivity (up to 751 W m–1 K–1) enhance CO2 adsorption and stability under elevated temperatures, while Prakash et al. highlight SiC’s mechanical robustness (Young’s modulus ∼610–660 GPa for nanorods) and chemical inertness, enabling operation in corrosive environments. , These findings suggest that strategic pore engineering and surface functionalization could amplify the separation capabilities of these materials beyond current benchmarks.

Notwithstanding comprehensive studies on individual materials, the potential of integrating graphene, BN, and SiC in hybrid nanochannel architectures remains predominantly unexamined. A synergistic amalgamation of these materials may yield improved separation efficacy by utilizing their complementary attributes. For example, graphene’s elevated permeability may promote CH4 movement, whereas BN’s polar surface could improve CO2 adsorption. Furthermore, the mechanical stability of SiC may facilitate the creation of resilient membrane structures that can endure industrial-scale operations. This integration may address the shortcomings of single-component membranes, resulting in multifunctional separation systems with enhanced performance.

This work examines CH4/CO2 gas separation utilizing graphene/BN and SiC-based nanochannels via molecular dynamics simulations. The study assesses gas adsorption characteristics, transport mechanisms, and separation efficacy under diverse configurations and operational parameters. Comprehending the relationship among pore size, surface chemistry, and gas-membrane interactions would facilitate the systematic design of sophisticated separation membranes. This study offers molecular-level insights into gas separation processes, potentially guiding the creation of advanced membranes characterized by excellent selectivity, permeability, and durability.

This finding has significant implications for environmental sustainability, energy efficiency, and industrial gas processing. This study enhances the understanding of nanostructured membranes for gas separation by advancing computational methods in material design. The results may expedite the advancement of scalable, energy-efficient, and economical separation technologies that can tackle urgent environmental and energy issues. This study aims to build a novel paradigm in CH4/CO2 separation by integrating graphene, BN, and SiC into hybrid nanochannels, thereby facilitating revolutionary solutions in clean energy and environmental conservation.

2. Methodology

To investigate the CH4/CO2 gas mixture separation using graphene, SiC, and BN nanochannels, molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were conducted within a simulation box measuring 25 × 25 × 80 Å3. To investigate the CH4/CO2 gas mixture separation using graphene, SiC, and BN nanochannels, molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were conducted within a simulation box measuring 25 × 25 × 80 Å3. Figure represents the structures of the three utilized nanosheets as a nanochannel in this project. The interlayer spacing between nanosheets forming the nanochannels, was optimized based on the kinetic diameters of CH4 and CO2 through preliminary simulations. This spacing ensures selective passage of CO2 while maintaining structural integrity of the nanochannel.

For graphene nanochannels, the interlayer spacing was set to 6.20 Å, while for SiC nanochannels, it was 6.20 Å with alternating atomic layers, and for BN nanochannels, the interlayer spacing was slightly larger at 6.80 Å to accommodate the molecular interactions and transport dynamics. These optimized distances ensure effective gas separation performance while maintaining stability within the simulation framework. The spacings (6.20 Å for graphene and SiC, and 6.80 Å for BN) were selected to exceed the kinetic diameters of both molecules, allowing for permeation while considering the structural stability and interaction dynamics of each material. These values were determined through preliminary MD simulations, where various interlayer distances were tested to identify a threshold that balances permeability and selectivity. The criteria included maximizing gas flux while maintaining structural integrity under applied pressures (100–5000 kPa), as assessed by monitoring the potential energy and atomic displacement of the nanosheets.

The dimensions of the box in three directions of x, y, and z are equal to 25 Å, 25 Å, and 80 Å, respectively. The periodic boundary conditions were conducted in three directions. Each nanochannel was centrally positioned in the box (−12.5 ≤ z ≤ 12.5), oriented along the z-axis, to facilitate gas transport. The simulation setup comprised three distinct regions: the feed section (z < −12.5 Å) containing a mixture of CH4 and CO2 gas molecules, the nanochannel region, and a downstream section (z > 12.5 Å) for analyzing gas permeation. At the end edge of the feeding section, a pristine graphene sheet is placed, acting like a barrier. Figure shows a visual depiction of the simulated systems.

The feed section was modeled with a CH4/CO2 gas mixture ratio of 50:50, incorporating 100 molecules of each gas. The 50:50 CH4/CO2 gas mixture ratio was chosen to represent a balanced composition that simplifies the analysis of competitive adsorption and permeation behaviors between the two gases, providing a clear baseline for comparing the separation capabilities of graphene, SiC, and BN nanochannels. This ratio is relevant to real-world applications such as biogas upgrading, where CH4 and CO2 are often present in comparable proportions (e.g., 40–60% CH4 and 30–50% CO2), depending on the source. It also aligns with conditions encountered in landfill gas recovery and certain natural gas streams, where CO2 removal is critical for enhancing methane purity. By using an equimolar mixture, the study ensures that neither gas dominates the initial conditions, allowing the intrinsic material properties (e.g., polarity, pore size) to dictate separation efficiency, as demonstrated in the permeation and density profile results. The Lennard-Jones (LJ) potential was employed to describe van der Waals interactions between gas molecules and nanochannel surfaces. The geometric optimization of the nanochannel structures was performed using density functional theory calculations at the B3LYP level with the 6–31G­(d,p) basis set. , Convergence criteria included an energy threshold of 10–5 hartree, a maximum gradient tolerance of 10–4 hartree/Å, and a displacement threshold of 10–3 Å, ensuring structural stability. These settings were chosen to balance computational cost with accuracy for the relatively small unit cells of graphene, SiC, and BN nanosheets. Basis set superposition error (BSSE) correction was not explicitly applied in this study, as the focus was on isolated nanochannel geometries rather than intermolecular binding energies, where BSSE is more critical. The 6–31G­(d,p) basis set, with polarization functions, mitigates BSSE to some extent, and the large interlayer spacing in the MD setup further reduces overlap effects. These calculations, conducted using the GAMESS software, ensured that the nanochannel geometries were optimized for stability and compatibility within the MD simulation framework. Partial atomic charges for the nanochannels were assigned using the CHELPG method, enabling accurate electrostatic interactions between the gas molecules and nanochannel surfaces.

The CHARMM force field and the NAMD package were used to execute the final simulations. − Afterward, the VMD software was used for postprocessing the obtained MD trajectory files. The system stabilization involved three distinct procedures. Initially, energy minimization was performed over 50,000 time steps at 0 K to eliminate unfavorable conformations. Subsequently, a gradual temperature ramping was applied, increasing the system temperature to 300 K over 20,000 time steps using the Langevin thermostat to maintain thermal stability. Finally, a range of pressures, from 100 KPa to 5000 KPa, was applied along the z-direction to drive gas transport through the nanochannels. Particles were driven through the nanochannels by applying pressure, creating the driving force for momentum transfer along the z-direction. Varying pressures exerted on the system resulted in a force on the particles, calculated using the following eq :

F=ΔP×An 1

in this equation, F is the external force (N), A is the area of mass transfer (m2; 25 × 25 Å2), ΔP is the difference in hydrostatic pressure (Pa; 100–5000 kPa), and n is the number of gas molecules (400 total). The simulation settings employed a 12 Å cutoff and 14 Å pair list distance with a 1 fs time step before conducting the MD simulations. This force was uniformly distributed across the gas molecules in the feed section (z < −12.5 Å), simulating a pressure gradient akin to experimental membrane setups. The pressure range aligns with realistic conditions in industrial gas separation processes, such as pressure swing adsorption (typically 100–1000 kPa) and high-pressure natural gas processing (up to 5000 kPa), ensuring relevance to practical applications.

For data analysis, the VMD software package was used to evaluate density profiles, gas permeation rates, and diffusion coefficients across the nanochannels. The interaction energy between gas molecules and nanochannel surfaces was computed to understand the preferential adsorption and transport mechanisms. Density maps of CH4 and CO2 were visualized for different pressure conditions to highlight the separation performance of graphene, SiC, and BN nanochannels. Additionally, diffusion coefficients were calculated using the Einstein relation to assess the mobility of gas molecules in the presence of nanochannels.

The separation performance of each nanochannel material was evaluated by comparing the permeation rates and selectivity for CH4 and CO2. This systematic computational approach provides molecular-level insights into the gas separation mechanisms of graphene, SiC, and BN nanochannels, contributing to developing advanced membrane technologies for efficient CH4/CO2 separation.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Permeation

The provided diagram in Figure illustrates the permeation behavior of CH4 molecules graphene, SiC, and BN nanochannels as a function of applied pressure ranging from 100 to 5000 KPa. According to the diagram, the permeation of CH4 molecules through graphene nanochannels is the highest permeability among the three materials. At lower pressures (100–1000 KPa), the number of passed CH4 molecules increases sharply, indicating that graphene is highly effective at allowing gas flow even under minimal pressure. Beyond 2000 KPa, the curve begins to level off, suggesting that the permeation rate approaches saturation at higher pressures. In contrast, the SiC nanochannels show moderate permeation performance. The number of CH4 molecules passing through the nanochannels increases gradually as the pressure rises, with no evidence of the sharp rise observed for graphene. This steady but lower permeability indicates that SiC nanosheets allow some gas transport but are significantly less permeable compared to graphene. The BN nanochannels exhibit almost no permeation across the entire pressure range. The negligible number of CH4 molecules passing through the BN membrane highlights its exceptional impermeability. This property makes BN an ideal candidate for applications requiring highly effective gas barriers. Overall, the diagram underscores the distinct permeation characteristics of these materials. Graphene, with its atomic thickness and unique structure, allows for significant gas flow and could be suitable for applications requiring high permeability. SiC provides a moderate alternative, while BN serves as an excellent barrier material due to its impermeable nature. The trends also reveal that while pressure enhances permeation in all materials to varying extents, the rate and mechanism of gas transport are highly material-dependent.

3.2. Density Profile

Figure represents the CO2 density profile through graphene, SiC, and BN nanochannels, which provides insightful observations into the interaction dynamics of CO2 molecules within these nanomaterials. Density profiles are time-averaged over the 10 ns simulation at a pressure of 500 KPa, computed using VMD by binning molecule positions along the z-axis every 100 fs, then averaging. This provides a steady-state view of gas distribution, not a snapshot, and is independent of time within the equilibrated system, as confirmed by stable energy and flux post-1 ns equilibration. The profiles reflect equilibrium behavior under constant pressure. Across the studied nanochannels, the density profile from −40 Å to around −20 Å exhibits a consistent trend for CO2, suggesting uniform behavior in the initial stages of molecular interaction with the channel surfaces. This phase likely represents the initial adsorption where CO2 molecules begin to interact with the nanochannel walls. From −20 Å, a divergence in behavior becomes apparent; while the density profile for CO2 in BN nanochannels increases, indicating enhanced adsorption or possibly the onset of layering of molecules at the surface, the profiles for SiC and Graphene remain similar. This suggests that BN nanochannels might offer a distinct advantage in terms of CO2 capture efficiency at this intermediate. In the range of −10 to 10 Å, fluctuations in the density profile are observable across all systems. Notably, Graphene exhibits the highest fluctuations, suggesting dynamic interactions and possibly multiple adsorption and desorption events. These fluctuations could be indicative of less stable CO2 adsorption within Graphene compared to SiC and BN. On the other hand, BN shows the lowest fluctuations, which may indicate more uniform and stable molecular layering at the surface.

The Figure represents CH4 density profile through Graphene, SiC, and BN nanochannels at 500 KPa. From −40 Å to −10 Å, the density profile exhibits a consistent trend across the nanochannels, with BN showing the highest CH4 density and graphene displaying the lowest. This part of the profile indicates that BN nanochannels, possibly due to their polar surface properties, provide stronger adsorption sites for CH4 molecules compared to the other materials. This strong interaction could be attributed to potential dipole-induced attractions within the BN structure, which are more effective in trapping CH4 molecules despite its generally nonpolar nature. Graphene’s minimal interaction at these positions likely reflects its inherent hydrophobicity and less favorable van der Waals interactions.

At −10 Å to 10 Å, the profiles diverge markedly for SiC and graphene. The fluctuations noted in this region for both materials suggest dynamic adsorption and desorption processes, with graphene showing less pronounced fluctuations. This behavior in graphene could be indicative of a smoother gas transport through the channel. In contrast, SiC exhibits more significant fluctuations, which could be related to stronger localized interactions within the channel. This might imply a more complex surface interaction in SiC nanochannels, potentially offering higher selectivity but possibly at the expense of reduced gas flow rate.

Beyond 20 Å until the end of the Z-axis position, the trend normalizes and becomes roughly consistent among the materials, suggesting that the earlier interactions have reached a state of equilibrium or that the molecular penetration into the nanochannels has stabilized. The observation that methane molecules do not enter the BN channels, with the density dropping to zero beyond −10 Å, highlights the exceptional selectivity and barrier properties of BN for methane.

This confirms BN’s role as a potent barrier to methane diffusion, making it ideal for high-performance applications that require robust separation and minimal cross-channel gas migration.

The diffusion coefficient was derived by analyzing the MSD over time while tracking the positions of particles throughout the simulation. Reliable estimation of the diffusion coefficient requires sufficient temporal sampling, as the relationship between MSD and time is a fundamental aspect of diffusion theory. To ensure statistical reliability, ten independent MD simulations were performed using identical initial coordinates, each with a duration of 10 ns. The MSD was computed for each simulation, and the diffusion coefficient was calculated by averaging the MSD values obtained from these trajectories over the entire simulation period.

For graphene nanochannels, the diffusion coefficient is initially high at low pressures, ranging between 6 and 8 Å2/ns. However, as pressure increases, a significant decline in the diffusion coefficient is observed, particularly at pressures around 2000 kPa, where the diffusion coefficient drops sharply. This trend indicates that intensified molecular interactions at higher pressures restrict the mobility of CO2 molecules within the graphene channels. SiC nanochannels exhibit the highest diffusion coefficients at low pressures, with values approaching 10 Å2/ns. This suggests minimal resistance to CO2 transport at lower pressures. However, as pressure increases, the diffusion coefficient decreases sharply, reaching approximately 2–3 Å2/ns at 2000 kPa, indicating substantial hindrance to molecular mobility under high-pressure conditions.

BN nanochannels demonstrate an intermediate behavior. At low pressures, the diffusion coefficient is lower than that of SiC but higher than graphene. Notably, BN channels maintain relatively higher diffusion coefficients as pressure increases, exhibiting less decline compared to graphene and SiC. This behavior indicates moderate interactions between CO2 molecules and BN channel surfaces, allowing for more consistent diffusion performance across a range of pressures. The diffusion behavior is governed by molecular interactions between CO2 and the nanochannel surfaces. Graphene’s strong π–π interactions result in increased molecular confinement at higher pressures, while SiC’s less interactive surface enables freer movement at low pressures. BN, with its polar nature, strikes a balance, moderating the pressure-dependent decline in diffusion.

Figure illustrates the diffusion coefficient of CH4 molecules as a function of pressure through graphene, SiC, and BN nanochannels. The trends reveal significant differences in how the nanochannel materials influence CH4 mobility under pressure. For graphene nanochannels, the diffusion coefficient remains consistently high across all pressures. At low pressures (∼500 kPa), the diffusion coefficient is approximately 7 Å2/ns and shows minimal variation even at 2000 kPa. This stability suggests that graphene nanochannels provide an environment with low resistance to CH4 diffusion, even under high-pressure conditions. In contrast, SiC nanochannels exhibit considerably lower diffusion coefficients at low pressures, around 2 Å2/ns. The diffusion coefficient remains relatively unchanged with increasing pressure, indicating stronger interactions between CH4 molecules and the SiC surface that limit molecular mobility. For BN nanochannels, the diffusion coefficient is the lowest among the three materials, remaining around 1 Å2/ns across all pressure ranges. This behavior suggests significant resistance to CH4 transport due to stronger molecular interactions between CH4 and the BN surface. The polar nature of BN likely contributes to this restriction, enhancing adsorption or interaction forces that hinder mobility.

These findings highlight the substantial influence of nanochannel material properties on CH4 diffusion. Graphene’s high diffusion coefficients reflect weak interactions with CH4, making it suitable for high-mobility applications. SiC, while less conducive to CH4 diffusion, maintains a moderate and stable performance. BN, with its strong molecular interactions, provides the least favorable environment for CH4 transport. Such differences emphasize the critical role of material selection for applications requiring precise control of gas transport, such as separation or storage.

3.5. Interaction Energy

The interaction energies in Figures and represent the total nonbonded energy (sum of Lennard-Jones and electrostatic interactions) between all gas molecules and the nanochannel surface, as computed in NAMD. These high values reflect the cumulative interactions across the system rather than per-molecule energies. The interaction energy between CO2 molecules and nanochannels in kcal/mol as a function of time during a 10 ns simulation is given in the Figure . This energy, serves as an indicator of the strength and stability of the interactions between CO2 and the nanochannel materials. For graphene, the interaction energy decreases steadily during the initial phase of the simulation, stabilizing around −800 kcal/mol after approximately 5 ns. This behavior reflects the moderate affinity of CO2 molecules for graphene’s surface, driven primarily by van der Waals forces and weak quadrupole interaction between CO2’s quadrupole moment and graphene’s π-electron system. The relatively constant energy after stabilization suggests that CO2 molecules are evenly adsorbed on the graphene surface without significant changes in the adsorption configuration over time. The interaction energy for SiC nanochannels also decreases over time, stabilizing at around −600 kcal/mol. Compared to graphene, the SiC nanochannels exhibit a slightly weaker interaction with CO2 molecules, which is consistent with SiC’s surface properties. The gradual decrease in energy during the initial phase suggests that CO2 molecules adjust their positions to optimize adsorption before achieving a stable configuration. In BN nanochannels, the interaction energy reaches the lowest value, stabilizing at approximately −1200 kcal/mol. This indicates that BN nanochannels have the strongest affinity for CO2 molecules among the three materials. The significant energy drop during the early part of the simulation suggests rapid adsorption and strong binding of CO2 molecules to the BN surface. This behavior is likely due to the highly polar nature of BN, which enhances electrostatic interactions with the quadrupole moment of CO2.

Therefore, the interaction energy analysis reveals material-dependent adsorption behaviors. BN nanochannels exhibit the strongest interaction with CO2, followed by graphene and then SiC. This trend highlights BN’s potential as a superior material for CO2 capture and storage applications, while graphene and SiC may be more suitable for scenarios where moderate interactions and easier desorption are advantageous. These findings provide valuable insights into the suitability of different nanochannel materials for gas separation and environmental applications.

4. Conclusion

This computational study provides detailed insights into the CH4/CO2 gas separation mechanisms of graphene, SiC, and BN nanochannels, revealing their distinct capabilities and limitations. Graphene demonstrated exceptional CH4 permeability due to its atomic thickness and minimal resistance to gas flow, making it suitable for high-throughput separation processes. However, its limited selectivity for CO2 highlights the need for surface functionalization or chemical doping to enhance its separation performance. SiC nanochannels exhibited a balanced performance, with moderate permeability and enhanced CO2 selectivity, due to their ability to form stronger adsorption interactions with polar gases. BN, with its polar surface and robust structural integrity, emerged as the most selective material, effectively capturing CO2 while rejecting CH4. Its impermeability underscores its potential as a barrier material in applications demanding high separation specificity.

The study also highlights the significant role of nanoscale features such as pore size, surface chemistry, and material polarity in determining gas transport behaviors. The interaction energy analyses revealed that CO2 exhibited stronger binding to BN and SiC surfaces compared to graphene, while CH4’s interaction was relatively weak across all materials. Diffusion coefficient trends further emphasized the material-dependent transport dynamics, with CO2 generally exhibiting higher mobility in polar materials. The findings advocate for integrating graphene, SiC, and BN into hybrid nanochannel systems to exploit their complementary properties. Such systems can enhance separation efficiency by combining graphene’s high permeability, SiC’s balanced selectivity, and BN’s impermeability. This approach offers a pathway to overcome the trade-offs between permeability and selectivity encountered in conventional membranes.
