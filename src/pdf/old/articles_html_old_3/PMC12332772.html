Abstract

With the aim of enhancing the HER activity of the previously described bioinspired [NiFe]-hydrogenase complex [LN2S2NiIIFeIICp­(CO)]+ (LNiFe, with LN2S2 = 2,2′-(2,2′-bipyridine-6,6′-diyl)­bis­(1,1′-diphenylethanethiolate) and Cp = cyclopentadienyl), the electronic structure of the LN2S2 site has been fine-tuned. In L OMe NiFe, the bipyridine (Bpy) unit was substituted with methoxy electron-donating groups, while in L Phen NiFe, the Bpy unit was replaced with the 1,10-phenanthroline backbone. These complexes were fully characterized, and their HER activity was investigated. A mechanistic study was conducted by using IR and EPR spectroscopies combined with density functional theory (DFT) calculations. Both complexes act as efficient electrocatalysts to produce H2, following an ECEC mechanism, starting from the monoreduced species. L OMe NiFe exhibits the fastest kinetics among the series (K obs = 1.6 × 104 s–1), attributed to its higher ΔpK a value for the protonation step of the two-electron reduced species, [L OMe NiFe] – . In contrast, L Phen NiFe exhibits the lowest overpotential, with a cathodic shift of 150 mV. This improved performance is attributed to the fact that the phenanthroline backbone is more easily reduced with respect to a bipyridine unit.

Keywords: bioinspired chemistry, NiFe hydrogenase, homogenous electrocatalysis, H2 production, mechanism elucidation

Introduction

Solar hydrogen production via artificial photosynthesis offers a promising pathway for generating clean and renewable fuel to contribute to the decarbonization of our society. − Switching from conventional fossil-fuel-based energy production, this alternative utilizes sunlight to produce H2 via the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER). The availability of efficient, robust, and cost-effective catalysts derived from abundant raw materials capable of splitting water into oxygen and hydrogen is critical for future applications.

The bioinspired approach has attracted significant interest in the search for efficient molecular HER catalysts. Drawing inspiration from hydrogenases (H2ases), metalloenzymes renowned for their remarkable ability to produce and oxidize hydrogen efficiently, , the development of active and robust mimics of their active site is becoming an active research field. − However, [NiFe]-H2ase has received considerably less attention than [FeFe]-H2ase, largely due to the synthetic challenges associated with developing heterodinuclear complexes. Its active site features a cluster with CO, CN–, and cysteine residues as additional ligands to complete the first coordination sphere of the metal ions. While several families of bioinspired models of the original heterodinuclear active site of [NiFe]-H2ase have been described, only a few synthetic molecular NiFe catalysts have shown significant activity in catalyzing the HER. − Among them, our group reported the complex [LN2S2 NiII FeII Cp­(CO)]+ (LN2S2 = 2,2′-(2,2′-bipyridine-6,6′diyl)­bis­(1,1′-diphenylethanethiolate), Figure ). LNiFe reproduces key structural and electronic features and HER electrocatalytic activity both in MeCN and in aqueous solutions (turnover frequency of 200 s–1 at pH 3, 7.2 × 106 turnover numbers in 10 h). Mechanistic investigations have shown that redox chemistry is mainly centered on the Ni site as in the [NiFe]-H2ase ,, and that the bipyridine (Bpy) unit acts as an electron reservoir. Consequently, the nature of the metal in the LN2S2 site has a limited impact on the HER performance. ,, With the aim of enhancing the HER activity of LNiFe, this study investigated how the electronic structure of the LN2S2 site can be modulated to impact the reactivity of the key intermediates, i.e., the reduced and hydride species. To achieve our objectives, we have synthesized two complexes (Scheme ), in which the Bpy unit was substituted with an electron-donor group (-OMe) or was replaced with the 1,10-phenanthroline (Phen). Adding an electron-donating group, such as a methoxy group, is supposed to increase the hydride donor strength for transition metal hydrides (lower ΔG H –). In contrast, the increased ligand rigidity from Phen is expected to lower the overpotential of the electrocatalytic process. In this work, we thus present the synthetic procedures of these two complexes, their structural characterization, redox properties, and HER activity under homogeneous conditions. Finally, we compare their reactivity with that of the Bpy derivative and rationalize their respective performance with the support of DFT calculations.

Results and Discussion

Synthesis and Characterization

The synthesis of the proligands LOMeTHP and LPhenTHP was derived from the method reported for the bipyridine-related compound , and is described in the Supporting Information along with the corresponding characterizations (Figures S1–S12). The neutral mononuclear [NiIILX] (x = OMe or Phen) complexes were obtained readily in relatively good yields by mixing methanolic solutions of NiCl2.6H2O with a DMF solution of the proligand, owing to the simultaneous deprotection of the thiols occurring upon complexation of the nickel center. The L X Ni II Fe II complexes were isolated as air-stable dark crystals from the reaction between the two respective mononuclear units (Figure ), that are, the dithiolate NiII complex [NiIILX] and the carbonyl FeII complex, [CpFeII(CO)­(MeCN)2]+. The corresponding cationic NiFe complexes [LOMeNiIIFeII]+ and [LPhenNiIIFeII]+ were fully characterized both in solution and in the solid state by 1H NMR and IR spectroscopy, mass spectrometry, electrochemistry, and X-ray crystallography as BF4 – salts. The X-ray structures of L OMe NiFe and L Phen NiFe reveal identical coordination configurations, featuring a {Ni­(μ-S)2Fe} core with two bridging thiolate ligands similar to the previously reported LNiFe complex (Table S3). The iron atom exhibits a typical three-legged piano stool conformation, with two sulfur atoms and one CO ligand serving as legs. While there are no notable differences in the bond distances and angles between LNiFe and L OMe NiFe, substituting the Bpy moiety with Phen results in a significant flattening of the {Ni­(μ-S)2Fe} butterfly core from 107.22 to 150.69 (Table S3). In L Phen NiFe, the Ni···Fe distance of 3.351 Å is significantly longer than that in LNiFe and L OMe NiFe (2.88(4) Å and 2.84(4) Å, respectively), and also longer than in the active site of [NiFe]-hydrogenases (∼2.8 Å range), inducing a quasi-planar {Ni­(μ-S)2Fe} core as observed in the NiFe mimic where Cp* has been used instead of Cp (3.391 A). This most likely results from the higher rigidity of the LPhen ligand, which forces the system to adopt a constrained quasi-planar {NiFeS2} core. Aside from these differences, the same stereoisomeric form is found in both L OMe NiFe and L Phen NiFe derivatives, corresponding to an exo conformation (Scheme S3).

The ESI-mass spectra of MeCN solutions of L OMe NiFe and L Phen NiFe display a peak at m/z 845.08 and 809.08, respectively, corresponding to [LXNiFeCpCO]+, indicating that the dinuclear structure is maintained in solution. As in the case of LNiFe, the NiII and FeII ions are low spin (S = 0), resulting in a diamagnetic complex, as attested by EPR-silent and diamagnetic 1H NMR spectra recorded in CD2Cl2 (Figures S9 and S12).

The IR spectrum of L OMe NiFe displays one CO stretching vibration (Figure S13), identical to that of LNiFe, consistent with a similar environment and conformation of the FeII ion in both complexes. For L Phen NiFe, two CO stretching vibrations are observed (1930 and 1950 cm–1, Figure ), indicating the presence of two distinct carbonyl groups, attributed to the exo and endo isomers, respectively. The DFT-predicted CO frequencies arising from the optimized structures of both isomers (see the SI for more details) agree well with the experimental values (Table ), supporting our assignment.

Redox Properties of LOMeNiFe and LPhenNiFe

The electrochemical properties of the complexes were investigated by cyclic voltammetry (CV) at a glassy carbon electrode in MeCN (0.1 M NBu4PF6 as the supporting electrolyte) under an atmosphere of argon. As shown in Figures and S17, the CV of L OMe NiFe displays two reversible and diffusion-controlled one-electron reduction waves at −1.36 and −2.0 V vs Fc+/0 (all potentials given in this publication are referenced to the Fc+/0 redox couple).

These waves are assigned to the successive reductions of NiII to NiI and of the bipyridine moiety in (LOMe)2–, as for the parent LNiFe complex and supported by DFT calculations (see below and the SI). , The cathodic shift (100 mV) of the ligand center redox event compared with LNiFe illustrates the electron-donating character of the methoxy groups substituted on the Bpy backbone. The CV of L Phen NiFe also exhibits two one-electron reduction processes (Figures and S18). However, the first reduction process at E 1/2 = −1.31 V centered on the Ni site is quasi-irreversible. Upon an increase in the scan rate, the system becomes fully reversible (Figure S19). This difference in behavior compared to the Bpy-based parent complex likely arises from the rigidity of the Phen ligand. The second reduction process displays a reversible signal at E 1/2 = −1.82 V and thus is assigned to a ligand-centered one-electron reduction process. The anodic shift (80 mV) of the ligand center redox event compared to that of LNiFe illustrates the backbone effect of the phenanthroline (Figure and Table ). The similar redox behavior observed across the three complexes highlights that, within this series, the Fe site does not exhibit intrinsic redox activity but instead serves to modulate the redox properties of the Ni ion. This effect is clearly illustrated by comparing the redox properties of the mononuclear Ni complexes and their heterodinuclear counterparts (Figures S24 and S25), which show a difference of approximately 600 mV in the potential of the first one-electron reduction process.

Characterization of LXNiIFeII

The monoreduced species were obtained from the reaction of MeCN solutions of L X Ni II Fe II with 1 equiv of cobaltocene at –40 °C (Figures and S13–15). These paramagnetic species were assigned to [LXNiIμ­(CO)­FeIICp] complexes, L X Ni I Fe II , based on IR and EPR spectroscopic experiments. The CO vibration is shifted to 1742 and 1740 cm–1 for L OMe Ni I Fe II and L Phen Ni I Fe II , respectively, in agreement with a CO bridging the Fe and Ni ions (Figures a and S13). The S = 1/2 EPR signature of the L OMe Ni I Fe II and L Phen Ni I Fe II solutions exhibits an axial signal with g-values characteristic of NiI-based compounds (Figures b and S14,15), closely matching those reported for LNi I Fe II . The structural and electronic properties are thus similar for all three complexes, confirming our assignment of the first redox event as the reduction of NiII to NiI.

Electrocatalytic Activity of LOMeNiFe and LPhenNiFe for H2 Production

Upon the addition of Et3NHBF4 as a proton source (pK a = 18.6 in MeCN), a catalytic process develops on top of the second reversible wave for both L OMe Ni II Fe II and L Phen Ni II Fe II with half-wave potential E cat/2 being −1.91 V and −1.75 V, respectively (Figures and S21). Bulk electrolysis experiments performed at −1.85 V on a Hg-pool cathode confirmed electrocatalytic production of H2 in the presence of L OMe Ni II Fe II and L Phen Ni II Fe II (Figure S22), with faradaic yields of 70% from Et3NHBF4 (50 equiv). After establishing that both complexes can catalytically reduce protons in acidic organic media, their kinetics were further explored. After the current is subtracted from the direct proton reduction at the glassy carbon electrode, a plot of i cat/i p against the acid concentration (Figure S23) exhibits two regions. At acid concentrations below 0.02 M (100 equiv), i cat/i p varies linearly with the acid concentration. The catalytic Tafel plot shown in Figure illustrates the relationship between turnover frequency (TOF) and overpotential (η) for H2 evolution, allowing a performance comparison of the L x NiFe catalysts with previously reported systems. Although L x NiFe complexes are less competitive than the cobaloximes , or Dubois’s bioinspired Ni complexes, they perform comparably to other noble-metal-free electrocatalysts, such as Fe­(TPP) (TPP: tetraphenylporphyrin). Introducing electron-donating methoxy groups on the Bpy ligand enhances the kinetics, notably at overpotentials above 500 mV, while replacing the Bpy unit with a Phen backbone lowers the overpotential.

Mechanistic Investigations and Discussion

To gain further insights into the HER mechanism with the LxNiFe catalysts, DFT calculations were performed, and a full mechanism is proposed in Schemes and S4. The selection of the DFT calculation framework, including functionals and basis sets, was guided by previous systematic investigations that identified the most suitable methodology for each type of calculations (optimized structures, redox potentials, and spectroscopic properties). , As LNiFe, the L OMe NiFe and L Phen NiFe electrocatalysts were shown to follow an ECEC mechanism for H2 production starting from the monoreduced LXNiIFeII species. , This LXNiIFeII species must first undergo one-electron reduction to enable subsequent protonation, leading to the formation of a hydride species. Other protonation sites were envisaged, especially the protonation of the thiolate. However, the corresponding intermediates are always less stable by a minimum of 10.5 kcal/mol with respect to the formation of the hydride species (Tables S10 and S11). This hydride species is further reduced at a potential that is predicted to be less negative than that required for the previous reduction. The resulting NiIIFeII hydride species undergoes a second protonation at one of the thiolate ligands, leading to the decoordination of the corresponding thiol. This highlights the importance of the hemilability of the thiolate/thiol couple in assisting catalysis by serving as a proton relay. The reaction between the thiol and the hydride thus generates H2, which is then released.

Along this series, [L OMe NiFe] – exhibits the best kinetics. Regarding the DFT-predicted hydricity ΔG H‑ or ΔpK a values of both catalysts (Table ), a significant difference is observed only in the case of the ΔpK a values. Such an increase in ΔpK a aligns with the electron-donating effect of the methoxy groups, which enhance electron density on the metal center, facilitating protonation.

Regarding the overpotential, [LPhenNiFe]+ exhibits the best performance, which can be rationalized by the fact that the LPhen-centered reduction is easier, as attested by the cathodic shift of 80 mV when compared to the parent LBpy compound in the absence of protons (see Table , [LPhenNiFe]0/– and [LNiFe]0/–).

Conclusions

In summary, we have successfully synthesized two novel bioinspired [NiFe]-hydrogenase complexes, L OMe NiFe and L Phen NiFe. Both complexes were found to be efficient electrocatalysts for proton reduction, and a mechanism consistent with the experimental and computational evidence is proposed, highlighting how ligand design can be used to finely tune catalytic reactivity. Substituting the Bpy unit with methoxy groups promotes the protonation ability of the two-electron-reduced species, thereby enhancing the catalytic kinetics. In contrast, the introduction of a phenanthroline backbone, which is more easily reduced, lowers the overpotential required for H2 production. To achieve a significant impact on the HER catalytic activity, other strategies include introducing a proton relay in the second coordination sphere by modifying one of the ligands (LS2N2 or Cp) or replacing Cp with alternative ligand types, considering the strong influence of the Fe site on the redox properties of the Ni center.

Experimental Section

[LOMeNiIIFeII(Cp)­(CO)]­BF4

Solid NiIILOMe (40 mg, 0.057 mmol) was added to a solution of [CpFe­(CO)­(MeCN)2]­BF4 (18 mg, 0.057 mmol) in CH2Cl2 (2 mL) under stirring at room temperature. After 24 h, the solution was filtered and concentrated. Then, diethyl ether was allowed to slowly diffuse into the concentrated solution, and dark brown crystals corresponding to [NiIILOMeFeII(Cp)­(CO)]­BF4 were obtained. After filtration and washing with diethyl ether, the compound was isolated in a 70% yield (30 mg).
