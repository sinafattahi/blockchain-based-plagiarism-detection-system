Metallogel Synthesis from Cu(II) Complex with Benzotriazole-5-carboxylic Acid for Enhanced Congo Red Dye Adsorption and Anion Exchange

Abstract

A new metallogel was assembled by Cu­(II) and benzotriazole-5-carboxylic acid (H2btca) as gelators via a solvothermal method. The study of various parameters influencing the metallogelation process, including solvent types, metal ions, counter-anions, and the mole ratios of gelators, was investigated. The morphology and surface properties of xerogels were characterized by FTIR, FE-SEM, and EDX techniques. The xerogel demonstrated selective adsorption of anionic dyes, particularly Congo red dye (CR), with a removal efficiency of 98%. The comprehensive study of the adsorption process coincides with the pseudo-second-order model, the Langmuir isotherm, and the spontaneous exothermic process. The adsorption capacity of CR is 666.4 mg·g–1. The dye adsorption mechanism was investigated. Furthermore, the synthesis of Cu-metallogels can be achieved with various anionic species, including NO3 –, ClO4 –, and BF4 –, allowing for the development of reversible anion exchange behaviors.

1. Introduction

Metallogels are a kind of gel that consists of three-dimensional networks and falls under the category of supramolecular gels. , These gels have garnered considerable interest recently, owing to their prospective use in chemo-sensor, ion recognition, adsorption, catalysis, etc. Metallogels are formed by the self-assembly of metal complexes or precisely specified coordination polymers, using noncovalent interactions such as hydrogen bonding, π–π stacking, hydrophobic interactions, and van der Waals forces. − This process also involves trapping a significant quantity of solvent molecules within the structure. The physicochemical stability of the metallogels is primarily governed by various crucial controlling factors, such as metal ion, ligand, solvent composition, pH, temperature, etc. , Consequently, alteration of any of these variables can often disrupt the formation of the gel matrix. It has been discovered that organic ligands with multiple coordinating sites, especially those containing nitrogen atoms such as pyridine, triazole, and tetrazole rings, effectively promote the production of metallogels. − This is because these ligands possess hydrogen bond donors and acceptors that enable the assembly of supramolecular structures. In this work, we fabricated a metallogel using benzotriazole-5-carboxylic acid (H2btca). The carboxylate group and nitrogen atoms in the triazole ring possess exceptional affinity for binding with metal ions. Furthermore, metal complexes and coordination polymers containing the H2btca ligand with different metal ions have been reported; − however, there are currently no reports regarding its metallogels.

Water pollution produced by hazardous and non-biodegradable organic dyes is a significant environmental issue in society. − Various techniques have been used for the treatment of wastewater, including photodegradation, reverse osmosis, adsorption, electrochemical processes, and others. − Adsorption is widely regarded as a favored technique for treating water, owing to its exceptional efficiency, cost-effectiveness, ease of operation, and ability to effectively remove many types of dyes. ,, Several types of materials with porous architectures and large specific surface areas, including graphene oxides, silica, hydrogels, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), etc., have been thoroughly investigated as adsorbents for the removal of organic dyes. Although these materials have achieved some success in adsorption, developing a novel material with a simple fabrication procedure, rapid substance adsorption, high adsorption capacity, and recyclability remains challenging. ,− Thus, it is crucial to create adsorbents that can quickly and effectively adsorb organic dyes using a simple and efficient method. Metallogels are known for having a porous structure, large surface areas, and high thermal and chemical stabilities, which make them very promising adsorbents for effectively removing organic dyes. , Hence, it is critical to investigate novel metallogel materials for efficient organic dye adsorption.

Herein, we describe the solvothermal synthesis of Cu­(II)-metallogels with H2btca, which further drying yields the xerogels. The synthetic parameters of metallogelation, including metal ions, solvents, counter-anions, and metal–ligand ratios, were examined. Furthermore, the synthesized xerogel has exceptional adsorption ability for anionic Congo red dyes in aqueous solutions. The adsorption kinetics, adsorption isotherm, and thermodynamics were also investigated. Moreover, the metallogels show high chemical stability and exhibit the anion exchange phenomena involving counter-anions, including NO3 –, ClO4 –, and BF4 –, respectively.

2. Experimental Section

2.1. Materials and Methods

The reactants and solvents were obtained from chemical sources and used without further purification. The ATR-FTIR spectra were collected using a Bruker Tensor 27 FTIR spectrophotometer, covering the wavelength range of 4000–600 cm–1. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was measured using a TGA Q50 (TA Instruments) at a heating rate of 10 °C·min–1 in the temperature range of 30–700 °C under an N2 atmosphere. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns were obtained on an EMPYREAN PANALYTICAL using monochromatic CuKα radiation with the 2θ range from 5 to 50° at a speed of 0.5 s/step at room temperature. N2 adsorption/desorption isotherms at 77 K were performed using BELSORP Mini X (Microtrac). Before gas adsorption measurements, the samples were pretreated at 100 °C in a vacuum for 24 h using BELPREP-vac (MicrotracBEL). The field emission scanning electron microscopy (FE-SEM) images were conducted utilizing a Helios NanoLab G3 CX microscope equipped with a gold coating. The energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX) data was obtained using a Merlin compact scanning electron microscope and a desktop scanning electron microscope SEC SNE-4500M. The UV–vis spectra in the liquid state were done using an Agilent HP8453 spectrophotometer in the range of 200–800 nm. The zeta potential measurement was carried out using a Zetasizer Nano S90. The metal content study was conducted by using a PerkinElmer PinAAcle 900F atomic absorption spectrometer (AAS).

2.2. Preparation of Cu­(II)-Metallogel

Cu­(NO3)2·3H2O (0.0241 g, 0.1 mmol) was dissolved in 2 mL of acetonitrile in a 15 mL glass vial. Benzotriazole-5-carboxylic acid (H2btca) (0.0163 g, 0.1 mmol) was separately dissolved in 2 mL of N,N′-dimethylformamide. Then, the two solutions were combined, and the vial was left at a temperature of 60 °C for 24 h in the oven. After cooling down to room temperature, the dark green Cu­(II)-metallogel of Gel 3 was obtained. The xerogel was obtained by heating the metallogel at 60 °C for 24 h. Moreover, the synthetic parameters, including the effects of solvents, metal ions, counter-anions, and metal–ligand ratios, were investigated.

2.3. Adsorption Experiment for Dye Removal

Before the adsorption investigations, the xerogels were immersed in water for 24 h to remove any surplus Cu­(II) ions. Adsorption tests were conducted to investigate the adsorption of Congo red (CR) anionic dye. 5 mg of xerogel was introduced into 10 mL of 100 mg·L–1 CR dye, thereafter stirred slowly for a designated duration, and then centrifuged. The supernatant solution was analyzed by using a UV–vis spectrophotometer. The several adsorption parameters, including the effects of pH, initial concentrations of dye, adsorption kinetics, adsorption isotherms, and adsorption thermodynamics, were investigated.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Synthesis and Characterization

When an acetonitrile solution of Cu­(NO3)2·3H2O (2 mL, 0.1 mmol) was simultaneously mixed with a DMF solution of H2btca (2 mL, 0.1 mmol), it immediately resulted in a green solution color. After heating at 60 °C for 24 h, a dark green metallogel was obtained. The variation in solvents dissolved in copper salt was studied (Table S1). The gelation was found to be specific to binary and pure solvents, including MeOH/DMF, EtOH/DMF, MeCN/DMF, and pure DMF for Gel 1–Gel 4, respectively. The state of gelation was verified by the retardation of the materials upon inverting the glass vial (Figure ). The gelation process can be initiated using either pure DMF or a mixture of DMF with other polar solvents (Gel 1–Gel 4). On the other hand, the use of other pure solvents resulted in the precipitation or dissolution of a copper complex (Table S1). Gel 3 exhibited long-term stability at room temperature even after being exposed to air. In contrast, Gel 1, Gel 2, and Gel 4 were stable for just a few hours at ambient temperature before transitioning into their sol form (Table S2). However, the sol forms can regel when heated at 60 °C. Thus, we chose the MeCN/DMF system (Gel 3) to study the synthetic parameters and characterizations.

After drying the Gel 3 at 60 °C for 24 h, we obtained the xerogel 3 for characterization. FTIR spectra of xerogel 3 and the free H2btca ligand were recorded (Figure a). The vibrational peaks at 3201 and 1696 cm–1 of the free ligand confirmed the triazole ring (−NH) and carboxylic group (−COOH), respectively. For xerogel 3, the appearance peaks at 1602 and 1370 cm–1 indicate the characteristic vibrational peaks of asymmetric (νas) and symmetric (νs) stretching for the carboxylate group of the btca2– ligand. This indicates the coordination bond between the carboxylate group and the Cu­(II) ions. To predict the coordination mode of the carboxylate ligand, the difference between νas and νs (Δν) was calculated to be 232 cm–1 for xerogel 3. The distinct value indicates the monodentate mode of the carboxylate group. The FTIR signal at 1078 cm–1 in xerogel 3 is attributed to the N–N stretching of the btca2– moiety, which is lower than the peak observed for the free ligand at 1110 cm–1, implying the coordination bond between Cu­(II) and the triazole ring. , Moreover, the FTIR spectrum of xerogel 3 shows the peaks at 1706 and 1306 cm–1, corresponding with the characteristic vibration of the DMF molecule and nitrate anion (NO3 –), respectively. The diffuse reflectance absorption spectrum of xerogel 3 was measured within the wavelength range of 200–1200 nm (Figure S1). A broad band at 646 nm (15723 cm–1) was observed, corresponding to a hexacoordinated Cu­(II) ion. In addition, the adsorption spectrum of xerogel 3 displays the adsorption band around 240 nm, which corresponds to the n to π* and π to π* transition of btca2–. The FE-SEM image revealed the aggregated sheetlike morphology of xerogel 3 (Figure c). The EDX analysis reveals the existence of Cu, C, N, and O atoms in xerogel 3, confirming the presence of both organic ligands and Cu­(II) ions incorporated into the self-assembled structure with excellent dispersion (Figure d). TG analysis confirmed the thermal stability of xerogel 3, as shown in Figure b. The TGA curve shows that the sample starts the initial step at around 30 °C and continues up to 150 °C. The total weight loss of 12.8% within this temperature range can be ascribed to the elimination of solvent molecules from the metallogel. Powder X-ray diffraction of xerogel 3 reveals that the xerogel is amorphous (Figure S2a). N2 adsorption isotherms for xerogel 3 were recorded at 77 K to evaluate its specific surface area by using the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) method (Figure S2b). Xerogel 3 exhibits isotherms indicating the absence of pores or voids in its structure, with a specific surface area of approximately 3.9 m2 g–1.

3.1.1. Effect of Metal Ions and Counter-anions

Various metal ions with a nitrate salt were used to induce the metallogelation process, including Mn­(II), Fe­(III), Co­(II), Ni­(II), Cd­(II), Eu­(III), and Tb­(III) in place of the Cu­(II) ion. The results revealed no gelation, and a clear solution or precipitation was obtained (Figure S3). Consequently, the H2btca ligand was very selective for the Cu­(II) ion, which led to the formation of a successful metallogel. Moreover, the different counter-anions of Cu­(II) salts were examined for their influence on the metallogelation process. A series of Cu­(II) salts with various counter-anions, including BF4 –, ClO4 –, SO4 2–, CH3COO–, Cl–, and Br–, were examined. The results show that except for Cu­(NO3)2·3H2O, the other salts, including Cu­(BF4)2·nH2O and Cu­(ClO4)2·nH2O, can also be used for the gel formation with H2btca (Figure a), while the others display the precipitation after mixing with a ligand (Figure S4). Although BF4 – and ClO4 – can undergo gelation, they can transition to a sol state within a few hours at room temperature. After drying Gel 3@BF 4 – and Gel 3@ClO 4 – at 60 °C for 24 h, xerogel 3@BF 4 – and xerogel 3@ClO 4 – were obtained. The FTIR spectra of xerogel 3@BF 4 – and xerogel 3@ClO 4 – show the vibrational peak at 1055 and 1074 cm–1, which correspond to the characteristic of ν­(BF4 –) and ν­(ClO4 –), respectively. The other characteristic peaks are very similar to those of xerogel 3@NO 3 – (Figure b). The EDX analysis exhibits the F and Cl elemental compositions of xerogel 3@BF 4 – and xerogel 3@ClO 4 – (Figures S5a,b), confirming the presence of the anion in the xerogel structure. PXRD patterns of xerogel 3@BF 4 – and xerogel 3@ClO 4 – exhibit amorphous structures similar to those of xerogel 3@NO 3 – (Figure S5c).

3.1.2. Effect of the Metal–Ligand Ratio

A particular amount of the H2btca ligand was dissolved in a precise volume of DMF (2 mL) among several 20 mL glass vials. Then, Cu­(NO3)2·3H2O at varying concentrations from 0.1 to 1.0 mmol in MeCN (2 mL) was added to the ligand solution. The vials were sealed with a screw lid and maintained at 60 °C for 24 h. The gelation was found to occur from 0.1 to 0.6 mmol of Cu­(II) onward (Gel 3 (1:1) to Gel 3 (6:1)), as shown in Figure S6. The vial containing 0.7 to 1.0 mmol of Cu­(II) does not yield any gel; instead, it forms a precipitate (Figure S7). This result indicates that at higher Cu2+ concentrations, the triazole nitrogen atoms may be used to form coordination bonds with Cu2+, hence, leaving insufficient capacity for hydrogen bonding required for gel formation. After the gels were dried, the xerogels were characterized by FTIR and PXRD techniques. The FTIR spectra of xerogel 3 (1:1)–(3:1) show the same vibrational peaks (Figure S8a), and their PXRD patterns display an amorphous nature, whereas xerogel 3 (4:1) shows the characteristic diffraction patterns of Cu­(NO3)2 salt at 2θ = 12.9 and 25.8° (Figure S8b). This result indicates that the copper salt is contaminated during the gelation process when the Cu­(II) ratio exceeds 0.4 mmol. Furthermore, when the ligand concentration exceeds 0.1 mmol, the FTIR and PXRD patterns show characteristic vibration and diffraction peaks of H2btca. This finding indicates that the free ligand is detectable upon the addition of more than 0.1 mmol. The results indicate that the selection of suitable solvent molecules, metal ions, counter-anions, and gelator stoichiometry is a crucial factor in the formation of gel networks.

3.2. Dye Adsorption Study

3.2.1. Choice of Dyes

Prior to the adsorption studies, the xerogels were immersed in water for 24 h to eliminate any excess Cu­(II) ions. The common water-soluble cationic dyes, such as methylene blue (MB) and rhodamine 6G (Rh6G); anionic dyes, such as methyl orange (MO), orange II (OR-II), and Congo red (CR); and neutral dye, such as methyl red (MR), were used for the adsorption experiment. The molecular structures of the dyes are shown in Figure S9. The xerogel 3 (10 mg) was added to 10 mL of 100 mg·L–1 of different dyes. After stirring for 120 min, the supernatant of the solution was measured. The removal efficiency (RE) of dyes is calculated by the following eq :

RE=C0−CtC0×100% 1

where C 0 (mg·L–1) and C t (mg·L–1) are the initial and residual concentrations at time t of the organic dyes, respectively.

The adsorption capacity (q) was calculated respectively by the following eqs and :

qt=(C0−Ct)Vm 2
qe=(C0−Ce)Vm 3

where qt (mg·g–1) and q e (mg/g) represent the adsorption capacity at time t and at equilibrium, and C 0 (mg·L–1) and C e (mg·L–1) are the concentrations of dyes at initial time and at equilibrium, respectively. V (L) is the volume of an aqueous solution of dye, and m (g) is the mass of the xerogel.

The removal efficiency of anionic and neutral dyes reaches 52, 80, 98, and 80% for MO, OR-II, CR, and MR, respectively, but the efficiency of cationic dyes is 10 and 2% for MB and Rh6G, respectively (Figure ). These results indicate that xerogel 3 exhibits a high selectivity for anionic dyes. Based on the results, we selected the CR dye that had the best adsorption performance to study the adsorption process model in more detail.

3.2.2. Choice of Adsorbent

Xerogel 3 showed excellent CR adsorption performance; thus, the effect of the mole ratio between Cu­(II) and H2btca of xerogels, including xerogel 3 (1:1), xerogel 3 (2:1), and xerogel 3 (3:1), was studied for CR adsorption. 5 mg of xerogels was added to 100 mg·L–1 CR solution with continuous stirring at room temperature. The adsorption process was monitored by recording the intensity of the absorption peak at 498 nm. Upon the addition of xerogels, the peak at 498 nm gradually decreases with increasing time (Figure ). Xerogel 3 (1:1), xerogel 3 (2:1), and xerogel 3 (3:1) show removal efficiencies of 55, 71, and 98%, respectively. The release of Cu­(II) ions after CR adsorption was confirmed by atomic absorption spectroscopy (AAS). The Cu­(II) contents in the supernatant were found to be 0.075, 1.25, and 2.0 mg·L–1 for xerogel 3 (1:1), xerogel 3 (2:1), and xerogel 3 (3:1), respectively. These amounts are not higher than the WHO maximum permissible level of Cu­(II) ion concentration in drinking water. Xerogel 3 (3:1) shows the highest CR adsorption efficiency when compared to the others under the same conditions. Thus, xerogel 3 (3:1) was selected to study the adsorption parameters.

3.2.3. Effect of pH

The pH is an important factor in the CR adsorption process. The pH level can affect both active sites on the adsorbent surface and dye formation. To investigate the effect of pH, 5 mg of xerogel 3 (3:1) was added into 10 mL of 100 mg·L–1 CR solution in different pH values at room temperature. The results showed that the adsorption capacities of xerogel 3 (3:1) were stable at pH 3–11 (Figure S10a). The highest removal efficiency and adsorption capacity were observed at pH 7, with values of 98.2% and 196.5 mg/g, respectively. In acidic conditions (pH < 2), the sulfonate groups of CR, carboxylate groups, and triazole rings of btca2– ligands may be protonated, while in basic conditions (pH ≈ 13), OH– ions interfere during the adsorption process. These results indicate a decrease in adsorption capacities under strong acidic and basic conditions. ,

3.2.4. Effect of Initial Concentration

3 mg of xerogel 3 (3:1) was added into 10 mL of varying initial CR concentrations ranging from 100 to 1000 mg·L–1 at room temperature and pH 7 (Figure S10b). The adsorption capacity of CR on xerogel 3 (3:1) increased from 161.6 to 666.4 mg·g–1 when increasing the initial concentrations. This result can be explained by the limited occupancy of adsorption sites on the adsorbent. At lower concentrations of CR, the small amounts of solute can adsorb on the active sites, increasing the capacity. When the CR concentration increased, the adsorption capacities remained almost constant due to the saturated adsorption of the CR molecules and adsorption sites of the adsorbent.

3.2.5. Adsorption Kinetics, Isotherms, and Thermodynamic

To evaluate the adsorption mechanism on the surface of xerogel 3 (3:1), pseudo-first-order, pseudo-second-order, Elovich, and intraparticle diffusion kinetic models were used to fit experimental data. Figure S11 illustrates the plot of kinetic models for CR adsorption with 100 mg·L–1 of initial concentration. The kinetic parameters for all models are listed in Table S3. The pseudo-second-order kinetic model shows a high correlation coefficient value (R 2 = 0.9959), and the calculated adsorption capacity (q cal) is closer to the experiment (q exp) than those of the other models. According to this model, the dominant stage in the process is the adsorption of CR molecules onto xerogel 3 (3:1) via interactions. ,

The adsorption isotherm was evaluated to analyze the interaction between CR and xerogel 3 (3:1). Several isotherm models, including Langmuir, Freundlich, Temkin, and Dubinin–Radushkevich (D-R), were used to calculate the maximum adsorption capacity and estimate the isotherm parameters. Several adsorption isotherm parameters and plots of isotherm models are given in Figure S12 and Table S4, respectively. The Langmuir isotherm model is better fitted with the adsorption of CR on xerogel 3 (3:1) than others, as evidenced by the R 2 value of 0.9950. This result indicated that the CR adsorption on xerogel 3 (3:1) is a monolayer adsorption. The maximum adsorption capacity derived from the Langmuir isotherm model is 675.7 mg·g–1, which is consistent with the experimental result of 666.4 mg·g–1. Thermodynamic investigations were performed to clarify the adsorption process at pH 7.0 and 100 mg·L–1 CR solution. The standard enthalpy (ΔH°), entropy change (ΔS), and Gibbs free energy change (ΔG) can be described as the adsorption process of CR on the xerogel 3 (3:1), determined by the following equations: ΔG = −RT ln K and ln K = ΔS/R – ΔH/RT. The calculated thermodynamic parameters are shown in Table S5. The values of ΔH and ΔS were derived from the slope and intercept of ln K against the 1/T graph. The results indicated that the negative values of ΔG suggested the spontaneous nature of CR adsorption on xerogel 3 (3:1). The ΔS and ΔH values were −0.92 kJ·mol–1·K–1 and −40.53 kJ·mol–1, respectively. The negative values of ΔS and ΔH indicate a reduction in randomness at the interface between the CR and the adsorbent during the adsorption process, which occurred via chemisorption with exothermic behavior.

3.2.6. Adsorption Mechanism

Based on the adsorption isotherm and thermodynamic results, the adsorption of CR by xerogel 3 (3:1) involved both physical and chemical adsorption, with chemical adsorption being the predominant mechanism in the process. The color of xerogel 3 (3:1) also changed from green-blue to red-brown, indicating CR adsorption on xerogel 3 (3:1). The zeta potential of xerogel 3 (3:1) in DI water (pH approximately 6) is −2.44 mV, as shown in Figure b. This result indicates that the surface charge of xerogel 3 (3:1) is negative above pH 6, whereas the CR also exhibits a negative charge at pH > 5. It means that the electrostatic interaction between xerogel 3 (3:1) and CR is not the primary mechanism of adsorption. The adsorption capacities of CR also exhibited a small variation within the pH range 3–11 (Figure S10a), indicating validity across a broad pH range. The adsorption capacities decrease at both low (pH 1) and high (pH 13) pH values due to decomposition of the adsorbent and interference from H+ and OH– species. Therefore, the adsorption mechanism of CR can be elucidated as follows. The anion exchange between nitrate and CR anions could play a crucial role in the CR adsorption process (Figure c). FTIR spectra and zeta potential analysis can confirm the anion exchange process between the nitrate anion and CR. The FTIR spectrum of xerogel 3 (3:1)@CR shows the characteristic vibrational peak at 1221 cm–1, which corresponds to ν­(SO3 –) of the CR molecule, while the characteristic peak of ν­(NO3 –) has disappeared. (Figure a). Moreover, the zeta potential value decreased from −2.44 to −11.80 mV after the adsorption process (Figure b). This change corresponds to the binding interaction of CR and xerogel 3 (3:1) at the surface, induced by the functional groups of ligands, followed by the anion exchange process between CR and NO3 –. The xerogel 3 (3:1) is rich in functional groups, comprising carboxyl, triazole rings, and benzene rings derived from H2btca. As a result, CR could interact with the carboxyl and triazole groups on the surface of the xerogel 3 (3:1) through H-bonds and π–π interactions between benzene rings of xerogel 3 (3:1) and the CR structure.

3.3. Stability and Anion Exchange of Metallogel

Gel 3 (3:1) remained unchanged even after sonication for 1 h and heating at 100 °C for 5 h. Moreover, after breaking the gel with a spatula, it can be recovered at room temperature after the addition of a small amount of the DMF solvent (Figure S13). Furthermore, the chemical stimuli can be investigated by soaking Gel 3 (3:1) in different solvents and pH values for 24 h. The results show that gel 3 (3:1) displays high chemical stability because it does not dissolve after soaking (Figure S14). These results support the existence of strong noncovalent interactions among the gel components, in contrast to the reversible gel-to-sol transition. Based on the gel preparation process, we can prepare the Cu-metallogel by using different counter-anions by varying Cu-salts, including Gel 3@NO 3 – , Gel 3@BF 4 – , and Gel 3@ClO 4 – . Therefore, to confirm the role of counter-anions for metallogelation, the anion exchange properties of metallogel were investigated. 1 g of Gel 3@NO 3 – was soaked in 0.5 mmol of NaBF4 or NaClO4 solution for 24 h, then dried in an oven at 60 °C overnight to obtain the xerogel after the anion exchange process. The anion exchange properties of Gel 3@BF 4 – were carried out in the same way as Gel 3@NO 3 – but soaking in NaNO3 or NaClO4, while Gel 3@ClO 4 – was soaked in NaNO3 or NaBF4, respectively. The FTIR spectrum (Figure S15) of xerogel 3@NO 3 – shows an intense band at 1306 cm–1, which indicates the presence of ν­(NO3 –). After the anion exchange, the gels soaked in NaBF4 and NaClO4 exhibit new characteristic peaks at 1055 and 1074 cm–1, corresponding to the characteristic peaks of ν­(BF4 –) and ν­(ClO4 –), respectively. Moreover, the FTIR spectra of xerogel3@BF 4 – , which display the characteristic peak of ν­(BF4 –) at 1055 cm–1, after anion exchange with NaClO4, exhibit new peaks of ν­(ClO4 –) at 1074 cm–1. These results indicate that reversible anion exchange can be achieved among NO3 –, ClO4 –, and BF4 – (Figure ).

Besides CR adsorption by xerogel 3, the CR adsorption of Gel 3 (3:1) was investigated (Figure ). 1 g portion of Gel 3 (3:1) was immersed in 100 ppm of CR. The solution color changed from red to purple immediately due to a change in the pH value. After adsorption at time intervals, the supernatant solution was collected and filtered before measurement by the UV–vis spectrophotometer. The UV–vis spectra of CR are decreased with an increase in the adsorption time (Figure a). After adsorption for 24 h, the solution color changed from purple to colorless, and a maximum removal efficiency was achieved at 98.6% (Figure b). The results confirm that the Cu-metallogels are capable of adsorbing the CR dye in both gel and xerogel states.

4. Conclusions

A Cu­(II)-metallogel was fabricated by the solvothermal method of Cu­(II) and H2btca. The synthetic parameters, including solvents, metals, counter-anions, and metal–ligand ratios, play a key role in metallogel formation with a nitrate counter-anion. The xerogel has a high efficacy in removing Congo red from water. The adsorption process corresponds to the pseudo-second-order kinetic model and the Langmuir adsorption isotherm. Thus, the adsorption mostly includes chemisorption on a monolayer of CR molecules surrounding the uniform solid surface of the xerogel. The adsorption process of CR is a thermodynamically spontaneous exothermic reaction. The adsorption mechanism involves anion exchange between nitrate anions and CR molecules. In addition, the metallogels display high physical and chemical stability when influenced by external stimuli. The NO3 – counter-anions can be reversibly exchanged with BF4 – and ClO4 –. Also, the gel states demonstrate a notable capacity for efficient CR adsorption.
