Abstract

Carbon dioxide (CO2) is one of the major contributors toward global warming and climate change activities, and significant efforts are being taken to mitigate it in terms of reducing release, capturing, sequestering, and converting it to useful products. Among various decarbonization strategies, CO2 hydrogenation is considered a suitable technology, but the process suffers from lower conversion, low yield, low selectivity, and cost-effectiveness issues of the catalyst employed. The literature gap lies in the inability to correlate the catalyst complexity with chemical production. The aim of this study was to synthesize an efficient and selective novel catalyst by combining two separately tested and distinct materials to create a sustainable catalyst. The constituents were chosen for their distinctive thermal and structural properties: Ni3Ga and Cu-ZSM-5. Ni3Ga was synthesized via facile coprecipitation, and Cu-ZSM-5 was synthesized via impregnation. The nanocomposite Ni3Ga/Cu-ZSM-5 was synthesized by mechanochemical synthesis and was characterized by X-ray diffraction (XRD), thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), BET analysis, Barrett–Joyner–Halenda (BJH) analysis, transmission electron microscopy (TEM), and FTIR. Finally, it was reduced in an H2 atmosphere at 700 °C for 2 h and tested in a thermal feed-flow reactor at different conditions. Among five different test conditions, the highest conversion and improved selectivity are observed for the Ni3Ga/Cu-ZSM-5 sample. This enhanced efficiency can be attributed to the well-designed structural integrity of the nanocomposite and the efficient cultivation of an acidic and highly porous surface environment for the catalyst. The thermal stability of the nanocomposite can be attributed to the utilization of ZSM-5, which acts as a protective layer, lasting for a continuous stable 20 h operation production. Thus, the Ni3Ga/Cu-ZSM-5 composite is proven to be a cheap and efficient thermal catalyst for CO2 hydrogenation.

1. Introduction

Commercial chemical processes require extensive energy, utilize fossil fuels, and have an enormous carbon footprint. Anthropogenic carbon dioxide production has been linked to severe global effects, including climate change, sea level rise, skin cancer, diabetes, and ocean acidification. More than 85% of global energy is supplied by the combustion of fossil fuels: natural gas, petroleum, and coal. The emission of greenhouse gases has increased, causing significant climate change; carbon dioxide is the main greenhouse gas, with over 55% of the total composition. The distribution of the total emission comes from coal (42%), oil (33%), gas (19%), cement (6%), and gas flaring (1%). Mitigation of CO2 emission can be done through three possible strategies: (a) extensive shift of the global energy base from fossil fuels to green renewable energy like hydrogen, (b) storage of carbon dioxide, and (c) utilization of carbon dioxide. Strategy (c) is considered the most viable and practical solution over a long span (20 years and more). It is considered 20 to 40 times as efficient as the two previous strategies. Hydrocarbons and oxygenates and even synthesis gas are vital for the fuel and energy industry, and at the same time, olefins and aromatic parts of it are being used in the polymer and fine chemical industries without any replacement yet. ,

The International Renewable Energy Agency recommends green fuel production via CO2 hydrogenation, in which clean fuels and valuable chemicals are produced from the mixture of CO2 and H2. This approach reduces the use of fossil fuels and subsequently reduces greenhouse gas emissions. CO2 hydrogenation is an attractive approach because of its potential; however, it has not yet been explored well due to the lower conversion and selectivities of the catalysts available. Figure demonstrates a general schematic diagram of the carbon dioxide hydrogenation process where fuel products like oxygenates and hydrocarbons can be produced. From this perspective, catalysis involved in this mechanism is considered a promising area of research that can resolve the low selectivity and output of carbon dioxide hydrogenation. Even the conversion of CO2 to carbon monoxide (CO) is worth doing as it can be utilized as Fischer–Tropsch reaction feed. Below are the balanced equations of expected/possible reactions during the hydrogenation of CO2, along with their enthalpy values, ΔH.

CO2+3H2→CH3OH+H2O⁣ΔH=−49.5kJ/mol
CO2+H2→CO+H2O⁣ΔH=41.2kJ/mol
CO2+4H2→CH4+2H2O⁣ΔH=−165.0kJ/mol

The produced CO being reactive with hydrogen, it may undergo further transformation to carbon or methane as shown below

2CO→C+CO2⁣ΔH=−172.6kJ/mol
CO+3H2→CH4+H2O⁣ΔH=−206.0kJ/mol

Compared to carbon monoxide feed, carbon dioxide lets the reaction slow down due to the increased stability of the latter. The catalyst must have specific thermal and structural properties: mesoporous structure, acidicefficient electron transport, thermal stability, thermal conductivity, high surface area, and low thermal expansion coefficient. A mesoporous structure is necessary to facilitate the absorbance of carbon dioxide. A high surface area increases the number of active sites; thus, there is an increase in the activity. Zeolites have been extensively studied for a long duration of time, especially for ZSM-5. They are known for their high porosity and have shown well-defined pores, thermal stability, and easy modification. The presence of silica provides stability support for the material, while the presence of alumina provides acidic active sites that allow the conversion of carbon dioxide to chemicals. Copper can be an excellent addition to the ZSM-5; adding copper precursors to zeolite is promising because it can facilitate the presence of active sites on the surface of the zeolite. Active and stable catalysts in carbon dioxide hydrogenation must overcome excessive water production to increase selectivity. Bimetallic catalysts are attention-gaining because they allow catalytic properties to be tuned by adjusting their active sites and neighboring atoms; hence, they modify the binding strength of reactive intermediates. Bimetallic catalysts like Pd–Cu, Ni–Cu, Ag–Cu, Pt–Cu, Pd–Ga, Pd–Zn, Pd–In, and Rh–W are being used; however, the high cost and scarcity of these metals always challenge their usage, and many studies shifted to this mode of coupling precious metals. The presence of additional metal can activate carbon dioxide molecules and facilitate the reaction with adsorbed hydrogen, which can assist in breaking the C–O bonds. In addition, it has been reported that nickel is highly stable and can be regenerated, which is highly advantageous for this mechanism. Duyar and co-workers reported the use of a Ni–Ga catalyst for CO2 hydrogenation to produce methanol, methane, and carbon monoxide depending on the intermetallic phase present. With this idea in mind, we have prepared the composite material containing Ni–Ga and zeolite and tested it for CO2 hydrogenation in fixed-bed mode. Gallo et al. studied and observed the catalytic behavior of Ni5Ga3 material supported on silica for CO2 hydrogenation and reported the synergy between Ga2O3 and NiGa intermetallic compounds, and also, their redox nature facilitates the hydrogen spillover effects. It is also possible to prepare different intermetallic compounds containing Ni and Ga, as reported by Studt et al. in their communication in Nature Chemistry in 2024. It could be Ni3Ga, NiGa, Ni5Ga3, Ni2Ga3, or even Ni13Ga9, and they are possible to differentiate via X-ray diffraction analysis; gallium and nickel alloy formation are suggested to exhibit high catalytic performance. − Among these compounds, Ni3Ga has been studied for CO2 reduction, mainly for electrochemical reduction of CO2 to produce higher-order products like C2–C3 hydrocarbons. Paris et al. reported the electrochemical application of supported Ni3Ga to reduce CO2 to value-added chemicals. In general, the hydrogenation activity of nickel can be tweaked by alloying it with other metals to shift the reaction pathway to CO and methanol.

Bifunctional catalysts offer the advantage of integrating multiple reaction steps to enhance the production of desired products; each component of the bifunctional catalyst does its job, and this is more important when CO2, a stable molecule, is the reactant. Among the various physical integration methods like dual tube, dual bed, granule mixing, and powder mixing/compacting for bifunctional catalysts, the powder phase mortar mixing method offers the lowest interaction distance of nanoscales, where the intermediate transfer from one active site to another will be faster. However, the prediction of the end result is complicated due to the contribution of other factors like porosity, morphology, acidity, the type of metals and support present, and, obviously, the process parameters. The effect is more pronounced in the formation of long-chain and aromatic hydrocarbons, as reported by various researchers; nevertheless, in the current study, we have applied the powder mortar mixing method, which offers a lower interaction distance.

In the current study, we tried to tap out the advantages of compositing Ni3Ga with the modified zeolite material, for example, Cu on HZSM-5. The formation of a heterojunction joins all these diverse and promising materials to synthesize a thermal catalyst. Zeolite addition to Ni3Ga creates the heterojunction and facilitates an optimized acidic environment and high porosity with small, well-defined poresmicropores. Incorporating copper in the zeolite increases the acidity and serves as a more significant number of active sites. Ni3Ga increases activity, provides high quantities of energy, and has a high thermal conductivity to dissociate carbon dioxide molecules and hydrogenate them. The current study produces a nanocomposite of copper- and bimetal-incorporated ZSM-5 in a 3:1 ratio that outperforms the CO2 conversion, activity, and selectivity in the production of chemicals through CO2 hydrogenation.

2. Materials and Methods

2.1. Materials

The following chemicals were utilized for the synthesis just as obtained without additional purification: H-ZSM-5 with a Si/Al2 ratio of 30 from Zeolyst International, copper nitrate trihydrate [Cu­(NO3)2·3H2O], nickel nitrate hexahydrate [Ni­(NO3)2·6H2O], gallium nitrate trihydrate [Ga­(NO3)3·3H2O], aqueous sodium carbonate [Na2CO3], and ethanol (99%). All of these chemicals were purchased from Merck Life Sciences Pvt. Ltd. All of the solutions for the synthesis were prepared in deionized water.

2.1.1. Synthesis of Cu-ZSM-5 (CZ)

A 1 g sample of ZSM-5 was well suspended in 30 mL of deionized water at room temperature. After adding the necessary amount of copper trihydrate, the mixture was stirred for 4 h. To gradually evaporate the solvent, the suspension was heated to 60 °C over a hot plate while being stirred throughout the night. The powder was collected, dried at 100 °C overnight, and then calcined at 550 °C for 5 h, ramping the temperature at 5 °C/min.

2.1.2. Synthesis of Ni3Ga (NG)

Nickel nitrate hexahydrate and gallium nitrate trihydrate were dissolved in a molar ratio [N2+]/[Ga3+] = 6/4 to obtain an aqueous solution (0.5 M). An aqueous solution (1 M) of sodium carbonate was used as a precipitant for the facile coprecipitation of metal ions in the precursor at 70 °C for 2 h with an ultrasonic cell crusher, followed by vigorous stirring with an autocontrolled frequency of 90 W for 45 min until the pH was 7.0 ± 0.5. The filtered catalyst precursors were dried in an oven at 100 °C and calcined at 600 °C under hydrogen flow for 6 h.

2.1.3. Synthesis of Ni3Ga/ZSM-5 (NGZ) and Ni3Ga/Cu-ZSM-5 (NGCZ)

The above synthesized Ni3Ga (NG) powder was mixed with HZSM-5 powder in a ratio of 3:1 and blended together for an hour by the mechanochemical method to get the composite, Ni3Ga/ZSM-5 NGZ. Similarly, to prepare Ni3Ga/Cu-ZSM-5 (NGCZ), Ni3Ga (NG) powder was mixed with Cu-ZSM-5 (CZ) powder in the same way mentioned above. The mechanochemical method allows both the materials to interact and form the intended composite materials.

2.2. Characterizations

Characterization provides a deeper understanding of the catalyst’s composition, structure, and morphology, and it will be the basis on which the catalyst’s activity can be predicted. The initial step for comparing the catalyst and its constituents is determining whether the composite will display catalytic activity more significant than that of its constituents: Cu-ZSM-5 and Ni3Ga. The catalyst was characterized by X-ray diffraction (XRD), BET surface area, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), Barrett–Joyner–Halenda (BJH) pore size, and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). The crystal phase of the catalyst was examined by a Rigaku X-ray diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ = 0.154178 nm, 40 kV, 30 mA) recorded with 2θ ranging from 3° to 90°. The surface area and pore size distribution were determined by nitrogen-absorption–desorption isotherms at 77 K using a Micrometrics ASAP 2020 surface area and porosity analyzer. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was made by the Mettler Toledo DSC 2 STARe system, set from 12 to 800 °C. A Laser-FIBSEM (Zeiss Crossbeam 550) was used to capture high-resolution images of the sample surface (the composite material), which was sputter-coated with a 30 nm gold layer by using a Quorum 150T sputter coater. Elemental investigations were then carried out by utilizing an Oxford Ultimax 170 EDS detector, INCA Energy 200, and Aztec software. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) was performed by a Hitachi HT7700 at four different magnifications: 20, 100, 200, and 500 nm. The ammonia temperature-programmed desorption (TPD) analysis was performed on a Microtrac BELCAT II unit at 30 mL/min helium gas flow over 30 mg of sample, which is activated in helium before admitting 10% NH3-balance helium mixture gas and later purged at 100 °C in helium flow. Peak areas below 200 °C were considered weak, 200–400 °C were medium, and above 400 °C were taken as strong acidic sites during the quantitative measurements.

2.3. Testing of Catalytic Performance

After characterization, the catalyst was tested in a thermal feed-down-flow reactor for the hydrogenation of CO2. The catalyst and other samples were tested under the same reaction conditions. The catalytic performance of catalysts was monitored and evaluated for around 4 h under one condition. The catalyst was reduced at 700 °C under a H2 atmosphere for 2 h at a 50 mL·min–1 feed flow rate. After activation, a feed mixture containing CO2 and H2 at a mole ratio of 1:3 was used for the hydrogenation test. Four vol % nitrogen (as an internal standard) was always present in the feed. The reactor was gradually heated and pressurized in the above feed mixture at 180 °C and 20 bar pressure for 2 h. Then, the feed flow was set at a 15 mL min–1 feed flow rate under the aforementioned pressure and temperature. The desired reaction conditions were set. In the reaction, we tested the samples at 250 °C, 50 bar pressure; 300 °C, 20 bar pressure; 350 °C, 30 bar pressure; 350 °C, 40 bar pressure; and 400 °C, 30 bar pressure with a continuous feed-flow rate of 15 mL·min–1. The temperature, pressure, and other relevant parameters are monitored continuously. CO2 will not react without the combination of heat, a catalyst, and reactants. To ensure this, we have done a blank test without any catalyst but with all other conditions kept the same.

For product analysis, the reactor product stream was sent through a heated transfer line at 150 °C to flush the 0.25UI loop of an Agilent 7890A online gas chromatograph (GC) in gaseous form. The GC contained three separate channels outfitted with two flame ionization detectors (FIDs) and one thermal conductivity detector (TCD), calibrated for all of the investigated components. The FIDs were fitted with a GS-Gaspro capillary and HP Innovax columns for independent analysis of oxygenates and hydrocarbons. For analyzing permanent gases, the Haysep precolumn and Mol-Sieve 13X were used with the TCD. Product selectivity was evaluated based on molar carbon, usually on an hourly basis.

The conversion of carbon dioxide, selectivity, and yield were calculated by equations provided in the literature:

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Characterizations

The characterization of the prepared samples was done using X-ray diffraction, N2 adsorption, thermogravimetry, and morphological analysis as described below. Figure displays X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis of CZ, revealing distinct peaks at various 2θ angles, namely, 7.92° (100), 8.80° (110), 14.75° (300), 22.94° (600), and 24.3° (530), with corresponding intensities. It depicts the well-crystalline MFI structure of the material matching the standard diffraction pattern for ZSM-5 material (JCPDS card no. 44-0003). The incorporation of copper does not hamper the zeolite structure, and we have seen additional peaks corresponding to the diffractions from copper oxide species, as the copper content is 8%; this does not rule out the well-dispersed state of copper species on the porous support, ZSM-5; however, the copper oxide has become well crystalline during calcination at 550 °C. The identified crystal structure for CZ is tetrahedral. The observed peaks indicated an ordered arrangement of the atoms in the crystal lattice. The peak at 8.72° corresponds to the (110) plane, further affirming the crystallographic integrity of the material. A well-defined (110) plane contributes to the stability of the catalyst’s active sites, enhancing its thermal performance. These insights into the zeolite structure are crucial for tailoring the activity and selectivity of the final composite material, NGCZ, in optimizing its performance for catalytic thermal processes. The structural orientations can influence the exposure of active sites on the catalyst surface that impact its efficiency. ,

XRD analysis of the “NG” precursor, which is nothing but the as-prepared material before reduction, unveils a well-defined crystalline structure with distinct peaks at various 2θ angles, signifying specific crystallographic planes. The peaks at 32.02° (110), 38.98° (201), 42.6° (300), 60.24° (500), and 61.58° (510) highlight the ordered atomic arrangement and crystallographic orientation of NG precursor, a hydrotalcite type of structure as reported by Men et al. This confirms the formation of the precursor to be converted to NG alloy material upon reduction in a H2 environment. The unique crystallographic features influence the exposure of active sites on the catalyst surface, impacting its efficiency in thermal reactions. The crystal structure for NG before calcination has an orthorhombic crystal structure. Additionally, Figure displays the X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis of NG postreduction, revealing a well-defined crystalline structure, as evidenced by distinct peaks at various 2θ angles corresponding to specific crystallographic planes. The intense peak at 44.24°, corresponding to the (620) plane, displays the success of the reduction process and the formation of the Ni3Ga phase with significant crystallinity. The specific crystallographic planes and orientations unveiled by the XRD analysis provide insights into the potential interactions between NG and CZ in NGCZ, such as CZ. The reduction step for Ni3Ga is evident, as it eliminates impurities and volatiles, contributing to the enhanced crystalline structure of the material. The identified peaks provide insights into the arrangement of atoms within the crystal lattice, aiding in tailoring the catalytic activity of NG, which plays a crucial role in determining its suitability as a component of NGCZ for thermal catalysis. When incorporated into NGCZ, these properties contribute to a well-defined, stable, and efficient thermal catalyst. The crystalline structure’s understanding enables the rational design of NGCZ for enhanced thermal catalysis, ensuring optimal performance in various applications. Upon integrating the peaks of high I/I o values, the particle size calculated using the Scherrer equation from the “full width at half-maximum” (fwhm) value is around 10 nm for NG before calcination. Overall, Figure displays the crystallography of the constituents with well-aligned crystal structures. It showed that the calcination and reduction were effective and necessary.

Figure displays the thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) curve of CZ, revealing a substantial weight loss of 42% at 800 °C. This weight loss involves the moisture present in the ZSM-5 zeolite framework or the desorption of other volatile compounds and also the moisture and water of hydration present in copper nitrate (boiling point: 170 °C), which is always seen below 150 °C. Further to that, the high-temperature weight loss corresponds to the decomposition of the copper nitrate precursor to form copper oxides. , The weight loss trend of CZ, which has only 8% Cu in it, matches with the TGA decomposition of copper nitrate hexahydrate materials, which showed 10% loss below 150 °C and 55% weight loss by 250 °C due to the decomposition of the nitrate phase.

The Ni3Ga precursor exhibits excellent thermal stability with an 8.26% weight loss at 800 °C. This weight loss could be due to oxidation or decomposition reactions, desorption of gases, or phase transitions, which were not complete or did not happen during the 600 °C reduction step. Combining NG with CZ in a 3:1 ratio offers a prospective material with enhanced thermal stability for the title application.

Table displays the surface area, pore size, and pore volume from N2 adsorption analysis calculated using BET and BJH equations of NGCZ and its individual components. CZ has the highest BET surface area of 289.6 m2/g and a relatively low pore size of 3.45 nm, indicating its favorable adsorption properties with smaller pores. However, NG, both before (BET: 14.17 m2/g, BJH: 21.9 nm) and after calcination (BET: 0.25 m2/g, BJH: 94.77 nm), shows inferior surface characteristics, with a notable decrease postcalcination. Our Ni3Ga precursor material matches with the XRD results of Men et al.; however, they have reported a high surface area value of 175 m2/g for their precursor material, which dropped down to 25 m2/g upon hydrogen reduction. We also observe loss of surface area upon hydrogen reduction, but the hydrotalcite-like structure we have obtained may not be a layered one. In contrast, the composite NGCZ (3:1) emerges as a superior catalyst, presenting a BET surface area of 17.58 m2/g and a BJH of 4.74 nm, signifying a microporous structure with high pore distribution.

Figure shows that CZ, NGCZ, and NG presented types IV, III, and V, respectively. Type III is a reversible isotherm with mesopores that has a large surface area for efficient absorption of reactants with a wide range of pore sizes that are supplied by the support and the metal, which can transport reactants and products, accommodate various reactants, and selectively absorb certain molecules due to the high distribution of narrow pores without the catalyst interfering with the absorption properties of the catalyst. Type IV are irreversible isotherms with a sharp rise in the absorption curve at low pressures, and they have a broad distribution of pore sizes. Type V are those with large pores (>50 nm) with a sharp rise in the absorption curve at high pressures. They require large pore size for efficient mass and electron transportation. The pore distribution of the NGCZ (3:1) composite is such that CZ is present in a composition ratio of one-quarter but presents a higher surface area than NG. This combination of BET surface area and BJH values makes it a superior microporous catalyst with enhanced surface properties for thermal catalytic applications.

Figure shows the SEM images of the NGCZ composite sample with two different magnifications. These images show the formation of a uniform and evenly composited form of the two components used. The average particle size is read around the 100–150 nm range; however, they are visible as aggregated particles. The morphology of the particles is irregular, but they are seen as having sharp edges and a crystalline appearance.

Figure represents the EDS and elemental mapping results of the sample and confirms the well-dispersed and uniform distribution of the composite components. The shining particles in the SEM images are Ni3Ga, showing a more lustrous nature due to their existence as a metallic alloy state, which is affirmed from their elemental mapping data. Elemental mapping is done for all the metals present in the sample, namely, aluminum, silica, copper, gallium, and nickel, and the results show the desirable levels of dispersion of these metals when the composite is formed. In particular, metals like nickel and copper are clearly seen as dispersed and visible aggregate islands, as is also gallium to an extent.

Figure displays the transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images of NG and CZ materials at different magnifications. Figure a depicts the 200 nm scale images of NG, with dark and bright blocks, which represent nickel and gallium spots, respectively. The nickel and gallium particles are seen to cover each other, or in other words, gallium particles are embedded into the dispersed nickel surface, indicating the active and, more importantly, the stable catalytic sites. The two different crystalline lattice fringes seen in Figure c reveal the highly dispersed distribution of two different metals, nickel and gallium, within the NG material.

In Figure d–f, TEM images of CZ material are displayed at three different magnifications of 200, 50, and 5 nm. Copper is evenly dispersed and appears as gray shades in Figure d, and upon further magnification, it is seen as finely distributed copper particles over the zeolite structure, highlighting its uniform incorporation within the catalyst. Gallium has the role of a protective layer for nickel particles, enhancing structural integrity and ensuring prolonged catalyst life during demanding reaction conditions. The composite material, NGCZ, is prepared by combining both NG and CZ at an equal weight ratio and ends up in a well-dispersed active phase, maximizing the utilization of catalytic sites. This configuration optimally positions the active elements for effective participation in the catalytic process. The high nickel content in the dark blocks signifies potent catalytic centers positioned along with gallium, ensuring stability critical for enduring the harsh reaction environment. The presence of Cu-ZSM-5 provides cavities for metals like nickel and gallium to occupy in their microporous channels and prevent them from disintegrating electronically. The three favorable attributes of the catalysts, namely, the microporosity, the protection layer of gallium, and the fine distribution of active sites, make this composite material synergistically active for the title application. Thermal stability and mitigation of coke deposition are essential for sustaining catalyst activity over extended periods to ensure prolonged activity of these catalysts. Overall, TEM displays that the catalyst has a microporous and core–shell-type morphology with nickel-rich zones facilitating the initial steps of the reaction, promoting the activation of carbon dioxide, and facilitating hydrogenation. Simultaneously, gallium contributes to the catalyst’s structural robustness, preventing degradation over extended reaction periods. The even distribution of copper supports uniform heat dissipation, preventing localized overheating and ensuring consistent catalytic activity. The dispersion of Ni, Ga, and Cu elements optimizes catalytic performance, promising enhanced efficiency and stability in this environmentally significant reaction.

NG has mainly weak acidic sites, whereas NGCZ has an optimized distribution of acidic sites across all three regimes, providing an optimized acidity profile for the final sample, NGCZ.

3.2. Catalytic Performance: Activity, Selectivity, and CO2 Conversion

The catalytic performance of the catalysts tested in this study, in terms of CO2 conversion, is given in Figure , a total run time of 20 h at different test conditions, as given in the x-axis. There are five different test conditions applied during the 20 h of operation; at each condition, the catalyst is tested for 4 h of duration. The following are the test conditions in their order of execution, provided the same catalyst bed loading is used for the whole 20 h of testing. Period 1 is with 250 °C and 50 bar, period 2 is with 300 °C and 20 bar pressure, period 3 is with 350 °C and 30 bar pressure, period 4 is with 350 °C and 40 bar pressure, and period 5 is with 400 °C and 30 bar pressure. The reaction system was kept for 1 h in the beginning at each period to reach a steady state, and then only the analysis of the product commenced.

For all the samples tested, obviously, the CO2 conversion increases with temperature due to the increase in the strength of adsorption and the number of collisions, and thereby, the reaction rate also increases. When the pressure was increased from 30 to 40 bar isothermally at 400 °C, the conversion rose significantly for all the samples. The results of test conditions 1, 250 °C and 50 bar, indicate that the CO2 activation does not occur before reaching the kickoff threshold activation temperature. Among all the samples tested, NGCZ showed the best conversion values in almost all test conditions. The values of CO2 conversion are given as data labels in Figure for quick reference. The NGZ sample showed inferior CO2 conversion values compared to the NG and NGCZ samples. This shows that zeolite alone could not contribute to the catalytic conversion of CO2, but it lowers the conversion rate as the total available active site concentration reduces. In the case of NGCZ, the presence of copper ions in the zeolite channels might have played a vital role in transforming the acidic property and the nature of acid sites of the nanocomposite material.

During the CO2 hydrogenation reaction over the different catalysts, various products are formed, especially CO, methane, methanol, ethane, DME, and traces of other products. The detailed representation of the product formed is given in Figures – separately for each catalyst demonstrated as the selectivity and yield values calculated using the equations provided in the “Materials and Methods” section of this communication.

Figure shows the product selectivity and yield over the NG catalyst, which is nothing but a pure Ni3Ga5 reduced sample. This sample showed a conversion of 0.51% only at 250 °C and 50 bar pressure, whereas it rose to 15.12% at 400 °C and 40 bar pressure with a selectivity of 80% to CO, 20% to methane, and around 1.5% to ethane. The rest of the products are less than 1% in their selectivities.

Figures and display the productivity and yield comparisons of NGZ and NGCZ samples. Carbon monoxide is the major product formed in all the catalysts, and it keeps increasing with increasing the catalyst bed temperature as well.

In every thermal reaction, the reaction progresses through the lower energy pathway for molecules to coordinate at the surface sites and further with the rearrangement of bond breaking and forming. It is conditional to meet Gibb’s energy requirements for the spontaneous acceleration of reactions depending on reaction parameters. Even though activation energy decreases by catalysis, the specific reactivity of the catalyst surface, heat of adsorption/desorption of reactants and products, etc., which are directly related to temperature, result in determining the rate and speed of the reactions. The catalyst has been shown to possess stable performance for 20 continuous hours at varying temperatures and pressures. This indicates that the catalyst has high thermal stability, which can be attributed to the low thermal coefficient of expansion and the morphology of the catalyst, in which CZ possesses a high content of silica that serves as a protective layer from oxidation, especially for sensitive materials like gallium. Also, the low thermal coefficient expansion of the materials is relatively low and matching; thus, it helps to minimize the stress and potential damage that can occur at the catalyst’s surface, which enhances thermal stability. NGCZ is the best catalyst of all of the samples studied in this work. It is understood the use of HZSM-5 fostered a more excellent acidic and porous environment that helped to improve the adsorption, reactivity, and desorption of CO2 and H2, the two reactants.

Figure discusses the observed trends in CO2 conversion and product selectivity for the tested catalysts under different reaction conditions and provides valuable insights into the complex interplay among temperature, pressure, and catalyst performance. The catalyst NGCZ outshines its counterparts, and NGCZ’s superior performance in selectivity and CO2 conversion underscores its efficacy in the catalytic process, making it a promising candidate for CO2 utilization. It excels in converting a significant portion of CO2, suggesting its potential to contribute to more sustainable and efficient processes for synthesizing valuable chemicals from carbon dioxide, the major products being CO and methane (CH4). This result is in alignment with previous reports of composite materials containing nickel and gallium in the literature. As reported by Duyar et al., Ni3Ga material preferably produced CO, whereas Ni5Ga3 produced methanol as the main product. The Ni5Ga3 phase present in their fresh catalyst is changed to Ni3Ga in the discharged spent catalyst, showing the oxidative phase transformation, which might occur in the presence of the moisture produced during the reaction or catalytic reduction. In our case, the major phase present is Ni3Ga, and hence it produced more CO, which could be effectively converted to hydrocarbon over the same catalyst bed through tubular reactors via the Fischer–Tropes (FT) reaction, as we use a supported composite catalyst. Table depicts the selectivity tuning achieved through this study toward hydrocarbons and oxygenates. NG samples offered close to 100% hydrocarbon selectivity, whereas NGZ tends to enhance the oxygenate formation. Upon addition of copper into the zeolites, the composite material thereby enhances oxygenate formation at lower temperatures. This has been attributed to the changes in the acidity of the catalyst samples; NG possesses the lowest acidity, NGZ has increased acidity but mostly weak sites, whereas NGCZ has the highest strong acidic sites as read from Figure and Table , pointing to the fact that as high-temperature acidity, represented by Bronsted sites, increases, the oxygenate formation enhances.

Carbon dioxide hydrogenation via thermal catalysts is a promising method for the scalable production of value-added chemicals. In the reaction, carbon dioxide is reduced by using hydrogen gas as the reducing agent in the presence of a thermal catalyst. The reaction is represented in the following equations, which include adoptive activation of CO2 and H2 molecules and consequently convert to products like CO, methane, methanol, DME, and other hydrocarbons. The adsorbed activated species like CO2* and H* are formed over the active sites on the catalyst, namely, metal centers and functional groups, and react to products through the intermediates like CO and HCOO–, the active sites of the catalyst, via the hydride transfer mechanism. , The adsorption of carbon dioxide is exothermic, while the adsorption of hydrogen molecules is endothermic. The adsorption facilitates the weakening of the CO2 bonds and prepares them for further reactions. The C–O bonds present in CO2 are broken down during activation, requiring much energy. H2 molecules are absorbed and start to break the H–H bond on the catalyst’s surface by interacting with the metal centers, requiring lower activation energy than carbon dioxide due to the lower stability of hydrogen molecules. The products are desorbed to create space for absorption of reactants to continue the catalytic cycle. The catalyst becomes deactivated due to byproducts or other species on its surface, and it can be regenerated to restore its activity and selectivity. The NGCZ composite displayed thermal catalytic activity because of the positive synergistic effect of Cu atoms present, the HZSM-5 pore and channel structure, and the active Ni3Ga phase.

4. Conclusion

The results matched the aim and hypothesis and successfully answered the research question. XRD, TGA, BET analysis, BJH analysis, ammonia TPD, and TEM revealed that the nanocomposite NGCZ has a crystal structure with an adequate pore size distribution and surface area. XRD revealed that the components possessed great thermal properties with different crystal structures, contributing to an overall highly crystalline catalyst with great thermal properties. Moreover, it shows that the nanocomposite presents excellent thermal stability due to the components’ low thermal expansion coefficient, low coke deposits, and low weight loss, according to the TGA. This indicates the presence of a multimetallic composite phase capable of activating CO2, which is relatively inactive. The BET and BJH analyses confirm that the catalyst has a great surface area and microporous structure, enabling greater selectivity and more active sites. The best composite material prepared in this study facilitated CO2 conversion of 17.8%, stable for 20 h. The current study sheds light on the opportunity of using nanocomposites prepared by combining an alloy of two or more metals along with suitable stable porous support; synergistic catalytic activity and high thermal and chemical stability can be achieved for CO2 hydrogenation at feasible thermocatalytic reaction conditions. The study can be expanded to designing and creating a diverse range of catalysts based on this nanocomposite that can be more selective and higher in the level of CO2 conversion for energy and environmental applications.

5. Applications

Catalysts are used to accelerate chemical reactions, and their importance in a chemical reaction makes them an essential component of the chemical reaction. The nanoengineered catalyst has a variety of applications: industrial and environmental. The catalyst can be used in (a) industrial applications to produce carbon dioxide methanol, making it a sustainable option. Also, it can be used in (b) environmental applications by utilizing light as an energy source and carbon dioxide as a feedstock; in return, it lowers the atmospheric carbon dioxide concentration and carbon dioxide emissions. It shifts the methodology to a greener one that reduces dependence on nonrenewable resources for methanol production. This decreases the earth’s average temperature and prevents extreme weather changes, resulting in better plant health and productivity. Finally, it can be applied in (c) future research as the field keeps on evolving and looks for mutual benefits for humans and the environment. Therefore, the nanoengineered catalyst could be used for analysis and modified to pursue more remarkable outcomes of methanol.
