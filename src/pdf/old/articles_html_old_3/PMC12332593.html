Abstract

Chickpea is cultivated in more than 50 countries around the world, with a growing market reaching more than USD 11 billion worldwide. The annual production of chickpeas is around 1 t/ha, which is less than the expected yield of 6 t/ha when grown under optimal conditions. Insect pests are considered the major hindrance to chickpea production, with chickpea leafminer ( Rondani) and chickpea pod borer ( Hübner) as the most spread and damaging ones. Host-plant resistance is the most effective control strategy to significantly reduce the use of pesticides and their side effects on beneficial organisms and the environment. This perspective aimed to highlight the mechanisms of defense of chickpea against its most damaging insect pests and . In chickpeas, antixenosis and antibiosis are the most commonly reported resistance types. The key identified traits associated with the antixenosis defense against insect pests include trichomes on the leaf surface, organic acid exudation, and leaf size. The antibiosis resistance of chickpeas is related to the pod thickness, high content of phenolics, flavonoids, organic acids, defense enzymes, proteinase inhibitors, and attractiveness capacity of natural enemies. Docking results indicated that flavonoid compounds were successfully docked into the chymotrypsin and carboxypeptidase active sites, predicting the inhibitory capacity of these compounds against the digestion of proteins in the pod borer. The overall mechanisms of defense of chickpeas are governed by several quantitative trait loci (QTLs) located in eight linkage groups (CaLG01–CaLG08). In addition to biochemical compounds and proteinase inhibitors, the mechanism of defense of transgenic chickpeas has also been discussed.

Introduction

Chickpea ( L.) is an annual plant belonging to the Fabaceae family and the only cultivated species in the genus of Cicer L. It was domesticated with wheat, barley, peas, and lentils during the origin of agriculture around 10,000 years ago in the Fertile Crescent. − Chickpeas are mostly grown throughout the spring in rainfed areas and on deep soils with a high capacity for water retention in arid and semiarid regions. , Assessment of nutritional value revealed that chickpea is a nutrient-rich legume containing a high amount of proteins, carbohydrates, and fats to meet the daily dietary requirement. − According to Iqbal et al., leucine, arginine, and lysine are the three essential amino acids that are present in the greatest concentrations in chickpeas. Furthermore, chickpea is rich in dietary fibers, vitamins (e.g., vitamins A, C, B1, B2, B5, B6, E, etc.), and minerals (K, Na, Mg, Ca, Fe, Cu, Zn, Mn, and P). , Chickpea represents the third most important grain legume crop after dry bean and peas in terms of production and is cultivated in more than 50 countries around the world, , with a growing market reaching over USD 11 billion worldwide. According to FAOSTAT, the chickpea crop was grown on 14 million hectares worldwide with an annual production of over 16 million tons, or around 1 t/ha, which is less than the expected yield of 6 t/ha when grown under optimal conditions. This is mainly due to numerous abiotic and biotic stresses affecting chickpea plants. ,

The chickpea crop is challenged by several abiotic (e.g., drought stress) and biotic stresses, especially diseases and insect pests. Chickpea crops might experience yield losses of 50–100% as a result of biotic stressors. This crop is threatened by several pathogens including bacteria, fungi, viruses, mycoplasma, and nematodes. It is reported more than 172 pathogens infect chickpeas worldwide. Among them, fungal diseases are the most damaging to chickpea plants. Ascochyta blight ( (Passerini) Labrousse) is reported to be the major disease affecting chickpea crops worldwide causing up to 100% yield losses in cool and humid conditions. Despite damage caused by diseases, insect pests represent a serious threat to the chickpea crop. According to Reed et al., nearly 60 insect species are known to feed on chickpeas worldwide. The chickpea leafminer (CLM) and chickpea pod borer (CPB) are the most widespread and most damaging insect pests to chickpea crops. They both might cause between 40 and 100% yield losses in favorable conditions, respectively. Integrated pest management by combining resistant/tolerant varieties, cultural practices, and the use of synthetic insecticides is still the main approach used to control the key stressors. However, the use of chemicals is economically costly and presents several side effects on the environment and human health. Host-plant resistance is often the best control strategy for economic and environmental reasons to manage insect pests and diseases in chickpea crops. During coevolution between the host plant and the pathogen/herbivore, the host plant develops a number of genetic, biochemic, and phenotypic modifications to defend itself against its aggressors. Those characteristics might be transmitted from one generation to another through reproduction to ensure the survival of the species and might be used in breeding programs to develop new resistant varieties.

Many sources of resistance to various diseases and pests have been found among the thousands of genotypes of chickpeas that have been tested, and germplasm has been developed to be used as parental material in breeding programs. Additionally, the interaction between resistant genotypes and insect pests has been studied for identifying the different features (phenotypical, biochemical, and genetic markers) involved in their mechanism of defense. Across all studies, the interaction between pod borer and chickpea is still the most studied. Genetic studies, using molecular markers tools, have revealed the presence of several genomic regions or quantitative trait loci (QTLs) controlling the defense against pod borer and located at different chromosomes or linkage groups in the genome of chickpea. Numerous defense genes related to recognition, signaling, reactive oxygen species (ROS) detoxification, and phytoalexin biosynthesis pathways, etc. have been identified from QTLs involved in chickpea defense. Moreover, when exposed to biotic stressors, phytochemistry investigations of resistant chickpea plants have shown an overproduction of phytoalexins, including phenolics and flavonoids. Moreover, primary metabolites have been reported to play a role in chickpea defense.

This review has focused on the biology of the main insect pests of chickpeas, including the chickpea pod borer, pod borer, and chickpea leafminer, as well as the defensive mechanisms involved in chickpea. The review also investigates the multiple genes and crucial molecular markers involved in defense by revealing the patterns of gene expression in chickpeas that are attacked by the pod borer. Then, chickpea defense-related enzymes and proteinase inhibitors, in addition to the key biochemical molecules and the volatile organic compounds (VOCs) and their potential role involved in defense. Furthermore, we conducted a molecular docking experiment to evaluate how certain flavonoids interact with specific insect midgut proteinases. Finally, we discussed the origins, types, defense mechanisms, and effectiveness of the resistant transgenic chickpea plants.

Molecular Docking Study on Flavonoids for Infestation

Molecular docking is a computational technique that allows to prediction and understanding of the binding affinity of ligands to receptor proteins. , This enables us to understand the behavior of a given molecule, such as flavonoids, within the binding site of a target protein and understand the fundamental biochemical process underlying this interaction. The present study aimed to predict the inhibitory capacity of some flavonoids, identified in resistant chickpea genotypes, on two proteinase enzymes present in the midgut of larvae known to be involved in the process of protein digestion in insect midguts, namely the chymotrypsin and carboxypeptidase A, to better understand the molecular mechanism by which they affect the larval survival and fecundity of . The identified flavonoids by Golla et al. were docked into two enzymes known to be involved in the process of protein digestion in insect midguts, namely the chymotrypsin and carboxypeptidase A, to better understand the molecular mechanism by which they affect the larval survival and fecundity of . The structures of the compounds were downloaded from Puchem (https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/). The molecular docking study was carried out through the software molecular operating environment (MOE), 2022.02; Chemical Computing Group Inc., Montreal, QC, Canada, H3A 2R7, 2023. The structures of the crystallized chymotrypsin from the gut of the fire ant ( Buren), PDB id: 1EQ9 and procarboxypeptidase A from the gut larva (PCPAHa), PDB id: 1JQG altogether with the corresponding bound ligands were downloaded from protein data bank (www.pdb.org). Preparing the proteins for docking was done by assigning the protonation state to add the missed hydrogen atoms, geometry, and energy minimization. Chickpea Pod Borer (CPB)

The pod borer, which is (Hübner) (Lepidoptera: Noctuidae) (Figure a), is the major pest of chickpea crops. It has a wide geographical distribution including Asia, Africa, the Mediterranean region, and Oceania. In addition, was also reported in many European countries, such as Hungary, Italy, Romania, Slovakia, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland, and the UK. causes heavy economic losses to a variety of crops like tomato, cotton, pigeon pea, chickpea, sorghum, and cowpea. It is estimated that this species causes global losses of over $3 billion per year. , In chickpea, the pod borer has caused more than USD 328 million in losses to chickpea production in semiarid tropical regions. , The yield loss caused by the pod borer can reach 400 kg/ha in chickpea production. The damages caused by this pest can reach 30–40% losses and may extended to 100% in favorable conditions. , Life cycles of take about 30–34 days with an average temperature of 28 °C from egg to adult. The female of can lay up to 3000 eggs (mainly at night) throughout the 5–24 day oviposition period on leaves, flowers, and pods, mostly at night. A single egg is laid on a leaf, a flower, or a young pod. The egg incubation duration depends on temperature and varies between 2 and 5 days (usually 3 days). The pod borer larva attacks crops from seedling through maturity, causing damage to all the plant’s parts (leaves, flowers, and pods). After hatching, the larvae feed on the leaves and tender twigs of the chickpea plant. When the pods are formed, the larvae bore into for feeding, resulting in a low yield. Larval development of chickpea pod borer is directly affected by the nature and quality of the host plant and by the environmental conditions, mostly temperature. There are 5–7 larval instars, with 6 being the most prevalent. The pupation of the larvae occurred in the soil. In nondiapausing pupae, pupal duration ranges from 6 days at 35 °C to over 30 days at 15 °C, while the pupae’s diapausing stage may last several months. For the management of chickpea pod borer, farmers mainly rely on chemical insecticides. It is estimated that this application costs more than USD 1 billion per year. However, insecticides are costly, and their indiscriminate use has induced pesticide resistance and caused environmental pollution. The use of varietal resistance, agronomic practices, and biological control measures in an integrated approach offers sustainable management of .

Different Categories of Resistance in Chickpea against Insect Pests

Several defense mechanisms are used by chickpea plants to defend, tolerate, or avoid insect herbivory. Plants’ defense mechanisms can be categorized into three categories including antibiosis, antixenosis or nonpreference, and tolerance. The various defensive mechanisms described for chickpeas, together with their components, are discussed here.

Antixenosis Resistance

Antixenosis resistance is the set of characters, including morphological and biochemical characteristics, leading to the nonpreference behavior (oviposition and feeding) of an herbivore toward a resistant plant. In chickpea–/ interactions, some studies have reported a significant lower number of eggs laid and plant damage percentage in resistant genotypes compared to the susceptible ones. , The antixenosis process of resistant plants is supported by a range of features such as the density of trichomes, thorns, leaf exudate, and volatile chemical substances. In chickpea, the presence of a high density of trichomes and organic acid exudates on the surface of chickpea leaves has a negative effect on the oviposition of . According to Golla et al., resistant genotypes of chickpea to exhibiting a high density of glandular and nonglandular trichomes and a high amount of organic acids showed a negative correlation with oviposition preference under multichoice and no-choice conditions. Shabbir et al. also reported that chickpea genotypes with higher trichome length and density were more resistant to infestation. In chickpea– interaction, genotypes with multipinnate leaves and small leaflets exhibited less percentage of infestation, leaf damage, and yield losses when compared to genotypes with simple and normal leaves. ,, Oubayoucef et al. have investigated the oviposition behavior under choice and no-choice conditions and its relation to leaf size and insect performance. They reported that, in both the choice and no-choice tests, oviposition was correlated with the leaf surface area. Despite the resistant genotype ILC 86 (normal leaf with intermediate size), genotypes with smaller leaflets (resistant) exhibited fewer eggs laid compared to genotypes with intermediate leaflet size (susceptible). The nonpreference of ILC 86 by leafminer was attributed to the presence of biochemical compounds on the surface of the leaves. According to Chrigui et al. the recombinant inbred lines (RILs) that were resistant to leafminer had mostly shorter plant height and smaller leaflet size. Additionally, resistant chickpea lines exhibited a significant difference in organic acid content (succinic acid) compared to susceptible ones. Recently, Soltani et al. reported significant and negative correlations between phenol content and infestation percentage and flavonoid content and percentage of infestation.

Antibiosis Resistance

The deleterious effect of a resistant plant on insect survival and fecundity characterizes the antibiosis category of resistance. Antibiosis category of resistance can be divided into two categories including direct and undirect defense.

Direct Defense in Chickpea

Direct defense is the deleterious effect of a set of characters present in a resisting plant on insect survival, and fecundity characterizes the antibiosis category of resistance. The primary technique for evaluating the antibiosis component of resistant plants’ defense is the detached leaf assay. In comparison to susceptible genotypes, a number of resistant chickpea genotypes, mostly wild genotypes, displayed an antibiosis defensive mechanism against , which was demonstrated by a considerable reduction in larval survival, weight, pupation, percentage of adult emergence, and fecundity. ,,, Moreover, resistance genotypes prolonged the times between the larval and pupal stages. It has been demonstrated that the density of glandular and nonglandular trichomes, as well as the amounts of organic acids, phenolics, and flavonoids, have a negative impact on the survival and feeding behavior of larvae. The feeding habit of larvae has been reported to be negatively impacted by high nonglandular trichome density, reflected in decreased leaf and pod damage, weight increase, and larval survival. According to Bheemaraya et al., larvae preferred glabrous chickpea genotypes over pubescent ones for feeding. The findings revealed a significant and negative correlation between the number of trichomes on leaves and larval population, larval survival, and the percentage of pod damage. Golla et al. reported that nonglandular and glandular trichomes density showed a negative correlation with leaf damage and larval survival, respectively. The negative correlation between glandular trichomes and larval survival is linked to the organic acids’ exudation on the leaves’ surface. Organic acids are reported to have negative effects on larval survival and weight gain. According to Golla et al., oxalic acid and malic acid had a significant and negative correlation with larval survival. In addition to malic acid, Narayanamma et al. reported that citric acid exhibited a negative correlation with larval survival. Furthermore, oxalic acid, acetic acid, citric acid, and some unidentified organic acids exhibited negative and significant correlations with larval weight. Phenolics and flavonoid changes in chickpea plants have deleterious effects on . According to Golla et al., phenols and flavonoids had a substantial negative correlation with larval weight, pupal weight, adult emergence, and fecundity. Protein and sugar contents are also reported to be involved in the antibiosis defense mechanism of chickpeas against . In chickpea– interaction, Oubayoucef et al. found that resistant genotypes of chickpea exhibited higher larval mortality and fewer adult emergences when compared to the control plants. These results might be attributed to some morphological and biochemical characteristics present in resistant genotypes. According to several studies, leafminer infestation increases the number of organic acids (such as succinic acid), phenolic acids, and flavonoids in chickpea plants. ,, In summary, the main players in the antibiosis mechanism of defense of chickpeas against insect pests include the presence of trichomes, exudation of organic acids, and their composition, sugar content, protein content, and phenolic and flavonoid content.

Tolerance of Chickpea

As a defense mechanism, tolerance reduces the harmful effects of herbivory on plant fitness, while maintaining insect populations similar to those of susceptible plants. − Pests cannot develop resistance to tolerant plants because, unlike antixenosis and antibiosis, tolerance has no impact on the biology or behavior of insects. It is challenging to identify tolerance in plants, and the mechanisms that confer it are poorly understood. Additionally, the genetics of tolerance is mostly unknown. Plant resistance to hemipterans research has significantly improved our understanding of tolerance mechanisms. Two physiological mechanisms have emerged as trends in tolerant plants: increased photosynthetic activity and upregulation of detoxifying mechanisms to counteract the harmful effects of hemipteran herbivory. Some chickpea genotypes are reported to be tolerant to infestation under field conditions. − High percentages of plant damage have been reported after infestation, however, they exhibited high grain yields compared to the resistant and the susceptible checks. − Plant damages are overcompensated by an increase in the photosynthetic activity, leading to an increase in the grain yield. Vikrant et al. screened different germplasms of chickpea against under field conditions during 2014–15 and 2015–16. Among the tested genotypes, PGD-4 was highly susceptible to larvae according to the pod infestation score (score 8 on a 1–9 relative resistance/susceptibility rating scale). However, it exhibited a high grain yield (3600 and 3600 kg/ha in 2014–15 and 205–16, respectively) in comparison to the highly resistant genotype, Avrodhi (score 2), with 3833 and 3850 kg/ha in 2014–15 and 2015–16, respectively. Ghugal and Shrivastava have also reported a susceptible genotype (CSJ-479) to with high yielding. The defense mechanisms in chickpeas that have been studied the most include antibiosis and antixenosis. However, there is no specific study of the tolerance mechanism in chickpeas under infestation. Therefore, some studies have reported an increase in enzymatic and nonenzymatic antioxidant activity in some resistant genotypes under infestation. ,

Genomic Regions Involved in Chickpea Resistance to CPB

There are certain chickpea germplasms expressing a partial resistance to CPB that have been used in the chickpea breeding program. Understanding the genetic architecture of CPB resistance component traits and identifying markers that are closely linked is essential for the quick and precise deployment of pyramiding pod borer resistance genes. However, there are few reports about the genetic regions involved in defense against CPB. According to Barmukh et al., a dense genetic map for various CPB resistance component traits has been generated using the AxiomCicerSNP array, and main-effect and epistatic QTLs have been revealed in the RILs population derived from ICC 4958 () and PI 489777 () crosses. A total of 3873 single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) covering the entire genome were mapped to eight linkage groups (CaLG01–CaLG08) occupying a distance of 949.27 cm. One M-QTL (qUlwv01) was detected for unit larval weight measured at the vegetative stage on CaLG01, which represents approximately 17.27% of the phenotypic variation. M-QTLs for unit larval weight at flowering stage (qUlwf03) and CPB larvae per 10 plants (qLpp03) were identified on CaLG03, which make up about 20.80 and 22.14% of the phenotypic variance, respectively. Four M-QTLs for flowering traits, namely, qDf03 on CaLG03 and qDf07 on CaLG07 associated with the number of days to first flowering, explaining 42.49 and 16.06%, the phenotypic variance, respectively. Furthermore, qD50f03 on CaLG03 and qD50f07 on CaLG07 were linked to the number of days to 50% flowering. Both qD50f03 and qD50f07 QTLs explain about 41.63 and 15.51% of the phenotypic variance, respectively. A number of genes associated with defense have been predicted based on the results of gene ontology analysis, including an MYB transcription, lectin receptor kinase, chalcone, and stilbene synthase, and flowering locus T (FT) protein. Further, the cysteine proteinase inhibitor 12-like, legumin A-like genes (which possess cysteine-type endopeptidase and amylase inhibitor activity, respectively), as well as salicylic acid carboxyl methyltransferase, were reported to be candidate genes associated with reducing CPB larvae in chickpea plants.

Genes Expression Patterns during CPB Infestation

The hypersensitive response (HR), in which cells immediately around the infection site die and fill with antimicrobial substances to inhibit the pathogen’s spread, is a very powerful defense against germs on a short spatial scale. The HR can be helpful against sedentary herbivores that target specific tissues. Still, it is ineffective against most free-living herbivores, which can simply move from one feeding site to another. As a result of coevolution between plants and herbivores, plants have developed defensive mechanisms that include morphological (e.g., spines, trichomes, tissue toughness, etc.) and physiological changes to prevent the insects’ attack. Osbeck, Bongard, Miller, etc. at the time of their interactions with insects, provide fundamental insights into the mechanisms underlying the plants’ insect resistance. Differential gene expression was observed for genes encoding proteins involved in defense. Moreover, a significant difference in the plant expression was observed in terms of cell signaling, ROS scavenging, phytohormone biosynthesis, transcription factors (TFs), secondary metabolite biosynthesis, and cell-wall reinforcement. In chickpea, some studies have reported the transcriptional change occurring in plants during infestation. Singh et al. used a variety of techniques to generate transcript profiles, including suppression subtractive hybridization (SSH), microarray analysis, Northern blot analysis, and cluster analysis, to define the defense response mechanism against . Among the 63 unique genes isolated, 29 showed differential expression in chickpea plants upon pod borer feeding. The differentially expressed genes play a role in several biological processes, including plant defense, secondary metabolite biosynthesis, cell-wall fortification, ROS detoxification, signal transduction, and phytohormone biosynthesis pathways (ethylene and auxin). Genes related to plant defense include PR proteins (PR-10 and PR-5), hevein-like proteins, and LTP/protease inhibitors (PIs). In secondary metabolite biosynthesis pathways, differentially expressed genes include leucoanthocyanidin dioxygenase, dihydrofolate synthetase, homogentisate 1,2-dioxygenase, cytochrome P450, and hydroxymethyltransferase. Cell-wall-strengthening genes include endo-1,4-b-d-glucanase, cellulose synthase, and pectin methylesterase. The thioredoxin h gene, the metallothionein-like protein, and RUB1 are genes involved in ROS detoxification. Identified signal transduction genes include GTP-binding protein and ATPase. Furthermore, the study identified a number of genes previously unknown to play direct or indirect roles in insect defense, including HMGB1 (High Mobility Group B1), Pi starvation-induced protein, GH1 protein, BnC24B protein, PPF-1, RAB11A, among others. Pandey et al. investigated early wound responses in chickpea leaves within 20 min of simulated herbivory through illumina-based transcriptome analysis. Oral secretions from caterpillars were applied to the leaf surface before wounding to mimic insect feeding. In total, 1835 genes were differentially regulated upon wounding, of which 1334 were upregulated and 501 were downregulated. According to KEGG (Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes) and MapMan tool29 analyses, used to determine the genes related to biotic/abiotic stresses, several secondary metabolites synthesis through the phenylpropanoid pathway, cell-wall modification, proteolysis, redox regulation, hormone signaling, and transcription factor families were affected significantly during wounding. To get an insight into the expression dynamics of some of the wound upregulated genes during early phases of the wound response, authors made a comparison of the gene expression profiles between mechanical wounding and simulated herbivory (mechanical + saliva treated samples) at 5 and 20 min. They found that the expression of the genes at corresponding time points was higher in simulated herbivory genes studied, indicating that salivary factors probably acted as elicitors in activating their expression. Plants are able to distinguish between elicitor signals from mechanical wounding, pathogens, or insects. The defense reaction of the plant during mechanical wounding is a prevention reaction against the attack of potential pathogens. It is a nonspecific defense reaction triggered by damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) elicitors similar to the nonspecific immune system reaction of animals, in which wounding, or infection triggered the recruitment of nonspecific immune cells. In animals, pathogen recognition and defense are ensured by specialized cells which constitute the immune system. However, in plants, each cell can recognize the signal elicitors from biotic or abiotic stresses and trigger the defense mechanism. Plants use phytohormones for long-distance signal transduction and triggering of the systemic acquired defense system. Jasmonic acid (JA), salicylic acid (SA), and ethylene (ETH) are the main phytohormones produced by plants during stress. Studies have shown that under biotic stress some genes associated with the production of phytohormones have distinct expression patterns in chickpeas. According to Pandey et al., chickpea pod borer infestation upregulates genes involved in JA and ETH pathways. A number of phospholipase genes, lipoxygenase genes for 9-LOX and 13-LOX, allene oxide synthases (AOSs), allene oxide cyclases (AOCs), jasmonic acid resistance genes (JAR1) homologues, transcription factors MYC and JAZ (JA regulators) were upregulated for the JA pathway. In the resting state, JAZs interact with and repress downstream TFs (e.g., MYCs and bHLH family transcription factors) to suppress JA responses. A perception of JA by the COI1-JAZ coreceptor (regulator of JAZ) causes JAZs degradation, releasing downstream transcription factors to regulate gene expression. The S-adenosylmethionine synthases (MATs), 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylate synthase synthetases (ACS), and 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid (ACC) oxidase (ACO) genes, among others, were upregulated for the ethylene pathway. Moreover, there was an increase in the expression of a number of ethylene response factors (ERFs). Furthermore, ethylene and JA inhibit the effects of auxin and gibberellic acid (GA) on cell growth and expansion. Overexpression of JA and ETH activates downstream response genes, such as those involved in the phenylpropanoid pathway, cell-wall modification, defense enzymes, proteinase inhibitors, etc.

Chickpea’s Defense Enzymes Involve in Resistance to CPB

During infestation, defense enzymes are crucial for chickpea plants to alleviate the effects of ROS, thus tolerating oxidative stress. Additionally, defense enzymes are involved in defense activation through the production of signaling compounds (e.g., H2O2, NOx) and in the direct defense against pests (e.g., catalase (CAT) activity produces H2O, a signal compound involved in the activation of defensive genes, and also toxic to herbivores). According to several recent research articles, the chickpea’s defense strategy against also includes the use of defense enzymes. Kaur et al. investigated the response dynamics of induced defense mechanisms in leaves, pod wall, and seeds of 10 chickpea genotypes. This study reported the resistance of eight chickpea genotypes to infestation was due to the integrative effect of upregulated defense components in leaves, pod wall, and seeds, by enhancing activities of catalase (CAT), peroxidase (POD), glutathione reductase (GR), polyphenol oxidase (PPO) and phenylalanine lyase (PAL) along with the accumulation of H2O2 and total phenols in leaves. Comparative study of antioxidative enzyme activity in leaves and pod wall of ten chickpea genotypes has shown that the activities of peroxidase (POD), ascorbate peroxidase (APX), glutathione reductase (GR), glycine betaine (GB), proline and NOx concentration were higher in leaves and pod wall of resistant genotypes than susceptible genotypes.

Proteinase Inhibitors in Chickpea Resistance to CPB

Pathogens and herbivores are equipped with proteolytic machinery, which hydrolyzes the proteins and makes the resultant amino acids available for uptake. The capacity to produce active protease inhibitors (PIs) is an essential component of some plant species (including legumes) to cope with pathogens and herbivores’ attacks. Plant protease inhibitors (PPIs) are typically small proteins that are frequently found in the storage tissues of plants, such as their seeds, tubers, and aerial portions. , They function as natural plant-defense proteins that bind stably to the target pathogen or pest proteases, inhibiting or preventing access to the active site of the enzyme. , Some studies have reported the PI activity of chickpeas on the pod borer and on Fusarium wilt. PIs can be classified as serine, cysteine, aspartic, and metalloprotease inhibitors based on the mechanism of action of proteolytic enzymes and the active amino acid in their active site. The most studied PIs is serine, which has two types (Kunitz and Bowman–Birk) and are found in large quantities in legumes. The Kunitz-type contains one or two 20 kDa polypeptide chains with four cysteine residues that create two intrachain disulfide bonds and block trypsin action. Kunitz PIs are considered to be the main reported proteinase inhibitors in chickpea. Srinivasan et al. assessed the responses toward Kunitz proteinase inhibitor (CaKPI), when fed as a purified recombinant protein to larvae, to determine whether the insect can adapt to CaKPI in a manner similar to that of other PIs. This research demonstrated that when exposed to gut proteinases (HGPs), CaKPI was not proteolytically destroyed and had the most inhibitory action against HGPs produced from the fourth instar larvae. The accumulation of CaTPI-2 mRNA (gene encoding a Kunitz-type PIs involved in cell elongation) in seedlings, which was further amplified by mechanical wounding in epicotyls and leaves, suggests the involvement of TPI-2 in the response to wounds. During coevolution between plant and pathogens or insect pests, mutation in PI genes tends to generate an array of allelic variability in the plant genome and, as a result, the plant gene pool is expected to be rich in PI variants with diverse properties. , Recently, a genome-wide study of proteases together with proteinaceous PIs reported a significant number of PIs identified in the chickpea genome. Furthermore, the gene expression profiling of PIs in five different plant tissues revealed a differential expression pattern in more than one plant tissue. Earlier, Patankar et al. indicated that considerable variability exists in the chickpea PIs expression patterns during seed maturation in different cultivars. However, due to the polyphagous behavior, acquires the ability to be insensitive to chickpea PIs. This behavior complicates field management of chickpea pod borer infestations, requiring the development of novel cultivars with high PI activity or the use of PIs, both of which have negative effects on resistant species of larvae.

Several studies reported a significant negative correlation between organic acid exudate at the leaf and pod surface and plant damage, number of egg laying, number of larvae, larval survival, and larval weight. Biochemical analysis of leaf exudates through the chromatography method has shown that malic acid and oxalic acid have a negative effect on feeding behavior and survival. Golla et al. studied antixenosis and antibiosis mechanisms of resistance to in a diverse array of wild relatives of chickpea. Investigation of leaf surface exudate fingerprints through high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) showed a negative correlation of oxalic acid with oviposition, and both oxalic acid and malic acid showed a significant negative correlation with larval survival. There have also been findings of a negative correlation between phenolic acid concentration and . According to Girija et al., the chickpea lines with lower percent pod damage showed significantly higher total phenols compared to the susceptible ones. Recently, San et al. also reported that resistant genotypes to exhibit a high content of phenolic compounds. According to Golla et al., pupal weight and fecundity were negatively correlated with phenol content, but the pupal period was positively correlated. Biochemical Bases of Chickpea Resistance to Chickpea Leafminer

In response to infestation, chickpea plants exhibit increased secondary metabolite content. Soltani et al. , reported that a high content of secondary metabolites, including phenolics and flavonoids, had a negative effect on chickpea leafminer infestation. According to reported results, infested plants contained higher levels of total phenols and flavonoids and nonenzymatic antioxidant activities compared to noninfested plants. This increase was especially noticeable during the vegetative stage compared to the flowering and pod stages. , The findings showed that levels of total phenols and leafminer infestation, as well as flavonoids and infestation levels, had significant and negative correlations. , Perception of insect attack by plants triggered an oxidative burst and secondary metabolites production. An increase in the phenolic and flavonoid contents plays a key role in oxidative stress mitigation and direct defense against insect pests. However, it was also noted that levels of phenolics, flavonoids, and antioxidants significantly decreased throughout the flowering stage. , This drop was attributed by Soltani et al. to the reallocation of carbon flow to the pathway of flavonoids implicated in pollinator attraction. Exudation of organic acids is reported to also have negative effects on leafminer infestation. According to Chrigui et al., the results of the HPLC analysis showed that succinic acid content was significantly higher in resistant chickpea line RILs compared to susceptible ones during the leafminer infestation. Moreover, the scores for the resistant line (RL) were negatively correlated with succinic acid, oxalic acid, and quinic acid. Contrary to their role in resistance, malic acid, and citric acid were positively correlated with the resistance to chickpea leafminer infestation. Similarly, tartaric acid was also positively correlated with this resistance. Based on these findings, we may assume that the biochemical foundations of the chickpea plant’s defense against the leafminer are composed of phenolics, flavonoids, and organic acids (succinic acid, oxalic acid, and quinic acid). A metabolomic study on the chemical composition of chickpea plants with different levels of resistance toward chickpea leafminer will shed light on the key phenolic and flavonoid components involved in the biochemical mechanism. Understanding their mechanism of action will provide useful information for the integrated pest management program.

Chymotrypsins are endopeptidases belonging to serine proteinases that cleave aromatic amino acids preferentially on their carboxyl side. A chymotrypsin-like enzyme contains two β-barrel domains that are connected by catalytic residues. − The initial phases of protein and peptide degradation occur through endopeptidase (trypsin and chymotrypsin-like proteases) activities. The product of endopeptidase cleavage will be lately degraded by exopeptidases (carboxypeptidases) into amino acids and small peptides to be taken up by gut cells. − Carboxypeptidases hydrolyze single amino acids from the C terminus of the peptide chain. In this present study, we used the chymotrypsin identified from the fire ant () (PDB ID: 1EQ9) and the procarboxypeptidase A (carboxypeptidase A) from larva (PCPAHa) (PDB ID: 1JQG). Transgenic Chickpea

Development of improved biotic stress-resistant chickpea varieties by conventional breeding can be limited by the narrow genetic base, limited genetic diversity for this trait, barriers for sexual incompatibility and a high degree of autogamy. , The manipulations that are not feasible with cell fusion and breeding techniques are made possible by nonsexual DNA transfer methods. Genes can be acquired from unusual sources (plant, animal, bacteria, and viral) and introduced to the target crop. Today, there are different methods including Agrobacterium-mediated transformation and microprojectile bombardment transformation that are used to introduce the gene of interest into the target crop. In chickpeas, the main biotic stress-related transgenic plants developed are insect resistant. There have been reports of successful chickpea insect-resistance transformation utilizing (Bt) genes for chickpea pod borer resistance, − bean α-amylase inhibitor 1 (α-ai1) for bruchid (Callosobruchus spp.) resistance and lectin (ASAL) against aphids. BT transgenic chickpeas are the main transgenic-chicken-resistant plants against . The Bt gene codes for the synthesis of a proteinaceous crystalline inclusion body during sporulation of (Bt). At high pH levels in the midgut, the crystalline proteins become soluble and release proteins called δ-endotoxins. In the brush boundary membrane of the midgut epithelium of sensitive insects, the active toxin first binds to glycoprotein receptors. After binding, the toxin quickly and permanently inserts itself into the cell membrane. Insertion causes the creation of a pore, which causes the lysis of epithelial cells. This cytolysis paralyzes the gut, which stops eating, and the insect finally dies from starvation and/or septicemia. Early transgenic plants only express a modest fraction of Bt genes, which is not sufficient for field protection against important pest species. ,− The codon usage was engineered to be more in accordance with the desired usage of plants rather than the A and T rich Bacillus preferred usage. The necessary improvements in expression levels were made possible by removing the undesired mRNA secondary structure and polyadenylation signals. Today, Bt genes have now been introduced into and expressed in a wide range of crop species, including tomato, cotton, rice, maize, and chickpea. Most larvae fed on transgenic chickpea displayed high levels of mortality and decreased relative weight gain. Table summarizes the insecticidal activity of some Bt transgenic chickpea against . Increasing and maintaining the level of gene expression in target tissues or using multiple/novel/fusion proteins to provide complete protection against the Gram pod borer are two methods that could be used to generate insect-resistant lines. This will help to prevent the evolution of resistant pests against transgenic chickpea plants harboring a single BT gene. e.g., Acharjee et al. introduced into chickpea a cry2Aa gene with a sequence-modified open reading frame encoding the insecticidal Bt crystal protein to confer resistance to chickpea pod borer. This was possible by using a twin T-DNA binary vector containing the nptII gene for selection and a chimeric cry2Aa gene driven by the Arabidopsis small subunit gene (ats1A) promoter. Western analysis of different organs showed high levels of Cry2Aa protein in all of the green parts as well as in petals and cotyledons. Mehrotra et al. have shown the significance of pyramiding and coexpression of two Cry toxins for efficient protection against lepidopteran pests of chickpea. In contrast to the high level of production of a single toxin, pyramided transgenic plants displayed high levels of resistance and protection against larvae (15–20 ng/mg of total soluble protein). More recently, Das et al. generated a chimeric insecticidal crystal protein by designing a plant codon-optimized chimeric Bt gene (cry1Aabc), using three domains from domains I, II, and III from cry1Aa, cry1Ab, and cry1Ac, respectively. General Discussion

This review intended to emphasize the mechanisms of the chickpea’s defense against its most damaging insect pests, including the chickpea leafminer, and the chickpea pod borer (), which is summarized in Figure .

Adapting to abiotic and biotic stresses, chickpea plants have evolved two defense systems, including constitutive defense and induced defense. The constitutive defense system is the first defense barrier that protects the plant against physical environment variation and biotic stresses. It is the structural (morphology) and biochemical barriers preventing the infection of a pathogen or leading to the nonpreference (antixenosis) to insect pests. In this review, we discuss the antixenosis defense mechanism of the chickpea against their key insect pests. The primary known features of the chickpea’s nonpreference defensive mechanism against insect pests include trichomes on the leaf surface, organic acid exudation, and leaf size. ,,,− In chickpea– interaction, genotypes with high glandular and nonglandular trichome density showed fewer eggs laid and plant damage, compared to genotypes with low trichome density. , Exudation of some organic acids, including oxalic acid and malic acid, also showed a negative effect on oviposition. Succinic acid, as well as oxalic acid and quinic acid were reported to be negatively correlated with the resistant line (RL) to leafminer, damage. Additionally, chickpea plants with shorter plant heights and smaller leaflet sizes exhibit fewer eggs laid when infested by . ,, Some chickpea plant species can release some volatile organic compounds to repel insect pests through their unpleasant odors. Several VOCs have been identified in the chickpea; however, there is no report on their potential role in the antixenosis defense mechanism.

The overcome of the constitutive defense system of a plant by a pathogen or insect pest triggers the induced defense system, leading to the synthesis of several components including ROS scavengers, structural reinforcement components, antibiotic and antifeedant components, and natural enemies’ attractants (VOCs). All of these features constitute the direct defense (antibiosis), indirect defense, and tolerance defense mechanisms. During insect attacks or pathogen infection, the elicitor signals are recognized by plant cell surface-localized pattern recognition receptors (PRRs); including plant-derived damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), microbe-associated molecular patterns (MAMPs), and herbivore-associated molecular patterns (HAMP); to activate the pattern triggered immunity. , Signal recognition by PRRs induces changes in plasma membrane potential (V m), followed by a Ca2+ influx through voltage-dependent protein channels located at the cell membrane. − Accumulation of Ca2+ in cells induces the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and reactive nitrogen species (RNS) through the activation of NADPH oxidase as well as a rapid increase in phytohormones. , Some phytohormones (including salicylic acid (SA), jasmonic acid (JA), ethylene (ETH)), ROS (H2O2), and RNS (NO) act as secondary messengers and transduce the elicitor signal to the noninfected or nondamaged tissues, thus inducing the systemic acquired response in the host plant. These changes are the first events occurring in plants when faced with biotic stress. This cascade of reactions results in the activation of genes involved in defense.

Some studies have reported the transcriptional changes occurring in chickpea plants during infestation. , Recognition of DAMPs and HAMPs, from activity, triggered the overexpression of genes coding for proteins related to defense, signal transduction, and phytohormones biosynthesis pathways (ethylene (ETH), jasmonic acid (JA), and salicylic acid (SA)), ROS detoxification, transcription factors, secondary metabolite synthesis, and cell-wall fortification. , Furthermore, it reported the downregulation of genes related to cell growth and expansion through the suppression of GA and auxin actions by JA and ETH. Overexpression of defensive genes leads to the redistribution of carbon flux toward pathways involved in the biosynthesis of defensive enzymes, secondary metabolites, and phytoalexins deleterious to insect survival and reproduction.

Some defense enzymes, including CAT, POD, GR, PPO, APX, and PAL have been reported to be overexpressed by resistant genotypes of chickpea during infestation. , ROS detoxification, signal transduction by converting superoxide ions into H2O2 for use as a secondary messenger, and the production of toxic compounds by oxidizing phenolic compounds are important functions of defensive enzymes. Phenolic compounds, flavonoids, and organic acids are reported to be overproduced by infested chickpea plants compared to noninfested plants. ,,,, Chlorogenic acid, ferulic acid, naringin, 3,4-dihydroxyflavones, quercetin, naringenin, genistein, and formononetin are phenolics reported to exhibit deleterious effects on larval survival, feeding behavior, and fecundity of . Similar deleterious effects of the same compounds against other insect pests; including the armyworm , , and ; have been reported. − The deleterious effects on larval survival and feeding behavior are linked to the number of eggs hatched, larval stage duration, larval weight, and adult emergence. Compounds in the host plant may either decrease digestion and consumption; by reducing the activity of digestive enzymes; or act as toxins for herbivores.

A study by Su et al. found that larvae that were given rutin, chlorogenic acid, quinic acid, naringenin, quercitin, kaempferol, myricetin, and catechin at 1000 ppm had lower levels of serine protease and trypsin.

The same results have been reported earlier by War et al. on larvae. Genes involved in detoxification have also been upregulated in the gut and saliva of insects fed diets pretreated with these biochemical compounds. The chlorogenic acid binds to free amino acids and proteins, reducing the digestibility of plant tissue. In herbivores, caffeine and chlorogenic acids cause protein oxidation, resulting in gut toxicity. Chlorogenic acid inhibits the conversion of acetylcholine to acetylcholine in the insect cholinergic synapse by inhibiting acetylcholinesterase, AChE. There are several reports on the negative effects of flavonoids on insect pests in general; however, their exact mechanism modulating the behavior of insects remains unknown.

In this review, we did a molecular docking experiment (in silico) to assess the interactions of some phenolics with two proteinase enzymes, such as chymotrypsin and carboxypeptidase from and fire ant () midgut. All of the compounds were successfully docked into the enzymatic active sites expressed by high docking scores. Naringenin, naringin, and chlorogenic acid have shown the best docking scores. These results showed that flavonoids and phenolic acids might inhibit the activity of digestive enzymes from larval midgut by linking to their active sites. Many studies reported that the resistant chickpea lines exhibited a significant difference in the organic acid content. Organic acids, malic and oxalic acids, showed negative correlation to larval survival. It was also noted that succinic acid exudation increased during the chickpea leafminer infestation. Proteinase inhibitors (PIs) serve as naturally occurring plant-defense proteins that bind stably to the target pathogen or pest proteases, reducing or preventing access to the active site of the enzyme. This is in addition to the antifeeding effect of secondary metabolites on larvae. , In resistant genotypes of chickpea, when exposed to larval infestation, studies have shown overexpression of PIs. ,, According to many findings, Kunitz proteinase inhibitor is the most expressed PI in resistant chickpeas. In chickpeas, the antibiosis defense mechanism refers to the combined impact of defensive enzymes, PIs, secondary metabolites, and organic acids on larval survival and the fecundity of an insect pest. Some studies have reported that resistant genotypes of chickpea exhibited significant and negative effects on different parameters, including larval survival, larval weight, pupation, pupal weight, percentage of adult emergence, and fecundity of chickpea pod borer compared to susceptible genotypes. ,, Higher larval mortality and fewer adult emergences were also reported in chickpea– interaction.

An indirect response is when plants use VOCs to attract natural enemies of their herbivores, which act as plant-defense agents. Defenses of this type can be constitutively expressed or induced by the combination of mechanical wounding and low- or high-molecular-weight elicitors from the attacking herbivore. Although several VOCs have been found in chickpeas, there is little information available on their possible contribution to the attraction of natural enemies of chickpea insect pests.

These mechanisms of defense in chickpea are governed by several QTLs located in eight linkage groups (CaLG01–CaLG08). Their expression depends on different factors, including the developmental stage of the plant, tissue location, and environmental factors.

This led to the development of transgenic chickpea plants resistant to that mostly encodes the BT crystal protein. Conclusions

This review aimed to highlight the literature review on the mechanisms of defense of the chickpea against the most damaging field chickpea insect pests; including the different types of defense mechanisms, genomic regions involved in defense, gene expression patterns, defensive enzymes, and primary and secondary metabolites and their role in defense. Several genotypes that are resistant to and have been identified, and germplasm has been developed. However, little is known about their mechanism of defense, especially for chickpea leafminer . The interaction between chickpea and sheds some light on the several direct defensive mechanisms used by chickpeas. Some flavonoids and organic acids have been reported to have negative effects on insect performance. Their mechanisms of action in the insect organism remain unclear. Deleterious effects of plants on insects constitute a selection pressure, making them adapt and develop new strategies to cope with the harmful effects of plants. This later led to a breakdown of the resistance of plants. As defined, tolerance reduces the harmful effects of herbivory on plant fitness while not affecting insect performance.

Resistance will become more appropriate and adapted to sustainable agriculture by finding new sources of resistance, especially those with combined resistance to multiple pests, and diseases, and identifying new gene candidates. In IPM programs, tolerant genotypes may be a good alternative since they provide low selection pressure on pests, preventing the development of pest resistance while compensating for the damage caused by insects by boosting photosynthetic activity.

A solid understanding of the mechanism of resistance in chickpeas, appropriate metabolic pathways, the causal agents of diverse stressors, and the processes involved in response to each of them is necessary to build new strategies in breeding programs and, finally, to prevent more damage.
