Abstract

As the demand for both fluoropharmaceuticals and single enantiomer drugs increases, there is a need for enantioselective synthetic methods toward chiral fluorinated molecules. Trifluoromethyl (CF3) and the emerging pentafluorosulfanyl (SF5) fluorinated groups bear characteristic high electronegativity and lipophilicity, while exhibiting distinct steric properties, which make them attractive substituents in drug discovery. Our group's previous exploration of the gold-catalyzed hydration of CF3- and SF5-alkynes to furnish the corresponding α-CF3- and α-SF5-ketones presents an accessible springboard for the enantioselective synthesis of β-CF3 and β-SF5 alcohols. To this end, Noyori–Ikariya asymmetric transfer hydrogenation (ATH) conditions afforded high enantioselectivity (up to 96% ee) and yields (up to 84%) across a scope of eight CF3 and six SF5 substrates.

Keywords: Fluorine, pentafluorosulfanyl group, trifluoromethyl group, asymmetric transfer hydrogenation, alcohols, ketones

Introduction

Chiral centers feature in over 50% of pharmaceuticals, yet among the 340 fluoropharmaceuticals approved between 1954–2019, a mere 18% contained a fluorine or fluorinated group directly connected to a stereocenter (including racemates). Among these chiral fluoropharmaceuticals, trifluoromethylated fluorohydrin derivatives, or β-trifluoromethylated alcohols, have demonstrated applications toward treatments for neuroinflammation (PF-06795071), psoriatic disease (TYK inhibition), and Alzheimer’s disease (BACE inhibition), among others (Figure A).

Frequently juxtaposed, trifluoromethyl (CF3) and pentafluorosulfanyl (SF5) groups bear similar but different magnitudes of properties, such as their Pauling electronegativity (χr of 3.36 and 3.65 respectively), Hammett substituent constants (σm of 0.43 and 0.61, and σp of 0.54 and 0.68 respectively), , and hydrophobicity (π of 0.88 and 1.23 respectively). Given these slight variations, context-dependent application of fluorinated functional groups has been explored to tailor molecular features.

One synthetic avenue toward chiral alcohols, the enantioselective reduction of prochiral ketones, has long been a topic of investigation, with the Noyori–Ikariya asymmetric transfer hydrogenation (ATH) standing out of the group as a particularly effective approach. − The latter method typically leverages CH/π interactions of the catalyst’s electron-deficient η6-arene with the ketone substrate’s π-system to convey stereochemistry. In the past decade, several groups have applied this system to prochiral fluorinated ketones, including five examples toward enantioenriched β-CF3 alcohols (Figure B). A single iridium photocatalyst was utilized in a one-pot sequential transformation to first perform the trifluoromethylation of silylated enols and subsequently the asymmetric reduction of the resultant ketones (Figure B, eq 1). Through the homochiral ruthenium-catalyzed ATH-DKR (asymmetric transfer hydrogenation-dynamic kinetic resolution), β-CF3, β-SCF3, and β-OCF3 alcohols of different ring sizes were obtained (Figure B, eq 2). When a rhodium catalyst was applied for this type of transformation, CF3-chromanol, indanol, and tetralol derivatives were obtained (Figure B, eq 3). In the synthesis of trifluoromethylated syn-1,2-diols, various Noyori–Ikariya ATH catalysts were tested to evaluate interactions between the ketone’s α-substituent and the catalyst SO2 group (Figure B, eq 4). The installation of an α-positioned Z-methoxyimino group on the ketone substrate was demonstrated to yield high enantioselectivity and yield when applied to β-ketoesters (Figure B, eq 5).

Recent advances toward the enantioselective formation of β-CF3 alcohols have gained traction, yet the synthesis of β-SF5 alcohols has been limited to racemic and diastereoselective methods. − Given their complementary properties, the development of an analogous synthesis of β-CF3 and β-SF5 alcohols would be advantageous. Our group previously disclosed parallel access points to achieve α-CF3 and α-SF5 ketones through the gold-catalyzed hydration of the corresponding fluorinated alkyne. , With these ketones as our model substrates, we here report the asymmetric reduction of α-CF3 and α-SF5 ketones via Noyori’s asymmetric transfer hydrogenation (ATH) to afford the corresponding β-CF3 and β-SF5 alcohols with high enantioselectivity (Figure C). This in turn expands the existing, yet limited, library of chiral SF5, yet racemic, compounds − and enables predictions of enantioselectivity by comparison with known transformations of CF3 compounds.

Results and Discussion

We began by preparing an assortment of aromatic α-trifluoromethyl ketones (Scheme A) and α-pentafluorosulfanyl ketones (Scheme B) from the corresponding fluorinated alkynes using a gold-catalyzed hydration reaction previously developed in our group. , The requisite CF3-alkynes were prepared from terminal alkynes using a copper-catalyzed nucleophilic trifluoromethylation. Yields for the CF3-alkynes ranged from 36–87%, with homocoupling as the predominant cause for loss. The SF5-alkynes were obtained through a chloropentafluorosulfanylation reaction using SF5Cl followed by an elimination reaction with lithium hydroxide, , and the SF5-alkynes were obtained in 19–82% yield over two steps. Slight modification of our group’s previously developed gold-catalyzed hydration conditions , enabled lower catalyst loading and higher reaction concentration.

Given the many precedents for this transformation, we turned our attention to the optimization of a CF3 ketone model substrate (i.e., 1), which required minimal modification from the initially tested conditions. While an initial attempt afforded low yield albeit good enantioselectivity (Table , entry 1), the addition of a cosolvent provided up to a 10-fold improvement in yield (Table , entries 2 and 3). Pleased with the obtained results (Table entry 3), we were further gratified to find that these conditions were applicable to the equivalent SF5 ketone (9) without further modification. Raising the reaction temperature gave a slight decrease in yield with no improvement in the enantioselectivity (Table , entry 5). However, a lower temperature greatly improved the enantioselectivity, to the detriment of the yield (Table , entries 6 and 7). Therefore, if the yield is of less consequence, the reaction could be performed at lower temperature to augment the enantioselectivity. However, for the purpose of this study, which focuses on understanding the implications of pentafluorosulfanyl versus trifluoromethyl α-substitution, it was deemed appropriate to consider the conditions in entries 3 and 4 as optimal.

Upon identification of suitable reaction conditions, we turned our attention to the evaluation of the reaction scope (Scheme ). Across the series, it was demonstrated that enantioenrichment increased upon comparison of the trifluoromethylated species to the pentafluorosulfanylated substrates. It was also determined, by comparison with literature accounts for the enantioenriched trifluoromethylated alcohols and SCXRD for the pentafluorosulfanylated alcohols, that the reaction generated the same enantiomers of β-fluorinated alcohols. Further, more sterically congested ortho- substrates were successfully demonstrated in up to 95% ee for the SF5 alcohols. Unsurprisingly, distancing the aromatic moiety in the cases of 23 and 30 resulted in a drop in enantioselectivity due to a lack of interaction between the ketone’s aromatic group and the η6-arene; however, markedly greater selectivity was obtained for the SF5 compound than for the CF3 compound. This could be due to directing interaction effects or simply a result of the greater steric girth of the pentafluorosulfanyl group. ,

Conclusion

With asymmetric transfer hydrogenation conditions, high enantioselectivity (up to 96% ee) and yields (up to 84%) were obtained for a suite of eight CF3 and six SF5 substrates. The SCXRD of two enantioenriched alcohol products enabled absolute structure determination. This successfully expands the library of enantioenriched pentafluorosulfanylated compounds while further evaluating its stereodirecting ability relative to the related-but-different trifluoromethyl group. Evaluation of further enantioselective transformations of pentafluorosulfanylated substrates is underway and will be reported in due course.

Experimental Section

General Information

All commercial compounds were used as received. Gaseous SF5Cl was condensed in hexanes at −196 °C and was used as a normal reagent in solution (1 M solution in hexanes). As SF5Cl is highly toxic, it should be used in a well-ventilated fume hood. Solvents were used as purchased unless stated as dry. Et2O, THF and CH2Cl2 were purified using a Vacuum Atmospheres Inc. Solvent Purification System. All air- and water-sensitive reactions were carried out under an argon atmosphere. Reactions were monitored by TLC on precoated plates (Silicycle silica gel 60 Å F254 230–240 mesh), and products were visualized under 254 nm UV light followed by staining with KMnO4 when appropriate. Purification by flash column chromatography was carried out on silica gel (Silicycle silica gel 60 Å F254) or was carried out under a Biotage Isolera One Flash Chromatography System (AFP) using a SiliaSep silica gel cartridge. NMR spectra were recorded on an Agilent DD2 500 or Varian Inova 400 MHz spectrometer in the indicated solvent at 298 K. Chemical shifts for 1H and 13C spectra are reported on the δ scale in ppm and were referenced to residual solvent references or internal TMS reference. For 19F spectra, calibration was performed using a unified scale. Resonances are reported as follows: chemical shift (δ ppm), multiplicity (s = singlet, d = doublet, t = triplet, q = quartet, p= quintet, m = multiplet, br. s = broad signal, or a combination of the above), coupling constant (Hz), integration. High-resolution mass (HRMS) spectra were obtained on a LC/MS-TOF Agilent 6210 instrument using electrospray ionization (ESI) or atmospheric pressure photoionization (APPI). GC–MS analyses were performed on a Thermo Trace ULTRA GCMS equipped with an Agilent J&W HP-5 capillary column, an ITQ 900 mass selective detector using Electron impact Ionization (EI) or Chemical Ionization (CI). Infrared spectra were recorded on a Bruker Alpha II FT-IR spectrometer using a diamond ATR module. Optical rotations were measured on a Jasco DIP-360 Polarimeter with a sodium lamp at ambient temperature. Melting points were measured on a Stanford Research System OptiMelt MPA100 automated melting point apparatus. Enantiomeric excesses were determined by HPLC analysis using an Agilent 1200 Series and Daicel Corporation columns (ChiralPak OJ-H, AD-H or IB). The following terminal alkynes were obtained from various commercial sources: 1-(tert-butyl)-4-ethynylbenzene, 1-ethynyl-4-methoxybenzene, and but-3-yn-1-ylbenzene.

General Procedure for the Noyori–Ikariya Asymmetric Transfer Hydrogenation

Inspired by a literature protocol, into an oven-dried microwave vial equipped with a magnetic stir bar were combined the ketone (1.00 equiv) and RuCl­(p-cymene)­[(S,S)-TsDPEN] (1.0 mol %). The vial was flushed with argon. Sodium formate (5.00 equiv) and a 1:1 mixture of MeOH and H2O (1 M) were added. The vial was flushed with argon, then sealed, and stirred at 80 °C for 24 h. Upon completion of the reaction, H2O was added. The aqueous layer was extracted with Et2O (2×). The combined organic layers were dried over Na2SO4, filtered, and concentrated in vacuo before being purified by automated flash purification.
