Abstract

Understanding the effects of single-nucleotide variations on the three-dimensional structures of drug-metabolizing enzymes at the atomic level is not a well-studied field and deserves attention as it could provide insights into tackling both drug resistance and drug efficacy/toxicity issues. Here, we examined molecular dynamics simulations of the isoniazid-metabolizing enzyme arylamine N-acetyltransferase 2 (NAT2) and established a computational approach to decipher how NAT2 transitions from being a rapid acetylator to a slow acetylator in the presence of residue variations. After revealing slight differences between two rapid acetylators (the NAT2 reference allele and the legacy reference allele), we compared them to five variants with slow clinical phenotypes. In all slow acetylators, we observed a significant reduction in hydrogen bonding and dynamic residue network metric values in functional residues. For example, in the R64Q+K268R variant, the R64Q substitution causes a substantial reduction of hydrogen bonds, resulting in increased root-mean-square fluctuation and changes in residue networks. This is evidenced by the significantly decreased betweenness centrality values of catalytic D122 and putative isoniazid-binding residues S125 and F217, as well as the significantly decreased eigencentrality values of N72, D122, and the active site loop residue G124. Also, in this variant, the average distance between the putative isoniazid-binding residue F217 and catalytic residues H107 and D122 is significantly reduced. This structural shift may affect ligand binding affinity and catalysis. Overall, our findings offer new insights into how allosteric variations alter the NAT2 mechanism, leading to changes in drug exposure.

Introduction

There are three major challenges facing marketed drugs: drug resistance, lack of efficacy due to insufficient drug exposure, and adverse reactions due to increased exposure. All three are mainly linked to nucleotide variations, the first due to evolutionary changes in the pathogen genome under drug pressure and the second and third being due to population-specific single-nucleotide variations/polymorphisms (SNVs/SNPs) in drug-metabolizing enzymes (pharmacogenes) in humans. There is also a growing body of knowledge on the connection between drug resistance and human pharmacogenomics. This is particularly important in populations with a high incidence of infectious diseases and distinct, population-specific variation profiles. Therefore, understanding the effects of SNVs on drug-metabolizing enzymes might provide insight into addressing both drug resistance and drug efficacy/toxicity issues.

Here, we focused on the isoniazid-metabolizing enzyme arylamine N-acetyltransferase 2 (NAT2). The rate of metabolic activity of NAT2 is affected by population-specific single-nucleotide variations/polymorphisms (SNVs/SNPs), which in turn impact the rate of metabolism of isoniazid. − NAT2 is characterized as a slow or rapid acetylator, depending on residue variation. ,,− In the NAT2 nomenclature, rapid acetylators represent increased-functioning proteins, which are associated with a higher risk of treatment failure , due to insufficient exposure, whereas the slow acetylator phenotype represents decreased-functioning proteins, which are linked to increased isoniazid plasma exposure , and a higher risk of isoniazid hepatotoxicity. − Therefore, studying the structure, function, and dynamics of human NAT2 enzyme alleles with a slow acetylation phenotype could reveal how these residue substitutions induce allosteric effects and cause reduced enzyme activity.

Until recently, the reference allele in the NAT scientific community was NAT2*4, , a rapid acetylator, ,,− , with lysine (K) at position 268. The current PharmVar reference allele, NAT2*1 (legacy allele NAT2*12A), is also a rapid acetylator but has arginine (R) at position 268. Here, we chose to use the legacy reference enzyme (NAT2*4) as our reference because (1) all previous functional studies have used it as a reference, and this is still the functional reference, and (2) all global populations have a higher frequency of the legacy reference allele, with the global average being approximately 57%. Hereafter, the terms “legacy reference protein” and “reference protein” will be used interchangeably to refer to the K268 protein system (NAT2*4).

In this study, our aim is to understand how residue variations may shift the enzyme function from the “rapid” to “slow” clinical phenotype. As a first step, we structurally and dynamically compared the two rapid NAT2 proteins (K268 and R268) to determine whether the residue difference at position 268 has any effect. After identifying the protein changes caused by this variation at residue 268, we further investigated how additional residue variations might alter the enzyme function to the “slow” clinical phenotype. Overall, we analyzed five NAT2 enzyme alleles with the following variations in addition to K268R: (1) R64Q (NAT2*46); (2) I114T (NAT2*5); (3) D122N (NAT2*48); (4) R197Q (NAT2*34); and (5) R64Q and I114T together (NAT2*29).

We found that the slow acetylation phenotype in these five variants is primarily driven by localized structural alterations, rather than global conformational changes, resulting from increased residue fluctuations, disrupted hydrogen bonding, and altered residue communication networks: (1) in variant R64Q+K268R, the loss of E38-R64 and N41-R64 hydrogen bonds caused extensive structural changes in domain I, the interdomain, and the C-terminal tail, leading to increased RMSF. Furthermore, the betweenness centrality (BC) values of catalytic residue D122 and isoniazid-binding residues S125 and F217, and the eigencentrality (EC) values of functional residues N72, D122, and G124, were reduced compared to rapid acetylator systems. Additionally, the average minimum distance between the putative isoniazid-binding residue F217 and the catalytic residues H107 and D122 was significantly decreased. (2) For the I114T+K268R variant, changes in hydrogen bonding of the active site loop disrupted catalytic D122 and key binding residues. There were significant increases in the average distance between active site loop binding residue S125 and catalytic residues acetyl-C68, H107, and D122. (3) In the D122N+K268R variant, the loss of ionic interactions between catalytic residues N122 and H107, along with a significant reduction in N122-mediated hydrogen bonds, destabilized the catalytic triad. This was accompanied by substantial decreases in EC and closeness centrality (CC) values. Additionally, many significant distance decreases between active site residues were observed. (4) Structural changes in the R197Q+K268R variant included reorientation of binding residue F217 as well as reduced BC value of catalytic D122. (5) The combined effects of both R64Q and I114T substitutions in the R64Q+I114T+K268R variant disrupted E38-R64 and N41-R64 hydrogen bonds and affected the active site loop. These changes led to decreased BC value of residue N72 and reduced EC of residues G124, S128, and I290, likely affecting substrate and cofactor binding.

Methodology

Protein Selection and Preparation

The human NAT2 structure PDB ID 2PFR (chain A) was chosen because it is the only experimentally solved human NAT2 structure; additionally, it has good quality metrics according to the PDB and no missing residues. Notably, residue 268 is a lysine in structure 2PFR, corresponding to the current legacy reference allele. Coenzyme A was removed to achieve the apo enzyme state. Regarding the residue rotamers, only the first rotamer was retained. Using the protein mutagenesis wizard in PyMOL version 2.5, missing atoms were added to residues E4, E8, R16, N17, R61, K173, E174, K183, D248, E261, and E265. The first residue in the structure, serine, is a cloning artifact that was mutated to the first residue of the UniProt sequence, methionine, by using PyMOL.

The structure was then protonated using the H++ Web server at physiological pH 7.4. In accordance with the catalytic mechanism for a substrate-binding NAT2 system at physiological pH, , the hydrogen on the sulfur atom of catalytic C68 was replaced with an acetyl group using PyMOL, and catalytic H107 was protonated at the epsilon position (HIE: neutral, ε-nitrogen protonated).

Selection of Variants and Preparation of Allele Proteins

Along with the legacy reference structure (legacy and current NAT2*4), we prepared six other protein systems, including the K268R variant protein and all of the alleles with known clinical data that exhibit a slow acetylation phenotype as a result of additional variation(s) to the K268R variation (Table S1). Although each of these alleles is associated with a different genotype, some were included in one system due to their common missense mutations (residue substitutions/variations), while their synonymous mutations were ignored (Table S1). Each allele protein was generated by mutating the relevant residues in PyMOL and then protonated individually, as explained above.

Molecular Dynamics (MD) Simulations

For the MD simulations, the AMBER 14SB force field was used with GROMACS 2020.1. The antechamber, prepgen, and parmchk2 tools of the AmberTools18 package were utilized to create parameters for the acetyl-cysteine residue via the AM1-BCC charge method. The tleap program, also from AmberTools18, was used to generate the topology. During the previous step with the TIP3P water model, water molecules were added as a solvent to a cubic periodic box with a minimum distance of 10 Å. The systems were neutralized using 0.15 M NaCl, and the van der Waals and bonding parameters from general amber force field 2 (GAFF2) were implemented. The topology was then converted to GROMACS using the ACPYPE (or AnteChamber PYthon Parser interfacE) program. For the production runs, all molecular systems were energy-minimized using a conjugate gradient being energy relaxed with a force tolerance of 1000 kJ/(mol nm) and capped at a maximum of 50,000 steps. The temperature of the systems was then equilibrated at 310.15 K over 100 ps utilizing the velocity rescale thermostat (modified Berendsen thermostat). The pressure was equilibrated using the Parrinello–Rahman barostat to maintain 1 bar of system pressure. Production runs used one Nvidia Tesla v100 GPU in conjunction with 10 CPU cores at the Centre for High Performance Computing (Cape Town, South Africa). All simulations were run at a pH of 7.4 and a temperature of 310.15 K (37 °C). Each of the MD simulations was run for 700 ns with 2 fs time steps, and coordinates were written every 10 ps. The Linear Constraint Solver (LINCS) algorithm was used for the duration of the simulations to correct for rotational bond lengthening. Coulombic and van der Waals short-range cutoffs were set at 1.4 nm. Long-distance electrostatic interactions were calculated using the Particle–Mesh Ewald (PME) algorithm, with a Fourier grid spacing of 0.16 nm.

Molecular Dynamics Trajectory Analysis

Following the completion of the MD simulations, periodic boundary conditions of the trajectories were corrected, jumps across boundaries were removed, and the protein was centered inside the simulation box. The trajectories were visually inspected using VMD. The first 100 ns were omitted due to equilibration, and the final 600 ns of the simulations (100–700 ns) were utilized for analysis. The GROMACS tools gmx rms, gmx rmsf, and gmx gyrate were used to determine the root-mean-square deviation (RMSD), root-mean-square fluctuation (RMSF), and radius of gyration (R g), respectively. Protein backbone atoms were used to calculate the protein RMSD after least-squares fitting to the backbone atoms. The protein backbone RMSF was used to analyze the motion of individual residues during the simulation. To determine protein compactness, the R g was computed.

Triplicate independent simulations for the legacy reference and K268R systems were run to check consistency and confidence in the system and force field chosen. The RMSD and R g values of these triplicate runs were consistent and are summarized in Figures S1 and S3 as line plots and in Figures S2 and S4 as violin plots, respectively.

Hydrogen Bonding

Inter-residue hydrogen bonding information was calculated using the “hbond” command in AMBER’s cpptraj program with default parameters. For each MD system, hydrogen bond frequencies between atoms were summed by (1) residue pair and (2) individual residue. A single residue pair can exhibit a frequency above 100% due to two or more simultaneous atom-pair hydrogen bonds within the residue pair.

Dynamic Residue Network Analysis

DRN calculations were performed using MD-TASK, , where DRN metrics were computed every 10th MD frame, and residue centrality values were averaged. The DRN metrics included averaged betweenness centrality (BC), averaged closeness centrality (CC), and averaged eigenvector centrality (EC). From this point onward, the averaged BC, CC, and EC will be referred to as BC, CC, and EC, respectively.

Clustering Analysis

Clustering analysis was performed with GROMACS using the GROMOS method at an RMSD of 1 Å, backbone atoms, and a step-size of 10. Cluster 1 of each system was used as a representation of the major structural conformations of the enzymes for enzyme mapping. The MD frame frequency of the clusters is shown in Table S2.

In-House Python Scripts to Calculate per-Residue Metric Differences (Delta)

During comparisons, delta calculations help limit noise and clearly highlight major differences. − The steps are as follows:

  • Step 1: Selection of the Baseline System: First, a baseline (reference) system is selected for comparison with other systems. We made two comparisons in the study: (1) the legacy reference average (averaged per-residue values over triplicate simulations) was used as the baseline system to compare with the rapid K268R system. (2) The rapid system average (averaged per-residue values over the six rapid system simulations) was used as the baseline system to compare with the slow systems.

  • Step 2: Per-Residue Delta Calculation: For each system compared, per-residue delta values for each metric (RMSF, hydrogen bonding, or DRN) were calculated by subtracting the residue value of the baseline system from the residue value of the system being compared (system minus baseline system).
    Deltaij=Metrici,j−Metrici,baseline
    where i is the residue number, j is the allele/system ID, and “baseline” refers to the selected baseline system.

  • Step 3: Identification of Residues with the Largest Delta Values: For each system compared, residues with the largest delta values (top and bottom 5%) were identified, corresponding to the 14 highest and 14 lowest per-residue delta values (28 residues total per system). In addition to the per-residue hydrogen bond frequency values, the hydrogen bond frequencies between residue pairs were identified (the highest and lowest 14 residue pairs per system).

  • Step 4: Visualization: The metric values of the highest and lowest 5% delta residues per system were plotted in heatmaps using Seaborn and mapped to the enzyme structure, shown as sticks with PyMOL. The corresponding residues of systems not in the highest and lowest 5% delta residues were included in the heatmaps for comparison purposes. The blue color indicates a decrease from the reference, while the red color indicates an increase. The color intensities of the residues in the heatmaps and structures match for easy analysis. Hydrogen bond residue pairs were connected by lines between their alpha-carbons, with the line thickness corresponding to their hydrogen bond frequency. In the text and figures, residue pairs are always ordered as “acceptor-donor”, e.g., E38-R64, unless more than two atoms per-residue pair are involved.

  • Step 5: Statistical Significance of Delta Values: The significance of the highest and lowest 5% delta values was determined using raw values to calculate the standard deviation across baseline systems. Residues with delta values greater than three standard deviations from the baseline mean were considered significant. Within the highest and lowest 5% delta values, significant changes from the baseline system average are marked with an “X” in the heatmaps, whereas nonsignificant changes are marked with a “-”. Only residues with significant changes are labeled on the enzyme structure figures.

Results & Discussion

The human NAT2 enzyme contains domain I, domain II, the mammalian insertion which is located within domain II, the interdomain region, and domain III , (Figure ). The C-terminus extends across the enzyme surface, enters the active site, and forms part of the binding site , with help from the mammalian insertion, which acts as a lid to bury the C-terminus via van der Waals interactions and hydrogen bonds, contributing to overall protein stability. , Domains I and II maintain the orientation and geometry between the catalytic triad residues C68, H107, and D122, and are much more conserved between NAT enzymes than domain III. ,, Potential human NAT2-isoniazid-binding residues were identified by a consensus of interactions shared between the bacterial 1W6F-isoniazid crystal structure, the only published human NAT2-isoniazid docking study, our NAT2-isoniazid docking result (unpublished), and the NAT2-CoA crystal structure (PDB ID 2PFR). Three functional motifs are conserved between NAT enzymes ,− (Figure S5: FENL (residues 37–40), RGGWC (residues 64–68), and the “P-loop” motif YIVDAGSG (residues 119–126). The RGGWC motif contains the catalytic residue C68, as well as R64, which forms an important salt bridge with FENL motif residue E38. , This salt bridge maintains the structure and stability of domain I. The active site loop (residues 119–134), consisting of the P-loop, the η3 helix, and the following loop, is located near the center of the enzyme and contains catalytic residue D122 as well as several binding residues (S125, S127, and S129) that have an important role in determining the size of the binding pocket of different NAT enzymes. , Additionally, the active site loop is thought to be important for the structural integrity of the protein. −

In this study, analysis was conducted in two parts. In the first part, we analyzed the two NAT2 rapid acetylators using RMSF, hydrogen bond, and DRN calculations. In the second part, the analysis was extended to the slow acetylators, each of which contained additional variations relative to the rapid acetylator proteins.

Part 1: Comparative Analysis of Two Rapid Acetylator NAT2 Proteins with “K” or “R” at Position 268

Root-Mean-Square Fluctuation Analysis Revealed Minimal Differences between Rapid Proteins

The average RMSF values from independent triplicate MD simulations of the reference protein (K268) are mapped onto the structure using varying intensities of red to represent fluctuation levels (Figure a), where darker shades indicate higher RMSF values. The residues with the highest RMSF values (top 10%) are shown as sticks (Figure a) and are presented separately in a bar plot (Figure b).

Notably, the three regions that form the active site entrance are all within the top 10% of RMSF residues: P96 to N99 (310 helix η2), T211 to T214 (just after helix α5), and D285 to S287 (C-terminal tail). These regions exhibit high flexibility, which may facilitate the entry and exit of the substrate and cofactor into the active site pocket. This is further supported by the trajectory clustering analysis, where the top three conformational clusters from each reference MD simulation are superimposed, revealing slight up-and-down movement of helix α5 and a shift of the 310 helix η2 away from the active site pocket (Figure S6).

We then examined the RMSF of the K268R variant relative to the reference enzyme to determine whether this residue variation affects the dynamics of the protein (Figure c,d). The K268R system is also a rapid acetylator, as discussed in the Introduction. Functional studies of the K268R variant recombinantly expressed in bacteria, , yeast, , and mammalian cells revealed no changes in protein expression, protein stability, or catalytic activity. Walraven et al. suggested that the conserved activity is due to the surface location and lack of interactions with surrounding residues, rather than the conservative nature of the K268R substitution. They also proposed that replacing K268 with arginine is unlikely to affect the α-helical structure of helix α7, where this residue is located.

Our comparative analysis using delta-RMSF calculations revealed minimal differences between these two protein systems (Figure c). Examining the top and bottom 5% delta values to identify the residues with the greatest deviation relative to the reference enzyme indicated decreased flexibility in the N- and C-terminal regions. However, the delta-RMSF heatmap for the rapid system (Figure S7) shows that the RMSF changes in the terminal regions do not exceed the statistical threshold of 3 SD from the reference replicates and are therefore not significant. In contrast, domain I residues Q46 and M48, and domain II residues K184 and K185, show statistically significant RMSF increases, though these remain minor, all below 0.16 Å (Figure d). Since the K268R system is a rapid acetylator, like the reference, these changes are unlikely to have a substantial effect on enzyme activity or metabolism.

Delta Hydrogen Bond Analysis Showed Hydrogen Bond Gain around the Variation

Figure summarizes the hydrogen bond results for the rapid proteins. In Figure a, the reference hydrogen bond frequencies are mapped onto the enzyme structure, while Figure b highlights the top 10% of residues with the highest hydrogen bond frequencies.

Our calculations show that the catalytic residue D122 exhibits the highest hydrogen bond frequency among all residues (470.8%; Figure b), forming hydrogen bonds with N72, Q130, G124, H107, Y190, and S125 (in order of frequency). D122 plays a significant role in maintaining the conformation and stability of the active site loop, as G124, S125, and Q130 are all located on this loop. Catalytic H107 is known to be stabilized by D122 via ionic interaction to maintain the catalytic triad conformation; , our results showed that hydrogen bonding contributes to this stabilization.

R64 has the second-highest hydrogen bond frequency and shares the highest residue pair hydrogen bond frequency with E38 (206.9%). As mentioned earlier, R64 is known to form a salt bridge with E38, which helps maintain the structure and stability of domain I. , R64 forms hydrogen bonds with E38, N41, R9, and H58, while E38 hydrogen bonds with R64, G65, L40, and G66. These interactions contribute to the conformation and dynamics of the domain I tertiary structure. R64, E38, and N41 are highly conserved in mammalian NAT proteins, suggesting that they play important roles in enzyme function and/or stability. Significant structural changes in this region could alter the position of catalytic acetyl-C68, potentially impacting enzyme activity.

I290 and R242 have the third- and fourth-highest hydrogen bond frequencies, respectively (Figure b), and together they form a high hydrogen bond frequency residue pair (104.6%). I290 forms bonds with R165, R242, S128, L180, T289, and S127, whereas R242 interacts with V231, I290, Q168, and D251. The many hydrogen bonds of I290, R242, D251, R165, and Q163 produce a network connecting the C-terminal tail, active site loop, mammalian insert, and domain III, which likely contributes to the structure and stability of the enzyme and the active site pocket, especially the C-terminal tail.

N72, the fifth residue with the highest hydrogen bond frequency, is located directly behind the catalytic site (Figure a) and likely plays a role in maintaining the structure and geometry of the catalytic triad, forming hydrogen bonds with catalytic D122, L108 (adjacent to catalytic H107), A123 (adjacent to catalytic D122), Y76, and catalytic acetyl-C68. Figure a highlights three notable residue pairs with high hydrogen bond frequencies, shown by blue lines, which were not formed by any of the top 5% hydrogen bond residues. The C-terminal residue pair D285-S287 helps change the direction 90 deg of the C-terminal tail from downward across the enzyme face to probing the active site. The F37-S224 residue pair serves as the primary connection between domain I and the interdomain. F37, a key residue for binding isoniazid and the cofactor, ,,, is positioned next to E38, which hydrogen bonds with the critical R64 residue. Finally, G91 and D122 form hydrogen bonds with catalytic H107 from opposite sides, contributing to its position and ensuring catalytic triad geometry and enzyme function.

With the exception of D285 and S287, which primarily form hydrogen bonds with each other, the residues in the three active site entrance regions mentioned earlier (P96→N99, T211→T214, and D285→S287) exhibit low hydrogen bonding frequencies, placing them among the bottom 10%. This indicates an inverse correlation between RMSF and hydrogen bonding in these residues, suggesting that they are likely unrestrained and flexible to accommodate drug entry and binding.

Comparing the K268 system to K268R via delta-hydrogen bonding calculations (Figure c,d) revealed a significant hydrogen bond frequency gain around the variation site in residue pairs E265-R268 (89%), E264-R268 (34%), and E265-N269 (18%). The change of K268 to the larger and slightly longer arginine induces hydrogen bonding between the R268 hydrogens and the oxygens of both E264 and E265, pulling E265 closer to N269, thereby inducing hydrogen bonding between these residues too. These changes in helix α7 propagate through the loop to beta-sheet β15 and the mammalian insert, as demonstrated by the significant increase in hydrogen bonding between the residue pair D251-R166 and the decrease between the residue pair Q163-R165 (Figure c,d). The effects of the residue variation may also propagate from beta-sheet β15 across the antiparallel beta-sheets β14, β13, and β12, leading to many hydrogen bond changes in residue Q226 (at the end of β12), particularly the increase in hydrogen bonding between the residue pair I32-Q226. This may result in significant hydrogen bond changes in residues R33, I32, E29, and D26. Given that the K268R system is a rapid acetylator, these hydrogen bond changes, compared to those of the reference protein, are unlikely to affect enzyme activity or metabolism rate.

Dynamic Residue Network Analysis Identified Functionally Important Residues

Here, we further analyzed the protein MD simulation trajectories using the DRN approach and its specific metrics. , In previous studies, we showed that integrating information from multiple DRN metrics provides complementary insights. −

The DRN analysis of the reference enzyme revealed that residues with high BC values are located within and around the active site (Figure a). Mapping the top 5% BC residues onto the structure exhibited an adjacent-residue pathway formed by N72, D122, G124, Q130, S129, S127, I290, S224, F37, P36, and V35, indicating information flow between the catalytic site, active site loop, and domain I (Figure S8). Residue S127, which has the highest BC value, formed hydrogen bonds with nine residues: T289, Q226, Q130, S128, S224, S125, G126, I290, and S129, more than any other residue. Located within the active site loop, S127 likely contributes to the structure and stability of this loop by hydrogen bonding to S125, G126, S128, S129, and Q130. It also connects the loop to the interdomain region above and the C-terminal tail in front by forming hydrogen bonds with S224, Q226 (interdomain), T289 and I290 (C-terminal tail).

Corresponding to the definition of CC, ,, the reference residues with the highest CC values are located near the geometric center of the protein (Figure b). On the other hand, residues with high EC values cluster within and behind the catalytic site (Figure c; Figure S8). EC measures the extent to which a node is connected to other well-connected nodes in the network , and can identify distinct functional groups or communities of nodes. Residue N72 has the highest EC value and, as mentioned earlier, plays a crucial role in maintaining the structure and geometry of the catalytic triad as it forms hydrogen bonds with many residues around the catalytic site.

Six residues were commonly shared among the top 10% of BC, CC, and EC residues: F37, V71, N72, L109, catalytic D122, and G124. These residues are primarily located in domain I and in the active site loop (V121-Q130) and are likely important for enzyme residue communication and/or structure and function. N72, as previously discussed, plays a significant role in maintaining catalytic triad stability. Residue V71, positioned next to N72, forms hydrogen bonds with W67, which likely helps maintain the position of the catalytic residue acetyl-C68. F37, a substrate/cofactor binding residue, forms hydrogen bonds with interdomain residue S224 (top 5% BC), which in turn forms hydrogen bonds with active site loop residue S127 (also top 5% BC). This creates a linkage between domain I and the active site loop via the interdomain region. This is likely the most important communication pathway of the enzyme as all three of these residues are among the top 5% BC residues and are connected through hydrogen bonds at the intersection of three different domains. L109 forms hydrogen bonds exclusively with L89, connecting the antiparallel beta-sheets on which they are situated. The catalytic D122 forms more hydrogen bonds than any other residue (Figure b), while G124 exclusively hydrogen bonds with D122, supporting its catalytic role.

Comparing the reference system to K268R using DRN calculations revealed limited differences, particularly around the variation site (Figure d–f). However, delta calculations highlighted significant deviations in the BC values of residues C148 and R165 (Figure a), possibly due to hydrogen bonding changes in the region (Figure ). In domain I, BC values decreased for residue E38 and increased for N39, while within the active site loop, residues D122 and G124 showed a decrease and increase, respectively. The only significant change in delta CC was observed for C148 (increase), which also showed a significant increase in BC (Figure e). With regard to delta EC, increased EC was observed in a group of residues within and around interdomain α-helix 5, whereas decreased EC was seen in another group within and around the active site loop. However, only residues L69 (increase) and L110 (decrease) displayed significant changes compared to the reference replicates. As the K268R system retains the rapid phenotype, these alterations are unlikely to significantly affect the metabolic rate.

Part 2: Toward Understanding the Slow Acetylation Effect of Additional Variations on the Reference Protein

Figure compares the rapid average values (baseline) with those of the slow variant systems using various metrics. We interpret that residues showing significant differences (equal to or greater than 3 SD) in the slow acetylation systems may contribute to the reduced acetylation rates observed in these variants. Figures – illustrate these differences within the 3D protein structures.

R64Q+K268R

Significant changes from the rapid systems observed in this variant were primarily located in domain I, the interdomain region, and the C-terminal tail residue R277 (Figure ). It is known that the critical hydrogen bond interactions of residue pairs E38-R64 and N41-R64 are weakened when R64 is substituted with Q64, resulting in reduced protein thermostability, which may alter the conformation and dynamics of the domain I tertiary structure. , Our hydrogen bond calculations confirmed this decrease (Figure b,c).

Notably, the RMSF increase in domain I corresponds to multiple hydrogen bond changes within the same domain I. Specifically, significant hydrogen bond decreases of domain I residues E38, N41, A47, Q64, and G65 explain the RMSF increases observed in residues N41, M42, A47, and M48 (Figure ). Additionally, the changes in hydrogen bonding involving the C-terminal tail (residue pairs E264-R277, E264-L279, and S274-Q64) correspond to increased RMSF at the start of the C-terminal tail (L275 to V280). This may disrupt hydrophobic interactions among residues L40, I273, L275, and L279, which are in close proximity to one another. Consequently, all of this could influence the hydrogen bonding patterns of interdomain residue pairs L209-S215, S215-T219, and S216-T220 (Figures and b).

Although clustering analysis showed no significant interdomain conformational change, the average minimum distance between the putative isoniazid-binding residue F217 and the catalytic residues H107 and D122 significantly decreased (Figure S11). Conversely, the distance between binding residues F217 and L288 significantly increased, which could affect ligand binding and catalysis.

Regarding the DRN, the substituted Q64 residue showed significantly decreased BC and CC values (Figure ), likely due to the substantial loss of hydrogen bonds. Furthermore, the catalytic residue D122, which ranked in the top 5% in the reference rapid system (Figure S8), and the putative isoniazid-binding residues S125 and F217 showed significantly decreased BC values, indicating that these key residues were less frequently involved in the shortest paths connecting other residues in this variant. Additionally, D122 and S125 showed significantly reduced EC and CC values, respectively (Figure ), suggesting an altered inter-residue communication network that could impact catalysis and ligand binding. Notably, the residues with significantly decreased EC values formed a cluster within the catalytic site (Figure S12), including the important residue N72. In contrast, residues with significantly increased EC values clustered within the interdomain region and around residue E38. These findings suggest a functional reorganization of the network, marked by reduced communication within the catalytic site and enhanced connectivity and influence within the interdomain region and at residue E38potentially as a compensatory mechanism for the decreased connectivity elsewhere.

Overall, the slow acetylation phenotype of this variant is likely driven by the loss of the critical E38-R64 and N41-R64 hydrogen bond interactions. This disruption triggers widespread changes in hydrogen bonding across domain I, the interdomain region, and the C-terminal tail, leading to changes in residue communication networks involving active site residues. These effects are accompanied by significantly decreased distances between the putative isoniazid-binding residue F217 and the catalytic residues H107 and D122. This is further supported by the decreased BC values of catalytic D122 and putative isoniazid-binding residues S125 and F217, as well as decreased EC values of N72, D122 and the active site loop residue G124. Collectively, these changes may impair ligand binding and catalysis.

I114T+K268R

Our delta calculations showed the least amount of change for this variant compared to the other slow acetylators (Figure ). Significant differences from the rapid systems were primarily observed in domains II and III. Delta-RMSF calculations revealed slightly increased fluctuations at residues G104 and M105, while residue D115 became slightly more rigid. Hydrogen bonding decreased significantly for residue pairs V146-R166 and D251-R166, while it increased for Q145-R166, indicating that R166 traded hydrogen bonds with V146 for those with Q145 (Figure c). Additionally, increased hydrogen bond formation was observed in residue pairs S125-S127 and S127-Q130.

In the 2PFR structure, the side chain of residue I114 forms hydrophobic interactions with helix α2 residues L21 and L24 in domain I. According to Walraven et al. substituting the hydrophobic isoleucine with a polar, hydrophilic threonine could weaken these interactions. Consistent with this, we observed decreased EC values on helix α2 from residue T25 to Q31, likely due to the disruption of hydrophobic interactions with L21 and L24 caused by the I114T substitution. This disruption may also explain why helix α2 residue L28 shows a significant decrease in BC (Figure ). Conversely, the substituted residue T114 exhibits a significant increase in EC value, along with V112 (Figure ). T114 has also increased CC relative to rapid reference systems, as do residues D115, R117, I120, S125, and G126. These DRN changes in and around the active site loop highlight the alterations in residue communication networks, which may be linked to increased hydrogen bonding of active site loop residue pairs S127-Q130 and S125-S127 (Figure b). Consequently, this leads to a cluster of residues with significantly increased EC values, including active site loop residue S128, C-terminal tail residue I290, and antiparallel beta-sheet residues V231, C233, and R242 (Figure S12). Furthermore, residues I189, L161, and Y190 exhibited significantly decreased values in BC, CC, and EC, respectively, possibly linked to the significant hydrogen bond decreases of the residue pair Y190-L161 and residue Q187 (Figures and b,c).

The proposed cause of the decreased activity is the altered hydrogen bonding pattern observed in comparison to the rapid systems, especially within the active site loop. Additionally, we identified significant changes in DRN metrics for residues surrounding the active site loop, indicating notable shifts in the residue communication network. Collectively, these alterations may impact the catalytic residue D122 and binding residues in the active site loop. This hypothesis is further supported by the significant increases in average distance between the active site loop binding residue S125 and catalytic residues acetyl-C68, H107, and D122 (Figure S11).

D122N+K268R

Our delta calculations demonstrated that introducing the D122N variation into the fast acetylator NAT2 protein resulted in several significant changes (Figure ). The active site loop residue D122 is a catalytic residue. A functional study of the D122N variant demonstrated reduced protein levels and enzymatic activity. As expected, the largest changes from the rapid systems were observed around the D122N residue variation (Figure ). N122 experienced a 274.5% reduction in hydrogen bond frequency compared to the rapid average D122 residue value (Figure c). Near the D122N variation, residue pairs L108-N72, N122-N72, N122-H107, N122-G124, and N122-Y190 showed the largest decreases in hydrogen bond frequency among all variants, while residue pairs A123-Q31, G126-S129, and S129–S125 exhibited the largest increases (Figure b). Interestingly, residue A123 had the largest hydrogen bonding increase of any variant (Figure c), forming a pair with Q31 that had a 75% hydrogen bond frequency compared to 0% in the rapid systems. This may explain the structural shift of residue A123 observed in the clustering analysis (Figure ). These many significant hydrogen bond changes in the active site loop likely result in C-terminal residue I290 losing hydrogen bonds with S127 but gaining them with S128 (Figure b). Alternatively, the interdomain has significant hydrogen bond changes of residue pairs L209-S215, S215-T219, and F217-H43, possibly linked to a major interdomain structural difference seen in structural clustering (Figure ), which may affect substrate/cofactor active site entry/exit and binding. Furthermore, interdomain residues Q210 and T211 show significantly increased RMSF (Figure ).

The largest changes in BC occurred in the active site loop (Figure d), while several residues around the catalytic/active site showed substantial decreases in EC, including W67, acetyl-C68, L69, V71, N72, H107, L108, L109, N122, A123, and G124 (Figures S12 and f). These significant DRN changes highlight the potential of DRN in predicting deleterious residue variations. The BC decreases of residues N72, G124, S127, and I290, all found in the reference top 5% BC residue pathway (Figure S8), very likely affect the communication between the catalytic site, active site loop, and domain I. EC assesses the connectivity of residues to other well-connected residues. Notably, catalytic D122 showed the largest rapid system hydrogen bonding frequency, interacting with six different residues. The decreased EC observed in the catalytic site of this variant is likely due to a reduction in the hydrogen bonding involving residue N122 (Figure c), particularly with the other highly connected hydrogen bonding residue N72 (Figure b,c). Additionally, N72 displayed significantly reduced values in both BC and CC metrics, while the catalytic acetyl-C68 showed pronounced decreased CC and EC values.

The slow acetylation phenotype of this variant is likely due to the loss of ionic interactions between catalytic residues N122 and H107, , as well as the substantial reduction in hydrogen bonding involving N122, especially with catalytic H107 and residue N72. These interactions typically play a crucial role in stabilizing the catalytic triad. In addition, the inter-residue communication within the catalytic site is significantly weakened, as indicated by the marked decreases in EC and CC.

R197Q+K268R

As it is observed in the I114T+K268R variant, our delta calculations revealed minimal changes compared to the rapid acetylator NAT2 proteins. The R197Q variation causes only a slight decrease in thermostability − , due to what is thought to be a loss of relatively weak electrostatic interactions of residue Q197 with M105 and E195. Also, Walraven et al. suggested that hydrogen bonding between R197 and E195 might be possible but would likely not contribute to tertiary structure stability like the M105 interaction. We found that the rapid average E195-R197 residue pair had a hydrogen bond frequency of 18%, which decreased by only 4.5% in the R197Q variant. However, the substituted residue Q197 lost a considerable 47.5% hydrogen bond frequency (Figure c), with residue pair D201-Q197 being the main contributor to the loss (Figure b). On the other hand, residue pair T198-D201 had a significant increase in hydrogen bond frequency. These changes may affect helix α5.

Interestingly, the most change from the rapid average of variant R197Q+K268R occurred on the opposite side of the enzyme relative to the R197Q residue substitution: on antiparallel beta-sheets β12→β15, residue T219 (interdomain), and residue I273 (start of the C-terminal tail) (Figure ). The M105 electrostatic interaction loss and Q197 hydrogen bonding changes caused by the R197Q variation may travel through helix α5 and the rest of the interdomain to beta-sheet β12 to induce the abovementioned changes, possibly linked to the significant RMSF increase of helix α5 residues and subsequent interdomain residues I218 and T219 (Figure ). In addition, the clustering analysis shows an interdomain conformation change (Figure ). The altered hydrogen bonding between helix α2 and beta-sheet β12 (Q226-R33) as well as across the antiparallel beta-sheets β12→β15 (L225-Y232, Y241-E254, and E254-T240) (Figures and b) corresponds to many DRN changes within these antiparallel beta-sheets. Specifically, there are significant CC changes in residues F222, Q226, T227, P228, V231, T240, and L252; significantly increased BC in residues F222, V231, Y232, Y241, R242, and F255; and significantly increased EC in residues F222, C233, L234, G236, and T240 (Figure ).

Overall, the reduced acetylation phenotype of this variant is likely due to the structural change in the interdomain region, as indicated by the clustering analysis (Figure ). Notably, the side chain of the putative isoniazid-binding residue F217 is oriented in the opposite direction compared to the rapid replicates. Additionally, catalytic residue D122 shows significantly decreased BC, suggesting diminished inter-residue communication at this important site. This aligns with the significantly reduced average distance between binding residue F217 and catalytic residues H107 and D122 (Figure S11). Furthermore, the distance between binding residues F217 and L288 is significantly increased. Together, these changes may impact ligand binding and catalysis.

R64Q+I114T+K268R

Both R64Q+K268R and R64Q+I114T+K268R variants share the R64Q residue variation, which is known to disrupt critical E38-R64 and N41-R64 hydrogen bond interactions, resulting in reduced protein thermostability, which may alter the conformation and dynamics of the domain I tertiary structure. , Compared to the R64Q+K268R variant, the R64Q+I114T+K268R variant induced a greater number of hydrogen bond changes within domain I but fewer interdomain hydrogen bond changes (Figure b,c). Interestingly, both variants exhibit a significantly lower summed total enzyme delta-hydrogen bond frequency compared to the rapid average (Figure S13), likely due to the loss of E38-R64 and N41-R64 hydrogen bonds (Figure b,c). Furthermore, the more pronounced negative total enzyme hydrogen bond frequency observed in the R64Q+I114T+K268R variant, compared to R64Q+K268R, is attributed to decreased hydrogen bonding of residue pairs Q168-R242 and I290-R242 (Figure b) which is not seen in the R64Q+K268R variant.

However, here, the largest amount of significant change from the rapid systems was found in the active site loop (Figure ), particularly residues G126 and S127, which both showed significantly decreased BC and CC values. Residue S127 is found in the reference top 5% BC residue pathway (Figure S8); therefore, the large decrease in BC of S127 in this variant may affect communication between the catalytic site, active site loop, and domain I. Also, in the active site loop, residues G124→G126 had significantly increased RMSF, residue G124 showed significantly decreased EC, and residue S127 exhibited significantly increased hydrogen bonding with C-terminal residue I290. The significant changes of hydrogen bond frequency involving C-terminal residue I290 (residue pairs I290-S127 and I290-R242) correspond to a conformational change of the C-terminal tail, with residue S287 blocking more of the active site entrance than the rapid systems and putative isoniazid-binding residue L288 in a different position compared to the rapid systems (Figure ). This may affect substrate/cofactor active site entry/exit and binding.

The slow acetylation phenotype of this variant is likely due to the combined effects of the R64Q variation, which disrupts critical E38-R64 and N41-R64 hydrogen bond interactions, and the I114T variation, which appears to influence the active site loop. These changes result in significantly decreased DRN values for active site loop residues G124, G126, S127, and S128, potentially impacting substrate and/or cofactor binding. Additionally, the significantly decreased BC value of important residue N72 may impair the catalytic site functionality. Furthermore, the conformational change of the C-terminal tail could hinder substrate/cofactor entry, exit, or binding at the active site.

Conclusions

In general, understanding the atomic-level effect of variations on the 3D structures of proteins responsible for drug metabolism (pharmacogenes) is of utmost importance to develop population-specific pharmacogenomic approaches. In this context, this study focused on deciphering the structural consequences of known variations of NAT2 that shift the enzyme function from rapid to slow acetylation. This is particularly important because NAT2 is a key metabolizing enzyme of the TB drug, isoniazid.

Here, we presented a computational framework that can be applied to other pharmacogenes to investigate the atomic-level effects of variations, as we have recently demonstrated. Our approach is based on analyzing the metric differences (delta calculations) between a baseline system and variant systems, dynamically over MD simulations. In this study, we showed that while two rapid acetylator NAT2 proteins with K or R at position 268 have only slight differences overall in RMSF, hydrogen bonding, and DRN metric values, the NAT2 variants with a known clinical “slow acetylator” phenotype present drastic changes compared to the rapid systems. These changes include disrupted hydrogen bonds, highly localized structural and dynamic changes, and altered inter-residue communication networks.

In the R64Q+K268R variant, the loss of critical E38-R64 and N41-R64 hydrogen bond interactions likely triggers widespread hydrogen bond changes in domain I, the interdomain, and the C-terminal tail. This leads to decreased BC of catalytic D122 and putative isoniazid-binding residues S125 and F217, as well as decreased EC of N72, D122, and active site loop residue G124, ultimately affecting ligand binding and catalysis.

In the I114T+K268R variant, extensive hydrogen bond changes coupled with residue network alterations, especially in the active site loop, likely disrupt catalytic D122 and essential binding residues.

For the D122N+K268R variant, the loss of ionic interactions between catalytic N122 and H107, along with a significant reduction in N122 hydrogen bonds, particularly with catalytic H107 and residue N72, destabilizes the catalytic triad and reduces inter-residue communication, as evidenced by substantial EC and CC decreases.

R197Q+K268R exhibits reduced acetylation due to structural changes in the interdomain, including a reorientation of the F217 binding residue side chain, coupled with significantly decreased BC of catalytic D122, further impairing inter-residue communication.

Finally, in the R64Q+I114T+K268R variant, the combined effects of R64Q disrupting E38-R64 and N41-R64 hydrogen bonds and I114T affecting the active site loop led to the significantly decreased DRN values of active site loop residues G124, G126, S127, and S128, potentially compromising substrate and cofactor binding.

Overall, we demonstrated how introducing an additional residue substitution into rapid acetylation enzyme systems may result in a shift toward slow acetylation. Notably, all but one of these substitutions are located far from the active site, suggesting that their effects are allosteric and mediated through local, rather than direct, structural changes. This concept of dynamic allostery, as opposed to traditional conformational allostery, was first introduced by Cooper et al. and has since been further elaborated by others. − In our recent study on CYP3A4, we observed a similar pattern of allosteric behavior, supporting the idea that the functional impact of variants in pharmacogenes tends to be subtle and localized rather than global. In both studies, these local changes are evident through alterations in the RMSF, hydrogen bonding patterns, and residue network properties.

Our future work aims to elucidate mechanisms underlying the remaining slow acetylator variants, which may help us assess the accuracy of the metrics used in this study. Expanding the sample size to include these and other known variants will strengthen the robustness and generalizability of our findings.
