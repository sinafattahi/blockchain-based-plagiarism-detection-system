Abstract

In the construction of tunnels, water conservancy, mining, and other engineering projects, the water-rich weathered sandstone with large porosity, strong permeability, and highly developed fissures is often exposed, which brings a great threat to construction safety. The method of grouting can greatly reduce the occurrence of such disasters, thereby improving the safety and reliability of related projects. Based on this, the paper considered the disorder and random uncertainty of sandstone fractures and established a theoretical model between sandstone hydraulic conductivity and fractal dimension based on fractal theory. Furthermore, a fractal model for the amount of grouting cement in weathered sandstone was established by considering the injectability of granular grout in fractures, effectively revealing the variation of grouting cement amount with fractal dimension under one- and two-dimensional flow states, respectively. Based on the relationship between cement dosage and hydraulic conductivity, three physically defined grouting areas were defined, that is, the hydraulic splitting zone, the normal grouting zone, and the micro-crack zone. Finally, the Qingdao Metro was selected as the site for verification. The on-site measured data were mainly located in the normal Pearl River area and less in the grouting area A, indicating that the grouting feasibility of this area was relatively good. Through the above work, some theoretical guidance can be provided for the construction of weathered sandstone engineering projects.

1. Introduction

Sandstone is a sedimentary rock formed by dissolution, alteration, compaction, and cementation in a certain period of geological history, mainly composed of weak cementation materials such as medium fine sand particles and clay particles. The main mineral components of sandstone are quartz and feldspar, which belong to a type of weakly diagenetic rock. Under normal circumstances, sandstone has high strength and integrity in the dry state, so that it can effectively ensure the stability of tunnels and other projects when using sandstone as surrounding rocks. However, under conditions of seepage or strong disturbance, the strength of rock will rapidly decrease and easily disintegrate into fragmented blocks, leading to disasters such as water influx and collapse. , In the construction of tunnels, water conservancy, mining, and other engineering projects, highly developed fissures, high porosity, and strong permeability of water rich weathered sandstone are often exposed, posing a great threat to construction safety. Therefore, it is of great scientific significance and engineering value to carry out in-depth research on grouting reinforcement technology and seepage mechanism of water-rich weathered sandstone. −

Recently, fractal theory has effectively realized the unity of randomness and certainty, order, and disorder in complex systems, which has become an important theoretical tool to guide geotechnical, water conservancy, mining, and other engineering projects. Biswas et al. established a fractal analysis method for the roughness of rock shear fracture surfaces and proposed a new method for characterizing heterogeneous slip based on physical experiments. Ren et al. conducted indoor experiments to study the fractal dimension changes on the surface of porous rocks during the fracture process, and proposed precursor information for rock burst occurrence. Hao et al. found that the fractal dimension of cross-scale pores is closely related to the mechanical properties of rocks. Ding et al. studied the influence of static stress on the fractal characteristics and propagation behavior of fractures under blasting conditions, and the results showed that static stress increased the fractal dimension of blasting fractures. All of those studies indicate that fractal theory is an important and effective tool for studying the mechanical behavior of rocks containing complex pores and fractures.

A series of studies have shown that the distribution of fractures in weathered sandstone exhibits strong disorder and irregularity, and its fracture network has typical fractal characteristics in a two-dimensional plane. , Thus, fractal theory can be used to describe the irregular development of sandstone fractures and further study the seepage law and stress–strain behavior of weathered sandstone. Liu et al. constructed a two-dimensional random fracture network based on the Monte Carlo principle and established a fractal relationship expression for the rock permeability coefficient by using fractal theory. Hu et al. analyzed the mechanism of permeability changes in saturated sandstone under complex stress states and found that the evolution process of sandstone permeability is closely related to the fractal characteristics of its internal microcracks. Mortazavi and Maadikhah found that after grouting of strongly weathered and fully weathered sandstone, grout cementation with weathered fracture surface forms a stable overall structure, which effectively improves the antiseepage stability of the rock mass. Wang et al. analyzed the grouting mechanism of fractured rock masses by using fractal geometry methods and found that rock permeability is the key factor affecting grouting effectiveness, followed by the connectivity degree of the rock mass. Based on the fractal theory, Gong et al. established the Bingham fluid permeation grouting theoretical model and studied the permeation mechanism of grout in porous media under different pore structures.

At present, most research focuses on the construction of fracture network models and the exploration of the permeability mechanism of fractured rock masses, while there is a significant lack of research on the flow and diffusion characteristics of a grouting slurry in weathered sandstone. Therefore, in this paper, combined with fractal theory, the grouting mechanism of weathered sandstone under complex fracture networks is mainly studied. Based on the results of the theoretical model, grouting experiments were carried out on the weathered sandstone rock mass on site to conduct a comprehensive evaluation of the grouting feasibility of the weathered sandstone.

2. Fractal Characteristics of Weathered Sandstone

As a core parameter of fractal theory, the fractal dimension can effectively characterize the structural complexity characteristics of a sandstone fracture network. By quantifying the spatial distribution and morphological characteristics of the fracture system, the fractal dimension actually provides a quantitative evaluation index for studying the geometric heterogeneity and evolution of sandstone fractures. The fractal dimension has the characteristic of scale invariance, and its description of fracture development morphology is not affected by observation scale and accuracy, making it have unique advantages in the analysis and application of sandstone physical and mechanical properties. For weathered sandstone rich in discrete fractures, the relationship between fracture length and quantity can be quantitatively represented by the fractal dimension. The relationship between fracture length I of sandstone and its cumulative quantity M can be expressed as follows:

The fractal dimension D k is inversely proportional to the small fractures in the total fractures of weathered sandstone. That is, the fractal dimension D k will gradually decrease with the increase of short fractures. 3. Analysis of Grouting Characteristics Based on the Fractal Theory

3.1. Parallel Plate Model

The diffusion of the slurry inside sandstone is a complex three-dimensional flow process, which is influenced by various factors such as fracture roughness, aperture, and rock stress. Thus, it is difficult to quantitatively and accurately calculate the slurry flow rate, velocity, pressure, etc. In order to simplify analysis in engineering, the parallel plate model is often used to simplify the diffusion morphology and process of the slurry. In the model, the parallel plates are assumed to be rigid thin plates without thickness, and the slurry is assumed to be a homogeneous, viscous fluid with constant fluidity. 3.2. Fractal Model of Sandstone Hydraulic Conductivity

The fracture network is the main channel for groundwater flow and migration in sandstone. The fractures of weathered sandstone are complex and intricate, and their length, opening, spacing, and connectivity are important factors determining the hydraulic conductivity of sandstone. Due to geological structures, diagenetic environments, and temperature changes, the fracture channels inside sandstone exhibit typical anisotropic characteristics, which lead to strong nonlinear seepage behavior of groundwater. In fact, the sandstone seepage field is controlled by weak areas with strong fracture development, with a single fracture or several wide fractures controlling about 80% of the total seepage flow.

The study of seepage characteristics in single fractures is the basis of seepage theory in weathered sandstone. For flat plate fractures without considering frictional resistance, the hydraulic conductivity in laminar flow is positively correlated with the cubic power of the opening; that is, the cubic law of the fractures is satisfied, which can be expressed as

Cc=ρws3g12ηw 6

where C c represents the hydraulic conductivity of the smooth parallel plate, ρw is the density of water under standard conditions, s is the distance between the fracture walls, g is the acceleration due to gravity, and ηw is the viscosity of water in a laminar flow state.

To improve the antiseepage performance and mechanical strength of weathered sandstone, a sequential and segmented grouting construction scheme is often adopted on site. Assuming that a certain grouting hole passes through all the fractures in the fracture zone and the distribution of fractures in this area is uniform and continuous, then the total hydraulic conductivity can be expressed as

4. Grouting Mechanism Analysis of Weathered Sandstone

Under the action of pressure, the slurry drives the groundwater in sandstone, occupying the intersecting fracture channels. After solidification, the slurry forms a complete cemented body with weak structural interfaces, significantly improving the physical structure of the sandstone. Due to the strengthened antiseepage and mechanical properties, sandstone can effectively resist the erosion of wind, rain, and flowing water on its interior, thereby ensuring the long-term stability of underground structures such as tunnel surrounding rock and foundation bases.

The relationship curve between cement dosage and hydraulic conductivity can accurately reflect the cement consumption of sandstone grouting under one- and two-dimensional flow conditions. The relationship curve between cement dosage and permeability accurately reflects the cement dosage during sandstone grouting under 1D and 2D flow conditions. As seen in Figure , the cement dosage increases gradually with the increasing permeability. Assuming that slurry flow patterns are divided into three types: one-dimensional, two-dimensional, and mixed one-dimensional-two-dimensional, the relationship curve between cement dosage and permeability divides the entire diagram into three regions, namely, A, B, and C.

Region A is defined as the hydraulic fracturing zone, where the amount of cement used exceeds the calculated amount for two-dimensional flow, indicating that sandstone fractures undergo fracturing deformation under the grouting pressure. At this time, some cement slurry is used to fill the sandstone fractures. Region B is defined as the normal grouting zone, where there is both one-dimensional and two-dimensional flow exiting, and its typical feature is that the amount of cement used increases proportionally with the hydraulic conductivity and fractal dimension. Region C is defined as a microfine fracturing zone, where its typical feature is that the cement dosage is less than the calculation dosage under one-dimensional flow, indicating there are many micro fractures in the region. Therefore, the sandstone has poor injectability, and cement particles have not been fully and effectively injected into the sandstone fractures in region C.

5. Results and Discussion

In the paper, based on the disorder and random uncertainty of sandstone fractures and combined with the seepage characteristics of granular slurry in the fracture network, a theoretical model of hydraulic conductivity considering fractal dimension was established. By constructing a fractal prediction model for the amount of grouting cement used in weathered sandstone, the quantitative relationship between the consumption of grouting cement and the fractal dimension of fractures under two typical flow modes, one- and two-dimensional, was systematically revealed. Moreover, based on the sensitivity analysis of parameters and the nonlinear characteristics of the hydraulic conductivity cement dosage response curve, the grouting area is divided into three typical engineering geological zones, namely, the hydraulic fracturing dominant zone, the normal grouting zone, and the microfine fracture zone. The establishment of this zone model can provide a theoretical basis for the selection of differentiated grouting process parameters. Finally, this study conducted on-site experiments and performed analysis based on the Qingdao Metro project in order to verify the accuracy of the theoretical model.

5.1. Engineering Applications

Based on the Qingdao Metro project, the effectiveness of the theoretical model was verified in this section. The geological conditions between Huicheng Road Station and Xijing Station of Qingdao Metro Line 9 are complex, and the section tunnel crosses 5.7 km of quartz sandstone and feldspar sandstone rock layers. The groundwater in this area is very abundant, and shield tunneling is frequently affected by the invasion and interference of pressurized water, and cement slurry was used on-site to reinforce and treat the surrounding rock structure by grouting. Based on the YS­(B) borehole log, two typical sets of surrounding rock fractures from this area were selected for empirical analysis. This is closely related to the specific geological conditions on the site, and in the future, the theoretical model can be more widely validated by replacing different engineering sites and collecting more data from them.

5.2. Limitations and Applicability of the Theoretical Models

The paper assumed that the flow of slurry in a single fracture conforms to the basic assumptions of the parallel plate model and then studied the flow law of slurry in complex fracture networks based on fractal theory. The theoretical models simplified the flow of the slurry in the fracture network, thus ignoring the complex states in the actual flow process.

First, the theoretical models did not pay attention to the dynamic development characteristics of fractures and only analyzed based on the initial static model results, thus ignoring the possible changes in fractal dimension caused by newly generated fractures during the grouting process. Second, he theoretical models apply to the laminar flow of grout in rock formations. When the ground stress is low, the flow of grout can only be approximated as a laminar flow. However, when the ground stress is relatively high, the assumption of laminar flow will be satisfied. Therefore, the theoretical models apply to situations with low geostress, but they will no longer apply to situations with high surrounding rock stress. Third, the theoretical models did not take into account the influence of fluid-solid coupling. In the actual grouting process, the grouting pressure may cause the deformation of weathered sandstone fractures, resulting in changes in the length and width of the initial fractures. At the same time, the deformation of sandstone fractures, in turn, will affect the grouting effect.

In future research, we will strive to address the above issues and make our theoretical model more closely aligned with the real situation on site. In addition, it is considered to use numerical simulation methods to study the grouting process, conduct more detailed research on the grouting process, and achieve visualization of this process.

6. Conclusions

In this work, the grouting mechanism of weathered sandstone was discussed in detail by combining fractal theory. We established fractal models for sandstone hydraulic conductivity and injection cement dosage, revealing the variation of injection cement dosage with fractal dimensions under different flow states. According to the theoretical model results, different grouting regions were defined, providing theoretical support for efficient grouting. Finally, an on-site verification was conducted, demonstrating the accuracy of the theoretical model. The main conclusions are as follows:

  • 1.

    For weathered sandstone rich in discrete fractures, a fractal model of sandstone hydraulic conductivity was established by using the parallel plate model, and the relationship between sandstone hydraulic conductivity and fractal dimension was obtained. The theoretical model reveals a positive correlation between the hydraulic conductivity of weathered sandstone and the maximum hydraulic conductivity of a single fracture, as well as the complexity of sandstone fracture length, with fractal dimension values ranging from 1.5 to 2.0 under this condition.

  • 2.

    Based on the injectability of granular grout in cracks, a fractal expression of the amount of grouting cement with respect to hydraulic conductivity was established. The results indicate that the amount of grouting cement increases with the increase of hydraulic conductivity and fractal dimension. Compared with one-dimensional flow, the amount of cement increases faster under two-dimensional flow. When the hydraulic conductivity is greater than 0 and less than 0.5 in one-dimensional flow, the amount of cement increases rapidly. When the hydraulic conductivity is greater than 0.5 and less than 2.0, the increase in cement amount is gentle, showing a clear staged characteristic. The increasing trend of cement dosage in two-dimensional flow is relatively stable and has a linear relationship with hydraulic conductivity.

  • 3.

    A fractal model for the amount of grouting cement in one-dimensional and two-dimensional flow states was proposed. According to the relationship curves between cement dosage and hydraulic conductivity under different fractal dimensions, it was found that there exist three grouting zones, namely, the hydraulic fracturing zone, the normal grouting zone, and the microfine fracturing zone. The cement consumption in the hydraulic fracturing zone is the highest. The amount of cement used in the normal grouting area generally increases linearly with the increase of hydraulic conductivity and fractal dimension. The cement consumption in the microfine fracturing zone is the smallest, and its groutability is the worst.

  • 4.

    Qingdao Metro was selected for on-site testing verification, and the results showed that the measured results were basically consistent with the theoretical model results, indicating that the theoretical model and grouting area division method established in this paper are effective and can be used as guidance for the construction of weathered sandstone and related engineering projects.
