Abstract

Bacterial contamination remains a critical concern in the environmental and biomedical fields, driving the demand for multifunctional materials with reliable antibacterial properties. Cellulose acetate–graphene oxide (CA–GO) composites, as a class of polymer nanocomposites, offer an effective solution by combining the biocompatibility of cellulose acetate with the antimicrobial activity of graphene oxide. In this study, the antibacterial performance of CA–GO composites was systematically evaluated by using multiple analytical techniques to better understand their efficacy under diverse conditions. A suite of complementary methods, including the Contact Film Diffusion Assay, Surface Drop Viability Assay, and Dynamic Immersion Exposure Test, was employed to assess how variables such as GO concentration, bacterial strain type, and exposure settings influence antibacterial activity. Results showed that CA–GO composites exhibit strong antibacterial effects, particularly against Gram-positive bacteria, largely attributed to GO’s ability to disrupt bacterial membrane integrity through oxidative stress and physical interactions. This multimethod investigation highlights the importance of testing under varied conditions to capture the full spectrum of antibacterial behavior. The findings not only demonstrate the high potential of CA–GO composites for antimicrobial applications but also underscore the need for standardized evaluation protocols tailored to graphene-based polymer nanocomposites.

Introduction

Polymer nanocomposites have gained significant attention due to their potential application in various fields, including biomedicine, energy storage, electronics, and environmental remediation. − The increasing interest in polymer nanocomposites is primarily driven by their ability to significantly enhance material properties, including mechanical strength, thermal stability, and functional versatility, through the incorporation of nanoscale reinforcements. In such composites, the antibacterial properties are a key focus of research due to the rise of antibiotic-resistant bacteria and the growing demand for alternative antimicrobial materials. , These composites offer a promising route to effective antimicrobial materials by combining the biocompatibility and processability of a polymer matrix with the antimicrobial activity and structural advantages of nanomaterials. The enhancement of functional performance in such polymer nanocomposites is largely due to the incorporation of nanoscale reinforcements like graphene oxide (GO), which significantly improves properties such as mechanical strength, chemical stability, and antibacterial functionality. , Among the various polymer matrices used, cellulose acetate (CA) stands out as a biobased polymer due to its renewable source, biodegradability, and nontoxicity, making it an environmentally friendly option for composite materials. The natural origin of CA, derived from cellulose, offers excellent processability and versatility, allowing it to be easily fabricated into films, fibers, and coatings. − These characteristics, combined with its biocompatibility, make CA an attractive candidate for incorporation with nanomaterials, such as GO, to develop composites that can serve as effective antimicrobial agents. Consequently, the methods used to analyze the antibacterial efficacy of these composites are crucial, as the choice of the method can result in variations in the reported antibacterial properties, an aspect that often goes unnoticed.

GO stands out as a leading two-dimensional nanomaterial used as a reinforcement. Its large specific surface area, sharp edge-like structure, and surface oxygenated functional groups, such as carboxyl, carbonyl, and hydroxyl, enable strong interfacial bonding with polymer matrices. , Additionally, GO’s high electrical and thermal conductivity, exceptional mechanical strength, and tunable chemical properties have paved the way for its promising applications in electronics, food packaging, sensors, environmental remediation, and biomedical fields. − In biomedical applications, the primary focus is on its antibacterial properties, where polymer–GO composites have emerged as highly effective antimicrobial materials.

Polymer–GO composites antibacterial properties depend on several factors, which include the interaction between GO and the polymer matrix, the dispersion of GO, the surface texture and morphology of the composite, and the type of polymer matrix used. For example, if a bactericidal polymer matrix such as chitosan is used, it contributes to the overall antibacterial activity, enhancing the combined antibacterial activity of the composite. In contrast, if a nonbactericidal polymer matrix is used, the composite's bacterial killing will be solely due to GO. GO itself showed exceptional antibacterial activity with several mechanisms proposed to explain its bactericidal activity(1) physical interaction with the bacterial membrane, (2) oxidative stress generation, and (3) ability to facilitate charge transfer. , A two-dimensional sheet-like structure produces a knife effect, damaging bacterial membranes, and the presence of oxygenated functional groups generates oxidative stress, which hampers essential biomolecules within bacterial cells, such as lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids. In addition, charge transfer between the GO and bacterial cell membranes destroys the bacterial respiratory system. Combining these, GO is positioned as a potential antibacterial material, capable of addressing the killing of both bacterial strains, Gram-positive and Gram-negative. Studies on the antibacterial activity of polymer–GO composites have reported mixed results. This variability arises from multiple factors, including the type of bacterial strain, the weight percentage of GO in the composite, the exposure time, and most importantly, the method used to evaluate antibacterial efficacy, many of which are often overlooked. These variations in experimental design, testing protocols, and the absence of standard guidelines have led to conflicting results regarding the antibacterial properties of such composites, and because of these, it is difficult to compare the outcomes across studies, limiting the use of the GO–polymer composite as an antimicrobial material. While disk diffusion methods are widely used in recent studies to demonstrate the antibacterial activity of polymer–GO composites, this approach cannot differentiate whether the observed inhibition zones result from leaching GO fragments or reactive oxygen species (ROS) generated at the composite surface. ROS, being diffusible, could induce bacterial death without material loss, whereas GO leaching would raise concerns about the films’ structural stability, necessitating complementary assays to clarify the mechanism. Arfat et al. prepared a PLA–CLO–GO composite and evaluated its antibacterial activity using a different method. In this approach, 3 cm disk-shaped composite samples were immersed in broth inoculated with 0.1 mL (∼108 CFU/mL). After 1 and 7 days, the inoculated and diluted media were plated onto agar plates to assess bacterial growth. Liao et al. utilized the disk diffusion method to evaluate the antibacterial properties of a cellulose/carboxymethyl chitosan composite. In their study, 10 mm disks of the composite were placed separately on a solid medium containing 3.6 × 105 CFU/mL of and 2.7 × 105 CFU/mL of . Based on the zone of inhibition, the antibacterial properties of these CS-CMC composites were demonstrated. In a recent study by Li et al., AgO–Ag–carboxy methyl chitosan GO composites were investigated for their antibacterial activity using disk diffusion and optical density (OD) measurements after immersing the films in a bacterial solution. However, in composite materials containing multiple antibacterial agents, relying solely on disk diffusion or OD measurements may not provide an accurate representation of their antibacterial behavior or mechanisms. These methods make it difficult to distinguish the individual antibacterial efficacy of each component within the composite material. Considering these, we seek to address the inconsistencies and provide a comprehensive framework for evaluating the antibacterial efficacy of GO polymer composites. By employing multiple analytical techniques, we aim to establish more reliable and reproducible methods for evaluating the antibacterial performance of the CA–GO composite. This multifaceted approach not only enhances the accuracy of bacterial killing assessments but also provides deeper insights into the underlying mechanisms of action. Ultimately, our goal is to contribute to the development of a more standardized methodology for future antimicrobial studies and unlock the full potential of GO composites in antibacterial applications.

In this study, we prepared cellulose acetate (CA)-based graphene oxide (GO) composites with varying concentrations of GO to investigate their antibacterial properties. The CA–GO composites were thoroughly characterized to assess their structural and chemical attributes. Following characterization, the composites were tested for antibacterial efficacy against both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacterial strains. Three distinct testing methods were employed to provide a comprehensive understanding of their antibacterial mechanisms. Each method highlighted different facets of the bacterial killing process, offering a multidimensional perspective on how the CA–GO composites interact with and eliminate bacterial cells. Through this approach, we aim to better understand the specific factors that influence bacterial killing and the potential of these composites as effective antibacterial materials.

Materials and Methods

Cellulose acetate was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich, India. Phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), Mueller–Hinton broth (MHB), and Mueller–Hinton agar (MHA) were purchased from Hi-media. (ATCC 25922), a Gram-negative bacterium, and (ATCC 25923), a Gram-positive bacterium, were sourced from the Microbial Type Culture Collection (MTCC) located in Chandigarh, India.

Synthesis and Characterization of the CA–GO Composite

Graphene oxide was synthesized using a modified Hummer’s method. To prepare CA films, 100 mg of CA was dissolved in 10 mL of acetone while stirring continuously until a clear solution formed. This solution was then poured onto a glass petri dish and left to dry at room temperature. Once fully dried, the resulting film was carefully peeled off, yielding a freestanding CA film. For the preparation of CA–GO composites, different concentrations of GO (0.5, 1, and 2.5 wt % relative to CA) were dispersed in 10 mL of acetone using a probe sonicator for 30 min to ensure proper dispersion of the GO sheets. After GO dispersion, 100 mg of CA was added to the GO–acetone solution, followed by continuous stirring at room temperature for 1 h. The CA–GO composite films were then formed via solution casting, where the mixture was dried in a petri dish at 37 °C. Once the films were prepared, various physicochemical characterizations were performed. Fourier transform-infrared (FT-IR) spectroscopy (PerkinElmer spectrum TWO) and Raman spectroscopy (KAISER RXN1 Raman spectrometer) were used to study the interactions between the CA and GO sheets. X-ray powder diffraction (XRD; SmartLab, Rigaku) with Cu-K α radiation (λ = 1.54186 Å) was conducted to assess the changes in crystallinity of the CA–GO samples at varying GO concentrations. Furthermore, the chemical composition of the CA–GO composite films was obtained via X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS; k-alpha, Thermo Fisher Scientific) equipped with an Al-Kα radiation (energy = 1486.6 eV) source. The survey spectra were obtained with a pass energy of 200 eV and a step size of 1 eV, whereas the high-resolution spectra of C 1s and O 1s were obtained with a pass energy of 50 eV and a step size of 0.1 eV. The obtained spectra were deconvoluted and fitted in CasaXPS with a Gaussian–Lorentz function and a Shirley background. Furthermore, C 1s at 284.6 eV was taken as the binding energy reference for all XPS spectra. Additionally, contact angle measurements were taken using a goniometer to investigate the wettability of the CA–GO films. Surface morphology and topographical features were examined using both optical microscopy and scanning electron microscopy (SEM; JSM-7900F, JEOL). MTT assays were carried out on NIH3T3 fibroblast cells to assess the biocompatibility of the nanocomposite for potential biomedical applications. This colorimetric technique enables the evaluation of cell proliferation, viability, and toxicity. Briefly, the cells were cultured in DMEM supplemented with 1% penicillin/streptomycin and 10% fetal bovine serum and maintained in a humidified incubator with 5% CO2. Nanocomposite films with varying GO contents (0, 0.5, 1, and 2.5 wt % relative to CA) were prepared as circular disks (0.5 cm in diameter) and placed in a 96-well plate. Cells were trypsinized and seeded onto the CA–GO composite films at an initial density of 5000 cells per well. After a 24 h incubation, the medium was discarded and replaced with a fresh medium containing 0.5 mg/mL MTT. Following an additional 4 h incubation, the medium was removed, the resulting formazan crystals were dissolved in DMSO, and absorbance was measured at 570 nm using a microplate reader.

For the antibacterial study, a CFU assay was performed based on the ISO 22196 protocol with slight modifications. Bacterial strains were cultured by streaking them onto MHA plates, followed by overnight incubation at 37 °C. After that, a single bacterial colony was picked from that MHA plate and placed in MHB overnight at 250 rpm and 37 °C to prepare the stock solution. To achieve mid-logarithmic values, 20 μL of the stock solution was diluted in 2 mL of MHB for 2 h at 250 rpm to achieve OD600 of 0.4–0.6. After that, bacteria were pelleted down to replace the MHB with PBS buffer (pH 7), bringing the total number of bacteria to ∼105–106 CFU/mL. Using mid-log bacteria, the antibacterial efficacy of the composite films was evaluated using three different testing methods.

Method I (Contact Film Diffusion Assay)

A 20 μL aliquot of mid-log phase bacterial culture was plated onto MHA plates. Subsequently, circular composite film disks (0.5 cm in diameter) were placed on the bacterial lawn. The plates were incubated at 37 °C for 12 h. After the incubation period, images of the petri dishes were captured to observe any zones of inhibition formed around or beneath the composite disks, indicating antibacterial activity.

Method II (Surface Drop Viability Assay)

A 20 μL aliquot of mid-log phase bacterial culture in PBS was added to the surface of the composite films, followed by incubation at 37 °C for 12 h. After incubation, bacteria from the film surface were collected and serially diluted to 1 × 104, and 20 μL of the diluted solution was plated onto MHA plates. These plates were incubated for 16 h at 37 °C, after which visible bacterial colonies were counted to assess the bacterial viability.

Method III (Dynamic Immersion Exposure Test)

Composite films were immersed in 2 mL of mid-log phase bacterial culture and incubated at 200 rpm for 12 h. After incubation, the culture medium was collected and serially diluted, and 20 μL of the diluted solution was plated onto MHA plates. The plates were incubated at 37 °C for 16 h, and visible bacterial colonies were subsequently counted to determine the bacterial viability.

To confirm and understand bacterial killing, live/dead cell staining was performed, and images were collected using confocal microscopy. Morphological images of bacterial strains before and after treatment were collected using scanning electron microscopy (SEM).

Results and Discussion

The characterization data for the GO sheets, synthesized and used in preparing the CA–GO composite, are shown in Figures S1–S3. The XRD analysis of GO (Figure b) showed a prominent peak at 2θ = 9.8°, corresponding to an interplanar distance of approximately 0.9 nm, and the 2D surface morphology of GO sheets observed in the SEM image (Figure S1) suggests the successful oxidation of graphene to GO. Two peaks (D and G bands) in the Raman spectra (Figure S2) also confirmed the presence of oxygenated functional groups on the surface of graphitic sheets. Furthermore, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) provided additional evidence for the successful preparation of GO sheets. The TGA analysis (Figure S3) revealed a three-step degradation process. The initial weight loss can be attributed to the evaporation of water molecules adsorbed on the GO surface. The second stage corresponds to the degradation of oxygen-containing functional groups present on the GO sheets. Finally, the last stage signifies the combustion of the underlying carbon network. These findings align with the observations reported by Song et al., who also noted a similar multistage degradation pattern for GO sheets.

Figure presents the CA–GO composite films prepared with varying concentrations of GO. These films are self-supporting, i.e., they maintain their structural integrity without requiring external support. Additionally, they exhibit excellent flexibility, allowing them to be bent, folded, or shaped without cracking or breaking. The thickness of the prepared composite was in the range of 20–30 μm. These self-supported and flexible thin films are highly adaptable for a range of potential applications such as flexible sensors, health care devices and systems, food packaging and storage industry, etc.

The prepared composites demonstrate outstanding water stability, as confirmed by weight measurements after immersing the films in deionized (DI) water for up to 48 h. Even after 48 h, no swelling or degradation of the composite films was observed, with less than 1% change in weight. Film stability in different solvents was also checked, where these composites are highly stable in ethanol and methanol, whereas complete disintegration of films in acetone, dimethylformamide (DMF), and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) was observed. The water contact angle measurement study further revealed a slightly decreasing trend in hydrophobicity with increasing GO concentration, as shown in Figure S4. For pure cellulose acetate (CA) films (0 wt % GO), the average contact angle was 74.8°, indicating moderate hydrophilicity. However, as the GO content increased, the contact angle slightly decreased to 68° for the 2.5 wt % CA–GO composite. This decrease in the contact angle suggests that the wettability of the films improved with higher GO concentrations. The enhanced wettability is likely due to the incorporation of GO sheets, which contain oxygen-rich functional groups, such as hydroxyl, epoxy, and carboxyl groups, known for their hydrophilic nature. These groups increase the films’ affinity for water, leading to a lower contact angle.

Figure includes optical and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images of the CA–GO composites. As the GO concentration increases, a noticeable aggregation of GO sheets is observed, along with an increase in surface roughness for 2.5 wt % GO composites compared to 0.5 and 1 wt % GO composites. Due to the use of acetone as a solvent, known for its high volatility, the resulting composite films display a porous surface, as evident in the SEM micrographs. Except for the 2.5 wt % GO film, the surface morphology of the other three films appeared smooth and uniform, indicating well-dispersed GO sheets within the polymer matrix.

FT-IR (Figure a) and XRD (Figure c) analyses of the CA–GO composite films provide important insights into the structural changes induced by the incorporation of GO into the polymer matrix. The FT-IR results revealed a broad O–H stretching peak (3300–3500 cm–1), which decreased in intensity as GO content increased, indicating that the incorporation of GO reduced the number of free hydroxyl groups through interactions like hydrogen bonding. This is consistent with the XRD data, where the pure CA film exhibited broad, amorphous humps at 2θ = 12° and 18°, characteristic of the disrupted crystalline structure caused by the substitution of hydroxyl groups with acetyl groups in CA. The absence of the characteristic GO diffraction peak in the XRD patterns of the composite films, even at a GO concentration of 2.5 wt %, indicates that the GO sheets were exfoliated and well-dispersed within the polymer matrix. This improved dispersion enhances the interactions between the polymer and GO. A similar observation was reported by De Moraes et al. in their study analyzing the UV shielding ability of CA–GO composites.

The FT-IR peaks associated with C=O stretching (1730 cm–1) and C–H bonds (1370 cm–1) also decreased in intensity with increasing GO content, indicating significant interactions between the GO sheets and the functional groups of cellulose acetate. These interactions likely occur through hydrogen bonding or π–π stacking, which reinforces the polymer matrix but reduces the number of free functional groups detectable by FT-IR. The small peak near 1500 cm–1, corresponding to C=C bonds from graphitic carbon, became more pronounced with higher GO content, confirming the incorporation of GO. , Together, the FT-IR and XRD results suggest that the presence of GO significantly alters the chemical environment and structure of cellulose acetate, leading to strong interfacial bonding while maintaining the amorphous nature of the polymer matrix. Furthermore, the surface and subsurface chemical composition of the pure cellulose acetate and polymer composites was quantified via the XPS analysis and is presented in Figures S5–S9 and Table S1. The survey spectra of composite films and deconvoluted C 1s and O 1s confirmed the bond formation between CA and GO sheets. The overall C:O ratio was calculated to be 2.09 for the pure CA film and 2.33, 1.69, and 1.89 for 0.5 wt % GO, 1.0 wt % GO, and 2.5 wt % GO, respectively (Table S1). Additionally, the in vitro cytotoxicity results indicated that CA–GO composite films are not significantly toxic and support the normal growth of NIH3T3 cells, as demonstrated by the MTT assay (Figure d).

Method I (Contact Film Diffusion Assay)

In the contact film diffusion assay (Figure ), although no visible zone of inhibition was observed around the CA–GO composite disks for and , a closer inspection revealed that bacterial growth was completely absent beneath the surface of the CA–GO composites. This contrasts with CA-only disks, where bacterial growth was observed beneath the films. Collectively, these results demonstrate that while the CA–GO composite lacks visible inhibition zones characteristic of diffusion-dependent mechanisms, it achieves potent antibacterial activity through direct contact-mediated killing. This “contact-killing” effect is likely due to the sharp edges of the GO nanosheets embedded within the composite, which disrupt bacterial cell membranes. Additionally, the absence of GO leaching from the composite films suggests a strong bond between CA and GO, further affirming the stability and safety of the material. However, this method provides only qualitative insight into the antibacterial properties of the CA–GO composite, necessitating the use of alternative analytical techniques to obtain a more comprehensive and quantitative understanding of its efficacy. This highlights the limitations of traditional disk diffusion assays and underscores the need for more refined methods for assessing antibacterial materials.

Method II (Surface Drop Viability Assay)

A notable difference in bactericidal effectiveness between (Gram-positive) and (Gram-negative) was observed (Figures and S10) with this method. The CA–GO composite showed a significantly higher reduction in colony count for than for . This differential response can be attributed to the structural differences between Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria. Gram-positive bacteria such as lack an outer membrane, leaving their cell walls directly exposed to the environment. This exposure allows for more intimate contact between the CA–GO composite and the bacterial cell wall, enhancing the composite’s antibacterial efficacy. In contrast, Gram-negative bacteria such as possess an outer membrane that serves as a protective barrier, making it harder for the CA–GO composite to penetrate and disrupt the bacterial cell wall. The study also revealed that increasing the concentration of GO in the composite led to greater antibacterial activity against both bacterial strains, with the 2.5 wt % GO composite, resulting in a 90% reduction in viability and a 50% reduction in viability. The enhanced bactericidal effect at higher GO concentrations is likely due to increased membrane disruption and oxidative stress induced by the GO nanosheets. This method provides a more specific assessment of the composites’ antibacterial properties, correlating GO concentration with efficacy and emphasizing the importance of bacterial cell wall structures. A similar trend was observed in the study by Xie et al., where bacterial cellulose (BC)–GO–CuO composites were prepared. The antibacterial activity of these composites was demonstrated by using both disk diffusion and colony-forming unit (CFU) methods. The results indicated that BC–GO–CuO films were more effective at killing bacteria compared to .

Method III (Dynamic Immersion Exposure Test)

In this method (Figure ), the antibacterial effectiveness of the CA–GO composite improved significantly compared to the method-II. Even at the lowest GO concentration, more than 80% of both and bacteria were killed. This higher efficacy is likely due to the increased contact area between the composite films and the bacterial solution. In this method, both sides of the composite film were exposed to bacteria, and the continuous shaking of the solution increased the likelihood of bacteria coming into contact with the sharp edges of the GO nanosheets, leading to more effective bacterial killing. The mechanical agitation ensures a greater interaction between the bacteria and the composite, promoting the disruption of bacterial membranes by the GO nanosheets. This method provides a more dynamic and realistic assessment of the antibacterial properties of the CA–GO composite under conditions that mimic practical applications, where materials are more likely to be exposed to fluid environments.

As shown in Figure S11, live/dead cell staining revealed a significant increase in bacterial inactivation for both and as the GO concentration increased from 0 to 2.5 wt %. Notably, displayed a greater reduction in viability compared to , consistent with the findings from method II. Furthermore, SEM images (Figure ) of the ruptured surface morphology of both and cells confirm the contact-based killing mechanism induced by the GO sheets.

Discussion

Three widely used strategies were selected to evaluate the antibacterial activity of the films (Figure ). While the disk diffusion assay offers rapid screening, it cannot discern the material’s specificity toward Gram-positive versus Gram-negative bacteria or quantify activity when inhibition zones are absent (as in contact-killing mechanisms). The surface drop viability assay addresses these limitations by enabling quantitative differentiation between bacterial types, although its static conditions fail to replicate real-world applications and may introduce errors in porous films due to bacterial infiltration. In contrast, the dynamic immersion test emerges as the most viable method, closely mimicking real-life scenarios by simulating fluid flow and continuous bacterial exposure, thereby providing ecologically relevant insights into antimicrobial performance.

Conclusions

A comprehensive evaluation of the antibacterial efficacy of the CA–GO composite was conducted using the Contact Film Diffusion Assay, Surface Drop Viability Assay, and Dynamic Immersion Exposure Test. These methods provided a thorough assessment of the composite’s performance under various conditions. These methods offer valuable insights into the composite’s action mechanisms, showing how factors like GO concentration, bacterial cell wall structure, and exposure mode influence antibacterial performance. Together, they highlight the importance of both static and dynamic testing environments, revealing how the contact area and bacterial strain type affect antibacterial activity. The broader significance of these methods lies in their ability to generalize the testing of antibacterial properties for a wide range of polymer composites. By using a combination of static, surface, and dynamic immersion techniques, researchers can better capture the full spectrum of antibacterial mechanisms from contact killing to oxidative stress. These methods provide a standardized framework that can be applied across various studies, facilitating consistent comparisons and a deeper understanding of how different composites interact with bacterial cells under varied conditions. This standardized approach is crucial for advancing the development of effective antibacterial materials and ensuring their practical utility in diverse real-world applications. In this study, the prepared CA–GO composite exhibits significant antibacterial potential due to the GO’s sharp edges and oxidative properties. The composite can disrupt bacterial membranes, induce oxidative stress, and enhance contact killing, making it a promising material for antimicrobial applications. Its effectiveness against different bacterial strains, particularly Gram-positive bacteria, demonstrates its versatility. The combination of CA’s structural stability and GO’s antimicrobial properties makes the CA–GO composite an excellent candidate for advancing antibacterial materials.
