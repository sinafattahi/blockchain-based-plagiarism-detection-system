Abstract

In this paper, a dielectric-modulated buried source horizontally double-gate TFET-based (DM-BSHDG TFET) biosensors is proposed for detection of biomolecules. The channel partially encloses the buried source, and the drain half surrounds the channel. Horizontally double gate is located above the source and channel. The performance of the DM-BDSTG TFET-based biosensors was evaluated by two-dimensional simulation of TCAD (ATLAS). Biomolecules can be distinguished due to the dielectric constants of different biomolecules placed in the cavity and will lead to different transfer characteristics of the device. The results show that the drain current sensitivity can reach 1.41 × 1010 when the gate-source voltage is 2 V. In addition, the subthreshold swing sensitivity can reach 0.9. Therefore, the DM-BSHDG TFET-based biosensors can provide excellent detection and recognition capabilities.

1. Introduction

A biosensor is an analytical device that converts biological reactions into measurable signals and is widely used in biological sample analysis. The detection performance of chemical sensors and biosensors has been greatly improved due to the application of nanotechnology, , and they are widely used in chemical analysis, medical diagnosis, and food safety detection. , The metal oxide semiconductor field-effect transistor (MOSFETs) based biosensors are popular in the field of label-free detection due to their compactness and energy savings. Field effect transistor biosensors utilize electrical modulation to detect biomolecules. However, the subthreshold swing (SS) in MOSFET biosensors is limited to 60 mV/decade, and it has a long response time. Tunnel field effect transistors (TFET) can overcome this limitation due to the band-to-band tunneling mechanism, and many researchers have reported TFET-based biosensors, which can ensure high sensitivity and low power consumption in biomolecule detection applications. Subsequently, researchers have explored the dielectric modulated TFET (DM TFET) by creating a cavity below the gate to accommodate biomolecules for biosensing applications. , The conventional TFET is composed of a P–I–N structure with high concentration doping in the source and drain and intrinsic doping in the channel. Due to random doping fluctuations and limited point tunneling effects, the drain current in conventional TFETs often falls below theoretical predictions. , It is worth noting that a novel extended source double gate DM TFET for the purpose of increasing the point tunneling (tunneling confined to discrete points where the junction is nonparallel to the gate) and line tunneling (tunneling occurring along the junction interface parallel to the gate) is proposed by Sandeep et al., which can achieve excellent drain current sensitivity (S d). The conventional TFET suffers from a limited drain current, prompting numerous structural modifications by researchers to enhance its drain current. The point and line tunneling are realized, and the possibility of tunneling is improved due to the introduction of a buried source and horizontal double gate. Therefore, we propose a dielectric modulated buried source horizontally double gate TFET (DM-BSHDG TFET) based biosensors to obtain a high S d and subthreshold swing sensitivity (S SS).

The intellectual merits of this work lie in the novel device architecture and comprehensive simulation-based analysis of the proposed DM-BSHDG TFET-based biosensors. By introducing a buried source and horizontally double-gate configuration, this study demonstrates a significant enhancement in both the drain current sensitivity and subthreshold swing sensitivity (0.9). This work provides fundamental insights into the interplay among device geometry, electrostatic control, and biomolecule-induced dielectric effects, advancing the theoretical framework for high-sensitivity biosensor design. The buried source architecture, coupled with the horizontal double-gate design, exemplifies how strategic doping profile engineering and gate configuration can simultaneously amplify point and line tunneling effects, thereby boosting the drain current and sensitivity. The results underscore the importance of cavity positioning and dimensions in modulating dielectric effects, suggesting that cavity optimization should be tailored to target biomolecule sizes and dielectric properties. In this paper, the performances of DM-BSHDG TFET-based biosensors in terms of S d and S SS are discussed in depth. Section describes the device structure and the simulation model. Section describes the effects of different factors on the sensitivity of DM-BSHDG TFET-based biosensors. In particular, biomolecules with varying dielectric constants and charge states distinctly influence the transfer characteristics, electric field, and energy band diagrams. In Section , the results of the investigation are summarized, and the application prospect of DM-BSHDG TFET based biosensors in the field of biological detection is prospected.

2. Device Structure and Simulation Model

The schematic cross-sectional view of the biosensor based on the DM-BSHDG TFET is shown in Figure . In order to facilitate changing the sensitivity parameter, a gate with a work function of 4.72 eV is placed above the source and channel. A source with a thickness of 10 nm is located in the middle of the device and has a doping concentration is 2 × 1019 cm–3. Two 3-nm-thick and 20-nm-wide cavities are used to fill the biomolecules to achieve the function of the sensor. The channel partially encloses the source while being itself half-surrounded by the drain. The doping concentration of the channel and drain with a thickness of 10 nm is 1 × 1016 cm–3 and 2 × 1018 cm–3, respectively. An oxide layer of silicon dioxide with a thickness of 1 nm is added to immobilize the biomolecules. Silicon materials are adopted in the source, channel and drain, the band gap of silicon is set to 1.08 eV, the effective density of states for the conduction band and valence band were assigned values of 2 × 1019 and 1.04 × 1019 cm–3 in the default configuration, respectively.

The device sensitivity is evaluated through TCAD simulations to systematically analyze the performance characteristics of the DM-BSHDG TFET-based biosensors. It is widely recognized that the use of an appropriate model is essential for ensuring the accuracy of the simulations. Consequently, a standard local band–band tunneling model is employed to capture the spatial variation of the energy band structure. This model facilitates the precise calculation of the actual potential distribution across the tunneling barriers as opposed to relying on a constant or averaged electric field along the barriers. Furthermore, other models such as the Shockley-Read-Hall recombination, Fermi–Dirac statistics, band gap narrowing, and the Lombardi mobility model are utilized. − Given that the film thickness of the proposed device is 10 nm, quantum mechanical effects have been neglected in this study. However, for film thicknesses below 10 nm, quantum confinement effects become significant, necessitating the incorporation of a quantum confinement model in the simulations. A dielectric constant of k = 1 for the cavity represents an air-filled state. Additionally, the positive and negative charges of biomolecules are analyzed to assess the sensitivity of the proposed devices. The simulation parameters of me.tunnel and mh.tunnel are 0.21 and 0.16, respectively. Two-dimensional tunneling effects were captured by using a polygonal region approach. The accuracy of the simulation framework was validated through implementation of a prefabricated TFET device within the computational model. Figure validates the simulation results against the transfer characteristics reported by Wang et al., showing excellent correlation.

S d and S SS are analyzed in detail, , and they are written as

where SS air and I d(air) are the subthreshold swing and on-sate drain current on-current, respectively, when the cavities are filled with air. Similarly, SS bio and I d(bio) are the subthreshold swing and on-sate drain current, respectively, when the cavities are filled with biomolecules.

3. Results and Discussion

3.1. Influence of Different Biomolecules on Biosensors

Different biomolecules have varying dielectric constants. For instance, the dielectric constants for uricase, biotin, bacteriophage T7, keratin, and gelatin are 1.54, 2.63, 6, 8, and 12, respectively, as reported in reference. , The variation in the dielectric constants among these biomolecules influences the capacitance value of the cavity, thereby altering the drain current. Consequently, the detection of biomolecules is facilitated by monitoring the alterations in drain current. , As depicted in Figure a, the transfer characteristic curve of the biosensor is observed to shift to the left with an increase in the dielectric constant. The highest on-sate drain current (I on) reaches 10–7 A when the dielectric constant is k = 12 in this structure due to the occurrence of both line tunneling and point tunneling at the tunneling junction. Furthermore, the device achieves a high S d due to the fact that the I on is reduced to 10–18 A at k = 1. Figure b,c shows the influence of the dielectric constant of the biomolecules on I on and the on-current and off-current ratio (I on/I off) for the DM-BDSTG TFET-based biosensors. As k increases, the band bending becomes more severe, resulting in a smaller potential barrier width at the source-channel junction, thereby enhancing the probability of tunneling and increasing I on and I on/I off. The variation of S d with k is shown in Figure d, where the S d increases from 1 × 101 to 1 × 1010 as k increases from 1.54 to 12. An increase in the dielectric constant leads to an enhancement of the electric field near the tunnel junction, which reduces the tunnel width and in turn increases the S d. A high S d response means that the transistor has a high sensing speed and sensing capability.

Figure a,b exhibits the variation of SS and S SS: SS decreases and S SS increases because of a larger dielectric constants of the biomolecule in the cavity. It can also be seen from the figure that the minimum SS is less than 60 mV/decade. Smaller SS means that the gate voltage on the transistor is more sensitive to changes in the subthreshold region, which will reduce the power consumption during switching operation.

In order to illustrate the tunneling probability of the proposed device, Figure shows the distribution of electric field diagrams along with the X-position and Y-position. It is observed that the electric field of DM-BSHDG TFET-based biosensors varies with the change of k. The electric field lines become tighter due to a higher electric field for higher values of k at the same voltage. Simultaneously, there is a peak in the electric field near the tunneling junction in Figure a,b. As shown in Figure a, the peak electric field near the tunneling junction reaches 1.77 × 106 V/cm in the X-position when k = 12, representing a 36.15% enhancement in electric field intensity compared to the case of k = 2.63. Similarly, Figure b demonstrates that the maximum electric field attains 7.38 × 105 V/cm in the Y-position at the tunneling junction when k = 12. The relationship between the tunneling probability and the electric field is as follows:

T(E)≅exp[−42m×Eg3/23qh(Eg+ΔΦ)εSiεbiotoxttbody]ΔΦ 3

Among them, T(E) represents the tunneling probability, m is the effective mass of the carriers, E g is the band gap at the tunneling junction, ΔΦ is the energy range that the charge carriers can tunnel, t oxt is the total thickness of the oxide layer and cavity under the gate, and t body is the thickness of the bulk region. Therefore, the tunneling probability exhibits a superlinear dependence on the electric field intensity, which enhances electron tunneling across the energy bands.

The larger the dielectric constants of biomolecules, the more responsive the electric field becomes, resulting in a more pronounced curvature of the energy band diagram. This curvature increases the probability of electron tunneling, thereby affecting the transfer characteristics of the device.Thus, variations in biomolecular dielectric constants induce distinct energy band curvatures, directly correlating with biosensor sensitivity. As seen in Figure a,b, biomolecules with a high dielectric constant exhibit more pronounced band diagram curvature, thereby amplifying the detection signal through enhanced tunneling. It is also observed that, compared to the Y-position, the energy band diagrams in the X-position are more distinguishable for different dielectric constants.

3.2. Impact of Charged Biomolecules on DM-BSHDG TFET

In Figure , biomolecules with different positive and negative charge densities are immobilized in the cavity, and dielectric constants are 2.63 and 8. The I on increases when the cavity charge density expands. Since the charge density is proportional to the current and the charge provides additional channels that make it easier for electrons or holes to cross the energy barrier. Conversely, I on increases as the negative charge density decreases. It can also be seen that the transfer curves of charged biomolecules have a wide range at k = 2.63.

Figure a,b respectively illustrate the change of electric field in the X-position with the concentration at k = 2.63, 8. As mentioned earlier, I on increases in proportion to the charge density, and as the charge density increases, the maximum value of the electric field increases. The increase in electric field directly promotes electron tunneling, leading to enhanced device performance.

4. Conclusions

This work describes an innovative DM-BSHDG TFET-based biosensors optimized for the device size. With the help of a two-dimensional simulation of TCAD, we have thoroughly analyzed the device characteristics. By varying the dielectric constant from 1 to 12 to represent different biomolecules in the cavity, we systematically investigated how k and charge density influence the biosensor’s performance metrics. The results show that the device has a better performance. Furthermore, it has been seen that a positive charged is able to obtain a larger I on as compared with a negative charged while analyzing positive charged and negative charged for different k. Meanwhile, the results show that the S d can be as high as 1010 and S SS can reach 0.9 compared to other TFET biosensors. It can be concluded that the proposed DM-BSHDG TFET-based biosensors has a high sensitivity and low power consumption. These superior characteristics position the proposed DM-BSHDG TFET-based biosensors as a promising candidate for next-generation nanoelectronic biosensing applications.
