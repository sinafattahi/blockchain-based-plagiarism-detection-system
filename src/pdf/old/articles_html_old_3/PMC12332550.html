Abstract

Leptospirosis, a re-emerging zoonotic disease, is challenging human and animal health due to the lack of early and rapid diagnostic tools and effective vaccines. The exoproteome of the pathogen expressed under pathogenic conditions possesses a rational diagnostic significance due to its consistent presence in body fluids. were challenged to conditions simulating infection using physiological temperatures and osmolarity. Using state-of-the-art extraction techniques, efficient enrichment of nonabundant proteins, and high-resolution LC-MS/MS, we identified 1575 exoproteins from both the pathogen surface and culture supernatant. The results indicate a significant upregulation of 155 exoproteins, of which 41 were predicted to have moonlighting properties, 35 were identified as adhesins, and several proteins were components of the type 2 secretion system (T2SS). Additionally, 10 proteins showed extracellular matrix (ECM) binding properties, out of which 4 orthologs were found using the T2SS. The overall characteristics of upregulated proteins show that they can help Leptospira establish infection, invasion, and protection from the host defense, thereby providing new insights into the pathogen to confront the host via an increased energy level, secretion system, and host ECM binding molecules. Furthermore, the study suggests potential candidates for efficient antileptospiral countermeasures.

Introduction

Leptospirosis is a neglected tropical disease of global importance that is caused by the helically coiled motile spirochete . About 1.3 million people are infected with leptospirosis annually, with a death toll of about 60,000. Leptospires enter the host through cuts, abrasions, or mucosal routes and then spread to various organs via circulation. The disease causes flu-like symptoms and, in its most severe form, can lead to multiorgan failure. Current serological diagnostic methods include microscopic agglutination tests (MAT) and enzyme-linked immunosorbent assays. However, these tests can detect infection only after significant progress. After the bacteria invade vital organs and cause severe damage, antibiotic treatment becomes less effective, making early diagnosis the primary challenge to detect leptospirosis. In the case of prevention, many attempts of vaccination using proteins, lipopolysaccharide (LPS), and heat-killed Leptospira were attempted, but these are inefficient or provide protection for a small subset of serovars. The identification of new target molecules provides greater sensitivity and specificity for diagnosis at the early stages of infection, and the active molecules, whose function is critical for pathogen survival, can be used to develop vaccines.

Leptospira can thrive in diverse environments, enabling efficient transmission of the disease. The adaptive capacity of Leptospira to sense its surroundings and produce differential proteins and cellular components indicates its remarkable versatility in surviving in the environment. It is well known that many pathogenic bacteria respond to changes in temperature and pH after invading a host by altering the production of virulence factors and other proteins. Some bacteria, such as and , require the environmental conditions of a mammalian infection to produce proteins associated with virulence and pathogenesis. , Another spirochete, , appears to be able to detect changes in temperature during infection in vertebrates. However, it is unclear how the leptospiral proteome changes when it migrates from its free-living natural environment to a human host. A rise from roughly 25 °C in ambient temperatures (in wet soil and water) to a human physiological temperature (PT) of 37 °C during the febrile phase of infection is necessary for subsequent hematogenous spread to target organs/tissues of the mammalian host. Similarly, Leptospira is exposed to physiological change from its natural habitat in water with an osmolarity of 60–66 mOsm to the physiological osmotic environment of mammals (300 mOsm). This transition of physiological conditions from a free-living to an invasive phase necessitates significant proteome modifications, which play a crucial role in the pathogen’s survival within the host. Although many studies on the differential expression of proteins were conferred to the outer membrane, limited studies were conducted on exoproteome identification. The surface-exposed proteins constitute the first line of contact between bacteria and host being leading actors in this complex interplay. These proteins, either exposed on the surface or dissolved in the extracellular milieu, are crucial in pathogenesis and host–pathogen interactions, making them valuable for developing effective antileptospiral targets. The exoproteins may contain many molecules useful to combat leptospirosis. Exoproteomes in Leptospira are crucial in a wide range of biological processes, such as host–pathogen interactions, immunological evasion, and virulence. , The bacteria release these proteins into the surroundings, which could impact the host’s immunological response and contribute to pathogenicity. Secretory enzymes, such as lipase, hyaluronidase, elastase, etc., are secreted by many infectious organisms along with toxins and proteases. The culture supernatant of has been demonstrated to have several protease activities that can destroy the extracellular matrix (ECM), contributing to infection and pathogenesis. Studies have shown that a 60 kDa hemolysin secretion can induce proinflammatory cytokine production through the Toll-like receptor 2- and 4-mediated JNK and NF-kB signaling pathways. , Researchers have utilized specific antisera to identify hemolysin and sphingomyelinase activity in the culture fluids of various pathogenic Leptospira strains. , In addition, patients with acute and chronic leptospirosis have exhibited diverse immunoreactive proteins in their serum. Like , which employs immune-evasion molecules like proteases to evade the host immune system and damage the extracellular matrix, Leptospira may utilize secretory proteins to enhance its survival within the host. These secretory proteins could activate host zymogens that target host-specific defense mechanisms, thereby suppressing the antibacterial actions of the host. Previous investigations have identified exoproteomes of in culture supernatants, further supporting the notion that exoproteomes can play many crucial roles in establishing leptospiral infection. To date, an extracellular proteomic analysis conducted by Zeng et al. has revealed 66 proteins in the culture supernatant of serovar Lai. Subsequently, Eshghi et al. identified a remarkable 325 exoproteins, marking the highest number of proteins reported in such studies. , With the biological significance in pathogenesis and host–pathogen interactions, secretory proteins hold promising potential for diagnosing leptospiral infections. Concerning this biomedical significance, the identification and characterization of exoproteomes are of vital importance.

Difficulties in isolating and purifying secretory proteins from biological materials and protein-enriched culture supernatants have hindered investigations on their use in antileptospirosis treatments. Our previous study identified 1176 exoproteins and their posttranslational modifications, showing that 57% of the extracellularly isolated pathogenic proteins are involved in nutrient acquisition, cell–cell communication, and environmental detoxification. The exoproteome expressed under pathogenic conditions could give more insight into the pathogen invasion mechanism and its strategies for host defense. So, in this study, we proposed challenging leptospires with physiological conditions similar to those of the mammalian host using significant temperature changes (from about 25 to 37 °C) and osmolarity (from 66 to 300 mOsm), which would mimic infection. A high-resolution LC-MS/MS quantitative proteomics was performed on to analyze the exoproteome and gain insight into their biological role in finding candidates for antileptospiral measures.

Exoproteins Involved in the Protein Export System May Provide Insight into Transport Strategies

In silico insights into the assignment of exoproteins were employed using the Phobius program to predict N-terminal signal peptides and the SecretomeP (version 2.0) server to identify nonclassical protein export. Of the 1402 wash proteins identified, 228 contained an N-terminal signal peptide, indicating their involvement in classical protein export. Additionally, 181 proteins were predicted to be exported through nonclassical pathways, defined as pathways for exporting proteins lacking a classical signal peptide. Among the 473 proteins identified in the supernatant, 31 were predicted to have an N-terminal signal peptide associated with classical protein export. In addition, nonclassical export was predicted to 86 proteins. These findings highlight the variety of export channels used by distinct groups of proteins and provide significant insight into the processes of protein export. In total, 8.25% of the exoproteins carried an N-terminal signal peptide, while approximately 11.87% were found to be exported via nonclassical pathways, and the majority did not carry any signal peptide nor secreted by nonclassical pathway.

Identification of DE Exoproteins under In Vitro Conditions Provides Insight into Host–Pathogen Interactions

Out of 155 upregulated proteins identified in the exoproteome, 101 were found to be upregulated under PT, 122 were upregulated under PO, and 110 were upregulated under PTO. Among them, 65 were found to be common to all of the treatment conditions, where 20 proteins were found to be pathogenic and 49 were found to be nonpathogenic. The upregulation of exoproteins under physiological conditions suggests their importance in infection (Figure A). Within the downregulated exoproteome, 56 of these proteins were identified as downregulated in all treatments, while 87 were identified in each PT, 80 in PO, and 84 in PTO, representing 12 as pathogenic and 73 as nonpathogenic (Figure B) and listed in Supporting Table S13.

Adhesins as a Key Contributor among the Upregulated Exoproteome Unveiling Molecular Mechanisms of Pathogenicity

Among the 155 upregulated exoproteomes, 41 moonlighting proteins were identified that can perform multiple biological functions with a single polypeptide chain. Out of 41 moonlighting proteins, 31 were nonpathogenic and 10 were pathogenic, as listed in the Supporting Table S14. Among these, 32.72% target plasminogen and fibrinogen, while 25.45% were directed toward laminin and fibronectin, 30.6% were adhesins, and 13.23% were involved in other cell binding activities. Moreover, the upregulation of 10 known surface adhesins, along with 4 prominent metalloproteases, were found in the study (Figure ).

Exploring Pathways in Associated with DE Exoproteins

The Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) analysis revealed a range of biological pathways displaying differences in expression levels. These pathways encompassed metabolism, genetic information processing, environmental information processing, cellular processes, biochemical hierarchies, and human diseases. The involvement of 155 upregulated exoproteins and 109 downregulated exoproteins in various pathways, with 130 proteins as part of metabolism, 1 involved in genetic information processing, 14 associated with environmental information processing, 12 proteins involved in cellular processes, and 1 in human diseases, respectively, is listed in Supporting Tables S16 and S17. Considering the abundance of peptides and the upregulated proteins, we identified 8 highly significant pathways: glycolysis, tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, protein export, chemotaxis, ribosomal function, motility, quorum sensing, and two-component systems listed in Supporting Tables S15–S18.

The gene ontology enrichment analysis of the DE exoproteins revealed three main categories: biological processes, molecular functions, and cellular components. The upregulated proteins strongly enriched gene ontology terms associated with metabolic processes and cellular components such as macromolecular complexes, cells, cytoplasm, ribosomes, ribosomal subunits, and proton-transporting ATP synthase. On the other hand, the biological process category showed upregulation in organic cyclic compound binding, structural molecule activity, and heterocyclic compound binding. In contrast, the downregulated proteins were primarily involved in catalytic binding and ion binding in the molecular function category and the de novo inosine 5′-monophosphate biosynthetic process and cellular nitrogen compound metabolic process in the cellular component category.

Discussion

Exoproteomes containing surface-associated proteins and those from the culture supernatant are significant due to their interactions with the host environment. , Studying exoproteome changes and differential expression under pathogenic conditions is vital to understanding the host’s biology and mechanisms of pathogenesis and the need to develop diagnostics, drugs, and vaccines rationally against the disease. In this study, we use an efficient exoproteome extraction method coupled with the elimination of cellular proteins from consideration. The stepwise centrifugation reduced the lysis of the cells, collection of the growth medium, and enrichment of the nonabundant proteins from the protein-enriched EMJH media using the ProteoMiner kit. Similarly, washing with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) containing 5 mM MgCl2 without any harsh conditions or solubilizers extracted the easily detachable surface-associated proteins bound to the surface of Leptospira. The proteins are then identified using TMT-labeled LC-MS/MS analysis, identifying the highest number of exoproteomes (1575) to date. The unique proteins identified in high abundance suggest an extraction efficiency with negligible cross-contamination. Exoproteome was sorted out using a rational method supported by robust statistical analysis. This is characterized by the enrichment of the proteins found in the exoproteome, which should exceed those present in the whole cell, eliminating the possibility of proteins being released from the lysis. Physiological conditions of infection, such as temperature, osmolarity, and a combination of both, are applied to obtain the maximum number of DE exoproteins. Compared to our previous study, which identified 1176 proteins in wash and supernatant, our present study reported 1402 proteins in the wash and 473 as supernatant proteins, with upregulation of 10.77 and 1.88% in the respective wash and supernatant fractions. Across all of the physiological treatments, the wash fractions exhibited more DE proteins than the supernatant fractions. Further, the percentage of upregulated proteins in each fraction was generally higher than that of downregulated proteins. We observed a significant overall upregulation of 155 (9.84%) exoproteins under all physiological conditions, and vice versa for downregulated proteins.

The exoproteome extraction method was very efficient in enriching and identifying the proteins. Compared to the cellular presence, the abundance in the exoproteome identified 1102 (69.96%) proteins unique to the wash fractions, indicating association with the surface. Additionally, 155 (9.84%) of the proteins were found in the supernatant, suggesting their classification as secretory proteins. About 318 (20.08%) of the proteins were found in both fractions. Furthermore, our analysis revealed that 3.42% of the exoproteins, consisting of 43, 5, and 4 proteins, were identified as wash, supernatant, and both, respectively, which is not reported in the cellular fraction. The present study identified nearly four times higher exoproteins than the 325 exoproteins reported by Eshghi et al. These significant differences could be attributed to the distinct identification methods employed for surface-associated and supernatant proteins. Additionally, our study identified 1400 proteins as abundant surface-associated proteins, which is 20-fold higher than the number reported by Techawiwattanaboon et al. Notably, 153 were upregulated among these proteins under the physiological conditions of infection, indicating that those surface proteins may be essential for leptospiral pathogenesis.

Pathogenesis involves a series of interrelated events, including adhesion, colonization, invasion, drug resistance, and evasion of host defenses. These are crucial for leptospiral invasion and dissemination within the host , Our study found that a relatively small percentage of pathogenic proteins were upregulated, most of which were associated with the surface (i.e., 20), and only 12 pathogenic proteins were found abundant in the medium. The limited number of pathogenic proteins may be due to the limitation of MP3 prediction software because many of the exoproteins were found to be immunogenic in experimental conditions and not predicted as pathogenic by the software. For instance, LipL45 and GroEL are antigens produced during leptospiral infection that induce cytokine generation, although predicted to be nonpathogenic by MP3. , In this context, we have performed additional studies to determine the functions of these proteins and found many to exhibit “moonlighting” properties. Our study identified 37 moonlighting proteins (Table ), which aligns with the 32 moonlighting proteins reported by Eshghi et al., indicating the potential presence of additional pathogenic proteins that contribute to infection, invasion, and pathogenesis.

Among the 155 upregulated exoproteins in our current study, 69 were consistently identified across all treatment groups (Figure A). These proteins showed a significant increase in expression compared to that of the control group, with a high confidence level. Moreover, many of these proteins have been extensively studied and are known to play critical roles in the survival, export, and pathogenesis of Leptospira. Importantly, these proteins demonstrate remarkable conservation among the pathogenic P1 strains, followed by P2 and the saprophytic S1 and S2 species. This conservation suggests their essentiality in survival and may contribute to leptospiral pathogenicity.

The in silico analysis of 1575 exoproteins revealed that Leptospira primarily exports proteins using a dedicated export mechanism. Among the 1402 and 473 proteins identified in the wash and supernatant, 228 (16%) and 77 (16.2%) contained an N-terminal signal sequence, suggesting classical protein export. However, 181 (12.7%) and 86 (18.18%) proteins were predicted to be exported through a nonclassical protein export system. In the wash fractions, 32 (2.25%) contained N-terminal signal peptide among the upregulated proteins and 24 (1.64%) suggested nonclassical protein export without the peptide. In the supernatant, there were 2 proteins with 4 N-terminal signal peptides and 2 proteins with nonclassical protein export systems among the upregulated proteins. Though the export mechanism of the remaining 993 proteins in the wash and 310 in the supernatant remains unknown, it is reported as a part of exoproteome analysis in our study. The exoproteome nature of these proteins is supported by the identification of the abundance of experimentally proven exoproteins like LipL32 and GroES on the surface , and GroEL in the supernatant fractions.

Our study observed a 4-fold rise in LipL32 (LIC_11352) in the wash fractions under simulating infection conditions that clearly shows the surface localization of LipL32 and its upregulation under pathogenic conditions, corroborating with earlier reports. The 4-fold rise under pathogenic conditions and earlier reports on its interaction with TLR2 on human kidney cells, stimulation of inflammatory response, and binding on the ECM shows this molecule will be active and available for targeting diagnostic or vaccine during infection. Further, our conservations analysis shows the presence of LipL32 in P1 and P2 (pathogenic) also shows its potential in the identification of pathogenic strains, which is supported by the use of LipL32 genes for the identification of Leptospira in clinical samples depicted in Figure A and Supporting Figure S2.

We report another well-known protein, LipL45 (LIC_11643), found to be upregulated with a 1.6-fold raise in PT, a 1.8-fold raise in PO, and a 2-fold raise in PTO in the wash fractions, which confirms surface association along with its presence in P1, P2 (pathogenic) and S1, S2 (saprophytic) groups of Leptospira. The surface presence is accompanied by the role of a vital immunogen involved in degranulation and heparin-binding stimulating inflammatory response, suggesting its potential to use as a vaccine candidate for which the possibility is explored by Ugen et al., as a DNA vaccine depicted in Figure B and Supporting Figure S3.

LipL71, a major surface molecule, exhibited a significant upregulation of 0.3-fold, 1.9-fold, and 1.5-fold expression under PT, PO, and PTO, respectively, that corroborated with a previous study, highlighting LipL71 as a potent inducer of antibody responses in patients and a promising vaccine candidate.

GroEL (LIC_11335), an extracellular protein interacting with host proteins, observed a 3.5-fold upregulation, corroborating an earlier study that demonstrated upregulation at 300 mOsm in the culture supernatant. Further, the GroEL is a moonlighting protein, resembling the multifunctional nature as predicted in . Another study also shows the usefulness of GroEL in IgM ELISA assay for screening leptospirosis cases, suggesting their distribution among the Leptospira species.

Proteases found in the wash and exoproteome are another set of potential zinc-dependent molecules that can degrade the extracellular matrix, affect the host immune system, and promote the invagination of leptospires. The M4 family metalloprotease (LIC_10715) exhibited 0.4-fold upregulation exclusively in PT, while the zinc metalloprotease (LIC_10857) displayed notable upregulation with 1.3-fold in PT, 1.8-fold in PO, and 1.5-fold in PTO on the surface depicted in Figure B and Supporting Figure S4. Another M43 family zinc metalloprotease (LIC_13434) showed upregulation of 1.68 in PT, 1.15 in PO, and 2.4 in PTO in the exoproteome. These findings are consistent with observations by Fraga et al., who reported similar proteins, such as Zmp1 in and zinc-dependent metalloprotease in , both demonstrating pathogenic activities in both in vitro and in vivo contexts.

The imelysin family (LIC_10713) is known to cleave insulin and fibrinogen and has been found to upregulate to 1.5-fold in PT, 2.3-fold in PO, and 1.7-fold in PTO in the supernatant. This protein in the medium may be uniformly available in serum for use as a diagnostic candidate. Recent studies show it can bind to many ECM components and its potential as a diagnostic marker.

We utilized the KEGG pathway analysis to identify potential signaling pathways among the DE proteins. Our analysis shows the upregulation of carbohydrate metabolism molecules (glycolytic pathways and the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, such as LIC_12090, LIC_12233, LIC_12475, LIC_13105, LIC_12002, LIC_12573, LIC_12574, and LIC_12829). Proteins (LIC_11241, LIC_11243, and LIC_12002) related to oxidative phosphorylation are upregulated in the exoproteome. Protein (LIC_10583) fatty acid metabolism showed downregulation in wash fractions listed in Supporting Tables S15–S18. The study also displayed significant modulation of proteins associated with signal transduction, chemotaxis (LIC_12454), flagellar assembly (LIC_11531, LIC_11889, and LIC_11890), and quorum sensing (LIC_12540 and LIC_13384), which may help the bacteria to survive. Interestingly, under physiological infection conditions, we observed significant upregulation of orthologs of the Sec-translocation system (SecA, SecD, YajC) in the exoproteome, indicating that Leptospira may be actively secreting proteins through the Sec translocase system during infection. The Type I secretory pathway, which relies on ABC transporters ATP-binding proteins (LIC_12684, LIC_13384, and LIC_10233) is upregulated in the exoproteome, determining its importance in transporting various exoproteins, including toxins, antimicrobial peptides, siderophores, and hydrolytic enzymes. Similarly, GspG and GspC, components of the type II secretion system, show upregulation in the exoproteome, and GspD in the wash fraction. This finding aligns with a previous study where GspD was identified as a secreted protein. Activating these secretion systems primarily aids Leptospira in interacting with the host’s fibrinolytic system, a significant factor in invasion mechanisms. Leptospira can bind plasminogen on its surface and convert it to plasmin, enhancing its aggressive spread. While associated with plasmin, Leptospira can degrade ECM proteins and interfere with the deposition of complement components C3b and IgG on its surface. This is evident through the existence and increased expression of high levels of adhesins (36.77%) serving a dual role, i.e., moonlighting proteins. Among these, 36.7 and 2% target plasminogen and fibrinogen, while 12 and 8.2% are directed toward laminin and fibronectin. Moreover, we observed the upregulation of (WP_000678902.1) OmpL47, (WP_000545443.1) LIC_12730, and (WP_000672495.1) Lsa25.6, in the wash (surface) fraction, which justifies that leptospiral directness toward fibrinogen, laminin, and fibronectins for initiating its dissemination into host. , These findings dwell on the significance of these proteins in contributing to leptospiral invasion of host cells. Further, the upregulation of M4 family metalloprotease (LIC_10715), M43 family zinc Metalloprotease (LIC_13434), S41 family peptidase (LIC_12303), and zinc metalloprotease (LIC_10857) are particularly noteworthy, as they are presumed to be secreted effectors through the type 2 secretion system (T2SS). , A total of 36.77% of the ECM binding molecules were found to be upregulated among the total upregulated exoproteome. The upregulation of exoproteins capable of ECM binding and degrading proteins can help Leptospira degrade the ECM molecules, thereby facilitating infection. So, with the upregulation and downregulation observed in the exoproteome, literature survey, and considering the peptide abundance in the corresponding fractions, we can conclude that secretion of these proteins along with specific virulence factors through the leptospiral secretion system may play an important role in infection, pathogenesis, and survival of the pathogen in the host (Figure ). However, each protein has to be investigated individually and in combination with the coexpressed proteins to find their exact role.

Conclusions

This study comprehensively analyzed the exoproteome profile of Leptospira under in vitro simulated infection conditions. A quantitative proteomic approach utilizing high-resolution LC-MS/MS analysis identified significant exoproteins that showed differential expression under the physiological conditions of infection. Three proteins, namely, LipL32, LipL45, and zinc metalloprotease, showed a notable increase in protein expression levels in the surfactome. Additionally, LIC_10713, an imelysin family protein, was found to be upregulated in the supernatant. Comparing pathogenicity, it becomes apparent that nonpathogenic proteins exhibit greater diversity, closely resembling many adhesins in their moonlighting role as significant ECM binding proteins. The predictions from KEGG and GO analyses further support the notion that the upregulated proteins participate in various biological processes. Notably, the upregulation of T2SS also stands out, strongly suggesting that the identified proteases are indeed secreted via the T2SS pathway. Additionally, the upregulated exoproteome under infection conditions, including GroEL, LipL45, and Peptidase M75, represents a promising candidate for leptospiral intervention strategies. Further research focused on individual proteins or pathways is essential to comprehending the functions of these distinctively expressed exoproteins fully. Nevertheless, these findings offer valuable insights for future exoproteome-related studies using this methodology and enhance our understanding of pathogenic mechanisms and their role in leptospirosis transmission and pathogenesis.

Materials and Methods

Bacterial Strains, Media, and Growth Conditions

serogroup Icterohememorrhagiae serovar Copenhageni strain Fiocruz L1–130 was obtained from the repository of the ICMR-Regional Medical Research Centre, Port Blair, India, a WHO collaborating center for diagnosis, reference, research, and training in leptospirosis. The standard culture of Leptospira was maintained in EMJH (Ellinghausen McCullough Johnson Harris) medium (Difco Laboratories, Franklin Lakes, NJ) supplemented with 1% bovine serum albumin at 29 °C until the mid log phase of growth.

Challenging Leptospires with Physiological Environments

As described earlier, the human physiological environment was induced to Leptospira, with the following modifications. A mid log phase culture of Leptospira was grown in EMJH, which is used for inducing the physiological temperature (PT) at 37 °C, inducing physiological osmolarity (PO) at 150 mM/L NaCl and both physiological temperature and osmolarity (PTO) for 24 h, respectively. The control was the standard growth condition mentioned above, and each test condition was replicated four times, each containing 25 mL of the culture.

Enrichment of the Exoproteome

Extraction of Protein from the Culture Supernatant

Proteins released by leptospires in the culture supernatant were extracted using the protocol outlined earlier with slight modifications. , Briefly, the Leptospira culture was centrifuged at 4000g, and the culture supernatant was collected to isolate supernatant proteins. The culture supernatant was again centrifuged at 6000g for 30 min, followed by additional centrifugation at 12,000g for 30 min, to remove any remaining Leptospira from the supernatant. This step was crucial to prevent Leptospira from being tightly packed and ruptured, which may release cellular proteins during pelleting.

The medium containing a low abundance of supernatant proteins was enriched using the ProteoMiner protein enrichment kit (Biorad, Hercules, CA, catalog no: 1633011) with slight modifications according to the manufacturer’s instructions. In brief, the supernatant fractions (i.e., media) were dialyzed against phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) to facilitate optimal binding to the ProteoMiner slurry. The ProteoMiner slurry, consisting of 100 μL of settled beads in the column, was washed twice with 1 mL of PBS. The washed beads were taken from the column, added to the dialyzed supernatant, and incubated at 4 °C overnight (>8 h) with gentle shaking to facilitate optimal binding. The ProteoMiner beads were collected by centrifugation at 2000g for 5 min, replaced in the column, washed with 100 μL PBS, and eluted with 2 × 20 μL buffer provided in the kit.

Extraction of Surface Proteins (Surfactome)

The Leptospira pellet from the culture was washed thrice with PBS containing 5 mM MgCl2 with gentle mixing that allows leaching out of surface proteins, and the washing buffer was collected by centrifuging at 2500g for 5 min at 4 °C. The collected wash was pooled and centrifuged again at 12,000g for 30 min to ensure the removal of any residual leptospires, it was precipitated using chilled acetone for further solubilization in suitable buffer and analysis.

Identification of Cellular Proteins

The cellular proteins were identified from the cell pellet after fractionation using Triton X-114. The total amount of each protein from all of the Triton X-114 fractions was considered cellular abundance. This cellular abundance was compared with their abundance in the supernatant or surface or combined as exoproteins. The exoproteins found <1.5 times that found as cellular are assumed to have come from the lysis of the cells during the extraction procedures and not accounted as exoproteins.

Protein Digestion and TMT Labeling

Equal amounts of protein samples from all conditions were subjected to in-solution digestion after reduction using 10 mM dithiothreitol (DTT) (I6125, Sigma-Aldrich, Bangalore, India), followed by alkylation with 20 mM iodoacetamide (D9779, Sigma-Aldrich, Bangalore, India). The proteins were then precipitated by using ice-cold acetone. In-solution trypsin digestion was carried out with a 1:20 enzyme-to-substrate ratio. The resulting peptide samples were lyophilized and labeled with tandem mass tags (TMT) according to the manufacturer’s instructions (Thermo Scientific, Waltham, MA). The reaction was halted by adding 8 μL of 5% hydroxylamine and incubating at room temperature (RT) for 15 min. Next, the pooled and TMT-labeled peptides were fractionated based on basic pH reversed-phase liquid chromatography (bRPLC) using an X Bridge BEH C18 Column (Waters Corporation, Milford, MA). The labeled peptides were resolved using an increasing gradient of 7 mM TEABC and 90% acetonitrile (pH 9) over a 30 min duration at a 1 mL/min flow rate, employing an Agilent 1100 LC system. This process yielded a total of 96 fractions, which were later combined into 12 fractions. The peptides were concentrated by vacuum centrifugation and subjected to a C18 StageTip (66883-U, Empore, Bellefonte, PA) cleanup before mass spectrometry analysis. ,

LC-MS/MS Analysis

The fractionated samples were dissolved in 0.1% formic acid and analyzed using a Thermo Scientific Orbitrap Fusion Tribrid mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Bremen, Germany) connected to an Easy-nLC-1200 nanoflow liquid chromatography system (Thermo Scientific, Bremen, Germany). The peptides were loaded onto a 2 cm trap column (nanoViper, 3 μm C18 Aq; Thermo Fisher Scientific) and separated on a 15 cm analytical column (nanoViper, 75 μm silica capillary, C18 Aq) at a flow rate of 300 nL/min. A linear gradient of 5–30% solvent B (80% acetonitrile (ACN) in 0.1% formic acid) was applied over 100 min, followed by a stepped gradient of 60–100% solvent B for 4 and 7 min, respectively. An MS analysis was carried out using an Orbitrap mass analyzer in data-dependent acquisition (DDA) mode, scanning the 400–1600 m/z mass range at a resolution of 120,000 mass. The injection time was limited to 10 ms. For the MS2 analysis, the data were acquired in top speed mode with a duty cycle of 3 s and subjected to higher collision energy dissociation at a normalized collision energy of 32. The MS/MS scans were performed on an Orbitrap mass analyzer with a resolution of 30,000 at 200 m/z, and the maximum injection time was 200 ms. The lock mass option (m/z 445.1200025) derived from ambient air was utilized for internal calibration.

Identification of Peptides and Proteins

The mass spectrometry data (Raw) files obtained from the analysis were searched against the serogroup Icterohemaemorrhagiae serovar Copenhageni strain Fiocruz L1–130, acquired from the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) database, which consisted of 3667 protein entries. In addition, 115 common contaminants, including bovine serum albumin, trypsin, and keratin, were included. Sequest HT and Mascot (versions 2.5.1; Matrix Science, London, U.K.) through Proteome Discoverer (Version 2.4) (Thermo Scientific, Bremen, Germany) were used to analyze the MS/MS data. Both algorithms were configured with specific search parameters, including a maximum of one missed cleavage, carbamidomethylation at cysteine, TMT 6-plex Lysine, and TMT 6-plex N-terminal as fixed modifications, and oxidation of methionine as dynamic modifications. The precursor mass error tolerance was set at 20 ppm, and the fragment mass error tolerance was set at 0.1 Da. The reliability of the result was ensured by searching the data against a decoy database, and peptides identified at a 1% false discovery rate (FDR) were selected for further protein identification. Normalization of the data based on total peptide amount was performed using the normalization feature available in the Reporter Ions Quantifier node of Proteome Discoverer.

Gel Electrophoresis and Immunoblotting

The concentration of protein in the supernatant and wash samples was estimated using the Bradford protein estimation protocol, following the method described by Kruger. Briefly, protein samples were treated with β-mercaptoethanol and then subjected to electrophoresis in an 8% SDS-PAGE gel. The separated proteins were transferred onto poly­(vinylidene fluoride) (PVDF) membranes (0.2 μm). Unbound sites on the membranes were blocked using 10% nonfat dried milk dissolved in Tris-buffered saline (TBS; pH 8.0) containing 0.1% Tween 20 (TBS-T) for 2 h at room temperature (RT). Following the blocking step, the membranes were probed with monospecific antisera specifically raised against each leptospiral protein and lipoprotein (LipL45, LipL32) and zinc metalloprotease (1:5000), respectively (GenScript manufactured the antibodies). The probing was performed overnight at 4 °C with gentle shaking. After incubation, the membranes were washed thrice for 4 min each using buffer (TBST). Subsequently, the membranes were probed for 2 h with goat antirabbit IgG conjugated with horseradish peroxidase (HRP) (GenScript: A01856) (1:5000) in TBST. Followed by washing with TBST for 4 min each, as described by Taylor et al. 2013. Visualizing and recording were done by subjecting the membranes to a chemiluminescent substrate and exposing them to blue-sensitive X-ray film. The quantification of bands in the immunoblots was analyzed using ImageJ software using the rolling disc background subtraction approach.

Conservation of Proteins among Leptospira Species

The conservation analysis to determine the phylogenetic relationship was carried out using NCBI-BlastP. Additionally, to determine the conservation of the proteins among the Leptospira, 11 different species (pathogenic and saprophytic) were used to extract total proteins for immunoblot assays. The transferred proteins were probed with LipL45 (1:5000), LipL32 (1:5000), and zinc metalloprotease (1:3000). Secondary antibody, goat antirabbit IgG conjugated with HRP at a dilution of 1:5000, was utilized for probing. The bands were developed by using a chemiluminescence substrate and recorded in a blue-sensitive X-ray film.

Bioinformatics Analysis

Prediction of Pathogenic Proteins

DE exoproteins were classified as pathogenic or nonpathogenic using MP3, an online program incorporating a support vector machine-hidden Markov model with better sensitivity and specificity. Assignments were carried out based on the following criteria: If the predictions of both HMM and SVM (Support Vector Machine) were in agreement and the prediction by MP3 is called “HS,” it indicated high accuracy. In the field, when the SVM and HMM modules provided different predictions, priority was given to the HMM. This choice was justified by the HMM demonstrating relatively higher sensitivity, specificity, and accuracy than SVM.

Prediction of Secretory and Moonlighting Proteins

We employed various prediction methods to predict protein secretion, considering canonical and noncanonical secretion pathways. SecretomeP (https://services.healthtech.dtu.dk/services/SecretomeP-2.0/) focuses explicitly on nonclassical secretion in bacteria and helps identify proteins secreted through signal peptide-independent pathways. Additionally, we utilized Phobius (https://phobius.sbc.su.se/), a comprehensive predictor that considers both transmembrane topology and signal peptides in protein analysis. Further, the Moonprot database was used to identify the heterologous functions of the exoproteome (http://moonlightingproteins.org/).

KEGG Analysis

Gene Ontology enrichment analysis was used to classify the proteins that showed treatment-dependent changes in expression levels in the supernatant and on the surface. The significance level (P-value) used in this study was determined using a false discovery rate (FDR) cutoff 0.05 (http://www.geneontology.org/). The pathways involved with these proteins were also interpreted with the help of the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG).

Statistical Analysis

The proteomic data were analyzed to identify proteins with significant fold changes using statistical methods performed with Microsoft Excel 2018. The analysis encompassed various steps: data filtration (removing proteins that do not have quantitative values), logarithmic data transformation (variances became more constant and the data fit the normal distribution), two-step normalization: data normalization by average and normalization by slope, imputation of missing value, subsequent statistical test (F-test and t test), fold change and P-value calculation. The P-value was then transformed into −log2 and volcano plots were generated to visually represent the log fold change treatment/control fold change on the x-axis and −log2 on the y-axis by GraphPad Prism version 10. A −log2 value >4.312 threshold indicated a P-value <0.05. Proteins with log2 fold changes >1.2 were upregulated, while those with log2 fold change <0.8 were considered downregulated. − However, Bonferroni was not used for low power (i.e., few replicates) and moderate conditions. In such cases, multiassay correction methods with conventional thresholds may be inadequate for actual positive detection, even if they exist.
