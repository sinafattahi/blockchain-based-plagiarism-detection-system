Abstract

A series of ortho/para-chlorinated N-(6-benzhydryl-2,4-dichlorophenylimino)-N’-aryliminoacenaphthylene derivatives as ligands (L1-L6, aryl = 2,6-dimethylphenyl (L1), 2,6-diethylphenyl (L2), 2,6-diisopropylphenyl (L3), 2,4,6-trimethylphenyl (L4), 2,6-diethyl-4-methylphenyl (L5), 6-benzhydryl-2,4-dichlorophenyl (L6)) were successfully synthesized and used for their corresponding nickel complexes (Ni1-Ni6). All organic compounds were comprehensively characterized using NMR, FT-IR, and EA techniques; the nickel complexes had their components determined through FT-IR and EA analysis, and the unambiguous molecular structures of representative nickel complexes were confirmed via single-crystal X-ray diffraction, revealing that Ni1 and Ni3 possessed a distorted octahedral geometry around the nickel center. Upon activation with EASC, all nickel complexes demonstrated high catalytic activity (4.7 to 37.9 × 106 g mol–1 h–1) for ethylene polymerization, with Ni1 achieving peak performance (12.2 × 106 g mol–1 h–1) at 50 °C in 30 min. Impressively, the catalytic system demonstrated exceptional thermal stability, maintaining high activity above 9 × 106 g mol–1 h–1 across a broad temperature range (40–70 °C) with minimal fluctuations. Contrary to typical chain-walking behavior observed in α-diimine nickel catalysts, the resultant polyethylene exhibited high crystallinity and minimal long-chain branching despite narrow polydispersity (Đ = 1.8–2.9) and moderate molecular weights (tens of thousands). Systematic modulation of ligand structure and polymerization conditions enabled precise control over crystallinity (X c: 6.4 to 62.6%), which was directly correlated with tunable mechanical properties (σb: 1.9–13.1 MPa, εb: 178.8–1030.6%). Representative samples of the resultant polyethylene displayed exceptional tensile strength (13.1 MPa), high elongation at break (1030.6%), and notable elastic recovery (65%), suggesting new types of thermoplastic elastomers.

1. Introduction

Polyethylene has firmly established itself as the cornerstone of the global plastics industry, with its widespread production and multifaceted applications driving transformative changes across numerous economic sectors. The advancements in metal-mediated catalytic systems have significantly propelled the field forward, particularly through the development of transition-metal catalysts capable of producing high-performance polyethylene variants with tailored properties. − Among these, Brookhart’s α-diimine nickel complexes (A, Figure ) represented a groundbreaking catalyst of late transition-metal complexes producing highly branched polyethylenes without using any comonomer as well as controlling the microstructures of resultant characteristic polyethylenes through fine tuning and modifications of ligands used. ,

Targeting the promising nickel catalysts, the systems should achieve reasonable activities with good thermostability. , More importantly, the resultant polyethylenes would be useful of properties being comprisable to industrial polymers. To enhance the catalytic performance forming highly branched polyethylenes, a significant improvement was made through introducing benzhydryl substituents forming unsymmetrical 1,2-bis­(imino)­acenaphthene-nickel­(II) halides (B, Figure ). , In the strategy incorporating bulky substituents along with optimizing electronic effects, the finely tuned nickel complexes demonstrated better performances in ethylene polymerization regarding the thermal stability of catalytic systems and controlling microstructures of resultant polyethylenes. − Besides the enhancement of thermal stabilities by employing bulky substituents, notably, nickel precatalysts containing electron-withdrawing groups commonly improved the activities in polymerization along with controlling the properties of polyethylenes obtained. ,− For example, halogen-substituted B Cl exhibited higher activities than B tBu (Figure ); meanwhile, the polyethylenes obtained by B Cl showed higher molecular weights and slightly lower branching degree under comparable experimental conditions. , By incorporating a single ortho-alkyl or chloro substituent (R = Me, Et, iPr and Cl), the symmetrical nickel complexes (C, Figure ) performed better activities compared to Brookhart-type nickel complexes (A, Figure ). In general, the substituents of benzhydryl derivatives could create sterically axial barriers around nickel cores to achieve exceptional activities (up to 1 × 107 g mol–1 h–1). ,,− Based on a normal aniline and one bulky-modified aniline, the unsymmetric nickel complexes (D, Figure ) , also enhanced activities as well as stabilized the active species by setting a reasonable steric hindrance around the nickel center. ,, Extensively, the ortho-chloro-group of N-aryl was found to significantly affect the catalytic performance of the nickel complexes (E, Figure ) , by adjusting the coordinative sphere characteristics. Very importantly, the nickel catalysts containing an ortho-chloro group on their N-aryls exhibited the remarkably thermal stability as 8 × 106 g mol–1 h–1 even at 70 °C, , representing a significant improvement over previous nickel catalysts. ,,−

Drawing inspiration from those nickel catalysts, the hybridized aniline compound bearing ortho-benzhydryl and ortho-/para-dichloro substituents has been prepared for the preparation of their nickel complexes (F, Figure ). Fortunately, the titled complexes showed good thermal stability and achieved high activities for ethylene polymerization up to 70 °C. Notably, the resultant polyethylenes could be regulated to some extent, displaying favorable properties that render them industrially applicable. Herein, the synthesis and characterization of titled nickel complexes, along with their performance in ethylene polymerization and the properties of the resulting polyethylenes, are reported.

2. Results and Discussion

2.1. Synthesis and Characterization of Ligands and Nickel Complexes

Following a modified literature procedure outlined in references, , a series of 1-(6-benzhydryl-2,4-dichlorophenylimino)-2-aryliminoacenaphthylene ligands was successfully synthesized. These ligands were designed with distinct N-aryl moieties, including 2,6-dimethylphenyl (L1), 2,6-diethylphenyl (L2), 2,6-diisopropylphenyl (L3), 2,4,6-trimethylphenyl (L4), 2,6-diethyl-4-methylphenyl (L5), and 6-benzhydryl-2,4-dichlorophenyl (L6). The synthesis commenced with 2-((6-benzhydryl-2,4-dichlorophenyl)­imino)­acenaphthylen-1­(2H)-one, which was prepared via a condensation reaction between acenaphthylene-1,2-dione and 2-dibenzhydryl-4,6-dichloroaniline, as illustrated in Figure . Following another ketoamine condensation reaction with anilines, five ligands (L1-L5) were synthesized with reasonable yields. In contrast, the symmetrical ligand L6 was readily prepared via a template synthesis and demetalation approach, achieving a high yield of 82% (Figure S1). To rigorously confirm the structural integrity and compositional purity of the newly synthesized ligands, comprehensive characterization was performed using FT-IR spectroscopy, 1H and 13C NMR spectroscopy (Figures S2–S17), and elemental analysis. These analytical techniques collectively provided unambiguous evidence of the ligands’ molecular structures and elemental compositions.

Subsequently, the corresponding nickel complexes (Ni1–Ni6) were prepared via stoichiometric reactions between each ligand (L1-L6) and (DME)­NiBr2, as depicted in Figure and Figure S1. The structural and compositional integrity of all five nickel complexes was characterized using a combination of FT-IR spectroscopy, elemental analysis, and single-crystal X-ray diffraction (SC XRD). In the FT-IR spectra of L1-L6, the characteristic νCN stretching vibrations of the imine moieties were observed within the range of 1681–1631 cm–1. Upon complexation to form Ni1–Ni6, these νCN absorptions shifted to lower wavenumbers, appearing in the range 1651–1616 cm–1. This bathochromic shift in the CN stretching frequency provides compelling evidence for the effective coordination of the imine nitrogen atoms (from the N,N-chelating ligands) to the nickel metal center, which aligns with prior studies on analogous 1,2-bis­(imino)­acenaphthene-nickel complexes.

Single crystals of Ni1 and Ni3 were grown by the slow vapor diffusion of diethyl ether into their respective dichloromethane solutions within an ambient environment containing moisture. The molecular structures of these two nickel complexes were unequivocally determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction (SC-XRD), as illustrated in Figure , with selected structural parameters summarized in Table . To facilitate a comparative analysis of the configuration differences induced by the introduction of ortho-/para-chlorine atoms and ortho-benzhydryl groups in the N-aryl moiety, the Brookhart-type nickel complex (A iPr ) bearing an N-2,6-diisopropylphenyl group was also included as a reference structure in Table . Following an extended recrystallization process, both Ni1 and Ni3 adopt a stable dimeric configuration, featuring a binuclear core of the type Ni2Br2(OH2)2(μ2-Br)2, in which the μ2-bridging bromide ligands (Br1) connect two adjacent metals. Additionally, monodentate bromide (Br2) and aqua (OH2) ligands coordinate to each nickel atom, completing the coordination environment. The coordination sphere is further stabilized by four nitrogen atoms from two neutral bidentate chelating ligands and a water molecule. This structural motif is consistent with previously reported α-diimine nickel complexes employed in ethylene polymerization. , The bite angles (N1–Ni–N2) are nearly identical for both complexes [79.48(10)° for Ni1 and 79.28(8)° for Ni3] indicating minimal distortion in the chelate ring. In contrast, the N(1)–Ni(1)–Br(1) angles are more open (169.21(8)° for Ni1 and 168.90(6)° for Ni3), suggesting greater flexibility in the axial coordination environment. In the case of the A iPr complex, a distorted four-coordinate tetrahedral geometry was observed, with no intermolecular bridging interactions detected. In all three structures, the observed Ni–N bond lengths are comparable to those reported for related α-diiminonickel complexes. , Due to the unsymmetric nature of Ni1 and Ni3, the N1–Ni bond lengths are slightly longer than N2–Ni bond lengths in these two nickel complexes [Ni1: 2.145(2) vs. 2.113(3) Å; Ni3: 2.163(2) vs. 2.144(2) Å]. This discrepancy is likely attributed to the steric hindrance imposed by the bulky N-6-benzhydryl-2,4-dichlorophenyl moiety at the N1 position. In contrast, the N1–Ni and N2–Ni bond lengths are nearly identical [2.014 vs. 2.027 Å] in the symmetric A iPr , again suggesting that the introduction of the bulky benzhydryl group exerts a steric influence on the N-aryl moiety. The Ni–Br bond distances are consistent between two complexes in the current work, with values of 2.5315(5), 25660(7) Å for Ni1 and 2.5541(5), 2.5072(5) Å for Ni3. Notably, the Ni–Br bond length in the monomeric A iPr complex is significantly shorter than those in the Ni1 and Ni3 complexes, likely due to the steric hindrance imposed by their dimeric structures. However, all of these distances are typical for such functional groups and indicate robust halide coordination. Moreover, the phenyl ring of N-6-benzhydryl-2,4-dichlorophenyl moiety adopts a near-perpendicular orientation relative to the coordination plane C1–N1–­Ni–­N2–C2, with dihedral angle of 79.48° for Ni1 and 79.28° for Ni3. Furthermore, the relatively large dihedral angle of 84.28° observed in the A iPr complex suggests that the nickel center is less sterically constrained by the small N-aryl moiety. This conformation is analogous to that observed in other nickel complexes bearing unsymmetrical α-diimine ligands. ,, There are no intermolecular contacts of note.

Notably, when comparing the results of X-ray diffraction and elemental analysis, inconsistencies in crystal structure and elemental composition may stem from the distinct states of nickel complexes in their solid form versus when dissolved in solution over extended periods. Specifically, the solvents employed to dissolve the nickel complexes and facilitate crystal growth in an ambient, moisture-laden environment induced the molecules to adopt a more stable configuration, such as a dimeric arrangement present in the current work, whereas powder samples exhibited long-term stability in air, yielding a structure that precisely conforms to the molecular formula.

2.2. Ethylene Polymerization

Previous studies have systematically established that polymerization activity and resultant polymer properties are critically dependent on reaction parameters, including the type and loading of cocatalysts (e.g., Al/Ni molar ratio), polymerization temperature, reaction duration, and ethylene pressure. − Building on these findings, we conducted comprehensive optimization of reaction conditions using all nickel complexes to maximize polymerization performance and elucidate the ligand structure–activity relationship. Notably, direct benchmarking was performed against a series of previously reported α-diiminenickel precatalysts ,,,, to validate our experimental design.

2.2.1. Activator Screening for Ethylene Polymerizatin

The initial research phase focused on identifying the optimal activator for ethylene polymerization. This systematic evaluation aimed to optimize cocatalyst selection and investigate the influence of cocatalyst identity on the microstructural features of the resulting polyethylenes. − The key outcomes of this screening study are summarized in Table .

All five Ni1/cocatalyst combinations yielded active catalytic systems, producing moderately branched polyethylenes with high molecular weights (>105 g mol–1) and narrow polydispersity indices (PDI < 2.7). Notably, alkylaluminoxane cocatalysts demonstrated minimal influence on both catalytic activity and the resulting polyethylene properties, indicating their structural variations (e.g., MAO vs. MMAO) do not significantly perturb the activation or propagation steps. In contrast, alkylaluminum chloride cocatalysts exerted a pronounced impact on polymerization performance, particularly through differential modulation of the activation kinetics and chain-transfer mechanisms. Specifically, the Ni1/EASC system (run 3, Table ) exhibited superior catalytic activity compared to other systems prompting its selection for further optimizing investigations (Figure S18).

2.2.2. Catalytic Evaluation of Ni1/EASC Catalytic System

The quantity of cocatalyst also plays a pivotal role in influencing catalytic performance with both insufficient and excessive amounts of the cocatalyst detrimental to the catalytic activity, as evidenced by prior research. , Therefore, an investigation into the optimal usage of EASC for ethylene polymerization was conducted at 30 °C by varying the Al/Ni ratio within the range of 200 to 400 (runs 1–5, Table ). The catalytic activity demonstrated a trend where a characteristic increased and subsequently decreased as the cocatalyst loading was elevated from 200 to 600 (Figure S19), consistent with the typical behavior of the α-diimine nickel precatalyst. Specifically, the activity of the Ni1/EASC system rose from 6.7 × 106 g (PE) mol–1 (Ni) h–1 when the Al:Ni ratio was 200, reaching a peak of 9.8 × 106 g (PE) mol–1 (Ni) h–1 at an Al:Ni ratio of 400, before declining to 8.1 × 106 g (PE) mol–1 (Ni) h–1 when the Al:Ni ratio was further increased to 600. Generally, the molecular weight of the resulting polyethylene exhibited a downward trend when increasing the aluminum regent amount and reached a minimum of 116.9 kg mol–1 at an Al:Ni ratio of 600 (run 5, Table ). The observed reduction in both activity and the molecular weight of polyethylene with the increase of cocatalyst consumption can be attributed to excessive chain transfer reactions that occur at higher cocatalyst concentrations. , In contrast, no discernible trends were observed with respect to the molecular weight distribution (Đ = 2.0–2.9), the melting point of polyethylenes (T m > 119 °C), as well as the crystallinity (X c > 40%), indicating that these properties were relatively insensitive to variations in the cocatalyst loading within the studied range.

When compared to the influence of the cocatalyst amount, temperature emerged as a more pronounced factor affecting the catalytic behavior of nickel complexes (Figure ). Examining the data presented in Table (runs 3 and 6–11, Table ), it becomes evident that the catalytic activity exhibited a distinct trend with respect to the polymerization temperature. Initially, as the temperature rose from 30 to 50 °C, the catalytic activity surged from 9.8 × 106 g (PE) mol–1 (Ni) h–1 to 12.2 × 106 g (PE) mol–1 (Ni) h–1. However, as the polymerization temperature continued to escalate to 90 °C, the activity gradually declined, although it still maintained a respectable value of 4.7 × 106 g (PE) mol–1 (Ni) h–1. This phenomenon can potentially be attributed to the decreasing solubility of ethylene in the reaction medium at higher temperatures which may hinder the availability of ethylene monomers for the nickel species and/or the partial deactivation of active species involved in the catalytic process at elevated temperatures, thereby reducing their catalytic efficiency. ,, Similar findings have been documented in previous studies involving asymmetric α-diimine nickel catalysts, lending further credence to this explanation. , Notably, within the temperature range of 30 to 70 °C, the catalytic activity remained remarkably stable, fluctuating around a value of over 9 × 106 g (PE) mol–1 (Ni) h–1 highlighting the good thermal stability of current Ni/EASC complexes and setting them apart from conventional thermally unstable α-diimine nickel analogues. In the case of these less stable counterparts, the optimal operating temperature was typically around 30 °C, and a sharp decline in activity was often observed when the temperature was increased. ,, When comparing the current α-diimine nickel complexes, which contained the N-6-diphenylmethyl-2,4-chlorophenyl moiety, with N-2-chloro-4,6-diphenylmethylphenyl-containing nickel analogues, the former demonstrated better activity underscoring the effective synergistic effects of ortho- and para-chloro in enhancing the thermal stability of the current nickel complexes. Furthermore, high-temperature runs were found to favor the formation of low-molecular-weight polyethylene. Specifically, as the temperature increased from 30 to 90 °C, the molecular weight of the polyethylenes decreased 6-fold, from 149.2 kg mol–1 to 24.5 kg mol–1. This can likely be explained by the fact that the higher reaction temperature promotes the easier chain termination reactions, thereby limiting the growth of the polymer chains. ,, Additionally, since the higher reaction temperature also favored the chain-walking mechanism, which allowed for the migration of the growing polymer chain along the nickel center forming branches, the melting point and crystallinity of the polyethylenes decreased as the temperature increased.

To investigate the long-term stability of the complex Ni1, we conducted polymerization experiments over varying time intervals, ranging from 5 to 60 min (runs 7, 12–14, Table ). Remarkably, the highest activity of 37.9 × 106 g (PE) mol–1 (Ni) h–1 was achieved when the polymerization time was set to 5 min. This activity was 1.75 times greater than that observed at a 15 min polymerization time (runs 12 and 13, Table ). As the polymerization time progressed, the polymer yields steadily increased, leading to the formation of a viscous reaction medium. The increased viscosity reduced the solubility of ethylene in the reaction mixture, limiting the availability of ethylene monomers for the reaction and hindering the coordination and insertion rates of ethylene molecules into the active catalytic species. , Additionally, the partial decomposition of the active species over time was also considered a significant factor contributing to the decline in catalytic activity. Although the activity of ethylene polymerization gradually decreased to 7.1 × 106 g mol–1 h–1 at the 60 min mark, several positive indicators underscored the sustained catalytic capability of the nickel complexes including a progressively increasing yield of polyethylene, a steady rise in polyethylene molecular weight, and a consistently narrow molecular weight distribution (Figure S20). Moreover, both the catalytic activity of Ni1/EASC and the molecular weight of the polyethylenes were highly sensitive to changes in ethylene pressure (runs 7, 15, and 16, Table ). As anticipated, the polymerization activity exhibited a clear positive correlation with the ethylene pressure. When the pressure was increased from 1 to 5 atm, the activity increased by a factor of 1.5. A further increase in the pressure to 10 atm resulted in a 1.74-fold increase in activity. This enhancement can be attributed to the improved mass transfer of ethylene within the polymerization solvent at higher ethylene concentrations, facilitating the polymerization reaction. A similar trend was observed for the molecular weight of the polyethylenes with higher ethylene pressures leading to the production of polyethylenes with higher molecular weights reflecting that the reaction conditions, particularly the ethylene pressure, play a crucial role in determining both the catalytic efficiency and the properties of the final polymer product.

2.2.3. Structural Effects of Ligands on Ethylene Polymerization

After a meticulous series of experiments aimed at optimizing the reaction conditions, the optimal parameters were determined: an Al/Ni molar ratio of 400, a reaction temperature of 50 °C, and a reaction duration of 30 min. With these optimal conditions, a systematic investigation into the catalytic performance of all ortho- and para-chloro substituted nickel precatalysts were conducted (Table ). Among them, Ni1[2,6-di­(Me)] exhibited the highest catalytic activity up to 12.2 × 106 g (PE) mol–1 (Ni) h–1. This notable activity difference among the precatalysts was not a random occurrence; rather, it was closely linked to the steric properties of the ortho-substituents on the nickel complexes. As a matter of fact, the overall activity order of these nickel precatalysts followed a distinct pattern: Ni1 [2,6-di­(Me)] > Ni4 [2,4,6-tri­(Me)] > Ni5 [2,6-di­(Et)-4-Me] > Ni2 [2,6-di­(Et)] > Ni3 [2,6-di­(i-Pr)] > Ni6 (Figure S21). Similarly, the molecular weight of polyethylenes was also affected by the ortho-substituent type with symmetric nickel complexes bearing the bulkiest ortho-isopropyl substituted giving polyethylene with the highest molecular weight among Ni1-Ni5. Meanwhile, delving deeper into the structural aspects, it is worth noting that unsymmetrical 1,2-diiminoacenaphthylenes, which feature bulky benzhydryl groups on their N-aryl groups, have a particular preference to combine with the second N-aryl moiety that has less steric hindrance. This preference is crucial, as it facilitates better ethylene coordination and insertion, which are key steps in the catalytic process. The consistently low activity and high molecular weight of the resulting polyethylenes observed in the symmetric Ni6 case strongly reinforce the aforementioned conclusion. Meanwhile, the presence of a para-methyl group positively affected the catalytic activities of their nickel complexes probably due to the improved solubility and better dispersion of the catalyst within the reaction medium. In addition to the catalytic activities, the molecular weight distribution of the polyethylene produced using all these nickel precatalysts was unimodal and relatively narrow, with values ranging from 1.8 to 2.0. This unimodal molecular weight distribution strongly indicates the presence of a single active species, suggesting a highly consistent catalytic process.

2.2.4. Structural Properties of the Polyethylenes

One of the most notable features of α-diimine nickel complexes lies in their remarkable ability to produce polyethylenes with a substantial number of branched chains through the chain-walking mechanism. ,, To gain a comprehensive understanding of the polyethylene microstructure, three representative polyethylene samples were subjected to high-temperature 13C NMR analysis. The experiments were conducted at 120 °C using 1,1,2,2-tetrachloroethane-d2 as the deuterated reagent for the high-temperature tests. Therefore, experimental time and temperature must be strictly controlled to minimize the impact of this side reaction. The branching degree of the polyethylenes was then calculated using a well-established literature method. The high-temperature 13C NMR spectra of polyethylenes obtained at 50 °C, 80, and 90 °C are shown in Figure S23, Figure , and Figure S24, respectively. A detailed summary of the branching analysis for these three samples is provided in Table . Upon a thorough examination of the branching data of the polyethylenes, a clear pattern emerges that the branching degree and long branch content are proportional to the polymerization temperature. 2.3. Comparison with Reported 1,2-(Arylimino)­acenaphthene-Nickel Complexes

To elucidate the catalytic advantages of contemporary nickel complexes, several previously reported unsymmetrical bis­(arylimino)­acenaphthylene-nickel complexes bearing halogen and benzhydryl groups (A, B Cl , E F , E CHPh2 , Figure ), ,,, along with their structurally analogous counterpart (C Me , Figure ) were selected. A comparative analysis was conducted to evaluate their catalytic activity and molecular weight of polyethylenes (Figure ). Structural and electronic modifications to Brookhart-type nickel complexes generally resulted in a significant enhancement of the catalytic activity, underscoring the beneficial effects of such structural adjustments. Specifically, the incorporation of electron-withdrawing groups could significantly enhance the catalytic activity. Except for the ortho-methyl-substituted C Me complex, other nickel complexes containing electron-withdrawing groups, such as chloro substituents, exhibited superior activities in ethylene polymerization than Brookhart-type nickel complexes (A, Figure ). This conclusion is robustly corroborated by computational results, which suggest that the introduction of electron-withdrawing groups effectively increases the net charge of the central nickel atom, thereby facilitating the chain propagation process. Furthermore, the strategic incorporation of bulky benzhydryl substituents into the N-aryl moieties enhanced catalytic performance by effectively protecting the active catalytic species (A vs E F , E CHPh2 , and F, Figure ). However, the number of ortho-benzhydryl substituents exerted a pronounced influence on the catalytic activity and thermal stability of the nickel complexes. Typically, nickel complexes bearing two ortho-benzhydryl groups exhibited the lowest activity and the lowest optimum operating temperature, indicating that an appropriate steric environment around the nickel center is crucial for enhancing monomer reactivity and stabilizing the active catalytic species (B Cl vs. E F , E CHPh2 , and F, Figure ). Additionally, reducing the steric hindrance of the N-aryl groups in ortho-chloro-substituted nickel complexes led to the formation of polyethylenes with notably higher molecular weights (E CHPh2 vs. E F and F, Figure ). This observation suggests that the para-benzhydryl substituents provided less steric protection to the nickel center, thereby exerting a minimal influence on the chain propagation step during polymerization. Notably, the catalytic activities of these complexes remained comparable (E CHPh2 vs. E F and F, Figure ), suggesting that a moderately crowded environment around the nickel center is advantageous for chain propagation.

In summary, the modulation of electronic and steric attributes through the strategic deployment of cholro- substituents and benzhydryl groups could remarkably enhance both the catalytic performance and polymer properties by the synergistic effect of steric shielding and electronic enhancement at the nickel center. Furthermore, the synthesized polyethylenes exhibited a notably low-branching degree (57/1000 C) and high crystallinity (Xc = 62.6%), indicating a preference for chain propagation over chain walking in the current ortho/para-chlorinated α-diimine nickel catalytic system.

3.1. General Procedure

All air- and moisture-sensitive chemicals were meticulously handled, following standard Schlenk techniques within a nitrogen-purged environment to ensure chemical integrity. MAO (1.5 M toluene solution) and MMAO (2.5 M n-heptane solution) were sourced from Anhui Botai Electronic Materials Co., Ltd. Meanwhile, Yantai Lianli Chemical Co., Ltd. provided ethyl sesquichloride (EASC, 1.0 M toluene solution), diethyl aluminum chloride (Et2AlCl, 1.0 M toluene solution), and ethyl aluminum dichloride (EtAlCl2, 1.0 M toluene solution). High-purity ethylene, purchased from Jiangsu Hongren Special Gases Co., Ltd., was utilized without further purification. 1H and 13C NMR spectroscopy of all ligands were performed at ambient temperature using a Bruker DMX 400 MHz spectrometer, with tetramethylsilane (TMS) serving as the internal reference standard. Fourier-transform infrared (FT-IR) spectra of all ligands and nickel complexes were acquired using a PerkinElmer System 2000 spectrometer, while elemental analysis was performed using a Flash EA 1112 microanalyzer. The molecular weight (M w) and polydispersity index (M w/M n) of polyethylene samples were determined via gel permeation chromatography (GPC) using a PL-GPC220 instrument at 160 °C, with 1,2,4-trichlorobenzene as the eluent. The melting point of polyethylene was assessed by using a TA Instruments DSC Q2000 differential scanning calorimeter at a heating rate of 10 °C/min during a second heating cycle to eliminate thermal history effects. High-temperature 13C NMR spectra of polymer samples PE50-Ni1, PE80-Ni1, and PE90-Ni1 were recorded in 1,1,2,2-tetrachloroethane-d2 on a Bruker AVANCE III 500 MHz spectrometer, with TMS as the internal standard. The acquisition parameters were optimized as follows: spectral width = 31.250 kHz, acquisition time = 0.52 s, relaxation delay = 2.0 s, and 1024 scans to ensure an adequate signal-to-noise ratio. Tensile stress–strain tests were performed at room temperature using a universal tester, stretching rectangular specimens (5 × 1 × 0.1 cm) at a stretching rate of 50 mm/min until fracture. Each sample was tested in quintuplicate to ensure statistical reliability. Stress–strain recovery behavior was evaluated using dynamic mechanical analysis (DMA) and a universal tensile tester to characterize elastic properties under cyclic loading conditions.

3.4. General Consideration for Ethylene Polymerization

3.4.1. Ethylene Polymerization Conducted at 5/10 atm

Ethylene polymerization reactions were conducted in a 250 mL stainless-steel high-pressure autoclave, which was outfitted with a mechanical stirrer and a temperature-regulated jacket to ensure precise control over reaction conditions. The reactor was initially loaded with 25 mL of freshly distilled toluene under an inert nitrogen ethylene atmosphere. After the system reached thermal equilibrium at the desired reaction temperature, it was purged with ethylene to eliminate residual oxygen and moisture. Once the system attained thermal equilibrium at the targeted reaction temperature, a 50 mL toluene solution containing the precatalyst (2 μmol) was rapidly injected, followed by the addition of a predetermined amount of cocatalyst to form the active polymerization catalyst system. To achieve the final reaction volume of 100 mL, an additional 25 mL of toluene was carefully added. Polymerization was initiated by agitating the reaction mixture at a stirring rate of 400 rpm for a predetermined reaction duration, ensuring uniform dispersion of the catalyst and monomer. Upon completion, the reactor was depressurized, and the polymerization reaction was promptly terminated by the addition of acidified ethanol (5% (v/v) HCl in ethanol), which deactivated the catalyst and precipitated the polymer. The crude polymer was isolated by vacuum filtration, rigorously washed with ethanol to remove residual impurities, and then dried under reduced pressure at 50 °C to a constant weight. The final polymer yield was determined gravimetrically.

3.4.2. Ethylene Polymerization Conducted at 1 atm

A 250 mL Schlenk vessel, fitted with a magnetic stir bar, was loaded with a precisely measured quantity of a nickel precatalyst. Subsequently, the vessel was evacuated and purged by first introducing nitrogen gas twice, followed by a single purge with ethylene gas. Next, 30 mL of toluene was added to the vessel along with the specified amount of an aluminum-based activator. The polymerization reaction was initiated by setting the ethylene pressure to 1 atm and maintaining the temperature at 50 °C. After a reaction period of 30 min, the ethylene supply was halted, and the residual ethylene pressure within the vessel was carefully vented. The resulting reaction mixture was then quenched by the addition of a solution of 15% hydrochloric acid in ethanol. The polymer formed was separated from the reaction mixture by filtration and subsequently dried at 50 °C overnight. Finally, the dried polymer was weighed to determine the yield.

4. Conclusions

A series of 1-(6-benzhydryl-2,4-dichlorophenylimino)-2-aryliminoacenaphthyl ligands and their corresponding nickel bromides were synthesized and fully characterized through a combination of analytical techniques, including FT-IR spectroscopy, elemental analysis, NMR spectroscopy, and X-ray single-crystal diffraction. Upon activation with EASC, all nickel precatalysts demonstrated high activity up to 12.2 × 106 g (PE) mol–1 (Ni) h–1 in ethylene polymerization at a low Al/Ni ratio of 400. Notably, the catalysts exhibited minimal fluctuations in activity across a broad temperature range from 40 to 70 °C and maintained a high activity level of 4.8 × 106 g (PE) mol–1 (Ni) h–1 even at 90 °C highlighting the good thermal stability of the current nickel system. The active and thermally stable characteristics of these catalysts can be attributed to the electron-withdrawing ortho- and para-chloro substituents, which effectively stabilize the nickel center and modulate the coordination environment of the active species. This modulation facilitates a well-controlled chain-walking process during polymerization, leading to the formation of polyethylenes with high crystallinity, minimal long-chain branching, narrow polydispersity indices (M w/M n = 1.8–2.9), and moderate molecular weights on the order of tens of thousands. Mechanical property tests revealed that the polyethylenes synthesized using ethylene as the sole monomer in this study exhibited thermoplastic elastomer (TPE)-like behavior with high tensile strength (13.1 MPa), exceptional elongation at break (1030.6%), and notable elastic recovery (65%), encouraging further advancements in catalyst design and the industrial production of high-performance polyolefin materials.
