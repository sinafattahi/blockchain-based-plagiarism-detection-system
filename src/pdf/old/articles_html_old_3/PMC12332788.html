Abstract

Here is described a theoretical and experimental study of regioselective [4 + 2] Diels–Alder cycloaddition reactions between electron-rich dienes and SF5-alkynes. These methods give straightforward and convergent access to SF5-phenols and aminophenols in short reaction sequences. Density functional theory (DFT) calculations combined with reactivity tools, activation strain model, and energy decomposition analysis provide a deeper mechanistic understanding of these Diels–Alder cycloaddition reactions involving an alkyne as a dienophile. We found that regioselectivity and reactivity originate from less destabilizing strain energy and reduced Pauli repulsion between occupied π-orbitals of the diene and dienophile, rather than from stabilizing highest occupied molecular orbital–lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (HOMO–LUMO) interactions. This can be ascribed to a higher degree of asynchronicity in the transition state of the privileged attack of the diene on the dienophile.

Keywords: SF5 , aminophenol, Diels–Alder cycloaddition, SF5-alkynes, DFT

1. Introduction

For decades, the preparation of functionalized SF5-containing molecules was highly challenging, and many efforts have been made since to simplify and shorten their synthesis. − Traditionally, aromatic molecules bearing an SF5 group are mainly accessible by oxidative fluorination of aryl disulfides, -thiols, or sulfur derivatives following either the direct fluorination approach or the stepwise oxidative fluorination in which arylsulfur chlorotetrafluoride Ar–SF4Cl intermediate undergoes a Cl–F exchange. − However, these approaches require a multistep reaction sequence and suffer from moisture-sensitive reaction conditions, limited substrate scope, and poor functional group tolerance due to harsh oxidizing conditions. Herein, we have investigated the synthesis of SF5-phenols and aminophenols which are functionalized SF5-building blocks for applications in drug design and material science. For instance, a 3-SF5-phenol fragment is found in meta-SF5 stavudine (d4T) ProTide (Scheme a), a drug candidate that showed good antiviral activity and high selectivity index as an anti-HIV reverse transcriptase inhibitor. Moreover, preliminary studies showed that SF5-2-aminophenols could be biodegraded by bacteria present in soil (Pseudomonas spp.). , In the literature, the synthesis of SF5-phenols usually requires a linear multistep sequence. In 2011, Beier’s group reported effective strategies to convert 3- and 4-SF5-nitrobenzenes 1–2 into SF5-phenols (5–6), by a nucleophilic aromatic substitution (SNAr) with sodium methoxide (3–4), followed by deprotection with a thiol (Scheme b). Then, they reported the selective hydroxylation of 1 and 2 with cumene hydroperoxide, delivering nitrophenols 7 and 8 in good yields. These compounds were further converted into ortho-aminophenols 10 and 11, or even into the catechol derivative 12. In 2022, Daugulis group developed a complementary strategy using the SF5 motif as a directing group to perform ortho-lithiation/silylation, leading to 14 which can be converted into phenol 15 using KF/H2O2 (Scheme c).

The method we propose herein is complementary to the strategies depicted in Scheme b,c, which have the drawback of starting from aryl-SF5. An alternative and unexplored strategy would be to construct the aromatic cycle simultaneously with the installation of functional groups. Initially, we were inspired by analogous Diels–Alder (DA) cycloaddition with CF3-alkynes , and more specifically by the seminal work of Hiyama (Scheme d) who reported Diels–Alder cycloaddition between Danishefsky’s diene 16 and CF3-alkynes 17. The reaction is performed neat at 150 °C producing 3-CF3-phenols 18 in yields ranging from 53 to 93%. Surprisingly, the regioselectivity of this transformation was not discussed, nor was the electronic mismatch assembly observed between the diene and the dienophile. Curiously, the synthesis of SF5-aromatic compounds using Diels–Alder cycloaddition reactions has been poorly investigated with SF5-alkynes − or other SF5-precursors. − SF5-Alkynes are readily available substrates, , gathering benefits of being polarized by the electron-withdrawing SF5 group and having a lower LUMO orbital compared with parent CF3- or ArSF4-alkynes. − Herein, we hypothesized that SF5-alkynes should be good dienophiles for normal electron demand Diels–Alder [4 + 2] cycloaddition reaction with electron-rich dienes (Scheme e). Thus, with a wise selection of the diene, it would be possible to access functionalized SF5-phenols in a convergent manner. Moreover, high regioselectivity is expected due to both the bulkiness of the SF5 and the polarization of the alkyne. A full mechanistic study supported by density functional theory (DFT) calculations, activation strain model (ASM) and energy decomposition (EDA) analyses is presented explaining an uncommon electronic mismatch of the Diels–Alder cycloaddition.

2. Results and Discussion

2.1. Optimization and Scope

We started the investigation with the [4 + 2] cycloaddition reaction of ([1,1′-biphenyl]-4-ylethynyl)­pentafluoro-λ6-sulfane 20a as model substrate with tert-butyl furan-2-ylcarbamate 19a under microwave irradiation, both prepared in our hands according to reported procedures. , Several conditions have been tested by changing the quantity of diene, the solvent, reaction time, and temperature (Table ). Performing the reaction in 1,2-dichlorobenzene for 40 min at 170 °C with 1.5 equiv of the diene 19a, the 2-SF5-4-aminophenol 21a was obtained in 78% 19F NMR yield (entry 1). The NMR yield was improved up to 96% using more diene (entry 2), but it dropped to 27% by decreasing the reaction temperature to 100 °C even with a longer heating time (entry 3). Replacing the 1,2-dichlorobenzene with nitrobenzene was detrimental to the conversion (entry 4). Finally, the optimal reaction conditions were obtained using 2.2 equiv of diene in chlorobenzene at 170 °C for 30 min, delivering 21a in 96% NMR yield and 76% isolated yield after purification over silica gel column chromatography. With optimal conditions in hand, the scope of the reaction with different alkynes was evaluated (Scheme ). In all cases, the cycloaddition reaction proved to be highly regioselective for the 2-SF5-4-aminophenol 21, while the other regioisomer (3-SF5-4-aminophenol) 22 was only obtained as traces in some cases. The structure of 21 was unambiguously confirmed by NMR studies and X-ray diffraction of deprotected aminophenol 30a (see Scheme ). Indeed, the reaction proceeded successfully with aromatic SF5-alkynes either substituted with electron-withdrawing or electron-donating substituents delivering the products 21a–l in yields ranging from 57 to 99%. Heteroaromatic substrates such as 1-tosyl-1H-indole 20m and 7-aza-indazole 20n are also well tolerated affording the phenols 21m and 21n in 77 and 57% yield, respectively. Alkyne substituted with a pyridine ring 20o was poorly reactive, and only traces of the product were obtained in this case. An unprecedented SF5-thioalkyne 20p was designed especially for this project as a new heterosubstituted SF5-alkyne and it proved to be a competent reaction partner yielding 21p in 43% after purification, even if the conversion was complete. The SF5-enynes 20q–r showed good reactivity yielding 21q–r in nearly 50% isolated yield. In contrast, a limitation was observed with aliphatic and triethylsilyl SF5-alkynes 20s–u which showed no conversion but also no signs of decomposition.

We then decided to extend the scope of the cycloaddition reaction with different dienes (19b–g, Scheme a) with the alkyne 20a used as the model substrate under the optimized reaction conditions mentioned above (Scheme b). The presence of a heteroatom (N, O) on position 2 of furan was crucial to achieve the opening of the intermediate cycloadduct, which leads to aromatization.

Commercially available 2-methoxyfuran 19b was first tested and the reaction was found to be optimal using 5 equiv of 19b to achieve a complete conversion in 10 min at 170 °C, delivering the SF5-methoxyphenol 23 in 76% isolated yield. No reaction took place with tert-butyl oxazol-2-ylcarbamate 19c, in contrast with tert-butyl oxazol-5-ylcarbamate 19d which did not deliver the SF5-pyridinol 26 but instead the 4-SF5-2-aminofuran 25 in 64% NMR yield and 31% isolated yield. The structure of 25 (CCDC 2429118) , was unambiguously determined by single-crystal X-ray diffraction (SCXRD). In this case, a competitive retro-Diels–Alder reaction occurred preferentially with the elimination of HCN (purple arrows pathway, Scheme c), instead of the ring opening and aromatization (red arrows pathway). This is in good agreement with the retro-Diels–Alder reactivity of 4-phenyloxazole previously reported by Dolbier in 2006. The Danishefsky’s diene 19e proved to be highly unstable under thermal conditions, but the corresponding 3-SF5-phenol 27a was obtained in 23% isolated yield. The cycloaddition with ((5,5-dimethylcyclohex-1,3-diene-1,3-diyl)­bis­(oxy))­bis­(trimethylsilane) 19f, prepared from dimedone, gave the product 28 in 25% isolated yield after 1 h under MW irradiation at 170 °C.

Inspired by the work of Moody, we prepared (E)-3-((tert-butyldimethylsilyl)­oxy)­but-3-en-2-one O-(tert-butyldimethylsilyl)­oxime 19g starting from 2,3-butanedione to obtain the corresponding free oxime which was finally protected using TBDMSOTf in basic medium (see SI for details). A first trial under MW irradiation gave pyridine 29 in 28% NMR yield along with 20a and other unidentified products. Performing the reaction with heating in an oil bath at 150 °C, with 14.4 equiv of the diene 19g added portionwise (2 × 2.2 then 10 equiv) for 48 h, resulted in the complete consumption of the alkyne and afforded 29 in 39% NMR yield. However, the pyridine 29 showed poor stability over silica gel, which resulted in a very poor isolated yield of 4%.

2.2. DFT Calculations

To rationalize the electronic mismatch of the [4 + 2] Diels–Alder (DA) cycloaddition reactions between electron-rich dienes 19a or 19e and SF5-alkyne and gain insight into the origin of the high regioselectivity, formation of 2-SF5-phenol 21c for the cyclic diene 19a and 3-SF5-phenol 27c (via cycloadduct 27c′) for the Danishefsky’s acyclic diene 19e (Scheme ), we carried out density functional theory (DFT) calculations (see SI for computational details) at PBE0-D3­(BJ)/def2-SVP level of theory. We used PhCCSF5 alkyne 20c as a model. To conduct an in-depth analysis of the origin of the regioselectivity observed experimentally in 19a, we combined the activation strain model (ASM) and energy decomposition (EDA) analyses.

Interestingly, the regioselectivity of the Diels–Alder reactions appears to exhibit a trend that contrasts with the polarization of the SF5-alkyne. The NPA charges (Figure ) indicate that the carbon bearing the SF5 group in model alkyne 20c has a negative charge of −0.29, while the β-carbon carries a positive charge of +0.09. For diene 19a, the most electropositive carbon is the one bearing the −NHBoc group (+0.52 vs +0.09 for the other carbon). For diene 19e, the terminal CH2 group has a negative charge (−0.56) while the CHOMe moiety shows a positive charge (+0.18). These findings reveal that the observed regioselectivity contradicts what would be expected based on the polarization of the reactants and is not charge-controlled, as expected for Diels–Alder reactions.

As a first approximation, we examined the molecular orbitals (MOs) of the reactants in this normal electron demand DA reaction (see Figures and S1), particularly HOMO of the dienes and LUMO of dienophile. We found that for the two carbons involved in the [4 + 2] cycloaddition, the highest coefficient is located on the less sterically hindered carbon: the carbon in α to the oxygen, which lacks the amino group in 19a and the carbon of the CH2 moiety in 19e (Figure ). This carbon seems to preferentially attack the carbon bearing the SF5 group in the alkyne (the largest coefficient of the carbon involved in the triple bond in LUMO), forming the preferred regioisomer, in line with Diels–Alder reaction being under orbital control. The calculation of the Fukui indices f – for the two dienes 19a and 19e and f + for dienophile 20c also supports the observed regioselectivity, with the highest f – values found at the two carbons showing the largest coefficients in HOMO and the highest f + value in 20c computed at the carbon α to the SF5 group (Table S1, SI).

To elucidate the cause behind the preferential formation of the 2-SF5 phenol when the diene 19a is involved, we computed the energy profiles of the two approaches of the diene toward the triple bond of the SF5-alkyne 20c (R = Ph) and 20v (R = Et) using DFT. We focused on the [4 + 2] cycloaddition step, which is the regioselective step. As depicted in the reaction profile in Figure , the transition state (TS) associated with the Diels–Alder reaction leading to the 2-SF5-phenol 21c (TS1-Ph, pathway I) is significantly lower in energy (24.8 kcal/mol) compared with that for 3-SF5-phenol 22c (TS2-Ph, pathway II, ΔG ‡ = 30.0 kcal/mol), with ΔΔG ‡ of 5.2 kcal/mol, which aligns with the experimentally observed reactivity. The formation of the cycloadduct product is thermodynamically favored, with a [4 + 2] cycloaddition that is more exergonic in pathway I than in pathway II (ΔG: −5.8 kcal/mol for INT1-Ph and ΔG: −1.7 kcal/mol for INT2-Ph). The overall process is strongly exergonic with ΔG > −50 kcal/mol, and 21c is 2.1 kcal/mol lower in energy than 22c. These results indicate that the [4 + 2] cycloaddition takes place under kinetic control rather than thermodynamic control. Analysis of the geometrical features of the transition state (TS) showed that the privileged TS (TS1-Ph) is significantly more asynchronous than TS2-Ph, with bond-forming C···C distances in TS1-Ph of 2.008 and 2.690 Å, whereas in TS2-Ph these distances are 2.202 and 2.315 Å (Figure ).

We also computed the energy profile for R = ethyl (20v) instead of phenyl (20c) (Figure , red pathway). We found that the activation barrier, leading to the 2-SF5-phenol 21v, increases from 24.8 (TS1-Ph) to 29.9 kcal/mol for TS1-Et. This is in agreement with experimental observations, where the reaction is ineffective with alkyl groups (21s–t, Scheme ). Interestingly, the transition state with the lowest activation barrier also exhibits in this case a high degree of asynchronicity, though less pronounced than with R = Ph (bond-forming C···C distances of 2.127 and 2.434 Å for TS1-Et). These results suggest that this degree of asynchronicity of the C···C bonds appears to be correlated with the value of the activation barriers (lowest TS, highest asynchronicity). To assess whether this trend can be generalized, we extended our investigation by computing the energy profiles for the acyclic Danishefsky’s diene 19e. Figure presents the optimized geometries of all of the computed transition states for the three reactions, including the forming C···C bond distances and their corresponding activation free energies. In this case, it is noteworthy that more accessible activation was found for the formation of 3-SF5-phenol, in line with experimental observations (ΔG ‡ = 22.2 kcal/mol, TS2-acyc-Ph). Here again, TS2-acyc-Ph (lowest TS) is also very asymmetrical, with bond-forming C···C distances of 2.336 and 3.000 Å.

Based on these observations, we computed the synchronicity (S y ) of the cycloaddition reactions using a previously reported method. The corresponding values are shown in Figure , where a higher value of S y corresponds to higher synchronicity. Interestingly, we observed a good linear correlation (R 2 = 0.93) between the activation barriers and the reaction synchronicity (Figure and Table S2). This suggests that the S y parameter acts as an indicator of both the feasibility of the process and the regioselectivity, as previously observed by Bickelhaupt et al. in Diels–Alder reactions between 1,3-butadiene and acrolein activated by different Lewis acids (AlCl3, Li+, and H+). ,

To go further and identify the factor(s) controlling the activation barrier heights for the formation of the 2-SF5 or 3-SF5-phenols in the [4 + 2] cycloaddition reaction involving diene 19a and the SF5-alkyne 20c, we applied the activation strain model (ASM) − of chemical reactivity along the entire reaction coordinate, selected as the shortest C···C bond-forming distance. As illustrated in Figure , the pathway associated with the highest TS (TS2-Ph) and lower asynchronicity involves significantly stronger stabilizing interactions between the reactants. This is reflected in the interaction energy term (ΔE int), particularly within the C···C distance range of 2.8–2.2 Å. However, in this same region, the strain energy term (ΔE strain), which quantifies the required energy to deform the reactants during a chemical reaction, is lower for the privileged pathway (TS1-Ph, more asynchronous TS), indicating that this term is the only factor governing the observed reactivity. It is noteworthy that such a correlation between the strain energy, ΔE strain, and the degree of asynchronicity has been recently observed in iminium-catalyzed Diels–Alder reactions, in Diels–Alder reactions between 1,3-butadiene and acrylaldehyde, catalyzed by different Lewis acids, as well as in Diels–Alder reactions with furans as dienes. ,

Our ASM analysis reveals that, although the interaction energy is less favorable for pathway I compared with pathway II (at the same consistent reaction coordinate of 2.25 Å, Figure and Table S3, ΔE int is −5.0 and −16.7 kcal/mol, respectively), the lower strain energy (ΔE strain) compensates for the weaker interaction, making pathway I the preferred route (ΔE strain = 13.4 vs 31.0 kcal/mol in pathway II). Further decomposition of the ΔE strain into the contributions from the two fragments (SF5-alkyne and diene) provides additional insights (Figure S5 and Table S4). At a C···C distance of 2.25 Å, the energy required to distort both fragments in the favored cycloaddition is relatively similar and more than 50% lower than the contributions for the unfavored mechanism (ΔE strain, diene: 6.5 kcal/mol and ΔE strain, alkyne: 6.9 kcal/mol in pathway I vs ΔE strain, diene: 13.4 kcal/mol and ΔE strain, alkyne: 17.5 kcal/mol in pathway II). Therefore, both fragments contribute to the reduction of the ΔE strain for pathway I, which is reflected in a highly asynchronous TS for the favored approach of the diene onto the dienophile.

We demonstrated that strain energy plays a key role in the regioselectivity of the reaction involving diene 19a. What about orbital interactions? Diels–Alder reactions are known to be under orbital control, and the frontier molecular orbitals of 19a and the dienophile appear to match the experimentally observed regioselectivity. In addition, recent studies on Diels–Alder reactions employing EDA-natural orbitals of chemical valence (NOCV) analyses provide evidence that enhanced reactivity results from reduced Pauli repulsion (four-electron interactions between occupied molecular orbitals). − Therefore, we considered using the EDA-NOCV − method to further explore the various factors contributing to the overall interaction energy between the distorted fragments and to more specifically quantify the orbital, electrostatic, and Pauli contributions for the two reaction pathways. Figures and S6 and Table S5 present the results of this analysis. At a consistent C···C bond-forming distance of 2.25 Å, the interaction energy ΔE int was found significantly less negative (i.e., less stabilizing) for the privileged pathway I, which also experiences lower Pauli repulsion (ΔE Pauli) energy (68.7 vs 85.1 kcal/mol for pathways I and II, respectively). This is because both orbital and electrostatic interactions between the reactants are much weaker in pathway I, particularly within the C···C distance range of 2.8–2.2 Å. The NOCV analysis (Figure S7) at a C···C distance of 2.25 Å indicated that the main stabilizing energy, Δρ1, from the deformation density due to the HOMO (19a) → LUMO (dienophile) interaction is stronger for the non-privileged approach between 19a and SF5-alkyne in pathway II, leading to a higher orbital energy ΔE orb. This analysis highlights that the regioselectivity of the studied Diels–Alder reactions is not driven by stabilizing orbital interactions or electrostatic interactions and suggests that the asynchronous reaction mode results in a loss of favorable HOMO–LUMO orbital overlap. Instead, it is the ΔE Pauli term, which comprises the destabilizing interactions between occupied orbitals and accounts for any steric repulsion, that plays a crucial role. The origin of the more destabilizing Pauli repulsion for TS2-Ph was investigated by comparing the overlaps between the main occupied molecular orbitals of the diene 19a and the dienophile for the nonprivileged and privileged approaches. The most significant occupied orbitals responsible for the magnitude of the Pauli repulsion are HOMO–2 (dienophile) and HOMO–1, HOMO–2, and HOMO–9 of 19a (see Figure S8 for a plot of MOs). The largest coefficients of the overlaps (Table S6, S = 0.07 and 0.09 for TS2-Ph vs 0.03 and 0.06 for TS1-Ph) involve HOMO–1 and HOMO–2 of 19a, which are associated with the πfuran moiety and the oxygen lone pairs of the CO2R group. For the privileged approach of the diene to the dienophile, these orbital overlaps are the weakest and are therefore mainly responsible for the reduced Pauli repulsion. As a result, the experimentally observed regioselectivity arises from the reduced two-center four-electron Pauli repulsion between the πCC⊥ orbital of the alkyne and, primarily, HOMO–1 (π-system of the furan moiety) and HOMO–2 (mainly O lone pairs) of 19a, in addition to the previously mentioned lower strain energy.

2.3. Functionalization

The facile and direct access to the large library of 2-SF5-aminophenols encouraged us to further valorize them and evaluate their potential applications in synthesis. We decided to study their functionalization as valuable SF5-containing building blocks, using the aminophenol 21c as a model substrate. Removal of the Boc protecting group was easily performed using an excess of trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) in dichloromethane (DCM) at room temperature for 3 h, giving the aminophenol 30c in 98% yield (Scheme ). The structure of 30a, obtained by a similar route in 63% from 21a, was unambiguously determined by SCXRD (CCDC 2429014). , Treating 30c with TBDMSCl in tetrahydrofuran (THF) in the presence of imidazole afforded the corresponding O-silylated aminophenol 31c in good yield (66%). Subsequently, the amino group in 31c could be further acylated in the presence of acetic anhydride, 4-dimethylaminopyridine (DMAP), and an excess of sodium hydride. The double-protected product 32c was obtained in moderate 39% yield after one night along with traces of acylated aminophenol 33c. On the other hand, starting from 21c, the hydroxyl group could be selectively acylated using acyl chloride in the presence of triethylamine to afford the O- and N-protected aminophenol 34c in excellent yield (92%). Selective deprotection of the Boc group was possible using TFA to afford product 35c in a quantitative yield.

Aromatic azides (Ar–N3) are versatile intermediates for the preparation of high-value molecules in bioorganic chemistry, with a pivotal reactivity in click chemistry, which is now widely used in drug discovery. , Combining the azido functional handle with an SF5-containing molecule would allow access to new types of functionalized click-chemistry precursors. In a first attempt to synthesize such compounds, we investigated the classical combination of tert-butyl nitrite (tBuONO) and azidotrimethylsilane (TMSN3) in reaction with 2-SF5-aminophenol 30c which gave only degradation of the starting material. Shifting to the O-protected aminophenols 31c and 35c, the corresponding O-protected azidophenols 37c and 38c were obtained only in moderate 36 and 32% yield, respectively, while 20% of the deprotected azidophenol 36c was observed in the case of the O-TBDMS aminophenol 31c (Scheme a). This modest reactivity was circumvented using NaNO2 and TMSN3 in excess to afford azidophenol 36c in 73% yield (Scheme b).

Furthermore, SF5-aryldiazonium tetrafluoroborate 39c was prepared in 63% yield starting from 30c in the presence of an aqueous solution of tetrafluoroboric acid (HBF4) and NaNO2 added slowly (Scheme c). We next evaluated the reactivity of SF5-aminophenols in electrophilic aromatic substitution (SEAr). The nitration of N-Boc-protected SF5-aminophenol 21c using tBuONO proceeded smoothly and selectively in position C-2 at room temperature delivering the product 40c in 58% isolated yield (Scheme a). Two conditions have been tested for selective bromination in C-3. The first one (Condition A, Scheme b) consisted of the use of [DMAPBr]Br in dichloromethane. This method allowed for the formation of 41c in 37% isolated yield. This result was improved up to 50% using N-bromosuccinimide (NBS) as the bromination reagent in the presence of ZnCl2 in acetonitrile (Condition B, Scheme b).

Finally, we targeted the synthesis of an SF5-coumarin from 2-SF5-methoxyphenol 23. After several attempts, we were inspired by the work of Banwell and used a two-step strategy starting with esterification of phenol 23 with an excess of propiolic acid using N,N′-dicyclohexylcarbodiimide (DCC) in a basic medium. Product 42 was isolated in 58% yield and was then engaged in a gold-catalyzed cyclization reaction using Echavarren’s catalyst. The reaction proceeded at 75 °C in dichloroethane for 17 h, leading to a complete conversion of 42. The desired coumarin 43 was isolated in 56% yield (Scheme ).

3. Conclusions

In summary, we have developed a straightforward approach to pentafluorosulfanylated phenols and aminophenols via a highly regioselective [4 + 2] Diels–Alder cycloaddition reaction between SF5-alkynes and activated dienes. The cycloaddition reaction proceeded smoothly under microwave irradiation and tolerated various SF5-alkynes, yielding 2-SF5-4-aminophenols as major regioisomers.

The regioselectivity of this Diels–Alder cycloaddition reaction involving an alkyne, as a dienophile, was analyzed using density functional theory (DFT) in conjunction with the activation strain model (ASM) of reactivity and energy decomposition analysis (EDA). The results from ASM and molecular orbital analysis (Kohn–Sham and EDA) challenge the conventional view that Diels–Alder reactions are primarily governed by stabilizing orbital interactions, as typically described in general chemistry textbooks. The preferred approach of the diene to the dienophile results in a loss of favorable HOMO–LUMO orbital overlap, due to the asynchronous reaction mode, and in reduced Pauli repulsion between the occupied π-orbitals of the diene and dienophile. Additionally, the high degree of asynchronicity leads to a lower degree of reactant deformation, resulting in a less destabilizing ΔE strain term for the favored approach of the diene to the dienophile. Therefore, both the reduced Pauli repulsions and the lower strain energy arising from high asynchronicity drive the formation of the regioisomer observed experimentally.

Different dienes have been tested, allowing them to cross the border to new SF5-phenols, -resorcinol, -pyridine, and -furan with decent yields. Various postfunctionalizations have been investigated, such as protection and deprotection of both the hydroxyl and the amino groups of aminophenols, diazotization and azidation of the aniline, regioselective bromination and nitration, and synthesis of an SF5-coumarin via a gold-catalyzed cyclization. We anticipate that this new library of functionalized SF5-phenols will prove to be valuable SF5-substituted building blocks.

4. Experimental Section

4.1. General Procedure for the Synthesis of SF5-Aminophenols 21

In a microwave tube equipped with a magnetic bar, SF5-alkyne 20 (1 equiv) and tert-butyl furan-2-ylcarbamate 19a (2.2 equiv) were dissolved in chlorobenzene (0.14 M). The tube was sealed, and the mixture was heated under microwave irradiation at 170 °C for 30 min. The solvent was removed under reduced pressure and the crude was purified by silica gel column chromatography (petroleum ether/ethyl acetate) affording SF5-aminophenols 21.
