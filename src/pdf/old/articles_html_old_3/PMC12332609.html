Abstract

Breast cancer surgery presents significant challenges, including difficulties in accurately localizing tumors, detecting small lesions, and identifying lymph node metastases, all of which compromise therapeutic outcomes and increase the risk of postoperative recurrence. Near-infrared fluorescence (NIRF) imaging technology has emerged as a promising tool for surgical navigation in breast cancer surgery due to its high resolution, real-time imaging capability, excellent biocompatibility, and minimal background interference. This review provides a comprehensive overview of recent advances in fluorescent probes for breast cancer surgical navigation with an emphasis on preclinical studies conducted in small animal models. These studies evaluate the feasibility and translational potential of various probes prior to their clinical application. The review examines the applications and performance evaluation of these probes in breast cancer, highlighting their potential to enhance tumor resection precision and reduce the risk of recurrence. Furthermore, future directions for developing fluorescent probes in breast cancer surgery are explored, emphasizing the role of technological innovation and interdisciplinary integration in improving surgical outcomes.

Introduction

Breast cancer is the most commonly diagnosed cancer worldwide and the leading malignancy among women. , Surgery remains a primary treatment modality for breast cancer and is currently the only approach with curative potential in clinical practice. , However, the complexity of tumor tissue and the occult nature of small lesions pose significant challenges for traditional surgical methods, often leading to an imprecise tumor resection. Consequently, the risk of postoperative recurrence and metastasis increases, ultimately impacting patient survival rates. Therefore, achieving precise tumor localization and resection, minimizing damage to healthy tissue, and enhancing patient survival and quality of life remain critical challenges in breast cancer surgery. The emergence of fluorescence imaging technology provides a novel solution for breast cancer surgical navigation. , NIRF enables the detection of primary breast tumors, lymph nodes, and metastatic sites, providing real-time visualization of tumor boundaries to guide the surgical process. This capability enhances the safety and thoroughness of breast cancer surgeries, ultimately improving patient survival rates. , Figure shows a schematic diagram illustrating the use of NIRF probes for surgical navigation in breast cancer.

Effective fluorescent probes are crucial for successful intraoperative fluorescence imaging. These molecular probes bind to specific targets and induce changes in fluorescence properties, enabling sensitive, selective, and efficient target detection. These probes typically comprise recognition and binding groups, signal-reporting groups (fluorophores), and linker groups. The recognition and binding group is the most critical component, determining the specificity and selectivity of the probe by identifying receptors overexpressed on cancer cell surfaces and enzymes overexpressed in tumor tissues. Fluorescent dyes emit light at a different wavelength after absorbing light of a specific wavelength. Fluorescent probes can be categorized based on chemical structure, working mechanism, targeting specificity, and imaging mode. ICG-Based Probes

ICG is a widely utilized fluorophore in breast cancer surgery for tumor and sentinel lymph node visualization. − These probes demonstrate good biocompatibility and are relatively easy to implement. However, their low specificity limits their use in detecting small or deeply located tumors. For instance, Wang et al. developed an ICG fluorescent probe for breast cancer detection and surgical navigation. This probe increased the survival rate of mice to 75% 5 weeks postsurgery and reduced the local recurrence rate. Nevertheless, the diagnostic specificity of such probes remains relatively low, posing a risk of misdiagnosis. Figure illustrates several key stages in the development and application of fluorescent probes for the treatment of breast cancer.

Receptor-Mediated Targeted Probes

These probes are specifically designed to target receptors overexpressed on cancer cells, enhancing tumor specificity and improving the delineation of malignant tissues. However, the primary challenge associated with this approach is the variability in receptor expression across different tumor types, which may lead to inconsistent tumor detection. For instance, Wu et al. developed a receptor-targeted fluorescent probe that exhibited excellent receptor-targeting performance in breast cancer detection and remained detectable for up to 24 h. Nevertheless, these probes often have slow metabolic and clearance rates, and fluctuations may influence their diagnostic specificity in receptor expression.

Enzyme-Targeted Probes

These probes leverage specific enzymes overexpressed in the tumor microenvironment, such as matrix metalloproteinases, to enhance tumor localization. , While these probes provide a high degree of specificity, their effectiveness can be limited by the heterogeneity of enzyme activity within tumors and the challenge of developing probes that selectively bind to tumor-associated enzymes without cross-reacting with normal tissues. , For instance, Wu et al. developed an enzyme-targeted fluorescent probe that demonstrated exceptionally high sensitivity (2.0 × 10–3 U/mL) and strong affinity (K m = 1.84 μM) in breast cancer imaging, making it suitable for intraoperative guidance during breast tumor resection. However, these probes generally exhibit poor stability, and their imaging duration remains limited. Figure shows a schematic diagram of enzyme-activated fluorescent probes used for diagnosis and surgical navigation.

Tumor Microenvironment-Responsive Probes

These probes are designed to respond to specific physiological conditions within the tumor microenvironment, such as acidic pH or hypoxia. , These characteristics enable selective accumulation in tumor tissues, enhancing tumor-specific imaging. However, optimizing these probes for widespread clinical application remains challenging, as the complexity of tumor microenvironments can result in variability in probe performance. For instance, Wu et al. developed a pH-activated fluorescent probe for detecting breast cancer bone metastases. The results demonstrated that the probe exhibited a strong penetration (8 mm) and rapid response (30 s). Nevertheless, the probe’s lack of effective targeting capabilities resulted in false-positive NIR signals in the left tibia.

Multimodal Probes

Multimodal probes integrate fluorescence with other imaging modalities, such as magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), computed tomography (CT), and positron emission tomography (PET), to provide complementary diagnostic information. Their primary advantage lies in their ability to enhance tumor localization and staging accuracy. However, challenges related to complexity, cost, and integration with existing clinical systems may limit their widespread adoption. For example, Privat et al. developed a multimodal fluorescent probe that combines single-photon emission computed tomography (SPECT) and fluorescence imaging, enabling precise delineation of breast cancer tumor boundaries and accurate surgical navigation. Nevertheless, the design of such probes remains relatively complex.

Theranostic Probes

These probes integrate diagnostic and therapeutic functions, allowing for both imaging and treatment of breast tumors. , For instance, theranostic probes can facilitate real-time monitoring of treatment progress while simultaneously delivering targeted therapy. , Despite their promising potential, their clinical application remains in the early stages, with concerns regarding safety, efficacy, and regulatory approval delaying their adoption into standard clinical practice. For example, Wu et al. developed a therapeutic fluorescent probe for fluorescence imaging-guided photothermal therapy (PTT) in breast cancer. This probe enables breast cancer imaging and provides a therapeutic effect, achieving an integrated diagnosis and treatment. However, the design of such probes presents significant technical challenges, and their application remains limited. These include the limited tissue penetration of NIR light in PTT, which can hinder the effective ablation of deeper tumor tissues. Moreover, difficulties in optimizing thermal conversion efficiency, preventing collateral tissue damage, and achieving precise real-time monitoring further constrain their translational potential. Figure shows a schematic illustration of a theranostic probe specifically designed for the targeted diagnosis and treatment of breast cancer.

Composite Probes

Composite probes integrate features from multiple probe categories, combining the advantages of various targeting and imaging mechanisms to enhance specificity, resolution, and therapeutic potential. However, their increased complexity presents challenges related to stability, biocompatibility, and potential for unintended interactions between different components. For example, Yang et al. developed a multimodal fluorescent probe for integrated diagnosis and treatment of breast cancer. This probe enables multimodal imaging through MRI and the NIR-II window, while enhancing the efficacy of radiotherapy for breast cancer. With high specificity (95%), this composite probe facilitates a precise breast cancer diagnosis and treatment. Nevertheless, its complex design and relatively poor biocompatibility remain limitations.

Each probe type offers distinct advantages in targeting efficiency, specificity, and real-time imaging capabilities. However, each category also presents unique challenges, particularly concerning probe specificity, clearance rates, and the variability of tumor characteristics. For instance, while ICG-based probes are relatively simple and effective for general tumor detection, they lack the specificity needed for precise tumor delineation. Conversely, receptor-mediated and enzyme-targeted probes provide higher specificity but may be limited by heterogeneous tumor expression.

Developing multimodal , and theranostic probes holds significant promise for enhancing both the diagnostic and therapeutic aspects of breast cancer surgery. By integrating multiple functionalities, such as imaging and treatment, these probes can potentially improve surgical precision while enabling real-time therapeutic monitoring. However, their complexity and high cost must be addressed before widespread clinical adoption can be achieved.

Finally, tumor microenvironment-responsive probes offer great potential for highly selective tumor imaging by targeting the unique conditions within the tumor. However, these probes require further refinement to ensure consistent performance across various tumor types and clinical applications.

Representative Examples of Classified Fluorescent Probes

In the preceding section, we provided a systematic analysis of the advantages and disadvantages of various fluorescent probes. In the following section, we will present representative examples based on the classification of these probes. It is important to note that all the probes discussed below share a common featuretheir potential application in breast cancer surgical navigation. However, to better highlight the characteristics of each category, the discussion within each subsection will not only emphasize their role in surgical navigation but also focus on the unique structural designs or functional mechanisms that define each type. This approach aims to better reflect the classification rationale and research value of each category.

ICG-Based Fluorescent Probe

ICG is a dye with emission wavelengths in the NIR-I region and is approved for clinical use. ICG has demonstrated promising results in identifying sentinel lymph nodes and facilitating intraoperative navigation in breast cancer surgery. − Kitai et al. first applied ICG dye for sentinel lymph node biopsy in breast cancer, demonstrating its effectiveness. However, ICG dye is rapidly cleared from the body. To address this limitation, recent studies have combined ICG with other molecules to develop ICG-based fluorescent probes. Notable examples include the human serum albumin (HSA)-ICG probe, created by coupling HSA with ICG, and ICG-C9-Kadcyla molecular imaging probes were synthesized by coupling ICG with Kadcyla, an anticancer drug. Figure shows the surgical resection of breast cancer guided by an ICG-based fluorescent probe.

Receptor-Mediated Targeted Fluorescent Probes

Receptor-mediated targeted fluorescent probes are designed based on specific receptors overexpressed on tumor cells. These probes typically consist of a fluorophore and a targeting ligand with high affinity for the receptors, such as antibodies, peptides, and small molecules. By binding to tumor receptors, these probes enable precise recognition and imaging of tumor cells. Notable examples include the carbon quantum dots (CQD)-KD1 probe conjugating CQD with recombinant st14 inhibitor (KD1), the estrogen receptor α (ER α) targeting NIR fluorescent probe, CD206-targeted NIR fluorescent nanoprobe MR780NPs, the CXCR4-targeted rare-earth nanoprobe ErNPs@POL6326, the GLUT1-targeting fluorescent probe CUM-5, the fluorescence probe YQGN-7, which targets gonadotropin-releasing hormone receptor (GnRHR), and the integrin αvβ3 receptor-targeted fluorescence probe 1FCG-FFGRGD. Figure shows receptor-mediated targeted fluorescent probes for breast cancer surgical navigation. Despite their advantages in specificity and targeting, receptor-mediated targeted fluorescent probes may have limitations in rapid tumor imaging due to the time required for probe-receptor binding and the metabolic and clearance rates of the probes in vivo.

Enzyme-Targeted Fluorescent Probes

Enzyme-targeted fluorescent probes are vital in studying human pathology, particularly in diagnosing and treating diseases, such as cancer. By targeting specific enzyme active sites, these probes enable highly precise enzyme activity monitoring, making them valuable tools for early detection and therapeutic interventions. Fluorescent probes are classified into two types based on their design principles: enzyme-responsive probes and enzyme–substrate probes. Enzyme-responsive probes undergo specific reactions with enzymes, resulting in the generation of fluorescence signals. In contrast, enzyme–substrate probes are catalytically decomposed by enzymes, releasing fluorescent substances. These probes have extensive applications across various fields, including inhibitor screening, cell and live imaging, biological research, and diagnostics. Notable examples include the DCM-Leucine aminopeptidase (LAP) probe targeting leucine aminopeptidase, 6QC-NIR20 and VGT-309 probe targeting cysteine cathepsins, HMRef-αMan probe targeting α-mannosidase, and Death-Cat-RATIO probe targeting tissue cathepsins and caspases. These probes demonstrate strong biocompatibility, high specificity, rapid cellular metabolism, and high sensitivity, making them widely applicable in fluorescence diagnostics and surgical navigation. However, their complex design is limited by the types and activity levels of enzymes.

Tumor Microenvironment-Responsive Fluorescent Probes

Tumor microenvironment-responsive fluorescent probes are designed to react to specific changes within the tumor microenvironment, , such as hypoxia, , acidity, elevated reactive oxygen species (ROS) levels, high glutathione (GSH) concentrations, and excessive adenosine triphosphate (ATP) release. , These probes can activate or modulate fluorescence signals, enabling highly sensitive monitoring and detection of the tumor microenvironment. Examples include the self-calibrating activatable nanoprobe Cy7–1/PG5-Cy5@LWHA, designed to elevate nitroreductase activity in hypoxic tumor cells, which demonstrated significant hypoxia-sensing ability in both in situ and metastatic breast cancer mouse models. Figure shows a schematic illustration of the tumor microenvironment-responsive fluorescent probe Cy7–1/PG5-Cy5@LWHA for breast cancer. The ROS-responsive fluorescent nanoprobe AmplexRed (ADHP) has also been utilized for image-guided breast tumor resection. In contrast, the tumor microenvironment ATP-reactive nanoprobe SPIOs@A-T has been developed to detect breast cancer lymph node metastasis. These probes have the potential to personalize breast cancer surgical navigation, enhance surgical success rates, and improve patient survival, ultimately leading to better treatment outcomes and a better quality of life for patients with breast cancer. However, despite significant advancements in tumor microenvironment-responsive fluorescent probes, their practical application remains challenging. The complexity of the tumor microenvironment introduces additional variables that may increase the risk of misdiagnosis and limit its clinical utility.

Multimodal Fluorescent Probes

Currently, the most commonly used breast imaging techniques in clinical practice include ultrasound (US) imaging, CT scan, PET scan, MRI, and SPECT. However, each imaging method has limitations and often struggles to meet the combined requirements of sensitivity, specificity, and targeting. Additionally, the light scattering and limited tissue penetration depth of fluorescence imaging restrict its effectiveness in imaging deep tumor tissue. To address these challenges, developing dual-mode and multimodal probes based on fluorescence imaging has become a prominent research focus in surgical navigation. Notable examples include the PET/fluorescence dual-mode imaging probe XB1034 and the fluorescence/MRI dual-mode imaging rare-earth core–shell nanoprobe ND-RENP. Multimodal molecular imaging probes can enable the simultaneous use of multiple imaging techniques, overcoming the limitation of single-modality imaging, providing complementary advantages, and expanding the application scope of molecular imaging technology. However, technical complexity, high costs, and potential toxicity must be addressed to facilitate their clinical application.

Although multimodal probes such as PET/fluorescence combinations provide preoperative staging information, their role in surgical navigation lies in guiding intraoperative decisions based on earlier imaging or in combining imaging modes for deeper or more accurate tumor visualization (Figure ).

Theranostic Fluorescent Probes

Theranostic fluorescent probes integrate diagnostic and therapeutic functions, enabling precise treatment through visualization and therapeutic effects on a single platform. These probes can emit fluorescence, release heat, or produce singlet oxygen upon photon absorption, facilitating phototherapy, including photothermal or photodynamic treatment. Notable examples include the Met-IR-782 probe, which combines methionine and IR-782 for detecting tumor cells overexpressing methionine receptor while increasing cell sensitivity to doxorubicin; ZM1068-NPs probe, which enables fluorescence imaging-guided surgery and PTT in vivo for mouse breast cancer; the probe EA5_c3@BSA, demonstrating significant potential in fluorescence, photoacoustic imaging, and PTT for subcutaneous and orthotopic breast tumors, and BETT-2 NP imaging nanoprobe, designed for fluorescence-guided photodynamic-thermotherapy synergistic treatment for orthotopic mouse breast tumors.

These theranostic probes offer a novel platform that combines breast cancer diagnosis and treatment, allowing for simultaneous diagnostic imaging and effective targeted therapy. By overcoming the limitations of traditional chemotherapy and radiotherapy, these probes can reduce patient discomfort and improve survival rates. However, their development faces challenges, such as molecular design and synthesis complexity, low clinical translation rates, high costs, and safety concerns.

Composite Fluorescent Probes

Composite fluorescent probes exhibit two or more characteristics, representing a promising advancement in molecular imaging research. , For instance, Liu et al. developed a dual-modal NIRF/MRI nanoprobe, NPs-Ate, which targets programmed cell death protein-1/ligand-1. This probe comprises ICG, gadolinium diethylenetriaminepentaacetic acid, HSA, and atezolizumab. Similarly, Ouyang et al. designed a nitroreductase-activated fluorescence/multispectral photoacoustic tomography (MSOT) dual-modal fluorescence nanoprobe, which specifically interacts with nitroreductase and enables photoacoustic and fluorescence monitoring of breast cancer metastasis. In another study, Chen et al. developed a GSH-responsive theranostic nanoprobe, HA-MnO2/ICG, designed for fluorescence/MRI dual-modal imaging and capable of photothermal/immuno-enhancement therapeutic effects under imaging guidance. Additionally, Gao et al. reported a rare-earth nanoparticle fluorescence probe, α-Er NPs, which exhibited significant tumor imaging performance and in vivo photothermal effects in a breast cancer mouse model. This probe has emerged as a potential candidate for real-time breast cancer imaging and PTT in the NIR-II window. It also possesses fluorescence/X-ray, CT, and US multimodal imaging capabilities.

These advancements underscore the potential of composite fluorescent probes to improve the specificity, sensitivity, and multifunctionality. However, challenges persist in optimizing probe design, ensuring biocompatibility, and translating these innovations into clinical applications.

Results

The latest advances in fluorescent probes for breast cancer surgical navigation have laid the groundwork for improved precision in tumor resection through the categorization of different probe types. Multiple innovative techniques are presented in this work that offer clear advantages over conventional methodssuch as manual palpation, visual inspection, mammography, and ultrasoundwhich, while informative, suffer from low resolution, limited detection of small lesions, and difficulty distinguishing benign from malignant tissue. In contrast, fluorescent probes enable real-time visualization of tumors and metastases with higher sensitivity, facilitating more precise localization and excision of cancerous tissue and thereby reducing the risk of postoperative recurrence and dissemination. Thus, research on fluorescent probes has already achieved significant milestones in terms of imaging sensitivity, specificity, and accuracy. However, current probe performance still faces several challenges that prevent its successful translation into clinical studies. A primary limitation is the potential for nonspecific binding, which can cause false positives or degrade the image quality. Although substantial progress has been made in designing probes with enhanced tumor selectivity, an ideal probe must combine high specificity with excellent biocompatibility and minimal toxicity to avoid adverse reactions. Furthermore, the pharmacokinetics and biodistribution of existing probes remain suboptimal: many exhibit insufficient tissue penetration and overly rapid clearance, limiting their applicability in lengthy or complex surgical procedures. Therefore, next-generation probes should be engineered to possess longer circulation times.

Discussion

An emerging direction is the development of multimodal and theranostic probes that integrate diagnostic imaging with therapeutic functions. Multimodal probescombining fluorescence with modalities such as MRI, CT, or PEThave shown promise in improving diagnostic accuracy, yet their complexity poses obstacles to clinical adoption. Theranostic probes, which can deliver localized therapy (e.g., chemotherapy payloads or photothermal agents) alongside tumor detection, offer the potential to both identify and treat cancer in a single platform, ultimately enhancing the treatment efficacy and patient outcomes.

Despite these advances, the clinical translation of fluorescent probes has been gradual. Rigorous preclinical evaluationincluding in vivo studies in animal modelsis essential to assess pharmacokinetics, safety, and targeting efficiency. Moreover, differences between animal tumor microenvironments and those of human patients often reveal unforeseen performance issues. To address this, probes that are responsive to specific microenvironmental cues (such as pH or tumor-associated enzymes) are under development to improve specificity and reduce off-target activation.

Looking forward, several key priorities emerge:

Targeting specificity versus broad applicability: Highly receptor-specific probes may be restricted to certain tumor subtypes; strategies such as dual-ligand or broad-spectrum targeting warrant exploration.

Pharmacokinetic optimization: Engineering tunable half-lives and activation mechanisms will balance the rapid clearance with prolonged imaging windows.

Balancing multifunctionality and simplicity: While multifunctional probes can combine imaging and therapy, increased molecular complexity may impede regulatory approval. Striking a balance between innovation and clinical practicality is crucial.

Bridging the preclinical–clinical gap: More predictive modelssuch as patient-derived xenograftsand advanced simulation platforms will help ensure that promising probes in animals translate effectively to human patients.

Regulatory and economic considerations: Beyond scientific feasibility, probe development must consider the manufacturing cost, ease of production, and compatibility with existing imaging infrastructure. Successful clinical translation requires alignment of molecular innovation with regulatory foresight and interdisciplinary collaboration.

In summary, while fluorescent probes have transformed intraoperative imaging by offering real-time, high-resolution tumor visualization, addressing these technical, regulatory, and translational challenges is vital for their widespread clinical adoption and for realizing their full potential in improving breast cancer surgery outcomes.
