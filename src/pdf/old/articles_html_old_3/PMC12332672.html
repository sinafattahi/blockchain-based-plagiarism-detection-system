Abstract

The global proliferation of nonbiodegradable petrochemical plastics presents severe environmental, health, and economic challenges, underscoring the urgent need for sustainable alternatives. Polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHAs), a family of biobased and biodegradable polyesters, offer a promising solution with their environmental compatibility, versatility, and ability to mimic conventional plastics. This review explores PHA production, encompassing microbial biosynthesis, metabolic pathways, and innovative extraction methods to optimize production and material properties. Genetic and metabolic engineering advances address cost and scalability barriers, and novel feedstock utilization. Innovations in copolymerization, blending, and nanocomposites have expanded PHA applications across packaging, agriculture, and biomedical fields. The evolution of smart and functionalized PHAs for high-value sectors such as regenerative medicine, drug delivery, and smart packaging further demonstrates their expanded potential beyond conventional bioplastics. Emphasis is placed on integrating PHAs into circular economy models by leveraging waste-derived feedstocks, emerging microbial strategies, including mixed microbial cultures (MMCs), halophilic systems, and aquaculture-integrated production, offer scalable and resilient routes for cost-effective, sustainable PHA biosynthesis. Despite their potential, PHAs remain hindered by challenges involving high production costs, inconsistent mechanical properties, and environmental trade-offs in downstream processing. PHAs are sustainable bioplastics that align competently with Green Chemistry principles, though improvements in energy efficiency and catalytic optimization are required for broader commercial applications. The review highlights novel recovery techniques aligned with a green bioeconomy, including green solvents, supercritical fluid extraction, enzymatic, mealworm, and other biorecovery methods. Integration with circular economy frameworks through waste valorization, biobased feedstocks, and zero-waste biorefinery models further enhances the sustainability of PHA production. Future research must optimize extraction methods, address microplastic risks, and expand into high-value markets. PHAs represent one of the most environmentally promising bioplastics to date due to their complete biodegradability, non-persistent microplastics, and lower emissions, with advancements in production further enhancing their viability. By advancing PHA technologies and fostering interdisciplinary collaboration, PHAs can accelerate the transition to a circular bioeconomy, offering a unique combination of full biodegradability, material tunability, and circular design that positions them as frontrunners in the transition away from fossil-based plastics.

1. Introduction

Plastics were initially developed as an environmentally driven alternative to ivory in products like billiard balls. Similarly, plastic bags were introduced in Sweden to reduce reliance on paper and save forests. However, plastics have since become a major contributor to global pollution. In response, a new wave of innovation is focused on sustainable alternatives, with PHAs emerging as a promising candidate due to their eco-friendly and versatile nature. −

Polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHA) have emerged as a promising class of microbial biopolymers that offer a sustainable alternative to conventional petrochemical-derived plastics, aligning with the principles of a circular economy. PHAs are biobased polyesters stored by bacteria as carbon and energy reserves under nutrient stress (Figure ). Despite their biodegradability and biocompatibility, PHA’s industrial scalability and mechanical limitations remain key challenges. Recent advancements in PHA microbial synthesis, recovery methods, and extraction technologies have driven innovation in next-generation biopolymers, expanding their applications across food packaging, textiles, agriculture, automotive, electronics, and medical fields.

Their versatility, demonstrated through tunable thermomechanical properties ranging from rigid plastics to flexible elastomers, enables diverse applications, from packaging to medical implants. For example, the P­(3HB-co-3HV) copolymer is widely used in industrial settings due to its thermal stability. , Overall, the combination of biobased origin, inherent EOL decomposition, and versatility positions PHAs as a frontrunner in the search aimed at sustainable and eco-friendly materials, with unique advantages that make PHAs a compelling alternative to conventional plastics and even other bioplastics such as PLA. Hence, according to the recent advancements, PHA will be the “bioplastics of the future”.

However, despite their substantial promise, PHAs face several challenges, including high production costs, scalability limitations, and the need for tailored properties for industrial use. In the United States, several corporations, such as Mango Materials, Danimer Scientific, RWDC Industries, and Newlight Technologies, are leading efforts to address these issues by leveraging innovative feedstocks, advanced fermentation techniques, and large-scale bioreactor systems.

This review focuses on PHAs as a key material for advancing a circular economy for plastics. By examining their biochemical synthesis pathways, exploring the influence of microbial factors on PHA production, and evaluating efficient extraction methods, this work aims to provide a foundational understanding of PHA production. Furthermore, the review delves into recent advancements in property enhancement strategies, including copolymerization, blending, and nanofiller incorporation, to expand the application scope of PHAs beyond traditional uses. Recognizing the critical importance of cost-effective production, this review critically analyzes the challenges related to feedstock availability and variability, process optimization, and downstream processing. By addressing these challenges through strategies such as utilizing biowaste as a carbon source and developing efficient biorefinery concepts, this review aims to establish a foundation for the next stage of PHA development and its successful integration into a circular economy for plastics. This knowledge will be invaluable for researchers, industry stakeholders, and policymakers in accelerating the transition toward a more sustainable and circular plastics economy.

2. Advancing PHA Performance for Sustainable and Circular Applications

Recent progress in understanding biosynthetic pathways, monomer diversity, material modification techniques, and microbial engineering, including genetic advancements, has significantly improved PHA properties. These developments support the production of high-performance, biodegradable materials as viable alternatives to conventional plastics.

2.1. Biochemical Mechanisms and Enzymatic Pathways in PHA Synthesis

PHAs have garnered significant attention as sustainable, eco-friendly alternatives to conventional petroleum-based plastics due to their biorenewable origin, biocompatibility, and biodegradability (Figure ). Their potential to mitigate plastic pollution and promote a more sustainable future is substantial. Enhancing PHA performance and properties is essential for their broader adoption in circular and sustainable applications, as such modifications impact PHA’s usability.

The structural diversity of PHAs, determined by their monomer composition and enzymatic synthesis pathways, plays a pivotal role in enhancing their material properties and expanding their applications in sustainable and circular systems. Furthermore, the biochemical mechanisms governing PHA biosynthesis, including enzyme specificity and metabolic pathways, influence their scalability and economic viability in industrial production. Understanding these mechanisms is essential for optimizing microbial synthesis, improving processing efficiency, and enabling a circular economy by utilizing waste-derived carbon sources.

The monomers of PHAs are hydroxyalkanoates, whereas the repeating unit is poly­(3-hydroxyalkanoate). The variability in the R group, from methyl to tridecyl, imparts diverse properties to PHAs. This diversity makes the PHA polymer family ideal for various applications. 2.2. Blending and Physical Modification

2.2.1. Blending with Other Polymers:

Mixing PHA with natural or synthetic polymers is a common method to improve its mechanical strength and durability. Despite the need for chemical treatment in certain cases, blending is still considered a physical modification since it does not alter PHA’s structure. This method offers benefits due to the affordability and widespread availability of materials like polylactic acid. PHA has been combined with several polymers, such as cellulose acetate, hydroxypropyl distarch phosphate, natural rubber, polyethylene glycol, polybutylene succinate, and polycaprolactone. , PHAs can also be blended with chitosan, chitin, starch, lignin, polyvinyl acetate, polyvinylphenol, poly­(ethylene oxide), and polypropylene carbonate. Studies have primarily investigated the compatibility of these PHA blends with other materials. Key areas of focus include biocompatibility, biodegradability, water and gas permeability, and resistance to UV, which are crucial for various applications.

2.2.2. Nanocomposites:

Combining nanoparticles, for instance, clay, graphene, or carbon nanotubes (CNTs), into PHAs creates nanocomposites with significantly improved mechanical, thermal, and barrier properties, making them suitable for diverse applications. Nanoparticles act as reinforcement agents, increasing tensile strength and modulus; for instance, adding 1.5 wt % multiwalled CNTs to PHBV boosted tensile strength and modulus by 150%, while 3 wt % sepiolite with 18 wt % PEG enhanced tensile strength 5.6-fold and elastic modulus 15-fold. They also enhance impact strength by dissipating energy upon impact, as shown by improved impact resistance in PHBV with nano silicon dioxide. ,

Barrier effects increase thermal stability from layered fillers like clay, which delay volatile decomposition; adding Cloisite 30B or Cloisite 25A to PHB raised the decomposition onset temperature. For packaging, nanoparticles like OMMT or modified kaolinite improve gas, aroma, and water vapor barrier properties. However, achieving uniform nanoparticle dispersion is critical, as agglomeration weakens the composite, and selecting the right type, concentration, and processing method is essential for optimizing performance. ,

The development of PHA-based biocomposites using natural fillers from agricultural and industrial waste can further improve mechanical characteristics and biodegradability, offering viable alternatives to nonrenewable materials and contributing to a circular economy.

2.3. Advancements in Genetic Engineering for PHA Production Optimization

Genetic engineering has become a cornerstone for optimizing polyhydroxyalkanoate (PHA) production, enabling breakthroughs in both yield and material performance. Recent strategies range from metabolic and pathway engineering to precise genome editing tools like CRISPR/Cas9 and CRISPRi, facilitating fine-tuned control over monomer composition, stress tolerance, and cell lysis. Innovations such as bridge recombination and synthetic biology have further expanded the capabilities for large-scale, cost-effective PHA biosynthesis. These approaches also support direct polymer secretion, growth in non-sterile continuous systems, and enhanced substrate flexibility which are key steps toward industrial viability and circular bioeconomy integration.

2.3.1. Gene Introduction and Metabolic Pathway Modification:

Introducing exogenous PHA synthesis genes into an organism can improve the production of an original product by altering the metabolic pathways. This process can also impart new properties to PHAs through product interactions. For example, when the P3HB synthesis route was engineered into indigo-producing , it not only intensified indigo production but also resulted in the formation of blue-colored P3HB. ,

2.3.2. Metabolic Pathway Optimization:

Through metabolic engineering, which refers to modifying one or several enzymatic pathways, PHA can be optimized to meet the individual needs of each field. This allows for customizing material qualities like ductility, elasticity, thermal conductivity, and transparency, making PHA versatile for different applications.

2.3.3. Carbon Flux and Energy Metabolism Optimization:

The optimization and integration of metabolic pathways can improve the conversion of carbon-rich waste into PHAs. Genetic engineering approaches targeting carbon flux and energy metabolism have boosted PHA production by enhancing substrate assimilation, increasing synthesis, and minimizing side reactions. Additionally, CRISPR/Cas9-based systems have proven effective in enhancing PHA metabolic efficiency.

2.3.4. CRISPR and Genome Editing for Enhanced Production and Property Modification:

The CRISPR/Cas9 systems have been employed to improve PHA production and functionality, addressing critical challenges in microbial biomanufacturing. In species, CRISPR/Cas9 has encountered hurdles such as double-strand break-induced cell death, mutation variability, and limitations in large DNA fragment insertion, which affect its efficiency. Traditional homologous recombination methods, while established, are slow and often leave genetic scars, whereas CRISPR offers a faster and more precise alternative. Optimization efforts continue to address these limitations, including stringent sgRNA design requirements and off-target effects. ,

In halophilic bacteria such as , CRISPR/Cas9 has been successfully applied to modify the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, increasing PHBV production from glucose. Targeted deletions of genes like sdhE and icl redirected metabolic flux to enhance the 3HV ratio in PHBV. Further optimization, including introducing phosphoenolpyruvate carboxylase and hemoglobin genes, enabled high-quality PHBV production under unsterile and continuous conditions. Similarly, CRISPR/Cas9 was used in sp. KM-1 to disrupt the pyrF gene, demonstrating its potential for genome deletion and integration, vital for improving the economic viability of PHA production in extremophiles.

CRISPR-based techniques, including CRISPR interference (CRISPRi), have facilitated fine-tuning of PHA copolymer composition. For example, in species TD01, regulating the expression of the prpC gene allowed manipulation of 3HV monomer ratios in P­(3HB-co-3HV) copolymers from <1% to 13% (23). In , CRISPRi was applied to regulate the sad gene, achieving up to 71.8% P­(3HB-co-3HV) copolymer content, showcasing its potential for coordinated gene manipulation to enhance PHA synthesis.

CRISPR/Cas9 also offers opportunities for addressing broader challenges, such as stress tolerance in halophiles and improving PHA recovery through engineered cell lysis mechanisms. These advancements highlight the versatility of CRISPR/Cas9 in modifying metabolic pathways, enhancing enzyme activity, and tailoring microbial systems for sustainable and cost-effective PHA production.

However, challenges remain, including off-target effects, efficient delivery of CRISPR components, and regulatory considerations. Careful design of sgRNAs, optimization of transformation protocols, and responsible use are essential for realizing the full potential of CRISPR-based technologies in microbial biomanufacturing. With continued development, CRISPR/Cas9 holds significant promise for advancing genome editing strategies to meet industrial and environmental demands. ,

2.3.5. Protein Engineering and Synthetic Biological Engineering for Direct Secretion:

Using low-molecular-weight proteins, such as phasins, PHA secretion has been shown to reduce extraction costs. Phasins facilitate PHA secretion directly from cells, minimizing the need for cell disruption, which is traditionally required in PHA extraction processes. With synthetic biological engineering, PHAs can be secreted directly, bypassing cell disruption, thus reducing the complexity and costs associated with traditional extraction methods. This approach is especially promising for industrial-scale applications, as it streamlines the production processes.

2.3.6. Strain Improvement for Cost-Efficiency and Property Customization:

Engineering organisms like spp. have permitted the production of specific PHA types with tailored monomer compositions, such as PHBV and P3HB4HB. These adjustments improve PHA’s mechanical properties and biodegradability, expanding their suitability for diverse industrial applications.

Developing strains that grow in unsterile, continuous production setups reduces the cost of large-scale PHA production by eliminating the need for sterile conditions, which are often time-consuming, costly, and energy intensive. Specific carbon sources and genetic deletions (e.g., knockout of gabD in ) are used to increase 4HB accumulation, significantly enhancing the material’s functional properties. The 4HB molar fraction can be adjusted by controlling glucose concentration, enabling the production of P3HB4HB with a wide range of applications in flexible bioplastics. Metabolic engineering allows for tailoring properties like ductility, elasticity, thermal conductivity, and transparency. Such property control allows PHA to be tailored to meet various industries’ specific requirements, expanding its application potential. Genetic and metabolic modifications, especially carbon-rich wastes, offer significant environmental benefits and cost reduction. By optimizing metabolic pathways, industrial-scale PHA production can reduce its environmental impact while making production more economically viable.

2.3.7. Bridge Recombination as a Breakthrough for PHA Biopolymer Research:

Bridge recombination, a novel gene-editing technique, offers a transformative approach to enhancing polyhydroxyalkanoate (PHA) bioplastics. Unlike CRISPR/Cas9, which relies on precise DNA cuts and cellular repair, this method enables large-scale genetic modifications in a single step, facilitating seamless gene insertions, deletions, or swaps. ,

Potential applications of bridge recombination in PHA biosynthesis include enhancing microbial production, improving polymer properties, optimizing feedstock utilization, and accelerating strain development. Genetic modifications in PHA-producing bacteria, such as and , can optimize yield and efficiency by integrating key metabolic genes. Tailoring biosynthetic pathways enhances PHA’s thermal stability, mechanical strength, and biodegradability, making it a more viable alternative to petroleum-based plastics. Engineered microbes can also efficiently metabolize diverse waste-derived substrates, supporting circular bioeconomy principles. Additionally, bridge recombination streamlines genetic modifications, reducing the time and complexity of developing high-performance PHA-producing strains. By integrating bridge recombination into PHA research, scientists can drive cost-effective and scalable Bioplastic production, advancing sustainable materials and circular bioeconomy solutions.

3.1. Chemical Extraction Methods

3.1.1. Solvent-Based Extraction:

Traditional solvents like halogenated solvents (e.g., chloroform) are effective but pose environmental hazards. Green solvents (e.g., dimethyl carbonate, ionic liquids) are eco-friendly but may require larger volumes and yield lower purity. , Figure illustrates the general steps involved in solvent-based PHA extraction.

3.1.2. Halogenated Solvents:

Chloroform and dichloromethane have been widely used as solvents for organic extraction due to their effectiveness; however their hazardous nature poses serious health and environmental risks, making them unsuitable for industrial applications. During the extraction process, polyhydroxybutyrate (PHB) is dissolved in these solvents, and techniques like evaporation or precipitation are used to separate and extract the PHB polymer from the solvent used. The recovery rates and purity of PHB achieved through these methods are typically high, ranging from 80% to 99%, which results in a high-quality biopolymer. Moreover, this process allows for operation at lower temperatures, which can be beneficial for preserving the polymer’s properties. Nevertheless, the toxicity of the solvents, combined with the high cost of solvent recycling, limits the sustainability and scalability of this approach for industrial-scale applications.

3.1.3. Green Solvents:

Greener alternatives, such as dimethyl carbonate and butyl acetate, are becoming popular as sustainable options for polymer extraction. These methods involve disrupting the cell walls to release the intracellular PHB using green solvents, which are typically nonchlorinated and halogen-free. ,, These solvents, which include alcohols and noncyclic ketones can dissolve PHB selectively without affecting other matrix components. The use of safe, environmentally friendly solvents supports a sustainable process that yields a biopolymer with high plasticity, elasticity, and quality. However, despite these advantages, this approach incurs significant processing costs. It requires large volumes of solvent, typically 20 parts solvent per part of polymer, which can be a limiting factor for large-scale applications.

3.1.4. Alkali Methods:

Intracellular PHB is extracted by treating cell membranes with an alkaline solution, typically NaOH or KOH, to induce lysis. After lysis, the solution is carefully neutralized to prevent polymer degradation, followed by PHB recovery through precipitation and purification to eliminate residual impurities. This method offers high lysis efficiency, easy access to alkaline reagents, and a favorable cost–benefit ratio. However, it has limitations, including low effectiveness in removing cellular contaminants, polymer hydrolysis, and often requiring additional chemicals to achieve high purity.

Apparently, procedures using sodium hydroxide, sodium hydroxide combined with sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS), and ammonia–water for non-PHA cell mass digestion emerged as the most favorable options. Whilenon-PHA cell mass digestion methods prioritize environmental and economic aspects, other methods like butyl acetate may be preferable when prioritizing safety, risk mitigation, and final polymer purity.

3.1.5. Supercritical Fluid Extraction (SCFE):

SCFE uses supercritical CO2, providing high purity without organic solvents. However, it demands specialized equipment and is energy-intensive, limiting its economic feasibility. , Therefore, it can be considered solvent-based, but it is distinct from traditional solvent-based methods due to its use of supercritical fluids rather than conventional organic solvents.

Supercritical fluids, notably supercritical carbon dioxide (sCO2), provide an efficient method for polyhydroxyalkanoate (PHA) extraction by combining the properties of both gases and liquids. This allows for mild extraction conditions that effectively dissolve and release PHAs without leaving harmful residues, making the process highly suitable for applications in pharmaceutical and biomedical products. Supercritical fluid extraction (SCFE) achieves high-purity results while avoiding using organic solvents, offering an environmentally friendly alternative. However, SCFE is typically limited to high-value products due to its batch operation mode, which is less feasible for lower-cost large-scale production. Additionally, the technique demands specialized equipment and can be energy-intensive, further constraining its scalability for large-scale industrial applications.

3.2. Mechanical Disruption Methods

3.2.1. Bead Milling:

Bead milling grinds solid materials into fine powders using rotating hard-material beads within a container. As the beads mix with the material, they gradually reduce particle size. Widely used in laboratories and industry for sample preparation, bead mills are particularly effective even at low biomass concentrations. This method is also known for its easy implementation and relatively low energy consumption. However, to achieve optimal performance, bead milling often requires pretreatment steps, and multiple cycles are typically necessary to reach the desired particle size.

3.2.2. High-Pressure Homogenization:

It is a high-pressure technique that breaks down biological cells or solid particles to ensure uniform distribution or isolate specific components. This method is recognized for its straightforward implementation and can deliver high yields without requiring pretreatment. It is particularly effective for micronizing PHAs, facilitating their use in various applications. However, the efficiency of this method can depend significantly on biomass concentration, which may influence its scalability and performance in industrial settings.

3.2.3. Ultrasonication:

Ultrasound-assisted extraction uses high-frequency sound waves to disrupt cell walls, enhancing polymer release. This traditional method is cost-effective and sustainable but requires optimization to prevent polymer degradation. , Ultrasonication, or ultrasonic homogenization, utilizes high-frequency sound waves to mechanically disrupt PHA-containing cells, releasing PHA granules without chemical or enzymatic treatment. This method provides a cost-effective and sustainable alternative for PHA extraction. However, pretreatment steps are often necessary to enhance yield and purity adoption.

3.2.4. Innovative Biological Techniques

3.2.4.1. Mealworm-Based Extraction:

Mealworms were used for the PHA extraction study, digesting bacterial cells and excreting PHB granules as fecal pellets. The PHA granules recovered maintain their native spherical morphology and can achieve nearly 100% purity after further purification steps involving water, detergent, and heat. , This method supports a circular bioeconomy, with the protein-rich mealworms repurposed as fish feed. ,

However, the high energy demands of current extraction methods and the environmental hazards of traditional solvents highlight the need for greener, cost-effective alternatives to align with sustainability goals in the bioplastics industry.

3.2.4.2. Biorecovery Strategies:

Promising biorecovery strategies, such as cell lysis, yeast surface display (YSD), and protein-mediated secretion models, optimize fermentation parameters for high-yield engineered strains, while adopting cleaner production principles requires interdisciplinary collaboration to implement greener technologies. ,

3.2.4.3. Enzymatic Extraction:

Enzymatic digestion offers an eco-friendly alternative to chemical methods for PHA extraction, using crude or pure enzymes to break down bacterial cells. It requires low energy and enables high-yield, high-purity aqueous recovery. However, enzyme costs limit scalability. A study using crude enzymes from achieved 98% recovery and 97% purity without chemical solvents, highlighting its potential for sustainable PHA extraction. Enzymes selectively disrupt cell walls, releasing PHAs without harming the polymer. While eco-friendly, the excessive cost of enzymes remains a barrier to widespread adoption. ,

However, enzymatic extraction is somewhat different in the context of other innovative biological techniques (e.g., mealworm-based extraction, yeast surface display (YSD), and protein-mediated secretion models). Those techniques involve metabolic engineering, microbial secretion, or bioassisted recovery strategies, whereas enzymatic extraction typically involves externally applied enzymes.

5.2. End-of-Life (EOL) Environmental Impact of PHAs

PHAs are considered a sustainable solution with attractive EOL properties due to their biodegradability in ambient environments. They are being developed as a sustainable substitute for traditional petroleum-based plastics owing to their renewability and ambient biodegradability, in accordance with the objectives of a circular bioplastics economy. Unlike nondegradable plastics that persist in landfills, PHAs degrade naturally in the environment. However, when landfilled, PHAs may pose challenges such as accumulating degradation intermediates and CO2 emissions, highlighting the need for innovative chemical circularity approaches. At full deployment, composting PHAs globally could release 230 million metric tonnes of CO2 annually, neutralizing the biogenic carbon benefits of their production. PHAs have limitations, including weak mechanical properties, high production costs, and thermal instability, which restrict their applications. These challenges can be mitigated by blending PHAs with biodegradable polymers or natural materials like starch and cellulose derivatives. Additionally, chemical modifications such as graft and block copolymerization improve their mechanical strength, thermal stability, and overall functionality. ,

Advancements in sustainable feedstock utilization also support the eco-friendly production of PHAs. For instance, converts carboxylic acids from acid whey into PHAs, while metabolizes cheese whey into PHAs. These innovative methods not only address waste management challenges but also contribute to the development of environmentally friendly biopolymers. Through continued advancements in material science, chemical circularity, and sustainable feedstock utilization, PHAs are poised to play a pivotal role in reducing global plastic pollution and promoting a sustainable future.

5.3. Economic Challenges and Viability of PHA Production

Despite their environmental advantages, the high cost of PHA production remains a key barrier to widespread adoption. For instance, the cost of producing PHB ranges from 2.60 USD/kg using to 6.69 USD/kg with , underscoring the importance of strain selection in reducing costs. , Production costs are influenced by factors such as carbon source, fermentation efficiency, and downstream processing (DSP). Key factors in downstream processing involve minimizing environmental impact and reducing costs, which can be accomplished through sustainable solvent extraction, solvent recycling, and residue reutilization. However, PHA recovery and purification remain challenging, accounting for 30–50% of total costs. Thus, significant progress has been made in reducing PHA costs. In 2002, PHAs were priced at 9.50 USD/kg compared to 1.06 USD/kg for conventional plastics. By 2019, the cost of PHAs had dropped substantially to 2.62 USD/kg, reflecting advancements in production technologies and increased economic feasibility.

The production cost can be reduced by optimizing fermentation processes, selecting hyper-productive microorganisms, and increasing PHA content in cell biomass. Projects such as PHARIO and RES-URBIS demonstrate how integrating waste streams and VFAs into production can yield high-quality polymers at competitive costs, supporting circular economy principles.

Economic assessments have analyzed producing various sorts of bioplastics across different scales and environments with particular focus on polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHA). For example, the production of PHA using molasses and mixed microbial cultures (MMCs) was estimated to have a manufacturing cost of 994,143 USD with an annual operating cost of 159,711 USD and a payback time of 6.79 years. The process achieved an internal rate of return (IRR) of 16%. Additionally, it was suggested that reducing product costs by 20% could increase overall benefits by 25%.

A simulation study on producing poly­(3-hydroxybutyrate-co-3-hydroxyvalerate) (PHBV) using cheese whey with expected a yield of 9700 MT per year (0.2 g PHBV/g lactose) with 87% conversion efficiency. The breakeven cost was influenced by enzyme and lactose costs, with a minimum of 4 USD/kg PHA. Another study explored Bioplastic production from sewage sludge at municipal wastewater treatment plants. A two-stage process involving (a) dewatering at a small-scale plant and (b) anaerobic digestion at a large-scale plant yielded minimum PHA costs of 2.26 USD/kg and 1.26 USD/kg, respectively. A single-stage process using secondary sludge reduced costs by 19.0% for large plants and 15.9% for smaller plants, mainly through lower capital investment.

Despite their high-profile properties, PHAs remain significantly more expensive than petroleum-based plastics, costing 5–10 times more than conventional materials like polyethylene. The primary contribution of this high cost is from the carbon substrate, which makes up about 50% of the total production expenses. To address this challenge, global research focuses on low-cost feedstocks like agricultural residues, waste streams, and CO2. This approach aligns with circular economic principles by transforming waste into a valuable resource for PHA production.

Cost reduction strategies also optimize fermentation processes through strain selection, cultivation strategies, and genetic engineering to increase PHA yields and efficiency. Efficient downstream processing methods, such as greener solvents, solvent recycling, and residue revalorization, are critical to reducing expenses and minimizing environmental impacts. Scaling up production and integrating PHA manufacturing with other biobased products in a biorefinery model can reduce costs through shared infrastructure and enhanced resource utilization.

5.4. Circular Economy Approaches

The integration of circular economy principles into PHA production exemplifies how sustainability and economic goals can be aligned. But the key issue with the classical circular economy for PHA is that, unlike traditional materials, it prioritizes biodegradability over recyclability, necessitating fresh production instead of material reuse, as current PHA recycling methods, including mechanical and chemical processes, require further research to improve efficiency and reduce environmental impact.

The PHARIO project achieved kilogram-scale PHA production using activated sludge biomass, while the RES-URBIS project utilized urban waste streams to produce high-quality polymers at reduced costs. Similarly, a pilot biorefinery in Treviso, Italy, combined biogas and PHA production using municipal solid waste and thickened sludge, demonstrating a closed-loop system.

The biodegradability of PHA is essential for circularity, enabling its natural decomposition into nontoxic byproducts and returning carbon and other elements to the environment, unlike persistent conventional plastics. PHA depolymerases, which facilitate this process, are also gaining interest as biocatalysts for breaking down PHA fractions in mixed plastic waste. Their enzymatic action in industrial bioreactors or composting facilities presents a potential upcycling pathway, aligning with the circular economy paradigm. −

Utilizing waste materials as feedstocks is a crucial approach to lowering production costs while advancing a circular economy by transforming waste into valuable resources. This strategy reduces dependence on virgin materials, minimizes waste generation, and supports the creation of a closed-loop system. ,

Reusing the digestion solution is an example of a circular economic approach in PHA production. The digestion solution can be recycled as a nitrogen source for PHA production in the purification process using dilute ammonia digestion. This practice reduces the reliance on external alkali, leading to cost savings and improved resource efficiency. Recycling protein-rich liquid streams demonstrate a circular economic approach in PHA production. The liquid stream generated during PHA production, containing microbial proteins, can be reused as a nutrient feedstock for subsequent production cycles. This minimizes the need for fresh nutrients, reduces waste, and supports a closed-loop, sustainable process.

Recovering valuable byproducts is a key strategy in improving the sustainability and economics of PHA production. By integrating PHA production into biorefinery plants, valuable byproducts such as proteins, polysaccharides, pigments, and biofuels can be recovered. This adds economic value and supports the development of a more sustainable bioeconomy. ,

When grown in aquaculture wastewater, the cyanobacteria species can accumulate up to 80% PHA by dry cell weight. , Beyond PHA production, improves water quality for fish farming by reducing nitrate, total organic carbon, biological oxygen demand, chemical oxygen demand, ammonia, nitrite, and orthophosphate levels, fostering a symbiotic relationship where both fish and cyanobacteria benefit. For instance, sp. biomass has been shown to improve growth and color in tilapia and enhance egg yolk pigmentation in chickens. , Integrating aquaculture into PHA production not only provides low-cost nutrients for cyanobacteria cultivation and wastewater remediation but also generates valuable byproducts for animal feed, exemplifying a sustainable and holistic circular economy.

5.4.1.7. Waste Vegetable Oils as Carbon Sources:

The fast-food industry produces waste frying oils in large quantities, which require proper disposal due to their high biological and chemical oxygen demands. These oils serve as effective substrates for producing rhamnolipids and PHAs. Numerous bacterial strains have been documented to synthesize PHAs from waste frying oils, yet the yields are often relatively small when utilizing plant-based oils. Another study revealed the generation of up to 7.6 g/L of PHAs and 10 g/L of rhamnolipids utilizing 47T2. The dominant monomers were C10:0 (47.8% w/w) and C12:0 (15.3% w/w), with unsaturated monomers comprising 26.2% (w/w) of the PHAs. Lipases hydrolyze fats to facilitate carbon transfer to bacterial cells, while rhamnolipids lower surface tension and enhance oil emulsification, improving bacterial access to the carbon source.

5.4.1.8. Methane as a Carbon Source:

Methane, available from diverse and increasingly sustainable sources such as natural gas, biogas, municipal gas, biomass, and urban waste, presents a cost-effective and abundant carbon source for the microbial production of polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHAs), particularly poly­(3-hydroxybutyrate) (PHB). Among methane-utilizing microorganisms, , a Type II methanotroph employing the serine pathway, has emerged as a robust PHB producer. Its versatility in metabolizing additional carbon sources such as acetate, methanol, ethanol, and glucose offers process flexibility. Cultivation strategies such as high cell density cultivation (HCDC) and nutrient limitation (e.g., magnesium or phosphorus) have been shown to enhance PHB accumulation (up to 85% w/w) and biomass yields in both loop and bubble column bioreactors.

Bioreactor design is pivotal in addressing the mass transfer limitations inherent to gaseous substrates. Loop bioreactors and bubble column reactors have been extensively investigated for methane-fed PHB production, with simulation tools like COMSOL and Fluent enabling detailed analysis of gas–liquid mass transfer, hydrodynamics, and microbial kinetics. Novel simulation studies have validated the effectiveness of vertical loop fermenters, achieving <5% deviation from experimental results and identifying an optimal 50:50 methane-to-air ratio for maximal cell growth and PHB accumulation. Comparative analyses of reactor configurations have further emphasized the potential of vertical tubular loop bioreactors (VTLBs), where achieved PHB yields exceeding 50% of cell dry mass.

Despite methane’s economic and environmental appeal, several challenges persist in scaling this process. Key bottlenecks include low gas solubility, high energy demand for gas compression, reactor contamination risk, and cost-intensive downstream processing, particularly under low biomass conditions. Addressing these hurdles through integrated strategies in strain optimization, reactor engineering, nutrient management, and simulation-driven process control is critical to realizing methane-based PHB production as a scalable, circular, and sustainable alternative to petrochemical plastics.

Wastewater is a carbon-rich, cost-effective feedstock that can reduce PHA production costs by 30–50%. MMCs are ideal for wastewater treatment and PHA production due to their metabolic diversity and resilience to environmental fluctuations. Studies have shown successful PHB production from sewage and industrial wastewater, though low COD levels often limit yields. For example, sp. CYR1 produced PHB from sewage, highlighting MMCs’ potential despite substrate limitations. Pilot-scale studies have demonstrated the feasibility of integrating PHA production into wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs). Activated sludge from WWTPs is also a promising feedstock, addressing waste disposal challenges while producing valuable bioplastics. One study achieved a PHA yield of 0.38 g/L of PHA (produced per every gram of volatile suspended solids) by coupling municipal wastewater treatment with sludge fermentation-derived VFAs.

However, scaling up remains challenging due to low carbon concentrations and microbial inhibitors affecting PHA accumulation. Techniques like ultrafiltration, charcoal bed filtration, and demethanolization help remove inhibitory compounds. Further research is needed to optimize wastewater-based PHA production, focusing on cost-effective organic waste sources, PHA-hyper-accumulating microbes, aquaculture wastewater reuse, and the long-term effects of PHA on aquatic species.

5.4.1.10. Advantages and Limitations of Using Waste Materials:

Utilizing waste products for new material synthesis addresses economic and environmental challenges. The use of waste-derived feedstocks for PHA production has proven to reduce costs while promoting sustainability. Life cycle assessment studies have shown that converting whey waste into PHAs is more economically viable than processing it into powder, highlighting the advantages of waste valorization in biopolymer production. PHA yields vary significantly depending on the waste substrate, ranging from 8% to 89.10% (g/g of CDW). Various waste materials, including whey, tallow, starch, vinasse, used coffee ground, wheat, and rice straw, spent wash, bean curd waste, molasses, sugar cane bagasse, fermented mash, and waste frying oils, have been explored as potential feedstocks for PHA production. While utilizing waste materials for PHA production offers environmental and economic benefits, impurities and variations in waste composition significantly impact the final yields. This variability necessitates prior optimization at the laboratory scale before large-scale production whenever a new waste feedstock is introduced, leading to increased costs and uncertainty in yield consistency. For instance, studies have shown that virgin oil results in higher PHA yields than waste frying oils under similar conditions, primarily due to residual contaminants in the waste oil. These inconsistencies highlight the challenges of using waste-derived substrates, necessitating additional processing steps to ensure reliable and efficient PHA synthesis. , While utilizing waste products for PHA biosynthesis offers significant benefits in waste reduction, the resulting PHAs are unsuitable for medical applications that require high purity and nontoxicity. PHAs derived from waste may contain contaminants such as viruses, plasmids, bacteria, and genetic material, limiting their use in medical-grade products. Reported impurities include proteins, lipids, DNA, endotoxins, antifoam agents, and hypochlorite. These impurities need additional washing procedures after the recovery, increasing production costs. However, these cost increases are generally acceptable for medical applications where high purity is essential.

5.4.2. Integrating PHA Production with Industrial Biorefineries for a Zero-Waste Circular Economy:

Integrating polyhydroxyalkanoate (PHA) production with industrial biorefineries such as bioethanol, biodiesel, and food processing facilities enhances resource efficiency and promotes a zero-waste circular economy. This approach repurposes industrial waste streams as cost-effective substrates for microbial PHA synthesis, reducing both production costs and environmental impact. Bioethanol refineries generate lignocellulosic waste, such as sugar cane bagasse, which can be converted into sugars for microbial fermentation. Biodiesel production results in crude glycerol, a valuable carbon source for PHA biosynthesis. Food processing industries produce organic waste, including fruit peels, starch residues, and spent coffee grounds, which can be fermented into volatile fatty acids to support microbial growth and PHA production. By utilizing these waste streams, PHA integration minimizes waste disposal costs, reduces pollution, and enhances the economic feasibility of biorefineries. ,,

Beyond waste utilization, coproduct valorization further strengthens sustainability. Algae-based biorefineries can simultaneously produce biodiesel, high-value compounds like astaxanthin, and PHA, improving overall profitability. In the sugar and bioethanol industries, fuel alcohols, which are byproducts of fermentation, can be repurposed for PHA extraction, making the process energetically self-sufficient. Additionally, integrating PHA production with wastewater treatment allows microorganisms to utilize carbon-rich effluents while contributing to bioremediation. This synergy reduces nutrient loads in wastewater, generates biogas from residual biomass, and supports nutrient recovery for fertilizers. As a biodegradable biopolymer, PHA aligns with circular economic principles, ensuring that EOL products reintegrate into natural cycles instead of contributing to plastic pollution. With packaging, agriculture, and biomedicine applications, PHA plays a vital role in the transition toward a sustainable and waste-free industrial ecosystem. ,

5.5. Innovative Microbial Approaches for Sustainable PHA Production

Innovative approaches like MMCs, halophilic microorganisms, and aquaculture integration are advancing cost-effective and sustainable PHA production.

5.5.1. Use of Mixed Culture of Microbes:

MMCs provide a cost-effective alternative to pure cultures for PHA production, as they do not require sterile conditions. Various bacterial groups, including Proteobacteria, Bacteroidetes, Firmicutes, Acidobacteria, Candidatus, and Saccharibacteria, have demonstrated potential in MMC-based PHA synthesis. Among them, species , are particularly advantageous due to their resilience in extreme conditions, higher growth rates, and uncomplicated PHA extraction, aided by their hydrolytic enzymes. , However, their tendency to sporulate poses a challenge, prompting research into optimizing pH and potassium levels to suppress sporulation and enhance productivity.

A promising application of MMCs is in wastewater treatment plants, where they convert waste residues into PHA, as demonstrated in European projects like PHARIO and RES-URBIS. PHARIO effectively generated PHA on a kilogram scale for more than 10 months utilizing activated sludge and VFA-enriched streams. RES-URBIS uses liquid waste from fruit waste, organic slurry, and excessive sludge.

PHA recovery from MMCs involves various methods, including solvent extraction, mechanical disruption, and biological digestion. The choice depends on PHA type, yield, cost, and purity requirements. Despite their advantages, MMCs face challenges such as optimizing fermentation processes, enhancing yields, and developing cost-effective recovery methods.

Several studies highlight MMC’s potential in utilizing waste streams for PHA production. Reddy (2019) found that sewage-derived PHB had lower yields than olive factory wastewater, emphasizing the need for optimized processes. Gomes Gradi’ssimo (2020) demonstrated PHB accumulation of up to 59% (DCW) from food industry sludge. Zhou (2020) compiled various waste feedstocks for PHA production, including sugar cane molasses and pulp and paper mill waste, noting butyrate as a preferred substrate for PHB production by . Andhalkar (2022) highlighted the potential of halophiles like in open fermentation processes, further supporting MMCs’ role in cost-effective PHA production.

Progresses in omics technologies and computational modeling provide more profound insights to microbial interactions and metabolic pathways, which are crucial for optimizing PHA biosynthesis. Vastano et al. discuss enzyme control in medium-chain-length PHA synthesis, a process further enhanced by omics and computational tools. Additionally, optimizing fermentation conditions, such as nutrient availability and C/N ratios, can be significantly improved through AI-driven high-throughput screening. ,

While challenges remain, integrating MMC-based approaches with emerging technologies holds great promise for scaling up sustainable PHA production, paving the way for a greener plastics industry.

5.5.2. Integration with Aquaculture:

Advancing PHA production involves leveraging renewable waste-derived substrates to reduce costs while optimizing microbial cultivation for higher yields. Innovations in SCP harvesting techniques, sterilization processes, and eco-friendly PHA extraction methods are critical for overcoming current economic and environmental challenges.

The mealworm-based PHA extraction method could be integrated with insect farming operations, potentially creating a circular economy within the aquaculture industry. Mealworms are already being explored as a sustainable protein source for aquaculture feed. Combining this with PHA production could offer additional benefits. ,

5.5.3. Use of Halophilic Microorganisms:

Halophilic microorganisms are promising candidates for sustainable PHA production, offering unique advantages due to their ability to thrive in high-salinity environments. Autolysis, the self-lysis of microbial cells, is another area of research for PHA extraction, particularly in halophilic microorganisms. Their resistance to contamination eliminates the need for energy-intensive sterilization, reducing operational costs. Notable species, such as , , and , exhibit robust growth, high cell density, and efficient PHA recovery through lysis in hypotonic solutions, simplifying downstream processing. , These microorganisms can produce high-value PHA copolymers like PHBV while utilizing seawater, saline wastewater, or low-cost renewable feedstocks such as crude glycerol, waste oils, whey, and acidogenic effluents, significantly reducing freshwater demands and supporting circular economy principles. , Their adaptability to open and continuous fermentation processes further enhances efficiency and cost-effectiveness. Recent advances focus on optimizing cultivation parameters, such as salinity, carbon-to-nitrogen ratio, and temperature, to enhance PHA yields. ,

Halophilic systems support sustainability by coproducing valuable byproducts, such as pigments, that can serve additional markets. Despite their advantages, challenges remain, including the need for specialized bioreactors to prevent corrosion, effective management of hypersaline wastewater, and limited understanding of extremophilic PHA accumulation pathways. By overcoming these barriers, halophilic microorganisms can play a key role in advancing cost-effective and environmentally friendly bioplastics production, offering a scalable solution to plastic pollution and promoting a sustainable bioeconomy.

5.6. Challenges in Market Adoption and Scalability of PHAs

The commercialization of polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHAs) faces significant hurdles, particularly in cost competitiveness, material properties, and market perception. Despite their biodegradability and biobased origin, PHAs remain substantially more expensive than conventional plastics, with production costs ranging from 4 to 7 USD per kg, compared to around 1 USD per kg for petroleum-based polymers. This price gap limits widespread adoption, particularly in cost-sensitive industries. Additionally, while PHAs offer tunable properties, achieving the mechanical and functional performance required for diverse applications remains challenging. The brittleness of P­(3HB), a commonly produced homopolymer, restricts its usability without costly modifications. Consumer unfamiliarity and regulatory inconsistencies further hinder adoption, with limited awareness of PHAs’ benefits and a lack of clear policies governing their integration into existing markets. Supply chain variability, particularly in sourcing consistent and sustainable feedstocks, adds another layer of complexity to market stability. −

Scaling up PHA production from laboratory research to industrial applications presents substantial technical and economic barriers. Large-scale fermentation requires advanced reactor designs, optimized fermentation conditions, and precise control of variables such as oxygenation and pH. Maintaining fermentation performance at scale remains challenging, as factors like reduced oxygen availability can lead to lower microbial productivity. The transition from small-scale extraction methods to cost-effective, industrial-scale downstream processing remains a critical bottleneck, with solvent extraction still being the dominant but environmentally concerning method. Furthermore, economic constraints, including high capital expenditures for infrastructure and operational costs for maintaining sterile cultures, contribute to the difficulty of scaling PHA production. Addressing these challenges necessitates a multifaceted approach, including investment in pilot-scale studies, advancements in metabolic engineering, improved waste-derived feedstock utilization, and more substantial regulatory and financial incentives to support the transition from niche applications to widespread industrial use. However, overcoming the market adoption and scalability challenges of PHAs is essential to realizing their full potential as the greenest plastic. While high production costs, technological barriers, and feedstock variability limit large-scale implementation, advancements in microbial synthesis, optimized recovery methods, and sustainable feedstock utilization can enhance their economic feasibility. Addressing these challenges will not only improve commercial viability but also reinforce PHA’s role in a circular bioeconomy by reducing reliance on fossil-based plastics, minimizing environmental impact, and expanding their applications in sustainable industries. ,

6. Future Directions and Research Opportunities

In 2019, global PHA production was ∼25,000 tons, representing only 1.2% of the bioplastics market. It is expected to quadruple by 2025, reaching 11.5% of the market, despite its limited market share being driven by production costs 5–10 times higher than traditional polymers. , Therefore, addressing challenges in sustainable PHA production, such as reducing costs, scaling processes, and improving downstream recovery, is crucial for competing with petroleum-based plastics. Utilizing waste residues that are locally available as feedstocks can reduce production expenses by as much as 50%. MMCs naturally reduce sterilization costs, offering further economic advantages. However, high costs, greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, and challenges in the complete degradation of polymers remain obstacles. Genetic engineering techniques, such as CRISPR/Cas9, show promise for enhancing Bioplastic production, but effective marketing strategies are also essential to encourage adoption across diverse economic regions.

Extremophilic bacteria, such as halophiles, offer cost-effective solutions by thriving in high-salinity environments, reducing contamination risks, and utilizing industrial waste as feedstocks. Advances in strain engineering, process optimization, and downstream methods are necessary to realize their full potential for sustainable PHA production. Natural biopolymers, such as starch and lignocellulosic fibers, provide promising reinforcement materials, enhancing the mechanical properties of PHAs. , Blending PHAs with other biopolymers, like PLA and TPS, further improves flexibility and durability while maintaining biodegradability standards.

However, apart from the typical challenges that have been discussed over the years, future research on PHA should also focus on mitigating its potential to fragment into microplastics and adsorb pollutants, while also advancing waste management systems and biodegradation processes to ensure complete and environmentally safe degradation, even under anaerobic conditions. This is crucial to fully realize the environmental benefits of PHAs as sustainable alternatives to conventional plastics while minimizing their ecological risks.

Advancing green recovery methods and sustainable feedstock use are vital for PHA production’s economic and environmental viability. Waste valorization transforms agricultural residues and industrial byproducts into valuable feedstocks, supporting a circular economy. , Microbial conversion of CO2 into PHAs offers dual benefits of reducing emissions and producing biobased materials.

Further research is needed to optimize renewable substrate utilization, improve microbial cultivation for higher yields, and develop greener, cost-effective PHA extraction methods, including integrating innovative approaches like mealworm-based systems and biorecovery strategies to address current economic and environmental challenges. Recovery innovations, including DMC and NADES solvents, along with ultrasound-assisted and mealworm-based extractions, present eco-friendly solutions for polymer recovery, though further optimization is needed for scalability. ,

Integrating artificial intelligence and machine learning into bioprocess development could accelerate the process in terms of strain selection, fermentation optimization, and predictive modeling, facilitating more efficient PHA production at scale.

Future research should focus on optimizing PHA recycling methods, particularly the impact of polymer composition on recyclability. While extrusion dominates mechanical and pyrolysis chemical recycling, understanding composition–recycling relationships remains limited. Designing closed-loop systems that enable chemical recycling or upcycling of PHAs into high-value intermediates could further enhance circularity and material efficiency.

Improving PHA material properties and market adoption is critical for broader implementation. Research on monomer composition and blends enhances performance for specialized applications. , Combining PHAs with biopolymers or additives broadens their utility, while policies like the European Green Deal incentivize bioplastics and restrict fossil-based plastics, fostering demand. Future research should also address the standardization of biodegradability assessments, regulatory certifications, and quality benchmarks to ensure safe and consistent deployment of PHAs across industries. Targeting high-value markets, such as medical devices and specialty packaging, can justify higher costs and support niche applications. Together, these strategies position PHAs as a scalable, sustainable alternative, advancing the circular bioeconomy and reducing plastic pollution.

7. Conclusions

The growing urgency to mitigate plastic pollution has driven the search for sustainable alternatives, with polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHAs) emerging as the most promising biodegradable plastic to date. This review has explored advancements in microbial synthesis, recovery, and functionalization, demonstrating how PHAs are evolving beyond conventional bioplastics into high-value materials suitable for diverse applications, including biomedical devices, smart packaging, and industrial sustainability. Synergies with existing industrial biorefineries, such as bioethanol and biodiesel production, can enhance scalability while aligning with circular economic principles.

Despite the advantages, challenges such as high production costs, variable mechanical properties, and environmental trade-offs in downstream processing remain significant obstacles to large-scale commercialization.

In order to overcome these limitations, genetic and metabolic engineering strategies, particularly CRISPR-based modifications, have improved strain efficiency and tailored monomer composition, enabling the development of PHAs with enhanced mechanical and functional properties. Innovations in waste-derived feedstocks and extremophilic microbial systems offer promising pathways to reduce production costs and improve scalability, supporting a more sustainable, circular bioeconomy. Advanced extraction techniques, including green solvents, mealworm-based biorecovery, and enzymatic digestion, further align with sustainability principles by reducing environmental impact. However, challenges in PHA fragmentation into microplastics and their potential adsorption of pollutants highlight the need for improved degradation pathways and waste management strategies to ensure minimal ecological risks.

As PHAs progress from niche bioplastics to multifunctional materials, interdisciplinary collaboration among biotechnology, material science, and environmental policy will be essential to bridge the gap between innovation and commercialization. Market adoption will depend on regulatory support, competitive cost-reduction strategies, and the continued expansion of high-value applications that justify the investment in PHA technology. With sustained research efforts and strategic advancements, PHAs have the potential to become a cornerstone of the bioeconomy, offering a viable solution to reducing plastic waste while advancing sustainable material innovations. Therefore, a concerted effort among researchers, industries, and policymakers is essential to overcome existing challenges, enhance cost-effectiveness, and fully realize the potential of PHAs as a sustainable plastic alternative.
