Abstract

Reactive dyes are well known for their color fastness. However, they also display a tendency toward carcinogenicity and mutagenicity. Hence, among the many methods for their removal from dye wastewater, membrane technology appears to be the most viable. In this work, zwitterionic polymeric nanoparticles poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI) were synthesized via precipitation polymerization following a free radical mechanism using N,N′-methylene bis­(acrylamide) (MBAAm) as a cross-linker and laboratory-synthesized 1-vinyl-3-(3-sulfanatopropyl)-1H-imidazole-3-ium (VSPI) as the monomer. The reaction was carried out in water by utilizing a water-soluble free radical initiator 2,2′-azobis-2-methylpropionamide dichloride (V-50). The resulting nanoparticles were analyzed using FTIR, SEM, EDS, XRD, TGA, zeta potential, DLS, and BET studies. Mixed matrix membranes were fabricated by the incorporation of laboratory-synthesized nanoparticles in the polysulfone (PSf) polymer matrix. Among the series of membranes fabricated, PM-2 showed the highest rejection of the reactive dyes, Reactive Black 5 (RB5 98%) and Reactive Orange 16 (RO16 86%), at 20 ppm concentration along with good pure water permeability of 82.34 L m–2 h–1 bar–1. Hence, this membrane has potential for the treatment of textile wastewater.

Introduction

Modern fast fashion has led to the rise of low-quality textiles that pollute the environment and the increase in textile effluents. Dyes are released into water bodies mainly by textile industries followed by the dyeing industry. The resulting water pollution is detrimental to aquatic life, since it results in decreased light penetration leading to decreased photosynthesis and subsequent reduction in oxygen level. Synthetic dyes have certain advantages over natural dyes since they are more colorfast and give brighter colors compared to the natural ones. Reactive dyes are a subset of synthetic dyes and are considered to be excellent dyes because of their tendency to form covalent bonds with the fibers of the fabric via the reactive groups. Hence, these dyes are not easily removed from the fabric, even in boiling water. However, these dyes are not environmentally friendly as they tend to show carcinogenic and mutagenic properties.

There are many methods to treat textile wastewater, which include photocatalytic degradation, adsorption, enzymatic degradation, and catalytic ozonation. However, among these, membrane technology is superior since the separation process is continuous, no additives are added to the effluent and it is cost-effective. Based on the pore size, there are four main types of membranes, ultrafiltration, microfiltration, nanofiltration, and reverse osmosis. Generally, solutes of lower molecular weight are removed using either nanofiltration or reverse osmosis membranes. , Since these membranes display poor flux and high cost of operation, ultrafiltration membranes containing suitable additives can be employed. The thus fabricated ultrafiltration membranes show good flux coupled with an improved membrane performance.

Zwitterionic nanoparticles have been traditionally used for drug delivery purposes. However, in the recent years, other applications like wastewater treatment, biosensor, antifouling coating, and chemical separation, which make use of the zwitterionic moieties, have been reported. Zwitterionic nanoparticles possess a unique ability to form a layer of water over its surface called a hydration shell due to the electrostatic interactions taking place between the zwitterionic moieties and the water molecules. It is this hydration shell that lends an antifouling property to the zwitterionic nanoparticle.

Zwitterionic polymeric nanoparticles are a subset of zwitterionic nanoparticles. Herein, the nanoparticles are composed of one or more monomers having a zwitterionic moiety and a cross-linker, which undergo a polymerization reaction to give the final zwitterionic polymeric nanoparticles. The polymerization techniques include emulsion polymerization, reversible addition–fragmentation chain transfer (RAFT), and free radical polymerization (FRP). Among these, the FRP method is cost efficient and less time-consuming, and the product is easy to isolate.

The scheme used for the synthesis of zwitterionic nanoparticles was initially outlined by Ibrahim et al. for the production of poly­(MBAAm-co-SBMA) using acetonitrile (ACN) as solvent. This work focuses on the greener synthesis of novel poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI) nanoparticles using N,N′-methylene bis­(acrylamide) (MBAAm) as a cross-linker and 1-vinyl-3-(3-sulfanatopropyl)-1H-imidazole-3-ium (VSPI) as the monomer. The monomer VSPI was synthesized in situ by a method adopted from the one followed by Yang et al., which utilizes a ring opening reaction of 1,3-propane sultone (PS) in the presence of ACN solvent. The synthesis of the zwitterionic nanoparticle poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI) using MBAAm and VSPI was carried out in water instead of the traditionally used ACN, giving rise to the greener synthesis of this class of nanoparticles involving a cross-linker. This was possible by the use of 2,2′-azobis-2-methylpropionamide dichloride (V-50), which is a water-soluble free radical initiator. The synthesis was carried out at a lower temperature (70 °C) compared to the one followed by Ibrahim et al. (100 °C). The reaction was precipitation polymerization, following FRP. The synthesized nanoparticles were characterized by ATR-FTIR, SEM, TGA, XRD, and zeta potential analyses. Due to the greener synthesis of the zwitterionic nanoparticles, they could have potential in biomedical applications.

The first reported application of the zwitterionic moiety in synthetic membranes was by Lee et al. in 2004. The imidazolium complexes of silver, which were zwitterionic in nature, were used for production of composite polyester membranes. The fabricated membranes were utilized for the separation of a mixture of isoprene and n-pentane. Later, Susanto and Ulbricht grafted sulfobetaine methacrylate in the presence and absence of cross-linker N,N′-methylene bis­(acrylamide) using the UV photografting technique to give zwitterionic composite poly­(ether sulfone) (PES) membranes, which showed good antifouling properties. A zwitterionic monomer p-vinylbenzyl sulfobetaine, which was synthesized in situ by Liu et al., was grafted using surface-initiated atom transfer radical polymerization (SI-ATRP) on a cellulose membrane in order to improve compatibility with blood. The first report on the incorporation of zwitterionic nanoparticles for membrane fabrication was by Chu et al., which was used for proton exchange. They used silica nanoparticles coated with zwitterion, which was doped into modified polybenzimidazole to give a hybrid membrane. Ibrahim et al. synthesized zwitterionic polymeric nanoparticles poly­(MBAAm-co-SBMA) in ACN, which were used in the fabrication of mixed matrix hollow fiber membranes of polysulfone (PSf) for dye rejection.

PSf is a thermoplastic polymer with good film-forming properties. Ultrafiltration and microfiltration membranes are fabricated from PSf for applications in the food and dairy industries. It is also used as a support membrane for reverse osmosis and nanofiltration membranes. PSf is prone toward fouling due to its hydrophobic nature. This can be remedied by use of suitable additives, which help to improve the hydrophilic property of the membrane. , One of the additives that can be incorporated into the PSf matrix are zwitterionic nanoparticles. The zwitterionic nanoparticles give rise to a hydration layer, which not only increases the hydrophilicity of the PSf membrane but also helps to improve its antifouling property. Although zwitterionic nanoparticles containing the MBAAm cross-linker have been utilized as additives in PSf membranes, the synthesis of these nanoparticles in aqueous medium and their incorporation into PSf matrix were not attempted before.

In this work, the zwitterionic nanoparticles poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI) synthesized in aqueous medium were incorporated into PSf polymer solution, and the resulting polymer solution was fabricated into flat sheet-mixed matrix membranes. The membranes were subjected to dye rejection studies using 20 ppm of Reactive Orange 16 (RO16) and 20 ppm of Reactive Black 5 (RB5). The addition of the zwitterionic nanoparticles is believed to improve the antifouling property of the PSf membrane by the formation of a hydration layer. The membranes were further analyzed using SEM and AFM and subjected to permeability studies, antifouling studies, and water contact analysis.

Experimental Section

Materials

Polysulfone (PSf, P-3500 Udel) was purchased from Solvay chemicals. 1-vinylimidazole (VI), Reactive Black 5 (RB5), and Reactive Orange 16 (RO16) were procured from Sigma-Aldrich. 1,3-propane sultone (PS) was purchased from Alfa Aesar. N,N′-methylene bis­(acrylamide) (MBAAm), bovine serum albumin (BSA), polyvinylpyrrolidone K30 (PVP), and N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) were procured from Loba Chemie. 2,2-azobis-2-methylpropionamide dichloride (V-50) was procured from Spectrochem. Acetonitrile (ACN) was procured from Merck. Ethanol (EtOH) was purchased from CS analytical reagents. Deionized Ultrapure (Type 1) water was obtained from Millipore Direct-Q 3.

Synthesis of VSPI

The synthetic method was adopted from Yang et al. In a 25 mL single-neck round-bottom flask (RBF), 10 mL of ACN was taken to which VI (1.0 g, 0.96 mL, 1.0 equiv, 10.6 mmol) was added. To this RBF, 1,3-propane sultone (PS) (0.9 g, 0.9 equiv., 7.36 mmol) in 7 mL of ACN was added (Figure ). The reaction mixture was heated in an oil bath at 50 °C for 12 h. After the reaction mixture was cooled to room temperature, the product VSPI was obtained by centrifugation. The product was washed twice with 30 mL of ACN and then dried under vacuum (−600 mmHg) at 40 °C overnight. The yield was 1.3212 g (57.51%). The melting point was 185.2 °C. The product was characterized by 1H NMR (400 MHz, D2O) δ 8.99 (s, 1H), 7.71 (s, 1H), 7.54 (s, 1H), 7.06 (dd, J = 15.5, 8.7 Hz, 1H), 5.72 (dd, J = 15.6, 1.1 Hz, 1H), 5.34 (dd, J = 8.7, 1.1 Hz, 1H), 4.33 (t, 2H), 2.86 (t, J = 1.1 Hz, 2H), 2.32–2.20 (m, 2H) and 13C NMR (101 MHz, D2O) δ 134.69, 128.16, 122.76, 119.60, 109.43, 48.09, 47.14, 24.93.

Synthesis of Poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI) Nanoparticles

MBAAm (1.0 g, 1.0 equiv, 6.4 mmol), VSPI (0.2 g, 0.2 equiv, 0.92 mmol), and V-50 (0.02 g, 0.02 equiv, 0.07 mmol) were transferred to a 100 mL single-neck RBF. To this RBF, an addition of 50 mL of deionized water was made. Nitrogen purging of the reaction mixture was carried out for 15 min in order to expel any dissolved oxygen. A 24 → 19 connector was attached to this RBF, and it was kept in an oil bath for 3 h at 70 °C under N2 atmosphere. The reaction mixture was allowed to cool with stirring for an additional 1 h (Figure ). After cooling the product was isolated by centrifugation at 3000 rpm. The product was washed twice with 30 mL of EtOH to remove the starting materials as well as any unreacted monomers and oligomers. The product was then dried under vacuum (−600 mmHg) for 12 h at 50 °C. The yield obtained was 1.3895 g.

Fabrication of Mixed Matrix Membranes

The polymer solution comprised 18 wt % PSf, 1 wt % PVP, and 84 wt % NMP. Except for the neat membrane that was labeled as PM-0, to the other membranes poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI) nanoparticles were added as 0.25, 0.50, and 0.75 wt % of polymer, which were labeled as PM-1, PM-2, and PM-3, respectively. For the preparation of these membranes, the nanoparticles, along with PVP, were sonicated in NMP solvent before the addition of the polymer. This was done to ensure that the nanoparticles were well dispersed in the polymer solution. The polymer solution, referred to as the dope solution, was stirred at 50 °C for 24 h. The dope solution was then degassed until the air bubbles disappeared and then cast as flat sheet membranes on a glass plate using an automated K-control coater (purchased from UK). The polymer-coated glass plate was then transferred to a coagulation bath containing deionized water for the phase inversion of the membranes to take place over a span of 24 h.

Characterization

VSPI

The structure of VSPI was analyzed by both 1H NMR and 13C NMR using Bruker AVANCE NEO 400 MHz with heavy water (D2O) as solvent. Infrared (IR) spectroscopy was carried out in a Bruker ALPHA I spectrophotometer in attenuated total reflectance (ATR) mode from 400 to 4000 cm–1.

Poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI)

The nanoparticle morphology was ascertained from scanning electron microscopy (SEM), and the elemental composition was ascertained by energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS); both were carried out using Thermo Fisher Scientific Apreo 2. The charge on nanoparticles was ascertained from zeta potential analysis while the size of the nanoparticles in aqueous environment was determined from dynamic light scattering (DLS); both of these analyses were carried out using Zetasizer Nano Malvern Panalytical (serial number: MAL1211069). The analysis of the functional groups present in the nanoparticles was carried out using PerkinElmer Spectrum 2 IR in KBr pellet form from 400 to 4000 cm–1. The crystallinity of the nanoparticles was ascertained from powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) carried out using a Rigaku MiniFlex 600 X-ray diffractometer. The porosity and pore characteristics of the nanoparticles was determined from Brunner–Emett–Teller (BET) analysis (Autosorb iQ-XR, Anton Paar). The thermal stability of the nanoparticles was ascertained from thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) using PerkinElmer TGA 4000 with a heating rate of 10 °C/min from 25 to 900 °C.

Membrane

The cross-sectional as well as the surface view of the membranes was obtained via SEM analysis using JEOL 7610FPlus. For the cross sectional view, the membranes were first fractured by using a blade after dipping in liquid nitrogen. A goniometer (DMe-211Plus, Kyowa) was utilized at room temperature to determine the water contact angle (WCA) using the sessile drop method. Atomic force microscopy (AFM) (Flex-Axiom AFM, Nanosurf) was utilized in the determination of the surface roughness of the membranes under tapping mode where 10 × 10 μm2 of the membrane was scanned. The mechanical stability of the membranes was assessed using a universal testing machine (UTM) (Instron 5967) at a maximum load of 10 N at a load rate 1 mm/min, while the thermal stability of the membranes was determined from 25 to 900 °C at a heating rate of 10 °C/min using TGA (TGA 4000, PerkinElmer). The zeta potential of the membranes was analyzed using an electrokinetic analyzer Anton Paar SurPASS 1 from pH 2 to pH 10.

Permeation and Rejection Study

The permeation and rejection studies were carried out by using a cross-flow system made for flat sheet membranes. The membrane was cut into a circle whose effective area was 28.27 cm2. The permeation study of pure water (J) was carried out at 2 bar pressure after first compacting the membrane at 3 bar pressure for 30 min. The permeability (J, L/m2 h bar) was then calculated using eq .

J=VA×t×P 1

where V is the volume of water (L) flowing through the membrane at time t (h), A is the area of the membrane (m2), and P is the operating pressure (bar).

The same setup was employed for the rejection study. The collection of the permeate was carried out at 2 bar pressure after compaction for 30 min at 3 bar pressure. The concentration of dyes used was 20 ppm of each reactive dye RB5 and RO16. A UV spectrophotometer (Analytik Jena SPECORD S600) was used for analysis of the dye concentration in the feed and permeate. Equation was employed for the calculation of the rejection coefficient of the membranes.

rejectionpercentage=(1−CpCf)×100 2

where C p is the concentration of the permeate and C f is the concentration of the feed.

Water Uptake and Porosity Study

2 × 2 cm2 square pieces of the membranes were cut and immersed for 24 h in distilled water. Later, the excess water on the membrane pieces was removed with tissue paper and the pieces were weighed to give the wet weight. Then, the square pieces of membrane were dried at 60 °C for 24 h. The dried membranes were again weighed to give the dry weight. The percentage water uptake was calculated using eq .

percentagewateruptake=Ww−WdWd×100 3

where W w is the wet weight of the membrane and W d is the dry weight of the membrane.

The percentage porosity (ε) can be calculated using eq

ε=Ww−WdA×l×dw×100 4

where A is the area of the membrane (cm2), l is the thickness of the membrane (cm), and d w is the density of pure water (g cm–3).

Antifouling Study

The antifouling nature of the modified membrane with respect to neat PSf was determined by measuring the amount of BSA adsorbed on the membrane surface. A membrane area of 2 × 2 cm2 was taken and placed in 15 mL of 0.8 mg/mL BSA solution for 24 h. After 24 h, the BSA solution was collected and its concentration was checked using a UV spectrophotometer. The BSA adsorption (Q, μg/cm2) was calculated using eq .

Q=(C1−C2)×VA×1000 5

where C 1 is the BSA concentration of the feed, C 2 is the concentration of BSA after adsorption, V is the volume of BSA solution taken (mL), and A is the total surface area including both sides of the membranes (cm2).

Results and Discussion

VSPI

The structure of VSPI was confirmed by using 1H NMR and 13C NMR (Figure S1). In 1H NMR, the presence of aliphatic hydrogens at 2.32–2.20 ppm (m, 2H), 2.86 ppm (t, 2H), and 4.33 ppm (t, 2H) showed that the ring opening reaction had taken place. The peak at 4.33 ppm (t, 2H) corresponds to the hydrogen present adjacent to the quaternary ammonium moiety and hence displays a pronounced downward shift. The peaks corresponding to the imidazole hydrogens showed singlets instead of doublets. The 13C NMR spectrum also showed the presence of aliphatic carbons at 24.93, 47.14, and 48.09 ppm with the latter corresponding to the carbon adjacent to the nitrogen of the quaternary ammonium group. The IR spectrum (Figure S2) showed peaks at 1162 and 1036 cm–1 corresponding to the SO groups of sulfonate. The peak at 1656 cm–1 corresponds to the CC group, while the peaks at 1556 and 960 cm–1 can be correlated with the C–N quaternary ammonium group.

Nanoparticles

SEM and EDS

From the SEM image, the nanoparticles appear to be aggregated, which can be attributed to electrostatic interactions between the nanoparticles (Figure ). The average diameter of the nanoparticles was found to be 121.5 nm. The EDS analysis revealed the presence of carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, and sulfur showing that the FRP reaction had taken place (Figure S3).

Zeta Potential and Particle Size

The nanoparticles showed a zeta potential of −12.1 mV due to the presence of sulfonate groups (Figure S4). From the DLS measurements, the hydrodynamic diameter was determined to be 4166 nm (Figure S5). This was probably due to the aggregation of the nanoparticles due to the presence of adsorbed water on the surface of the nanoparticles.

FTIR

The functional groups present in poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI) nanoparticles can be determined using FTIR, which is presented in Figure . The CC bending vibration at 988 cm–1 seen in MBAAm is absent in the nanoparticle, implying that the polymerization reaction is complete. In the nanoparticle, a peak at 954 cm–1 is seen, which correlates with the stretching vibration of the C–N bond of the quaternary ammonium group while the peak at 1037 cm–1 corresponds to the SO stretching vibration of the sulfonate group in the nanoparticle arising, both of which arise from the VSPI moiety. The C–N stretching vibration of the amine at 1216 cm–1 and the C–H stretching vibration of the methylene group at 1371 cm–1 are seen in both MBAAm and the nanoparticle. The stretching vibration at 1739 cm–1 corresponds to the CO of the amide. At 3444 cm–1, a peak is observed, which corresponds to the N–H stretching vibration.

X-ray Diffraction

The XRD data shows a single peak at 23.5°, implying that the nanoparticle is amorphous in nature (Figure S6).

BET

The BET surface area was determined to be 61.3 m2/g, the mean pore radius was 16.851 Å, and the pore volume was 0.131 cm3/g. The hysteresis loop was that of the H3 type, with pores having a wedge-shaped morphology (Figure S7).

Thermal Stability

One of the methods for the determination of thermal stability of a compound is by TGA. Four steps were seen and are presented in Figure . The first step is from 30 to 106 °C corresponding to the loss of water adsorbed on the surface of the nanoparticle. The second step from 250 to 282 °C corresponds to the loss of quaternary ammonium groups. Hence, the nanoparticles show good stability up to 250 °C. The third step, from 342 to 435 °C, corresponds to the loss of functional groups containing oxygen, which are more stable compared to the quaternary ammonium group. Above 435 °C, the complete degradation of the nanoparticle, leading to carbonization, takes place.

Membranes

SEM

The cross section of the polymeric membranes subjected to SEM analysis helped to determine the membrane morphology, as presented in Figure . All membranes showed an asymmetrical structure with a dense top layer and a porous sublayer. The poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI) nanoparticle-incorporated membranes showed a more porous structure compared to the neat membrane. The membranes PM-0, PM-1, and PM-3 showed the presence of vertical finger-like projections with PM-1 and PM-3 membranes additionally displaying horizontal macrovoids, while the PM-2 membrane showed a sponge-like sublayer structure, which is associated with a higher flux and better rejection of larger molecules like dyes. , It was observed that the PM-1 membrane showed larger horizontal marvoids compared with the PM-3 membrane. This reduction in the size of the horizontal macrovoids could be attributed to the increased concentration of the poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI) nanoparticles, which increased the viscosity of the dope solution, thereby decreasing the solvent and non solvent exchange rate in the phase inversion process.

The SEM analysis of the surface of the membranes was also carried out (Figure S8), which revealed that the addition of the nanoparticles increased the pore size on the surface of the membranes. The PM-2 membrane showed an anomaly of having an average pore size of 1 nm, which was greater than that of the PM-0 membrane (0.23 nm) but lesser compared to that of PM-1 and PM-3 (2 and 5 nm, respectively). This shows that the 0.5 wt % loading of the poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI) nanoparticles has a synergistic effect on the PSf membrane by improving the pore size compared to the neat PM-0 membrane while also showing the optimal pore size for better selectivity toward dyes.

Permeability Studies

The permeability of water through a membrane is determined by the hydrophilicity of the membrane. The addition of poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI) nanoparticles showed an increase in the permeability of the PSf membrane arising from the improvement of the hydrophilic property of the PSf membrane, as seen in Figure . PM-2 showed the highest permeability of 82.34 L m–2 h–1 bar–1 compared to PM-0, which showed a permeability of 5.81 L m–2 h–1 bar–1 (Figure a). This could be explained by the presence of sulfonate and quaternary ammonium groups of the zwitterionic nanoparticle, which display an electrostatic interaction with the water molecules, leading to greater hydrophilicity of the mixed matrix membranes. The permeability of the PM-2 membrane was repeatedly tested to ensure its reproducibility. Interestingly, the permeability dropped after PM-2, with PM-3 showing a pure water permeability of 13.68 L m–2 h–1 bar–1. This could be attributed to the agglomeration of nanoparticles, which leads to the blocking of the pore channels leading to an increased resistance toward the permeation of water.

The permeability study of water from aqueous solution of an antifouling agent such as BSA through the membrane series was also carried out. Again, PM-2 showed the highest permeability compared to the other membranes, which is presented in Figure b.

The time-dependent permeability of water through the membranes (Figure c) shows a decline over time due to the compaction of the membranes resulting in the deformation of the membrane polymer matrix. The time-dependent water permeability of the BSA solution through the membranes also follows a similar trend. However, the effect is more pronounced in the case of PM-2, which could be attributed to the blocking of some of the pores of the membrane by BSA molecules (Figure d).

Rejection Studies

The rejection of dyes RB5 and RO16, as well as the protein BSA, was carried out. The concentration of the dyes used for carrying out the rejection studies was 20 ppm, since at a lower concentration, there was no appreciable difference in the rejection capacity of the neat and the modified membranes. The PM-2 membrane containing 0.50 wt % of the nanoparticles showed the maximum rejection of 98% RB5 and 86% RO16 compared to the pristine PM-0 membrane, which showed rejection values of 82% for RB5 and 63% for RO16 at pH 7, as presented in Figure . The better rejection by the mixed matrix membrane in contrast to the neat PSf membrane can be attributed to the Donnan exclusion. The better rejection of RB5 compared to RO16 is due to the higher molecular weight of the RB5 dye molecule compared to the RO16 dye molecule.

Along with the rejection study, the permeability of the dyes was also checked, which was similar to the trend observed for pure water permeability.

PM-2 showed BSA protein rejection of 99% compared to the neat PM-0 membrane, which showed 91% rejection (Figure ). This is remarkable since PM-2 shows better BSA permeability compared to PM-0 and at the same time shows better BSA rejection. This is true for PM-1 as well as PM-3 membranes. Hence, the addition of zwitterionic nanoparticles improves the ability of the membrane to reject charged species based on Donnan exclusion.

Zeta Potential of the Membrane

The better rejection of the PM-2 membrane compared with the neat PM-0 membrane can be expounded by understanding the surface charge possessed by these membranes under various pH conditions. From Figure , it can be observed that from pH 10 until pH 4, the PM-2 membrane showed a greater negative zeta potential compared to PM-0. At neutral pH (pH 7), it can be seen that PM-2 shows a zeta potential of −16.48 mV compared to −13.31 mV shown by PM-0. The higher negative zeta potential of PM-2 helps in the better rejection of the anionic dyes due to electrostatic repulsion. The negative zeta potential of the PM-2 membrane is also responsible for the better antifouling property compared to PM-0 while also possessing relatively greater BSA rejection capacity.

Porosity and Water Uptake Studies

The increase in the concentration of poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI) nanoparticles showed a corresponding increase in the porosity of the membranes. The highest pure water uptake and porosity of 75.46% and 54.48%, respectively, were seen in PM-2 while the pristine PM-0 membrane showed water uptake of 73.57% and porosity of 35.87% (Table ). Hence, the addition of poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI) nanoparticles improved the porosity and water uptake of the PSf membrane. However, the excess of nanoparticles results in the decrease of pure water uptake and porosity as seen in PM-3 due to the agglomeration of the nanoparticles in the membrane channels.

Water Contact Angle Measurement

The measurement of the WCA helps to determine the hydrophilicity of the membrane. In principle, the lower the contact angle, the greater the hydrophilicity of the surface. This is because as the hydrophilicity of a material increases, it reduces the surface tension of the water droplet at the interface, decreasing the contact angle. From Table , it can be seen that the WCA is the lowest for the PM-2 membrane. The contact angle decreases from PM-0 to PM-2 and then increases for PM-3. The zwitterionic entities composed of the quaternary ammonium and sulfonate groups improve the hydrophilicity of the membrane. , In the PM-2 membrane, which contains 0.50 wt % of poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI), the hydrophilicity of the membrane is optimized while in the PM-3 membrane, the increased addition of the nanoparticles results in their aggregation, leading to the decrease in the hydrophilicity of the membrane. The lower loading of the nanoparticles in the PM-1 membrane does not show a significant improvement in the hydrophilicity of the membranes since smaller concentrations of the nanoparticles do not bring about the synergistic effect on the membrane matrix.

Surface Roughness

The surface roughness of the fabricated membranes was measured using AFM in tapping mode. The AFM images show the presence of troughs and peaks, as seen in Figure . The different roughness parameters that were taken into consideration included average roughness (R a), root-mean-square roughness (R q), and maximum peak to valley roughness (R max). From Table , it can be ascertained that PM-2 shows lesser surface roughness compared to that of PM-0. This can be attributed to the inclusion of nanoparticles in the membrane matrix, which shift toward the surface of the membrane during phase inversion. , Membranes having greater surface roughness are more prone to fouling due to the tendency of the foulants to accumulate in the troughs. This can lead to irreversible fouling, which can be rectified by chemical cleaning methods. Hence, a smoother membrane surface is less prone toward fouling. However, in Table , it is noticed that the surface roughness PM-3 is slightly higher than that of PM-2. This is due to the cluster formation of nanoparticles on the membrane surface due to the increase in the amount of nanoparticles incorporated in the polymer matrix.

Thermal and Mechanical Stability

The thermal stability of the PM-0 and PM-2 membranes was determined using TGA. From the thermogram (Figure S11), it was observed that there was a negligible difference in the thermal stability of the PSf membranes by the addition of the poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI) nanoparticles since a very small fraction of the nanoparticles is incorporated into the membrane matrix. The degradation temperatures for PM-0 and PM-2 were 550 and 552 °C, respectively, which is within the limit of error.

The mechanical stability of the PM-0 and PM-2 membranes was determined by means of UTM via tensile strength testing mode (Figure S12). The PM-0 membrane showed a greater tensile strength of 1.36 MPa compared to the PM-2 membrane, which showed a tensile strength of 0.82 MPa. This could be due to the increased porosity of the PM-2 membrane in comparison to the pristine PM-0 membrane. However, another interesting observation was that the PM-2 membrane showed greater elongation (6%) compared to the PM-0 membrane (3.5%), which implies that the nanoparticles help increase the elasticity of the membranes.

Antifouling Study

The antifouling study using 0.8 mg/mL BSA showed that membranes having the zwitterionic nanoparticles showed a greater antifouling property compared to the neat membrane. The antifouling property of the membrane improved with increased concentration of poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI) nanoparticles from 0.25 to 0.75 wt %, as seen in Figure . The neat membrane PM-0 showed adsorption of 151.66 μg/cm2, which was nearly double compared to PM-2 (76.53 μg/cm2). This can be attributed to the zwitterionic moiety of the nanoparticles, which imparts an antifouling property to the membranes. This property arises due to the hydrogen bonding between the positive and negative groups of the zwitterion with the water molecules, which gives rise to a hydration layer. The zwitterionic nanoparticles also form intermolecular bonds with the polysulfone matrix, which includes hydrogen bonds, van der Waals forces, and Π–Π interactions. Conclusions

Precipitation polymerization was employed for the synthesis of zwitterionic poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI), which followed a free radical mechanism. The nanoparticles were amorphous in nature with good thermal stability and a surface area of 61.3 m2/g. The nanoparticles were incorporated into the polymer matrix of polysulfone to give mixed matrix membranes. Various properties of the membrane like WCA, water uptake, porosity, zeta potential, surface roughness, antifouling property, and morphology of the cross section were analyzed. The PM-2 membrane displayed the highest pure water permeability of 82.34 L m–2 h–1 bar–1. The highest rejection of RB5 (98%) and RO16 (86%) at a concentration of 20 ppm at neutral pH was exhibited by PM-2. Thus, the poly­(MBAAm-co-VSPI)-incorporated mixed matrix membrane has the potential for application in the treatment of textile wastewater.
