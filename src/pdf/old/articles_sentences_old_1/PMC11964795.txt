Nuclear Overhauser effect (NOE) MRI has been used for in vivo brain imaging to assess lipid and protein composition and benefits from 7 T field strengths due to the larger chemical shift dispersion.
However, a continuing challenge is signal drop off observed in regions such as the medial temporal lobes due to â€œstanding waveâ€ effects from shorter radiofrequency (RF) wavelengths at ultraâ€high fields.
2D periodic unit cell metasurfaces have been a promising approach for providing improvements in anatomical imaging but have not yet been evaluated in chemical exchange saturation transfer (CEST)â€based sequences.
Here, we report the use of metasurfaces for enhancement of NOE imaging as well as for improvement of Lorentzian line fitting of full Zâ€spectrum data.
3D NOE image data, B1+maps, and B0maps were acquired on five healthy volunteers using a 7 T MRI system with and without metasurfaces positioned near the temporal lobes.

NOEMTRâ€weighted contrast maps were also calculated via Zâ€spectrum asymmetry analysis.

Nuclear Overhauser effect (NOE) MRI is a promising technique for assessing lipid and protein content within the brain by leveraging dipolar cross relaxation between saturated and unsaturated spins systems and producing endogenous contrast [1,2,3].
By repeating this measurement across a range of frequency offsets, a voxelâ€based Zâ€spectrum can be generated which includes signal contributions from the exchange of various functional groups such as aliphatic, amine, and amide protons (APT).
Additionally, the saturation of free (direct saturation) and restricted (magnetization transfer) water molecules also contributes substantially to the overall signal.
These additional contributions from direct saturation and magnetization transfer effects can make quantification of smaller pools, such as amine, APT, and relayed NOE (rNOE) less accurate when using asymmetry analysis.
Multiâ€pool Lorentzian line fitting models have been employed in the past to separate out these contributing signals, leaving the previously mentioned metabolite pools for individual assessment in both healthy and diseased populations [4,5,6].
Furthermore, NOE magnetization transfer ratio (NOEMTR) provides an additional derived contrast from this imaging technique that combines the aggregate effect from direct saturation and magnetization transfer, which has been applied to imaging of highâ€grade brain tumors, ischemic stroke injury [7], Parkinson's disease [8], and Alzheimer's disease [9,10].
The application of in vivo full Zâ€spectrum acquisition at ultraâ€high field (â‰¥7 T) strengths allows for substantial increases in SNR and spatial resolution as well as increasing chemical shift displacement, making it exceptionally useful in chemical exchange saturation transfer (CEST) based techniques, such as NOE [11].
However, a continuing challenge at these field strengths is the reduced transmit (B1+) field homogeneity, leading to signal degradation [12,13].
In neuroimaging applications where there is a strong relation between the signal and B1+field strength, such as for NOE imaging, the temporal lobes typically show a large amount of signal drop off.
Current mitigation strategies for dealing with B1+inhomogeneity have included the use of CEST specific postâ€processing corrections [5,14] as well as parallel transmit (pTx) techniques [15,16].
However, postâ€processing corrections can be unreliable in anatomical regions below a certain B1+threshold while specific absorption rate (SAR) restrictions for pTx hardware can limit use beyond the research space.
Dielectric pads have long been an effective technique for improving not only SNR in anatomical images [17,18,19] but also contrast uniformity in CESTâ€based techniques [20].
Previous work by our group showed that dielectric padding, in combination with standard linear postâ€processing correction, help mitigate B1+inhomogeneity issues leading to improved NOEMTRcontrast values in the brain at 7 T [21].
However, dielectric pads come with several drawbacks including a limited shelf life (12â€“18 months), limited corrective efficacy due to the permittivity limits of available materials, and bulky size that hinders placement inside the already space constrained 7 T radiofrequency (RF) coils.
Metasurfaces and metamaterials provide an alternative means for improving B1+homogeneity.
They function similarly to dielectric pads by supporting induced RF currents in the 2D or 3D periodic unit cell structures which generate a secondary RF field that can enhance the B1+field in regions of drop off [22,23].
Notable in vivo applications of this class of device at 1.5 T and 3 T include anatomical imaging of the wrist [24,25], knee [26], calf [27], and abdomen [28].
Additionally, metasurfaces for 7 T applications have also seen increased utilization including brain imaging [29,30], spectroscopy [31], and, recently by our group, in calf imaging [32].
However, neither metasurfaces nor metamaterials have been assessed in the context of CESTâ€based techniques, including NOE imaging.
In the present work we utilize a previously demonstrated metasurface design to assess the corrective effects of the metasurface on B1+inhomogeneities, NOEMTR, and multiâ€pool metabolite fitting derived contrasts not only on the entire imaging slab but also locally in the temporal lobes.

Metasurfaces increased the mean transmit efficiency values across subjects and decreased the B1+variability without introducing any unwanted offâ€resonance effects.
This was particularly evident when assessing the temporal lobe ROI which alone saw a 55.8% increase in B1+magnitude.
The improvements in transmit efficiency values were partially driven by lower transmit reference voltages needed for the metasurfaces to produce the same effective B1+field in comparison to the reference condition [23].
The other mechanism for transmit efficiency improvement comes from the additional secondary field induced via conduction currents that can sum with the primary currents.
This was seen not only as a substantial increase in the temporal lobe regions but also as a slight decrease in the central and superior brain regions.
Resulting qualitative contrasts improvements were seen in both the fit amplitude maps and in the NOEMTRderived contrast maps, particularly in the lateral temporal lobes where the largest amount of signal drop off typically occurs.
We were also able to establish intrasubject repeatability by consistently showing the in vivo enhancing effects across five subjects.
In previous work by our group, we showed that a set of conventional calcium titanate dielectric pads [19], in combination with a postâ€processing linear B1+correction, could provide more enhancement for NOEMTRimaging than dielectric pads alone [21].
When using the metasurfaces, we opted to incorporate the same multiâ€faceted correction approach using linear B1+correction for all image data.
The metasurfaces enhanced the B1+field in regions of low magnitude, particularly in the lateral temporal lobes, increasing the overall effective B1+distribution above a certain minimum threshold (~0.03Î¼T/V) and into a regime that is more suited for linear correction, as linear correction is not suitable for wider ranges of B1+magnitude [40].
In the case of the multipool Lorentzian fitting, the general model parameters used are more stable above a minimum B1+threshold.
When fitting the Zâ€spectrum, data in regions where the B1+is below this threshold, the model will attempt to overestimate the fit in certain pools and will appear nonuniform in the resulting contrast maps.
By increasing both the magnitude and homogeneity of the B1+field, the resulting amplitude maps appear more uniform in image contrast.
It should be noted that differences in MT and rNOE contrasts can be explained due to rNOE being a smaller fit peak compared to the MT peak as well as rNOE being more associated with mobile macromolecules likely associated with unsaturated fatty acids in the myelin membrane and neuronal cell body plasma membrane lipids.
Additionally, the APT signal in white matter appears to be lower than in grey matter due to both the overall lower concentration of proteins in the white matter and white matter proteins being significantly less mobile than those in the grey matter.
The metasurfaces described here utilized an inductive mesh on a flexible PCB substrate which incorporated 11 pF of added capacitance to tune the response of the metasurfaces.
With the relative simplicity of the design, involving only a single capacitance value, these metasurfaces could be optimized in a straightforward manner.
However, because our design is based on parallel plate capacitances, this may involve increases in substrate losses, in comparison to designs that use conductor traces corresponding to a specific RF wavelength fraction [25,34,41].
These additional losses may negatively affect the overall transmit efficiency and, by reciprocity, result in an increased amount of noise present in the images.
Furthermore, if placed improperly these metasurfaces can also produce regions of decreased image contrast due to potential reductions in the B1+field strength.
Varying the amount of capacitance across the metasurface to induce positionâ€based changes in unit cell reactance may be able to compensate for this [42].
Limitations present in this work include the acquisition only encompassing a relatively small 20 mm thick slab, precluding the assessment of whole brain NOE enhancements.
Additionally, due to the conservative 50% SAR constraint, sequences utilizing high SAR levels (higher B1+powers), such as glutamateâ€weighted CEST (GluCEST), could not be assessed with the metasurfaces at present.
However, since dielectric pads have been shown to be effective at enhancing GluCEST contrast [20], and the metasurfaces function similarly to dielectric pads, it would be expected that they would provide a similar (if not higher) level of GluCEST contrast enhancement.
This also holds true for CEST based contrasts using lower powers, such as hydroxylâ€CEST.
Additional RF safety assessments including phantom temperature measurements and electromagnetic simulations in heterogenous body models are therefore warranted to reduce the safety margin for such techniques.
Additionally, since metasurfaces are manually placed near the subject's head, small variations between left and right temporal lobe enhancement may be present.
Future studies using this metasurface design and modified versions include homogenizing the B1+field for creatineâ€weighted CEST (CrCEST) in the calf at 7 T as well as designing a version that fully encapsulates the head, which is expected to allow further B1+homogeneity gains.
We demonstrated the first use of flexible metasurfaces for enhancing in vivo CESTâ€based brain contrast at 7 T in the form of NOEMTRcontrast and multipool Lorentzian fitting.
Transmit efficiency maps showed an increase in both medial temporal lobe B1+magnitude (55.8% increase) and overall, across the imaging slab (9.6% increase) without the introduction of offâ€resonance effects.
Use of the metasurfaces yielded benefits in the NOEMTRcontrast maps by increasing medial temporal lobe signal and in the multipool amplitude maps (MT, APT, amine, and rNOE) by reducing image saturation and model overfitting.
Overall, this work has shown that metasurfaces not only improve anatomical based structural imaging but also CESTâ€based techniques at low RF powers.
