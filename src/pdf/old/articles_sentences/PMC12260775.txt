A multiband dualâ€VENC 2D PCâ€MRI (MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI) technique is proposed to simultaneously measure pulsatility index (PI) at multiple cerebral arterial segments within a single short scan.
The MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI demonstrates good reliability in PI measurements as well as damping factor (DF) along the cerebrovascular tree.
Arterial pulsatility is an established indicator of cardiovascular health that characterizes arterial flow pulsations due to the heartbeat, reflecting both local arterial stiffness and downstream vascular impedance.
Increased arterial pulsatility causes the transmission of excessive pulsatile energy into downstream arteries, inducing the dysfunction of microcirculation, which can result in vascular brain injuries, such as white matter lesions and enlarged perivascular spaces [1,2,3], and subsequently lead to cognitive deficits.
Therefore, arterial pulsatility has emerged as a sensitive marker of cognitive impairment and dementia [2,4].
Growing evidence indicates that directly assessing cerebral arterial pulsatility could provide better insight into cerebrovascular pathology [5,6,7] in Alzheimer's disease and small vessel disease.
Comprehensively assessing cerebral arterial pulsatility along the cerebrovascular tree could help further elucidate the mechanisms of cerebrovascular pathologies.
Arterial pulsatility is quantified by the pulsatility index (PI), which is calculated as the difference between the peak systolic and minimum diastolic flow velocities divided by the mean velocity throughout the cardiac cycle.
Cerebral arterial pulsatility is traditionally measured by transcranial Doppler (TCD) ultrasonography [8] and phaseâ€contrast MRI (PCâ€MRI) [9].
TCD measures blood flow velocities and PIs of larger intracranial arteries, for example, the middle cerebral artery (MCA).
However, TCD is operatorâ€dependent and relies on the availability of acoustic windows through the skull [10], and it is challenging to detect distal cerebral arteries with TCD.
PCâ€MRI has recently become a useful imaging tool to directly quantitatively measure arterial blood velocities across the cardiac cycle by utilizing a bipolar gradient to encode velocity through flowâ€induced phase shift along the gradient direction [11].
Cardiac gated timeâ€resolved 2D PCâ€MRI has been successfully applied to measure arterial blood flow velocities over a cardiac cycle to measure the PI of the internal carotid artery (ICA), vertebral artery (VA), and the major branches of the circle of Willis by placing the imaging plane perpendicular to the imaged vessel.
Studies have reported that PIs measured by 2D PCâ€MRI are linked to hypoperfusion and cognitive impairment [5,6,9,12].
However, to assess cerebral arterial pulsatility across different segments of the cerebral vasculature, multiple separate 2D PCâ€MRI scans are required to cover different vessel segments or levels [13].
4D flow [14], a timeâ€resolved 3D PCâ€MRI technique, provides volumetric image acquisition, allowing for the assessment of cerebral arterial pulsatility from multiple segments in a single scan [15,16].
However, given the tradeoff between scan time and spatial resolution, 4D flow studies have primarily assessed PIs of the major branches of the circle of Willis as well as the ICA and VA.
Given the limitations of both conventional 2D PCâ€MRI and 4D flow for assessing cerebral arterial pulsatility, this study is aimed at developing a timeâ€efficient, highâ€resolution multiband dualâ€VENC 2D PCâ€MRI (MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI) technique to simultaneously assess cerebral arterial pulsatility across multiple brain vessel segments within a single short scan.
Compared to singleâ€VENC, dualâ€VENC can enhance the velocityâ€toâ€noise (VNR) ratio defined as the ratio of measured velocity to the standard deviation of the velocity [17,18] and thus improve the accuracy of velocity measurements across a wide velocity range, which is suitable for cerebral velocity measurements across vessel segments.
The feasibility of PI measurements by MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI was evaluated by simulation and comparison with conventional singleâ€band 2D PCâ€MRI.
A testâ€“retest study was conducted to evaluate the reproducibility of PI measurements using MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI.
Additionally, the damping factor (DF), another useful index of vascular dysfunction, can be derived as the ratio of PI measurements between proximal and distal vessels, which reflects the damping of pulsatile energy down the arterial tree [19].
The sensitivity of MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI in detecting aging effects on both cerebral PI and DF was investigated.

Maximum intensity projection (MIP) image generation.
An MIP image was generated by extracting the maximum signal intensity per voxel from the complex difference images throughout a cardiac cycle.
Lowâ€VENC data were used for generating MIP images for each slice, as they offer an improved VNR, resulting in better delineation of small distal arteries with slow flow.
Region of Interest (ROI) selection.
Given their relatively bigger vessel sizes and good contrast with background tissue, the vessel masks of C1 and C2, V3 and V4, M2/3, and A2/3 were manually drawn.
For the distal arteries (M4, A4, and P4) in Slice 4, the vessel mask was generated automatically usingKmeans clustering [25] followed by a careful visual inspection.
The generated mask was then overlaid on the axial TOF MIP image to further divide the vessel mask into MCA, ACA, and PCA territories.
Vessels that were relatively parallel to the imaging slice were excluded if their crossâ€sectional length was at least twice the width [26].
To minimize the potential contamination of veins in Slice 4, vessels that showed negative velocities were also excluded, as venous flow is generally opposite to arterial flow.
The refined masks were then used for the subsequent analyses.
Background phase offset correction.
All phase images throughout a cardiac cycle were averaged to yield a mean phase image and standard deviation.
The background phase was extracted based on the standard deviation, and then polynomial fitting was performed to estimate the noise [27].
Subsequently, the resulting noise image was subtracted from the phase image of each cardiac phase.
All statistical analysis was performed in OriginPro (Version 2023b, OriginLab, Northampton, Massachusetts) and MATLAB (Version 2022b, MathWorks, Natick, Massachusetts).

In the present study, we developed a timeâ€efficient, highâ€resolution multiband dualâ€VENC PCâ€MRI technique that incorporates SMS and dualâ€VENC into the PCâ€MRI acquisition to achieve simultaneous measurements of PI across multiple cerebral arterial segments within a single short scan.
Our work demonstrated the feasibility of using MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI to assess arterial pulsatility as well as DF across multiple vessel segments in the brain.
Both testâ€“retest and crossâ€validation studies demonstrated good reproducibility and reliability in PI measurements using the proposed MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI.
A pilot aging study showed overall strong correlations between age and PI measurements at different vessel levels, which are consistent with the previous study [24,31].
Additionally, altered DF along the cerebral arterial tree was observed in the elderly adults.
These findings suggest that MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI could be a promising MRI technique to study cerebral arterial pulsatility.
Previous studies have utilized separate singleâ€slice 2D PCâ€MRI scans to study arterial pulsatility of major cerebral arterial segments [9,32].
However, the separate scans potentially suffer from interscan cardiac and other physiological variations and also potentially increase the total acquisition time.
Alternatively, 4D flow can be naturally used to assess cerebral arterial pulsatility across multiple vessel segments given its volumetric acquisition [15,16].
However, 4D flow inevitably compromises spatiotemporal resolution due to the extended scan time.
Assessing PI of distal cerebral arteries as a group using 4D flow with nonâ€Cartesian acquisition has been reported by Vikner et al. [31], which still suffered from the inherent limitations of 4D flow, such as the compromised spatiotemporal resolution due to the long scan time and limited VENC selection.
Additionally, distal small vessels may be challenging to reliably resolve due to stronger saturation effects of 4D flow.
Compared to conventional 2D PCâ€MRI and 4D flow, MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI enables simultaneous measurement of PIs across multilevel brain vessels at a higher spatial resolution in a single scan.
This approach effectively eliminates interscanâ€induced cardiac variations and also mitigates the saturation effects on slow flows in distal small arteries.
Our results demonstrate that MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI provides reliable PI measurements for both larger and smaller arteries.
In theory, 2D PCâ€MRI with 3D velocity encoding can sufficiently measure actual flow velocities for PI calculation when the blood vessel is not perpendicular to the imaging plane.
However, there is always a tradeoff between the temporal resolution, velocity encoding directions, and scan time.
In this study, 1D throughâ€plane velocity encoding was applied in MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI.
Both simulation and in vivo results demonstrate that PI estimation primarily depends on the image SNR and is relatively resistant to vessel tilting angle when SNR > 20 dB.
As the overall SNR of our in vivo data was greater than 30 dB, no big effect of vessel tilting was expected on PI measurements, which are also in good accordance with previous findings [33].
The SMS or MB technique has been widely used in fMRI and diffusion tensor imaging to shorten the scan time and improve the temporal resolution [34].
More recent SMS has been introduced in PCâ€MRI to improve imaging efficiency and flow velocity assessment [35,36,37].
In the present study, we evaluate the feasibility of PCâ€MRI with SMS for simultaneous cerebral arterial pulsatility measurements across multiple brain vessel segments.
Different from conventional SMS design with equalâ€spaced multislice positioning, varied gaps between adjacent slices were proposed in MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI, allowing for flexibly adjusting slice positions based on the vessel geometry.
CAIPIRINHA was implemented to increase the coil sensitivity profile differences for better image separation [20].
Our results showed that the simultaneously excited slices were adequately separated without apparent aliasing artifacts (Figure1).

In conventional phaseâ€contrast techniques, a single VENC is typically chosen above the anticipated maximum blood flow velocity to avoid phase aliasing during velocity calculation, which, however, makes PI measurement potentially unreliable, especially when measuring low velocities due to the insufficient VNR.
Applying a lower VENC can enhance the VNR, as the noise level is directly proportional to the VENC.
However, phase antialiasing errors can occur when measured flow velocities are much higher than the VENC.
Previous studies have demonstrated that PCâ€MRI with dualâ€VENC can effectively antialias the phase wraps in the low VENC data by referencing the highâ€VENC phase information while achieving higher VNR [18,38].
Therefore, dualâ€VENC can improve the delineation of vascular flow velocities across a wide range.
In this study, PIs across multiple cerebral arterial segments were assessed, in which the diameter of cerebral vascular trees varies from 0.75 to 4.67 mm, along with a wide velocity distribution [39,40,41].
To improve the delineation of flow velocity waveforms across different cerebral arterial segments, a dualâ€VENC module was employed in the proposed sequence.
As expected, velocity waveforms across different vessel segments were clearly depicted (Figure3).
Reliable PI measurements in all larger, medium, and small arteries were obtained using the MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI.
However, it is worth noting that the interpretation of velocity measurements using the proposed technique should be cautious, given the presence of partial volume effects and potential vessel tilting.
Similar to conventional 2D PCâ€MRI, in MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI, the greater arteries, such as the ICA, VA, M2/3, and A2/3, which were imaged in the lower three slices, can be easily determined using the magnitude images given their relatively large sizes and limited numbers.
However, for distal small arterial segments, such as M4, A4, and P4 in the top slice, due to their spread distribution and small vessel size, we introduced akâ€means clustering algorithm to automatically detect small vessels followed by a visual inspection, which significantly reduces the human burden as well as interoperator variations in the data processing.
Some constraints were also considered during the data processing to improve the reliability of the PI measurement, such as excluding the arteries that were relatively parallel to imaging planes and reducing contaminations from small veins.
All these approaches can help improve the reliability of cerebral PI assessments.
Aging is accompanied by the loss of elastin and increased collagen deposition in vessel walls, resulting in reduced arterial compliance, which can be represented as elevated arterial pulsatility and impaired damping along the cerebrovascular tree [23,42].
Assessing both arterial pulsatility and damping properties could advance our knowledge of the role of vascular dysfunctions in aging and neurovascular pathologies.
A large number of agingâ€related studies have demonstrated elevated arterial pulsatility in cerebral arteries but are mainly limited to the relatively larger arterial segments.
In this study, consistently increased PI was obtained across all vessel segments in elderly adults, suggesting that aging induces a global effect on PI throughout the cerebral arterial tree.
On the other hand, damping is another important vascular feature.
Previous studies have shown that alterations in arterial damping may increase the number of perivascular spaces and exacerbate cognitive impairment [32].
MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI simultaneously images PIs of multiple arterial segments along the cerebral arterial tree, allowing for assessing DF from proximal to distal arterial segments.
In this study, the young adults showed gradually decreased DF along the arterial segments from ICAâ€M2/3 to M2/3â€M4, suggesting the cerebral arterial system smoothly damps the pulsatile energy to protect the downstream vascular system.
Oppositely, the older adults had lower DF in the proximal ICAâ€M2/3 but much higher DF in the downstream M2/3â€M4 segment, indicating that increased pulsatile energy needs to be damped in the downstream arteries, which may cause a significant burden to downstream small vessels and eventually contribute to vascular dysfunction and parenchymal vascular injury.
Our study has limitations.
Firstly, arterial segments including M1, A1, and P1/2 were not imaged using MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI in this study, as those segments were relatively parallel to the imaging slice (Figure1a).
Theoretically, MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI with three velocity encoding directions can solve this issue, which, however, can double both scan time and minimal temporal resolution.
Previous studies have reported similar PIs between ICA and M1 and between VA and P1/2 using 2D PCâ€MRI [15].
In the present study, we imaged both proximal and distal ICA and VA segments, and the distal ones could approximate the arterial pulsatility of M1 and P1/2.
Secondly, a single throughâ€plane velocity encoding was used in this study given the fact that the majority of the orientation of cerebral arterial segments is along the interior to superior direction and is thus relatively perpendicular to the imaging slice.
However, according to the previous work as well as our simulation and in vivo data [9], PI measurement using a single velocity encoding direction does not cause a significant difference in PI measurement compared to that with three velocity encoding directions.
To refine the PI measurements, during the data processing, the detected vessels that had crossâ€sectional lengths at least twice greater than their width were excluded for further analysis [41].
Furthermore, PI is defined as the ratio of peak velocity difference to the mean velocity; therefore, the PI calculation is relatively insensitive to the absolute velocities.
Thirdly, we reduced the bandwidth to boost image SNR and thus improve the delineation of small distal arteries, that is, M4, P3/4, and A3/4.
However, the lower bandwidth adversely led to a relatively longer TE (9.62 ms).
Additional MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI with a shorter TE of (6.49 ms) was initially tested.
Compared to our current protocol, velocity curves with stronger noise were observed as a result of SNR loss with a shorter TE.
Further optimization of sequence parameters will be conducted and evaluated in the future study.
Fourthly, although this study primarily focused on the feasibility of multiband PCâ€MRI in cerebral PI measurements, the multiband RF can be further optimized in MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI.
In this study, the specific absorption rate (SAR) and RF highâ€voltage limits were not exceeded with the current multiband RF design [20].
However, advanced RF pulse design methods [43,44] can be considered to reduce peak amplitude without increasing SAR.
The gâ€factor map can be further optimized by considering the relative spatial distances among the four slices.
According to the slice distance, the four slices can be grouped into two and then shifted the two groups (the top two slices vs. the bottom two slices) by FOV/2 and the two slices within each group by FOV/4 to further reduce gâ€factor.
FigureS5shows a single case with slightly reduced gâ€factor through this optimization, compared to the current approach.
However, no notable difference was observed between the resulting PI measurements (FigureS5c).
Further evaluation will be conducted in the future study.
Furthermore, although the testâ€“retest study was conducted in a research cohort with a relatively broad age range, a systematic evaluation of the testâ€“retest reproducibility of the proposed technique, especially in elderly participants, will be needed when conducting ageâ€related research.
In conclusion, this study introduces a fast PCâ€MRI technique by integrating SMS or multiband technique and dualâ€VENC strategy into the conventional 2D PCâ€MRI sequence (MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI) to achieve simultaneous assessment of cerebral arterial pulsatility across multiple arterial segments in the brain.
Our work demonstrates the feasibility and reliability of using MBâ€DV PCâ€MRI to measure arterial pulsatility and DF across multiple arterial segments along the cerebral vascular tree.
