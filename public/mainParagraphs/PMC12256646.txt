Rice quality is evaluated based on both extrinsic and intrinsic characteristics such as appearance, cooking, and sensory attributes (Cuevas etÂ al.,2016). FLAR's Grain Quality Lab has created and applied methods to assess grain quality, focusing on three key components: shape and appearance, milling efficiency, and cooking and eating qualities. Grain shape and appearance, determined by length and width, are key breeding targets, as they play a crucial role in classifying rice into the market categories â€œshort,â€ â€œmedium,â€ and â€œlongâ€ types (Webb,1991). Milling efficiency refers to the rice grain's capacity to endure the dehulling and polishing processes without breaking. Various automated imageâ€based methods for quantifying WBRK proportions in rice have been developed, offering higher accuracy and lower labor costs compared to manual inspection techniques (Santos etÂ al.,2019; Wang etÂ al.,2022).

Rice's physical and chemical characteristics help determine its cooking and eating qualities. Amylose content (AC) and gelatinization temperature (GT) are the most common traits used by rice breeding programs to ascertain rice texture and cooking properties (Pang etÂ al.,2016). AC is the primary factor influencing the texture of cooked rice, determining whether it will be soft, firm, or inâ€between. Rice varieties with high AC are firm, dry, and fluffy when cooked, while low AC varieties result in a soft, moist, and sticky texture (Karim etÂ al.,2024). Due to its importance, AC has long been determined in breeding programs using the colorimetric iodine method. Recently, nearâ€infrared (NIR) spectroscopy has emerged as a nonâ€destructive, costâ€effective, and efficient method for estimating AC in rice (Lino etÂ al.,2024). GT dictates the cooking time for rice and is defined as the temperature at which rice absorbs water and starch granules undergo irreversible swelling. Along with AC and GT, the pasting properties of starch, which reflect the transition of the starch granules from swelling to breakdown during cooking, are closely linked to the eating quality of cooked rice (L. Rapid visco analyzer (RVA) instruments are cooking stirring viscometer with ramped temperature and variable shear profiles optimized for testing starch pasting properties such as breakdown viscosity (BDv), final viscosity (FV), setback viscosity (SBv), peak time viscosity (PkT), and pasting temperature (PsT). These properties reflect the stability of the rice during cooking. Typically, high BDv values indicate better gel consistency, while low SBv values are associated with softness and improved sensory qualities (Pang etÂ al.,2016). Creating RVA profiles is timeâ€intensive, expensive, and necessitates specialized equipment. Several studies have found strong phenotypic and genetic correlations between pasting properties, AC, and GT (Cruz etÂ al.,2021; Pang etÂ al.,2016).

To date, breeding for grain quality attributes has primarily depended on phenotypic selection. However, molecularâ€based approaches like markerâ€assisted selection (MAS) and genomic selection (GS) present opportunities to enhance breeding efficiency, increase selection intensity, and reduce the duration of breeding cycles for grain quality (Hayes etÂ al.,2017; Liu etÂ al.,2019). In our previous study, Cruz etÂ al. (2021), identified four functional markers, three located in theWxand one in theSSIIagene, that were significantly associated with seven grain cooking quality and viscosity traits, including AC, GT, BDv, FV, SBv, PkT, and PsT. Additionally, we conducted crossâ€validation analyses to evaluate the predictive abilities of four different univariate GS models for 10 quality traits, laying the groundwork for effective GS strategies for grain quality in breeding programs across Latin America and the Caribbean.

Grain appearance, milling, cooking, and eating quality are key breeding objectives for rice breeding programs. At FLAR, grain quality profiles generated with traits such as AC, GT, BDv, FV, SBv, peak time viscosity (PkT), PsT, GL, GW, GLWR, WBRK, OHRR, and DHRR are utilized by scientists to develop cultivars tailored for diverse markets across Latin America and the Caribbean. For instance, in the tropical region, FLAR aims to develop new varieties with AC â‰¥ 26.5%, GT â‰¥ 5, BDv â‰¤ 800 mPa/s, SBv â‰¥ 1600 mPa/s, GLWR â‰¥ 3.2, WBRK â‰¤ 7.08%, and OHRR â‰¥ 60%. Genetically, the architectures of these grain quality traits range from simple (such as AC, GT, GL, and GW) to highly complex (such as OHRR and DHRR) (Zhao etÂ al.,2022; H. Zhou etÂ al.,2020). As we showed in Figure1, these traits also exhibit varying degrees of genetic relationships, spanning from strong negative (AC and BDv; and WBRK and DHRR) to strong positive correlations (GT and PsT; and AC and SBv) (Alâ€Daej,2022; Cruz etÂ al.,2021; Pokhrel etÂ al.,2020). Several of these traits, including OHRR, DHRR, BDv, and SBv, are laborâ€intensive, destructive, and require large sample sizes and specialized equipment. As a result, primary milling and cooking traits are typically evaluated only in the later stages of the breeding pipeline (Aoun etÂ al.,2022). Frequently, advanced breeding lines with excellent agronomic characteristics do not meet milling and quality standards, preventing them from being released as varieties (Battenfield etÂ al.,2016). Therefore, it is crucial to assess and select breeding lines earlier in the breeding process to avoid such setbacks. Molecularâ€based selection methods, such as MAS or GS, offer a valuable solution by enabling earlier advancement and parental selection, thereby significantly improving breeding efficiency. In this study, we test how incorporating MC and easyâ€toâ€phenotype secondary traits in different GS models could improve predictive accuracies for primary grain milling and cooking quality traits.

Incorporating molecular markers tagging major genes or QTLs as fixed effects in GS models can improve predictive ability in wheat stem rust (Rutkoski etÂ al.,2014),capsaicinoidcontent in Capsicum (Kim etÂ al.,2022), and rice plant height and flowering time (Spindel etÂ al.,2016). Bernardo (2014) proposed that if the QTL explains >10% of the genetic variation, it could be incorporated into GS models and potentially increase the predictive ability. When we included WX or SSIIaâ€3B markers as fixed effects (MC models), we observed a substantial increase in predictive ability for our secondary cooking traits, AC and GT, ranging from 48% to 60% (Figure2; Table3), and our primary cooking traits, BDv, FV, SBv, PkT, and PsT, ranging from 21.5% to 44% (Figure2; Table3). Previous results showed that WX and SSIIaâ€3B explained 60% and 75% of the phenotypic variability for AC (Cruz etÂ al.,2021; Dobo etÂ al.,2010; Teng etÂ al.,2017) and GT (Cruz etÂ al.,2021; Umemoto & Aoki,2005; Umemoto etÂ al.,2004). These same markers were also significant in our GWAS results for BDv, FV, SBv, PkT, and PsT (FigureS2) for AC and GT. Although our GWAS identified additional SNPs asscociated with our primary and secondary traits, we selected the functional trait markers as covariates because the additional peaks were located within the same linkage blocks as the functional SNPs. Our results strongly suggest that FLAR will significantly benefit by incorporating WX and SSIIaâ€3B markers as covariates in the GS approach to improve rice cooking quality.

In this study, we observed strong and significant phenotypic and genotypic correlations between secondary grain quality traits, AC, GT, GL, GW, GLWR, and WBRK, with primary milling, OHRR and DHRR, as well as primary cooking traits, BDv, FV, SBv, PkT, and PsT, quality traits (Figure1). These correlations offer a valuable opportunity to enhance rice milling and cooking quality through an MTâ€GS approach (Jia & Jannink,2012). When compared to ST GS models, MTâ€GS has demonstrated improvements in predictive abilities across various crops, including resistance toFusariumin wheat (Gaire etÂ al.,2022), malting quality in barley (Bhatta etÂ al.,2020), baking quality in wheat (Gill etÂ al.,2023), milling quality in oats (Dhakal etÂ al.,2024), and trace elements in rice (Muvunyi etÂ al.,2022). MTâ€GS is particularly effective when secondary traits are highly heritable, costâ€effective, and easy to phenotype on a large scale compared to primary traits (Okeke etÂ al.,2017; Schulthess etÂ al.,2016). Our secondary traits exhibited relatively high broadâ€sense heritability's (Table1) and were assessed using grain imaging (GL, GW, GLWR, and WBRK), nearâ€infrared spectroscopy (AC), and the standard alkali spread digestion test (GT). These methods require only small grain samples (no >6Â g) and can be evaluated at any stage of FLAR's breeding program, making them ideal for integrating into MTâ€GS to improve the predictive abilities for primary milling and cooking quality traits.

Compared to ST models, the inclusion of secondary traits in MTâ€GS models led to an increase in predictive abilities for milling quality traits, with improvements of 13.5% for OHRR and 18% for DHHR, relative to their ST counterparts (Figure2; Table3). For primary cooking traits, BDv, FV, SBv, PkT, and PsT, MTâ€GS models resulted in predictive ability increases of 50%, 4.6%, 30.5%, 21%, and 41.3%, respectively (Figure2; Table3). The smaller increase observed for FV can be attributed to the already high predictive ability in its ST model.

To further assess the impact of trait MC, we tested whether adding WX or SSIIaâ€3B MC, in addition to secondary traits, could improve the predictive ability of primary cooking trait MTâ€GS models (MCâ€MT). Our results showed that the predictive abilities of the MCâ€MT models were similar to those of the corresponding MT models, except for PkT, where MCâ€MT showed a greater improvement (Figure2; Table3). For all primary cooking traits, AC, GT, or both were the traits most highly correlated with the primary traits. The similarities between the MT and MCâ€MT models suggest that AC and GT phenotypes provided similar predictive information as their respective trait markers, WX and SSIIaâ€3B. The improvement in prediction accuracy for PkT may be due to the addition of a covariate into the MTâ€GS model when secondary traits exhibited moderate correlations (0.30 and 0.34) with the primary trait.

Overall, our study demonstrates the feasibility of incorporating wholeâ€genome markers, trait markers, and secondary trait information to enhance the predictive ability of GS for grain milling, cooking, and eating qualities in rice. For milling quality, we suggest using MT models, while for cooking quality, either MC or MT models should be considered. These insights can help guide rice breeding programs in developing new strategies to rapidly meet global quality demands.
